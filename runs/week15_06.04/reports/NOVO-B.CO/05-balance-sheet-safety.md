## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure: Rapid but Manageable Increase.** Novo Nordisk's balance sheet has undergone a dramatic transformation in two years. Total debt rose from DKK 27B at end-FY2023 to DKK 131B at end-FY2025, with long-term debt accounting for DKK 112B of the total. Net debt stands at DKK 96B after subtracting DKK 27B in cash. This debt increase funded the DKK 75B acquisition of three Catalent manufacturing sites and ongoing greenfield construction. The company utilizes a Euro Medium Term Note (EMTN) programme listed on Euronext Dublin for bond issuance, with known issuances including a EUR-denominated bond at 3.375% maturing May 2034 (XS2820460751). The debt is predominantly fixed-rate, limiting exposure to floating-rate risk. While DKK 19B sits in short-term debt (current portion), the majority of obligations are long-term. Debt/Equity rose from 25.3% to 67.5% over two years, a meaningful shift but still moderate relative to the earnings base. The Debt/EBITDA ratio of 0.8x and Net Debt/EBITDA of 0.6x remain conservative by any standard and well below the 2.0x comfort threshold. This is a company that took on significant debt from a near-zero starting point, which is fundamentally different from a company layering leverage onto an already indebted balance sheet.

**Interest Coverage: Extremely Strong.** EBIT/Interest expense stands at 32.0x for FY2025, down from the extraordinary 194x of FY2023 but still far above the 5x comfort threshold. Interest expense rose from DKK 542M (FY2023) to DKK 4.2B (FY2025) as the debt load grew, but operating income of DKK 128B dwarfs this obligation. Even if operating income were to halve (an extreme scenario), interest coverage would remain above 15x. The cost of debt is low: the quant model derives a pre-tax cost of debt of 3.2% (after-tax 2.5%), reflecting the company's top-tier credit standing. Given EBITDA of DKK 157B, annual interest represents just 2.7% of EBITDA, meaning debt service consumes a trivial share of cash generation.

**Liquidity: Adequate but Tight on a Current-Ratio Basis.** Cash and short-term investments of DKK 27B provide a meaningful buffer, though not an excessive one relative to the company's scale. The current ratio of 0.8x (current assets DKK 173B vs. current liabilities DKK 216B) appears below the 1.0x threshold, but this is common for large pharmaceutical companies that manage payables aggressively and carry substantial deferred revenue and accrued rebates as current liabilities. Negative working capital of DKK -43B is a structural feature of the business model, not a liquidity warning: pharma companies collect receivables faster than they pay suppliers and accrue significant government rebate liabilities. Operating cash flow of DKK 119B per year provides ample coverage for any near-term obligations. The company also has access to undrawn credit facilities (typical for companies of this rating), though specific facility sizes were not disclosed in the publicly available data reviewed.

**Refinancing Risk: Low.** With Moody's Aa3 (positive outlook) and S&P AA (stable outlook) ratings, Novo Nordisk has among the strongest credit profiles in global healthcare. Both agencies upgraded the company in 2025, reflecting confidence in the GLP-1 franchise's durability despite near-term pricing headwinds. At these rating levels, capital markets access is essentially assured even in stressed environments. The EMTN programme provides a standing framework for efficient bond issuance across maturities and currencies. The fixed-rate structure of existing debt means the company is not exposed to near-term rate increases on outstanding obligations. The primary refinancing risk would come from a multi-notch downgrade, which is not realistic given the earnings profile: even under the MFN pricing scenario, Novo Nordisk generates over DKK 100B in EBITDA annually, making debt service a non-issue. Long-term debt of DKK 112B with a weighted average cost around 3.2% implies annual interest of roughly DKK 3.5B on the long-term portion, manageable under virtually any reasonable stress scenario.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Capital lease obligations were approximately DKK 8.6B as of end-FY2025, a modest figure relative to the balance sheet. Pension obligations for a Danish company of this scale are typically well-funded, as Denmark's pension system is among the strongest globally, and Novo Nordisk has historically maintained minimal net pension liabilities. The company does carry contingent liabilities related to ongoing patent litigation (semaglutide biosimilar challenges) and potential government pricing actions, but these are not quantifiable as balance sheet items. No material off-balance-sheet financing vehicles, guarantees, or unconsolidated entities were identified in the reviewed data. The acquisition of Catalent sites was a direct asset purchase, not a lease-back or structured finance arrangement, which is conservative.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** If revenue dropped 30% from DKK 309B to approximately DKK 216B and stayed there for two years, with gross margins compressing to 75% (conservative assumption given input cost stickiness and mix shift), gross profit would be approximately DKK 162B. Assuming operating costs (R&D + SG&A) could be reduced 15% from current levels (DKK 130B combined down to DKK 110B), operating income would be approximately DKK 52B. Interest coverage at that level would still be approximately 12x on current interest expense of DKK 4.2B. Net Debt/EBITDA (using stressed EBITDA of approximately DKK 65B) would rise to approximately 1.5x, still below the 2.0x comfort threshold. FCF would be positive even at stressed levels, assuming capex is cut to DKK 25-30B (maintenance levels). The company would not need to issue equity. It could continue operating, servicing debt, and maintaining R&D investment at a reduced but meaningful level. This is a fundamentally survivable stress scenario, reinforced by the chronic nature of demand for diabetes and obesity treatments, which makes a sustained 30% revenue decline unlikely absent a complete product safety event.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Balance sheet leverage is low relative to cash generation capacity, credit ratings are at multi-year highs, and the debt funds productive manufacturing assets that will generate returns for decades; Novo Nordisk can comfortably service its obligations even in a severe downturn.
- **Bear case:** Debt quintupled in two years during a period of peak earnings, and if the MFN pricing reset compresses EBITDA by 20-30% while debt remains elevated, leverage ratios could deteriorate from "very comfortable" to merely "adequate," reducing financial flexibility during a critical investment phase.
- **Confidence:** High — The absolute debt levels are manageable relative to cash generation, the credit profile is among the strongest in the industry, and the stress test confirms survival under severe conditions; the main uncertainty is the pace at which MFN pricing compresses the EBITDA cushion.

## Red Flags

- Total debt increased DKK 104B in two years (from DKK 27B to DKK 131B), the most aggressive leverage increase in Novo Nordisk's history
- Negative working capital of DKK -43B limits the liquid asset buffer available in a sudden liquidity crunch
- Share buybacks dropped from DKK 30B to DKK 1.4B, suggesting management sees the balance sheet as less flexible than in prior years
- If 2026 EBITDA declines 15-20% (consistent with guided revenue decline plus margin compression), Debt/EBITDA would rise to approximately 1.0-1.2x, still safe but moving in the wrong direction

## Score: 7 / 10

Novo Nordisk's balance sheet remains solid with 0.8x Debt/EBITDA, 32x interest coverage, Aa3/AA credit ratings, and clear stress-test survivability, but the five-fold debt increase in two years during a period of peak earnings and the coincidence of maximum leverage with a structural pricing reset prevent the fortress-balance-sheet score (8+) that would have applied just two years ago.
