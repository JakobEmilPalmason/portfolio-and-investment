## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Novo Nordisk's ownership structure is dominated by the Novo Nordisk Foundation, which holds approximately 28% of total equity but 77% of voting rights through unlisted Class A shares carrying 10x the votes of Class B shares. This makes the Foundation the de facto controlling shareholder. Executive insider ownership is reported at effectively 0% of total shares — a function of the massive market cap (DKK 1.1T) rather than lack of equity compensation. Executives receive long-term incentive plans (LTIPs) tied to share-based awards, but reported insider transactions in 2025 were predominantly sales, not purchases. The Foundation structure means "skin in the game" operates differently here than at a typical US corporation: the Foundation's charter mandates it support scientific and humanitarian purposes, and its economic interest in Novo Nordisk's long-term success is existential — the Foundation's entire endowment depends on Novo's performance. This is a meaningful form of alignment, arguably stronger than typical executive stock ownership, but it operates at the governance level rather than the management level.

**Capital allocation track record.** The capital allocation story over the past four years is one of radical transformation. In FY2022, Novo allocated DKK 14.8B to capex, DKK 24.1B to buybacks, and carried only DKK 25.8B in total debt — a classic capital-light, shareholder-return-heavy model. By FY2025, capex had surged to DKK 90.1B (6x the FY2022 level), buybacks collapsed to DKK 1.4B, and total debt rose to DKK 131B. This transformation was driven by the $11B acquisition of three Catalent manufacturing sites, the $6B Kalundborg expansion, the $4.1B Clayton, North Carolina facility, and the $2.2B Chartres, France upgrade. The question is whether this massive capital deployment was the right call. The bull case is that manufacturing capacity was the binding constraint on GLP-1 revenue growth, and Novo was rationally investing at the point of maximum demand visibility. The bear case is that the capex surge coincided with the period when competitive dynamics shifted against Novo (Lilly's efficacy advantage, pricing pressure), meaning Novo may have built capacity at peak-cycle economics. FCF conversion collapsed from 115% (FY2022) to 28% (FY2025), and free cash flow fell from DKK 70B to DKK 29B despite revenue growing 75% over the same period. The DKK 15B buyback program announced for 2026 represents a reduced but continued commitment to shareholder returns during the transition.

**Compensation design and stock-based compensation.** Novo's executive compensation follows a European pharma model: base salary, short-term cash incentive, and long-term share-based incentive (LTIP). The LTIP for 2023 vested at 92% of maximum, driven by strong FY2023-2024 performance, but the LTIP for 2024 and 2025 is tracking below targets — a direct reflection of the share price decline and weaker financial performance. This is genuine pay-for-performance. Former CEO Jorgensen received total separation compensation of approximately DKK 123.6M (salary, notice pay, severance, and non-compete), which is substantial but not egregious for a CEO who led the company through its most transformative growth period. New CEO Doustdar's 2025 compensation totaled DKK 20.7M for the partial year. Stock-based compensation was DKK 2.3B in FY2024 (0.8% of revenue) — well below the excessive levels seen at US tech companies. Share count has been declining over the medium term thanks to buybacks, though the pace slowed dramatically in FY2025.

**Candor and consistency.** The CEO transition itself is a mixed signal on candor. The official narrative cited "recent market challenges" and the share price decline, but the abruptness of the transition (Jorgensen had led the company since 2017 and was widely credited with the GLP-1 strategy) raised analyst concerns about undisclosed issues. BioSpace reported analysts worried "there's something pretty wrong here." Doustdar's subsequent communication has been more direct: at JPMorgan Healthcare 2026, he explicitly framed the challenge as "execution, execution, execution" and laid out three clear priorities (commercial acceleration, pipeline progression, financial discipline). The decision to cut 9,000 jobs and target DKK 8B in annual savings is a candid acknowledgment that the cost structure had grown unsustainable during the growth years. The FY2026 guidance of 5-13% revenue and profit decline was a stark but honest message to the market. On balance, the new management team appears to be communicating more forthrightly than the late-stage Jorgensen era.

**Governance structure.** The Novo Nordisk Foundation's 77% voting control is the defining governance feature. On the positive side: the Foundation has a 100+ year time horizon, eliminates activist shareholder disruption, and has demonstrated willingness to change management when performance deteriorates (the Jorgensen removal). The Foundation's charter requires it to maintain a "stable basis" for the commercial activities of Novo Nordisk, which provides structural protection against value-destructive short-termism. On the negative side: minority B-share holders have essentially no governance voice. The Foundation could theoretically make decisions that serve its scientific mission at the expense of minority shareholder returns. The dual-class structure is permanent and cannot be unwound by minority shareholders. Board independence is limited by the Foundation's control: four statutory board members plus three employee representatives, with the Foundation's influence permeating all major decisions. For a long-term investor aligned with the Foundation's mission (scientific advancement in metabolic disease), this governance structure is a feature. For a shareholder seeking standard corporate governance rights, it is a permanent limitation.

**Do they talk like owners or politicians?** The new CEO Doustdar talks like an operator. His messaging at JPM 2026 was specific, action-oriented, and acknowledged the competitive challenges directly. The 9,000-job reduction and DKK 8B cost savings target are concrete actions, not vague promises. The Wegovy price cuts were described as a strategic decision to expand the market rather than dressed up as altruism. The Foundation's stewardship, while paternalistic in structure, has produced a 100-year track record of reinvesting in the core business. The overall tone is closer to owner than politician, tempered by the reality that a Foundation-controlled company will always prioritize institutional continuity over pure shareholder value maximization.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The Foundation's permanent 77% voting control and 100+ year time horizon, combined with a new operationally focused CEO executing concrete cost discipline (9,000 job cuts, DKK 8B savings), suggest management is making rational long-term bets on manufacturing capacity while protecting the franchise through the current competitive downturn.
- **Bear case:** The abrupt CEO change, negligible executive insider ownership, massive capex deployment at potentially peak-cycle economics (DKK 90B in FY2025), and FCF collapse (DKK 29B vs DKK 70B two years prior) raise questions about capital allocation discipline during a period of deteriorating competitive position.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The Foundation governance structure provides genuine long-term alignment, but the new CEO has been in place for only 8 months and the capital allocation pivot from buybacks to capex has not yet been validated by improved competitive outcomes.

## Red Flags

- CEO abruptly replaced after 75% stock decline, with analyst commentary suggesting possible undisclosed issues beyond the official narrative
- Total debt surged from DKK 27B (FY2023) to DKK 131B (FY2025) to fund the capex cycle — a nearly 5x increase in two years
- Buybacks collapsed from DKK 29.9B (FY2023) to DKK 1.4B (FY2025), and the DKK 15B FY2026 program is being executed at depressed share prices (positive for per-share value but reflects constrained capital)
- Executive insider ownership is negligible and net insider transactions have been sales, not purchases
- Former CEO separation package of DKK 123.6M is not trivial, though within policy bounds
- Dual-class share structure permanently disenfranchises minority shareholders — this cannot be changed without Foundation consent

## Score: 6 / 10

Novo Nordisk's management and governance present a genuinely mixed picture. The Novo Nordisk Foundation provides a unique long-term stewardship structure that is superior to typical public company governance for patient, mission-aligned investors. The new CEO is communicating clearly and taking concrete cost actions. However, the capital allocation record of the past two years — a 6x capex surge, 5x debt increase, FCF collapse, and buyback suspension during a period of worsening competitive dynamics — raises legitimate questions about whether capital was deployed at the right time and at the right price. Executive insider ownership is negligible. The score of 6 reflects solid structural governance offset by a capital allocation pivot whose wisdom remains unproven and a management transition still in its early stages.
