## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Exceptional but Declining from Peak.** Novo Nordisk generated a 36.0% ROIC in FY2025, down sharply from the 70.5% peak in FY2023. This decline is not due to weakening operations but to a deliberate capital deployment cycle: invested capital rose from DKK 128B to DKK 316B in two years as the company acquired three Catalent fill-finish sites for approximately DKK 75B and ramped greenfield manufacturing across the US, Ireland, and Denmark. ROE followed the same trajectory, falling from 88.1% to 60.7%, while ROA dropped from 26.6% to 18.9%. Even at these "depressed" levels, a 36% ROIC is outstanding by any industry standard and reflects a business that converts capital into profit at rates most pharmaceutical companies never approach. The question is whether these returns stabilize here or compress further as the capital base continues to expand while revenue faces near-term headwinds.

**Margin Structure: Elite Gross, Compressing Operating.** Gross margins have remained remarkably stable in the 81-85% band over four years, reflecting genuine pricing power for semaglutide-based products and an efficient biologic manufacturing platform. Operating margins peaked at 44.2% in FY2023-2024 before declining to 41.3% in FY2025. The compression is driven by two factors: R&D spending rose from DKK 32B to DKK 52B (up 60% in two years) as the pipeline expanded into MASH (via the Akero Therapeutics acquisition), cardiovascular indications, and next-generation oral GLP-1 formulations; and commercial costs scaled alongside the global obesity therapy rollout. Net margins at 33.1% remain robust but are trending lower. The 2026 outlook is more concerning: the MFN agreement slashing US GLP-1 prices by up to 70% will directly hit gross margins on the highest-revenue products. Even with volume offsets, this represents a structural margin reset for the US business.

**Cash Generation: Temporarily Distorted by Investment Cycle.** Reported FCF of DKK 29B in FY2025 paints a misleading picture. Operating cash flow remained strong at DKK 119B, essentially flat with FY2024. The FCF collapse is entirely attributable to capex surging from DKK 15B (FY2022) to DKK 90B (FY2025) as Novo Nordisk invested aggressively in manufacturing capacity. The quant model separates this: maintenance capex was approximately DKK 22B (24% of total), with DKK 68B classified as growth capex. Owner earnings on an adjusted basis were DKK 102B, nearly unchanged from FY2024. This distinction matters enormously: the underlying cash engine is intact. FCF conversion of 28.3% is a temporary artifact, not a structural problem. Management guides capex of approximately DKK 55B for 2026, already a meaningful step-down, and as the Catalent sites and Clayton, NC facility come online, the capex intensity should normalize. The risk is that "normalize" takes longer than expected if demand growth slows under MFN pricing.

**Capital Intensity: Transitioning from Asset-Light to Asset-Medium.** Novo Nordisk was historically one of the most asset-light pharmaceutical businesses, with capex/revenue of just 8.4% in FY2022. That ratio exploded to 29.1% in FY2025 as the company embarked on a multi-year build-out to address GLP-1 supply constraints. The DKK 75B Catalent acquisition added 3 fill-finish sites (Anagni, Bloomington, Brussels), a new DKK 4.1B facility is under construction in Clayton, NC, and a EUR 432M oral GLP-1 tableting expansion was announced in Athlone, Ireland in March 2026. Total manufacturing footprint expanded from 11 to 14+ sites. This is a rational response to a demand problem: supply shortages constrained semaglutide revenues for over two years. But it permanently changes the capital profile. Even at steady state, maintenance capex on a DKK 300B+ asset base will be materially higher than the sub-DKK 15B levels of 2022. The business is becoming more capital-intensive, which will structurally moderate future ROIC even if revenues grow.

**Revenue Quality: Highly Recurring but Facing Pricing Reset.** This is among the highest-quality revenue streams in healthcare. Diabetes and obesity are chronic conditions; patients who respond to GLP-1 therapy typically remain on treatment for years. Wegovy sales reached DKK 79B and Ozempic DKK 127B in FY2025, with the branded GLP-1 obesity market growing 104% by volume. Novo Nordisk holds 59.6% branded volume market share. Revenue is driven by both volume (patient starts, geographic expansion) and price. The MFN agreement fundamentally alters this dynamic: US prices for Wegovy and Ozempic drop from over $1,000/month to approximately $350/month, a 65-70% reduction. Volume growth must dramatically outpace this pricing headwind for revenue to recover. Additionally, semaglutide exclusivity losses in certain international markets open the door to biosimilar competition. The revenue remains recurring, but the revenue per patient is being permanently re-rated downward.

**Operating Leverage: Negative in the Near Term.** From FY2022 to FY2024, Novo Nordisk demonstrated powerful operating leverage: revenue grew 64% while operating income grew 72%. This reversed in FY2025 as revenue grew 6.4% but operating income fell 0.5%, driven by R&D and commercial cost escalation. The 2026 outlook (sales decline of 5-13% at CER) implies further negative operating leverage as fixed costs in manufacturing, R&D, and commercial infrastructure cannot be easily reduced. The long-term operating leverage thesis remains intact if GLP-1 volumes continue expanding globally, but the next 12-18 months represent a trough in the leverage cycle.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The underlying owner earnings engine (DKK 102B) is intact, and the massive capex cycle is building manufacturing capacity that will underpin decades of GLP-1 volume growth once the pricing reset is absorbed.
- **Bear case:** The MFN pricing agreement, combined with semaglutide exclusivity losses and a permanently higher capital base, structurally lowers returns on capital and margins from the extraordinary levels of 2023-2024, with no guarantee volume growth can fully offset 70% US price cuts.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The operating engine is proven and the revenue is recurring, but the magnitude of the pricing reset and its interaction with the capex cycle creates genuine uncertainty about where normalized earnings and ROIC settle.

## Red Flags

- FCF conversion at 28.3% is the lowest in at least four years; even accounting for growth capex, the cash reinvestment rate is unprecedented for this business
- 2026 guidance for 5-13% sales decline at CER is the first revenue contraction in over a decade
- Capex has grown 6x in three years (DKK 15B to DKK 90B) with payback dependent on volume growth under a dramatically lower price regime
- Share buybacks collapsed from DKK 30B (FY2023) to DKK 1.4B (FY2025), signaling management is prioritizing capex over returns to shareholders
- Operating margin compression of 290 bps in FY2025 despite 6% revenue growth suggests cost structure is outgrowing the top line

## Score: 7 / 10

Novo Nordisk retains an exceptional economic engine with 36% ROIC, 81% gross margins, and DKK 102B in owner earnings, but the combination of a massive capex cycle, MFN-driven pricing compression, and near-term revenue decline prevents a higher score; the business is transitioning from an asset-light compounder to a capital-intensive manufacturer during a period of unprecedented pricing pressure, and normalized economics will be materially lower than the 2023-2024 peak.
