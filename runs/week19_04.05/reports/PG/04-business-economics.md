## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital — the core machine.** P&G's ROIC has sat in a tight 18.5%–19.5% band for four straight fiscal years (FY2022: 19.0%, FY2023: 18.5%, FY2024: 19.5%, FY2025: 19.4%). ROE is even higher at ~31% because of moderate financial leverage. These are not cyclical peaks — they are mid-cycle steady-state returns on $80–86B of invested capital. For a $342B market-cap business operating in mature categories (laundry, oral care, diapers, shaving, beauty), generating ~19% returns on capital this consistently is the signature of a portfolio of high-share, premium-priced brands with scale advantages in retail shelf space, advertising, and supply chain. By the rubric, this alone puts the score in the 8+ zone.

**Margin structure — pricing power confirmed, with a small wobble.** Gross margin moved from 47.4% (FY2022) → 47.9% (FY2023) → 51.4% (FY2024) → 51.2% (FY2025). That ~400 bps gross margin expansion through the post-pandemic input cost cycle is the best quantitative evidence of pricing power you can find — P&G raised prices, held volume well enough, and kept the spread when commodities and freight rolled over. Operating margin tracked the same path (22.2% → 24.3%) with a touch of operating leverage as SG&A grew slower than revenue. The Q3 FY2026 earnings call (April 2026) flagged that core gross margin slipped 100 bps year-on-year to 50.0% and core operating margin slipped 80 bps to 22.2% on tariff impacts and cost/mix pressure, partly offset by productivity. This is a real but mild headwind, not a structural break — pricing was still +1% and volume was +2%, so the volume engine is healthy. I'd treat the FY2026 margin compression as a one-cycle reset, not a new normal, but it's worth watching.

**Cash generation — FCF is the real metric.** Free cash flow was $13.6B (FY2022), $13.8B (FY2023), $16.5B (FY2024), and $14.0B (FY2025) — ~$58B of cumulative FCF over four years on a market cap of $342B. FCF conversion (FCF / Net Income) has run 88%–111% over the period; the FY2024 spike to 111% reflects working capital release. Owner earnings (Net Income + D&A − maintenance capex) were ~$15.0B in FY2025 per the quant model's calculation, very close to reported FCF, which means there's no accounting magic — net income and cash earnings are aligned. The fact that maintenance capex is ~75% of total capex per the quant model means the business does need ongoing reinvestment, but it's modest in absolute terms (~3.4% of revenue).

**Capital intensity and revenue quality.** Capex has crept up from $3.1B (FY2023) to $3.8B (FY2025), about 4.5% of revenue. That's not asset-light like Visa or Moody's, but it's far from asset-heavy. P&G runs a mix of owned manufacturing, distribution, and brand-building spend that requires steady reinvestment. Revenue is volume × price across ~10 categories and 7 regions; it is genuinely recurring (consumers buy detergent and toothpaste every month) but not contractual. There is no SaaS-style net retention number to point at, but global category share data and 100+ year brand histories (Tide, Pampers, Gillette, Crest, Olay) substitute. Volume-driven growth (+2% in Q3 FY2026) is healthier than pricing-driven growth, because pricing eventually has to be earned through innovation rather than just passed-through inflation.

**Operating leverage and capital returns.** Revenue grew from $80.2B (FY2022) to $84.3B (FY2025), only ~5% cumulative — but operating income grew from $17.8B to $20.5B, ~15%. That's modest operating leverage from productivity savings and mix, not from volume scale (volume has been roughly flat over the period). Net income compounded from $14.7B to $16.0B (~9% over three years). The bigger story for shareholders is share count: P&G has bought back $6.5B (FY2025), $5.0B (FY2024), $7.4B (FY2023), and $10.0B (FY2022) — roughly $29B over four years. With a market cap around $342B, that's ~8.5% of the float retired in four years on top of a 2.94% dividend yield. The economic engine is moderately growing the pie and shrinking the slice count.

**Q3 FY2026 caveat.** The April 2026 quarter (Q3 FY2026) was the softest in recent memory: core EPS up only 3% to $1.59, currency-neutral core EPS flat, and tariff impacts compressing margins. Management held the FY2026 guide of 0%–4% organic sales / 1%–5% net sales. This is consistent with a slow-growth, mature-portfolio business hitting a tariff/FX cost cycle, not a thesis break — but it caps how high economics can score versus a Visa or MSCI.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Productivity savings reaccelerate, Beauty (+7% organic in Q3) keeps leading, tariff impact fades through CY2026, and FY2027 margins recover to FY2025 levels (~24% operating) on $87–88B of revenue, producing ~$15–16B of FCF.
- **Bear case:** Tariffs and private-label pressure compress operating margin to ~22% sustainably, organic growth stalls at 0–2%, FCF flatlines at $13–14B, and ROIC drifts down to 17% as capex stays elevated.
- **Confidence:** High — P&G has 100+ years of history, the financials are clean, audited, and well-disclosed, and there is no single-product or single-customer concentration risk to the economics.

## Red Flags

- Q3 FY2026 core operating margin compressed 80 bps to 22.2% on tariffs/cost; if this persists into FY2027, the recent gross margin expansion to 51%+ would be at risk.
- Revenue growth of ~5% cumulative over FY2022–FY2025 is slow even by staples standards — the business needs price + mix to generate per-share earnings growth, since volume is roughly flat.
- Capex has risen ~22% (from $3.1B to $3.8B) while revenue rose only ~3% over FY2023→FY2025; if maintenance capex is genuinely rising, owner earnings are softer than reported FCF suggests.
- Working capital is structurally negative (-$10.7B) — not a flag in itself for a staples giant, but a reminder that the business runs on supplier financing; a credit shock would matter.

## Score: 8 / 10

Stable mid-to-high-teens ROIC, expanding gross margins, ~$14B of clean FCF, and conservative-yet-shareholder-friendly capital allocation put P&G squarely in the "strong economic engine" tier; what keeps it from a 9 is slow underlying growth and the recent FY2026 margin compression.
