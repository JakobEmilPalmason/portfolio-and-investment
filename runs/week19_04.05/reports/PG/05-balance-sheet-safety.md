## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and structure.** As of FY2025 (June 2025 fiscal year-end), P&G carried $35.5B of total debt against $9.6B of cash, for net debt of $25.0B. EBITDA was $23.9B, putting Total Debt/EBITDA at 1.5x and Net Debt/EBITDA at 1.0x. For context, the rubric calls "below 2x comfortable" and "above 4x concerning" — P&G is comfortably in the lowest-leverage band for a non-tech mega-cap. Long-term debt is $25.0B (the same as net debt, broadly), with the remaining ~$10B in short-term obligations (commercial paper and current portion of long-term debt). Debt has been remarkably stable across FY2022–FY2025 (range $32.3B–$35.5B), so management is treating the balance sheet as a steady-state capital structure, not a deleveraging or releveraging story.

**Interest coverage — comfortably fortress-grade.** Operating income (EBIT) of $20.5B in FY2025 against $907M of interest expense gives interest coverage of 23.2x. The historical range across FY2022–FY2025 is 21.3x–42.0x; the FY2022 spike reflected lower interest expense before refinancings at higher rates. Even at the low end of the range (FY2024's 21.3x), P&G could cover interest from operating earnings 21 times over. By comparison, the rubric flags below 3x as warning and below 1.5x as distress. P&G is not in the same ZIP code as either threshold. Interest expense has been $440M–$925M over the four years; the increase reflects the post-2022 rate environment, not increased borrowing — debt principal has been roughly flat.

**Liquidity and refinancing risk.** Cash and short-term investments of $9.6B is meaningful but not overwhelming relative to the $341B market cap. The bigger source of liquidity is FCF generation: $14.0B in FY2025, with the run rate consistently $13–17B. P&G also has substantial committed credit facilities (typical for an Aa3/AA- issuer) and has historically been a heavy user of commercial paper, which depends on capital markets staying open. That said, with Aa3/AA- ratings, P&G can refinance even in tight markets — its commercial paper is among the highest-rated unsecured corporate paper in the U.S. The fact that I could not retrieve a specific year-by-year maturity ladder from web search is a small gap in this analysis, but the rating agencies' affirmations and the EBITDA coverage make it implausible that P&G has a refinancing wall it can't handle.

**The current ratio question.** Current ratio is 0.7x — current liabilities ($36.1B) exceed current assets ($25.4B) by $10.7B. In isolation, this looks like a yellow flag. In context, it's how a high-velocity consumer staples business runs: P&G collects cash from retailers within 30–45 days while paying suppliers on 60–90+ day terms, plus it carries ~$30B of accounts payable and accrued liabilities funded by inventory turnover. Negative working capital is structural and stable (-$8.9B to -$13.1B over four years), and it's a feature of the business model, not a sign of distress. It does mean that in a true credit shock — where suppliers demanded faster payment — P&G would feel the pinch faster than a positive-working-capital business. But with $9.6B cash, $14B/year FCF, and committed credit facilities, this is a manageable risk, not a fatal one.

**Off-balance-sheet items.** I did not retrieve specific operating lease, pension, or contingent liability figures from web search. Under current accounting rules, operating leases are largely on the balance sheet already. Historical context: P&G's pension obligations have been a discussion point in years past but have generally been manageable for an investment-grade global staples company; a fully-funded or near-fully-funded plan is the norm at this rating tier. This is a small gap I'd close before sizing a position larger than ~3%.

**Stress test — could P&G survive a bad 2-3 years?** The relevant Buffett question: if revenue dropped 30% for two years, would the company need to issue equity? Roughly, $84B of revenue at 24% operating margin gives $20B EBIT. A 30% revenue drop with margin compression to ~18% would still produce ~$10–11B of EBIT — more than 11x interest expense. Even with FCF cut in half to ~$7B, that's enough to service the dividend ($10B/year, the one place this might pinch) and roll debt. P&G has paid an uninterrupted dividend for 130+ years through the Great Depression, both World Wars, and the 2008 financial crisis without cutting. The balance sheet is genuinely built for survival, not optimization.

**Credit rating context.** Aa3 (Moody's) and AA- (S&P) place P&G in the top tier of corporate credit globally — only a handful of non-financial corporates (Microsoft, Johnson & Johnson, a few others) carry equivalent or higher ratings. These are not BBB- "barely investment grade" ratings; they are fortress ratings affirmed in January 2025 with stable outlooks. The rating agencies have full access to P&G's debt schedule, off-balance-sheet items, and stress-test models, and their conclusion is unambiguous.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Net debt continues to drift lower through earnings growth (no active paydown needed), interest coverage stays north of 20x, and ratings remain at Aa3/AA- through any reasonable cycle — this is a balance sheet that essentially never becomes a constraint.
- **Bear case:** A multi-year tariff/cost shock combined with a strong dollar compresses EBITDA to ~$20B, pushing Net Debt/EBITDA to ~1.3x — still investment grade, still comfortable, just less of a margin of safety.
- **Confidence:** High — the numbers are simple, the ratings are explicit, and P&G's balance sheet history through multiple recessions provides strong empirical evidence.
