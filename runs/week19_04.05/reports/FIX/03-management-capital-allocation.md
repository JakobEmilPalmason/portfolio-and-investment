## Detailed Analysis

**Tenure and track record.** Brian Lane has been CEO for 16 years and is the most important person to evaluate here. Under his leadership, FIX has executed the playbook that defines the company today: roll up specialty contractors, layer in modular capacity, ride the data-center cycle. The hard numbers: revenue ~$1.4B in 2010 to $9.1B in FY2025 (~14% CAGR), operating margin from low single digits to 14.4%, and ROIC from sub-20% historically to 47.5%. That is a long, consistent compounding record — not a single lucky year.

**Capital allocation framework.** Public commentary and capital deployment patterns indicate a clear hierarchy: (1) reinvest in organic growth (modular factories, labor); (2) acquire bolt-on specialty contractors that bring labor, geography, or capability; (3) return what is left via dividends and opportunistic buybacks. M&A has been the dominant use of capital, consuming ~75% of capital deployed 2007-2024 according to LevelFields. The deal cadence has been 3-5 per year on average; 2023 alone saw 3 acquisitions, including DECCO (life-sciences piping); Summit Industrial Construction was acquired Jan 2024 for ~$360-400M of annualized revenue. The track record is solid — gross margin and ROIC expansion have run *while* M&A continued, which is the cleanest evidence of accretive deals (poor M&A typically dilutes ROIC). No material write-downs or impairments are visible in the financials.

**Buybacks.** $216M in FY2025, up from $58M in FY2024 and $21M in FY2023. The 2025 step-up is meaningful but small relative to the ~$70B market cap (~0.3% of cap). Buybacks have been persistent rather than aggressive. Critically, much of FY2025 buyback activity occurred at prices well below today's $1,991 — buying $216M of stock that has now run to $2,000 is value-creating in hindsight. The harder question is whether 2026 buybacks at current levels (P/E 58x trailing, P/FCF 64x) are still rational. So far in 2026 only $2.5M of repurchases have occurred — management appears to be appropriately price-disciplined, which is a strong positive signal.

**Dividends.** $68.8M in FY2025 with a 14-year streak of raises. The October 2025 raise to $0.60 quarterly and the May 2026 announcement of $0.80 quarterly continue the pattern. Yield is negligible (~0.13%) at today's price — the dividend serves as a discipline device rather than a return mechanism. The payout ratio is conservative (~7% of FCF), leaving ample dry powder.

**Share count and SBC.** Diluted EPS rose from $6.82 (FY2022) to $28.88 (FY2025) — share count has been roughly flat to mildly declining (35.2M shares outstanding). Stock-based compensation was $21.8M in FY2025 against $1.0B of net income — about 2% — which is a healthy ratio for a company of this size and well below most large-cap norms. No evidence of dilutive equity issuance to fund M&A; deals appear to be cash-funded from operating cash flow, supported by minimal net debt (net cash position of ~$0.5B at year-end 2025).

**Compensation design.** Reported total CEO compensation is ~$8.2M with ~14% base and ~86% bonus/equity. The mix is heavily incentive-weighted (good if metrics are right). Pay is reportedly tied to a mix of revenue, EBITDA, and TSR. Tying pay to EBITDA-style metrics is not ideal because EBITDA can be grown by M&A regardless of per-share value creation. That said, the realized record (ROIC up sharply, share count flat, EPS quadrupled) suggests management has not gamed the system materially. Worth monitoring as the company gets larger and acquisition multiples potentially rise.

**Insider ownership and trading.** Modest. CEO Lane owns ~180,888 shares (~$360M at $1,991), or ~0.51% of the company. All insiders combined own ~1.2%. That is meaningful skin in the game in dollar terms, but a low percentage of the company by Buffett standards. Over the past 18 months, insiders have been net sellers of ~$106M of stock, including Lane's sale of ~15,000 shares at ~$1,453/share. **This is the clearest red flag in the file**: insiders, including the CEO, have been selling consistently into strength, with the largest sales executed at prices below today's quote. That can be diversification, planned 10b5-1 selling, or a signal that internal valuation views are well below current market price. Without explicit 10b5-1 disclosure for each transaction, the prudent reading is that insider conviction at $1,400-1,500 was already cautious; conviction at $1,991 is presumably less, not more.

**Candor and consistency.** Earnings calls are notably plain-spoken. CFO William George's commentary about backlog ("things that came up 1 to 2.5 years ago") and Q1 2026 disclosure that ~$43M of gross profit was non-recurring closeouts (i.e., management voluntarily called out the non-recurring portion of the margin beat) both reflect candor that is rare in a roll-up. Guidance has been generally accurate and not chronically optimistic.

**Governance.** No dual-class shares. Board appears independent. No related-party transactions of note found in available disclosures. No major governance red flags surfaced.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A 16-year owner-operator with a cleanly executed roll-up playbook, conservative balance sheet, growing dividend, and price-disciplined buyback behavior in 2026 continues compounding capital at attractive incremental rates.
- **Bear case:** Insider selling at prices below today's quote signals internal caution; if the data-center cycle rolls over, M&A multiples that worked at smaller scale may not work at $9B+ revenue, and incremental capital returns lower yields.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the long-term track record is excellent and well-documented; the only meaningful negative is the insider-selling pattern, which has a benign explanation (diversification of concentrated wealth) but is still worth flagging.

## Red Flags

- Insiders sold ~$106M of stock over 18 months with no offsetting open-market purchases; CEO Lane's largest sale was at ~$1,453, well below current $1,991.
- Insider ownership at ~1.2% total / ~0.51% for the CEO is modest by owner-operator standards, even if dollar value is meaningful.
- Compensation reportedly tied partly to EBITDA — a metric that can be grown via M&A regardless of per-share value creation; worth monitoring as deal sizes scale.
- Buyback pace slowed sharply in 2026 ($2.5M YTD) — appropriate given valuation but means today's reinvestment runway is largely M&A or balance-sheet build, with no obvious capital-allocation catalyst.

## Score: 7 / 10

A long-tenured, plain-spoken CEO with a 16-year compounding record, disciplined M&A program, conservative balance sheet, and (so far in 2026) appropriate price-discipline on buybacks — held back from a higher score by modest insider ownership and a consistent pattern of insider selling into the recent share-price run.
