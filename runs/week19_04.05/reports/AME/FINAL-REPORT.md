## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: AMETEK is a holding company for ~40 niche-leading specialty engineering brands that sell highly engineered electronic instruments, sensors, and electromechanical devices into aerospace/defense (~30% of revenue), process & analytical, life sciences, semiconductors, and factory automation, generating $7.4B revenue at 26% operating margins through one-time hardware sales plus a sticky aftermarket/consumables/service tail.
2. **Moat**: The moat is an aggregated portfolio of switching-cost monopolies — instruments specified into validated workflows (pharma QC, semiconductor fabs, surgical robots, jet-engine vibration monitoring) that customers cannot easily swap without re-validating multi-year regulatory or design processes — proven by gross margins steady at 35-36% and operating margins steady at 24-26% through 2022-2025 inflation.
3. **Economics**: Operating margin expanded from 24.4% (FY22) to 25.8% (FY25) and Q1 2026 hit 26.7%, FCF/NI conversion has run 113-124% over the last three years (FY25 owner earnings ~$1.8B), capex is just 1.8% of sales, and Q1 2026 organic orders were +23% with a record $3.87B backlog — though GAAP ROIC is stuck at 12-13% because $11B of acquisition goodwill inflates the denominator.
4. **Management**: David Zapico (35-year insider, CEO since 2016) runs a disciplined Roper-style M&A engine with ~0.65% SBC-to-revenue (well below peers), opportunistic buybacks ($434M in FY25 under a new $1.25B authorization), an 11% dividend hike in 2025, and a multi-decade track record of integrating bolt-ons at preserved margins — though recent deal multiples (Paragon ~15x EBITDA, FARO at a 40% premium, the just-announced $5B Indicor) have crept higher and his open-market trading record is six sells, zero buys.
5. **Debt risk**: Pre-Indicor leverage is fortress-grade (Net Debt/EBITDA 0.7-0.8x, interest coverage 23x, only $100M of Series K notes due Sept 2026), and even after the ~$5B Indicor deal closes in H2 2026 pro-forma leverage rises only to ~2.0-2.3x with stress-test coverage still ~3.4x in a 30%-revenue-drop scenario — survival is not in question.
6. **Price**: For today's $238.83 to make sense, AMETEK must deliver ~13% revenue growth fading to 3% terminal AND hold a 18x EV/EBITDA exit multiple AND see WACC compress to 9.4% — i.e., the full bull case as the expected outcome, with Q1 2026's defense/aerospace order surge extrapolated as the new run-rate rather than a cyclical peak.
7. **Margin of safety**: There is none on price — current $238.83 is above the bull IV ($229), 0 of 25 sensitivity-grid cells produce IV at today's price, Monte Carlo P(IV > Price) is 1.3%, and the asymmetry is ~3:1 against the buyer (30%+ downside to base IV $165 vs. ~5% upside to bull) — only the business margin of safety (clean balance sheet, diversified niches, 25%+ margins) prevents catastrophic loss.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if FCF/NI conversion drops below 90% for 4+ quarters, GAAP ROIC drops below 10% for 2+ years (M&A discipline broken), Net Debt/EBITDA exceeds 2.5x post-Indicor and stays there beyond 18 months, or organic book-to-bill drops below 1.0x for two consecutive quarters signaling defense/aerospace cycle rollover.

## Key Risks

1. **Multiple compression to peer median (highest-likelihood, highest-impact)** — AME at 23x EV/EBITDA carries a ~30% premium to ITW (18x) and FTV (12.5x). A sentiment shift on industrials (rates, recession, sector rotation) re-rates the stock to 16–18x for a 25–30% drawdown without any business deterioration.
2. **Defense/aerospace cycle peak being extrapolated** — Q1 2026's record $3.87B backlog and +23% organic orders are partly cyclical. If book-to-bill drops below 1.0x for two quarters, organic growth reverts to 3–5% and the bull-case assumption breaks.
3. **Indicor integration and pro-forma leverage** — The just-announced ~$5B Indicor deal steps net debt/EBITDA from 0.7x to ~2.0–2.3x. A recession at close (H2 2026) could push leverage above 4x in stress, threatening investment-grade rating and raising borrowing cost just as cash is needed for paydown.
4. **Acquisition-treadmill returns** — GAAP ROIC has been flat at 12-13% for four years; rising deal multiples (Paragon ~15x EBITDA, FARO at a 40% premium, Indicor ~4.5x sales) suggest the engine is treading water on returns. If the next decade of M&A produces lower deal-level returns, reported ROIC drifts down further.
5. **Goodwill impairment risk** — $10.5B / 72% of total assets is goodwill + intangibles. Any single business-unit deterioration in a recession could trigger a writedown that pressures debt covenants even if cash flow stays intact.

## Full Analysis

### 01. Circle of Competence

**Score: 7/10** — Understandable industrial conglomerate with a clear playbook and clean financials, but the breadth of business units and acquisition-driven reporting keep this just below the "explain to a teenager" tier.

**What does the company sell, and to whom?** AMETEK is a 90-year-old industrial-technology company that designs and builds highly engineered electronic instruments and electromechanical devices. Think of it as a holding company for ~40 specialty engineering businesses, each a leader in a small niche: oscilloscopes for engineers, mass spectrometers for scientists, motion-control motors for surgical robots, vibration sensors for jet engines, thermal cameras, ultra-precise 3D laser scanners. Customers are engineers and procurement managers at OEMs, factories, labs, hospitals, defense primes, and utilities.

**How it makes money.** The bulk of revenue is hardware sold up front into capital-equipment budgets. On top sits a tail of aftermarket parts, consumables, software licenses, and service contracts. AMETEK does not break out recurring revenue cleanly, but 36% gross margin, 26% operating margin, and FCF/NI of 113-124% are consistent with a healthy aftermarket annuity.

**Two reporting segments** — EIG (~65% of revenue) and EMG (~35%) — across ~40 business units. Aerospace & Defense is the single largest end-market at ~30% of company revenue. No single customer is disclosed as >10%; customer concentration is low and diversified across thousands of OEMs globally.

**Two-minute pitch test passes.** "AMETEK buys mid-sized engineering businesses that lead in a small niche, bolts them onto a shared operating model, and runs them for cash." The harder line-by-line inventory of the 40 brands does not pass — but it does not need to; the investor question is whether the operating playbook keeps working.

**Red flags**: Two-segment reporting hides individual unit decay; recurring-revenue % is not cleanly disclosed; reported growth is heavily augmented by M&A; the portfolio expands faster than disclosure depth.

### 02. Durable Competitive Advantage

**Score: 7/10** — Real, durable, switching-cost-driven moat across a portfolio of niche leaders, with stable mid-20s operating margins as the proof — but not the wide, software-grade moat of a top-tier compounder.

**Moat type — niche-monopoly portfolio.** Each AMETEK business is the de-facto standard in its category — the mass spectrometer the lab has used for 15 years, the vibration sensor specified on the engine, the laser projector the aerospace assembly line was built around. Once a customer's quality-management or design system is validated against a specific instrument, switching is painful, slow, and risky. Pharmaceutical companies face FDA-touched re-validation; semiconductor fabs face requalification. The moat is primarily **switching costs** with secondary **specification lock-in / brand-in-the-spec**.

**Financial signature.** Gross margin steady at 35-36%, operating margin steady at 24-26%, FCF margin 22-25%, EBITDA margin 30%+. Pricing held through 2022-2024 inflation; margins did not compress. ROIC at ~12-13% looks ordinary, but the denominator carries $11B+ of acquisition goodwill — cash-on-cash returns on the underlying operating businesses are much higher (RONA-style metrics historically in the 25%+ range).

**Where the moat is weakest.** (1) The electromechanical group has commodity-adjacent components facing Regal Rexnord, Nidec, Chinese players. (2) Process and analytical instruments increasingly face software-defined alternatives. (3) Aerospace/defense moats rest on long programme cycles that can reset.

**Comparison with Roper.** Roper is the more-software, fewer-niche industrial compounder; AMETEK is the more-hardware, more-niches version. AMETEK's moat is real but one notch shallower — hardware faces a tail risk pure-software businesses don't.

**Red flags**: ROIC at 12-13% on goodwill-heavy denominator; R&D not separately disclosed; no corporate-level "AMETEK premium" brand.

### 03. Management & Capital Allocation

**Score: 8/10** — Disciplined, long-tenured insider CEO; clean proxy mechanics; very low SBC; disciplined M&A engine. Held back from a 9 by recent deal pricing and a CEO ownership profile that is professional rather than owner-operator.

**David Zapico** has been CEO since May 2016, Chairman since June 2017; he joined AMETEK in 1990 as a product engineer — 35+ years at the company. Owns ~345k shares (~$81M at Dec-2025 prices) — meaningful in dollars but only ~0.15% of shares outstanding. Form 4 history shows zero open-market buys and six open-market sells in the last five years.

**Capital allocation.** Heavy bolt-on M&A: Paragon Medical ($1.9B, 2023), Amplifier Research (Oct 2023), Virtek ($117M, 2024), Kern Microtechnik ($51.6M, 2025), FARO Technologies ($920M, 2025), LKC Technologies (Feb 2026). Roughly $3B+ in 30 months, plus the ~$5B Indicor announcement on May 6, 2026.

**Buybacks.** Opportunistic — $434M in FY2025, $212M in FY2024, only $7.8M in FY2023 (when M&A consumed cash), $333M in FY2022. New $1.25B authorization replaced the prior plan.

**Stock-based compensation.** Modest at ~$48M/year on $7.4B revenue (0.65% of sales) — well below tech-adjacent industrials' typical 2-5%. Essentially flat for four years; disciplined dilution control.

**Dividend.** Grew 11% in 2025 to a $1.24 indicated annual rate. Payout ratio ~19%, preserving cash for M&A and buybacks.

**Compensation design** structured around organic sales growth, operating income, EPS, and ROTC — sensible per-share metrics. No major restatement, accounting issue, or governance incident in recent memory.

**Red flags**: CEO Form 4 = six open-market sells, zero buys; deal multiples crept higher (Paragon ~15x EBITDA, FARO 40% premium); combined CEO/Chairman role.

### 04. Business Economics

**Score: 8/10** — A genuinely high-quality economic engine: 26%+ operating margins, 100%+ FCF conversion, ~2% capex intensity, expanding margins across both segments, and accelerating organic orders. Held below 9 by GAAP ROIC stuck at 12-13% and the just-announced $5B Indicor deal pushing further into "compounder by acquisition" territory.

**Returns on capital.** GAAP ROIC stable at ~12.4-12.8% across FY2022-FY2025; ROE 14.6-16.2%. The headline reflects a price paid for serial acquisitions, not a weak operating engine — invested capital is dominated by $10.5B goodwill + intangibles (72% of total assets). Tangible cash-on-cash returns are extremely high.

**Margins.** Gross margin stepped from 34.9% (FY22) to 36.0% (FY25); operating from 24.4% to 25.8%; EBITDA from 29.8% to 31.1%. Q1 2026 accelerated: 26.7% reported / 27.9% core operating margin (+160 bps YoY). EIG Q4 2025 core margin hit 32.3%; EMG hit 22.7% (+240 bps YoY). Both segments expanding margin simultaneously rules out a single cyclical lever.

**Cash generation.** FCF conversion: FY23 121.8%, FY24 123.7%, FY25 112.9%. D&A ($420M) runs well above capex ($130M). Quant owner-earnings = $1.8B in FY25, comfortably above $1.5B net income. SBC ~$48M (~3% of NI) — dilution negligible.

**Capital intensity.** Capex 1.8% of sales; quant model classifies 100% as maintenance. Asset-light specialty industrial — closer to Roper than a traditional capex-heavy industrial.

**Q1 2026 momentum.** Orders +23.3% YoY to $2.22B; record $3.87B backlog. Organic +5%, acquisitions +4%, FX +2% = 11% headline. ~6 months forward visibility.

**Red flags**: Goodwill + intangibles = 72% of assets ($10.5B at YE2024); ~$5B Indicor deal step-changes leverage; ROIC flat at 12-13% for 4 years.

### 05. Balance Sheet Safety

**Score: 8/10** — Pre-Indicor balance sheet is genuinely a 9: 1.0x leverage, 23x interest coverage, only $100M due Sept 2026 — recession is a non-event. One-point deduction reflects the ~$5B Indicor deal, which steps pro-forma leverage to ~2.0-2.3x. Still investment-grade and safe, but consumes financial flexibility.

**Pre-Indicor leverage.** FY2025: gross debt $2.3B, cash $458M, net debt $1.8B against EBITDA $2.3B. Debt/EBITDA 1.0x, Net Debt/EBITDA 0.8x. Q1 2026: gross 0.9x, net 0.7x with total debt net $2.18B at March 31, 2026 — leverage was actually trending lower before Indicor.

**Interest coverage.** EBIT/Interest 23.1x in FY2025. Even with Indicor adding ~$200M incremental interest at ~5% on ~$4B new debt, post-deal coverage of ~6.5x against current EBIT $1.9B is comfortable.

**Maturities.** Only one near-term maturity surfaced: $100M of 3.83% Series K Senior Notes due September 30, 2026 — a rounding error against $1.7B annual FCF. The $300M 3.73% notes already matured in Q3 2024.

**Indicor pro-forma.** Net debt steps from ~$1.8B to ~$5.5-6B; pro-forma EBITDA reaches ~$2.6-2.7B. Pro-forma Net Debt/EBITDA at ~2.0-2.3x at close, drifting back below 2.0x within 12-18 months. Meaningful step up but stays inside investment-grade thresholds.

**Liquidity.** $458M cash + $2.3B commercial paper program + committed bank revolver. Current ratio 1.1x; working capital $177M positive at YE2025. $1.7B annual FCF can absorb any near-term refinancing.

**Stress test (30% revenue drop x 2 years).** Stressed revenue $5.2B; stressed EBITDA ~$1.4B. Pro-forma debt $5.5B at ~5% = ~$275M interest. Coverage ~3.4x — uncomfortable but not distress (distress <1.5x). Net Debt/EBITDA ~3.9x — would draw negative watch but survives.

**Red flags**: ~$5B Indicor closing H2 2026; goodwill impairment risk; full debt ladder beyond Sept 2026 not visible in search snippets; pension/lease specifics not extracted.

### 07. Margin of Safety

**Score: 3/10** — Strong, resilient business — but you are paying full price plus a premium with no cushion. Essentially no price margin of safety, and the business margin of safety doesn't compensate for that asymmetry. A patient buyer waits.

**Price MOS — none.** Quant bear IV $109.07 vs price $238.83 = **MOS −119%**. Stock would need to fall ~54% to reach conservative IV. Sensitivity grid: **0 of 25 cells** produce IV ≥ $238.83 — max cell $230.43 (13.1% growth + 7.9% WACC). Monte Carlo P(IV > Price) 1.3%; P95 ($223.08) below current price.

**Business MOS — solid but doesn't bail out price.** Net Debt/EBITDA 0.8x, interest coverage 23x, current ratio 1.1x. Operating margin 25.8%, EBITDA 31.1%, FCF/NI 113%. Diversified across 38+ business units; no single end-market >~20% of sales. ROIC 12.8% — good, not in the league of true compounders. Business will not blow up. But not so dominant that 23x EV/EBITDA is justified by quality alone.

**Asymmetry — wrong-way.** Realistic downside to base IV $165 = ~31% drawdown. Deep downside to bear IV $110 = ~54% drawdown. Realistic upside to bull $229-$240 = -4% to flat. Stretch upside to high target $280 = +17% over 12 months. Risk/reward ~3:1 against the buyer (Buffett rule wants 2-3x upside vs downside — this runs the wrong way).

**Five concrete ways to be wrong**: (1) Defense/aero spending normalizes (medium-high likelihood, 20-30% drawdown); (2) Multiple compression to peer median (medium, 25-30%); (3) Acquisition integration disappointment (low, 10-15%); (4) Semi capex cycle reverses (medium, 10-15%); (5) Rates regime shift (medium, 5-10%).

**Red flags**: Negative price MOS -119%; 0/25 sensitivity cells; P(IV > Price) 1.3%; ~3:1 wrong-way asymmetry; multiple expansion doing the work; trading at 96% of 52-week range.

### 08. Temperament & Time Horizon

**Score: 6/10** — Excellent business for a 10-year hold and the thesis is durable, but the entry today fails the temperament test — buying near a 52-week high with a wrong-way risk/reward is the opposite of how this kind of name should be acquired. Add to a watchlist with a defined trigger price; do not initiate a full position at $238.

**Holding period suitability — yes, at the right entry.** Structurally one of the cleanest "hold for a decade" industrial businesses available. Compounded EPS ~10% over multiple cycles. The thesis depends on continued M&A discipline and durability of niche moats — does not require a near-term catalyst. The problem is the entry, not the hold.

**Volatility tolerance.** Beta 1.03. 2008-09 drawdown ~50%; 2020 ~35%; 2022 industrial sell-off ~25%. Recovered within 12-24 months — but only because entry valuation was reasonable. Buying at 23x EV/EBITDA leaves no margin for sentiment-driven re-rating.

**Catalysts — none required, none present.** Q1 2026 was a record, FY26 guide raised, analysts clustered just above price. Likely path is sideways-to-modest-up for 12-18 months.

**Position sizing.** Quant Monte Carlo P(IV > Price) 1.3% argues for a starter position at most, or none. **1-2% if you must own today**; full 4-5% justified only at $170-$185 (closer to base IV). Anything above 3% at today's price is over-sizing relative to conviction.

**Correlation risk.** Textbook industrial-compounder factor exposure overlapping with ROP, FTV, ITW, DHR, HEI. For a Danish investor on Saxo ASK: USD-denominated US-listed; FX cost on entry/exit is bigger structural drag than 0.58% dividend yield.

**Process discipline.**
- Entry rule: No buy at current price. Patience trigger at $185-$190 (~22% below current) for a starter; $165-$170 (base IV) for a full position.
- Sell rules: FCF conversion <90% for 4+ quarters; ROIC <10% for 2+ years; Net Debt/EBITDA >2.5x; major fraud/restatement; price >$300 with no underlying surprise.
- Review: Quarterly earnings release (book-to-bill, organic ex-FX, core margin); annual quant model re-run post-10-K.

**Red flags**: Buying at 96% of 52-week range tests temperament; cost-basis anchoring risk; sector-overlap risk; no catalyst means the wait could be long.
