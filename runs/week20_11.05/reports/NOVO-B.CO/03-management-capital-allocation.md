## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Direct executive ownership is small in absolute share count relative to the DKK 1.3T market cap, and 2025 insider transactions were net sales. The real alignment sits one layer up at the Foundation level. The Novo Nordisk Foundation, through its wholly owned holding company Novo Holdings A/S, owns about 28.1% of the equity and 77.3% of the votes. A shares carry 100 votes each and B shares 10 votes; the Foundation's charter requires it to maintain a majority of A-share votes and at least 25.5% of total share capital. This is not normal-CEO alignment, and it is not weak alignment — it is a different *kind* of alignment. The Foundation has a multi-century time horizon, no ability to liquidate, and an institutional mandate that ties its entire grant-making capacity to Novo's long-term performance. That is structurally stronger than the typical "CEO owns 0.2% of shares" alignment one sees at most US large-caps, but it does not give minority shareholders any governance leverage.

**Capital allocation track record.** The capital allocation pivot of the past four years is the central question for any investor here. In FY2022, Novo deployed DKK 14.8B to capex and DKK 24.1B to buybacks; total debt was DKK 25.8B. By FY2025, capex had reached DKK 90.1B (6× higher), buybacks had collapsed to DKK 1.4B, and total debt had reached DKK 131B (5× higher). The capex went to: the $11B Catalent three-site acquisition, the $6B Kalundborg expansion, the $4.1B Clayton (NC) facility, the $2.2B Chartres (France) upgrade. Free cash flow fell from DKK 70B (FY2023) to DKK 29B (FY2025) and FCF conversion fell from 84% to 28%.

Two readings are honest. **Bull reading:** manufacturing capacity was the binding constraint on the GLP-1 franchise from 2022 through 2024 (the FDA shortage list confirmed it); Novo invested at the moment of maximum demand visibility; FY2025 capex of DKK 90B will translate to multi-decade peptide capacity that no competitor can replicate; the buyback collapse was rational because the cash was needed for the capex cycle. **Bear reading:** Novo built capacity at peak-cycle economics while competitive dynamics shifted against it (Lilly took share, US pricing compressed), and the FCF collapse is the early evidence the capital was deployed at the wrong moment. The truth is probably somewhere in between, but a meaningful piece of new evidence dropped at Q1 2026: oral Wegovy delivered nearly 2× consensus revenue in its first full quarter, which would not have been possible without the manufacturing buildout. That is one data point in favor of the bull reading.

**Compensation design and SBC.** Novo follows a European pharma compensation model — base salary, short-term cash incentive, share-based LTIP — and the LTIP for 2023 vested at 92% of maximum while the LTIP for 2024 and 2025 is tracking below targets. That is real pay-for-performance, not theater. Former CEO Jorgensen received about DKK 123.6M in total separation compensation (salary, notice pay, severance, non-compete) — substantial but inside policy for a CEO who led an eight-year run of historic growth. New CEO Doustdar's 2025 partial-year compensation was DKK 20.7M. Stock-based compensation has been ~DKK 2.3B annually (~0.8% of revenue), an order of magnitude lower than US tech and lower than most US pharma peers — a real plus.

**Candor and consistency — Doustdar at 9 months.** The Jorgensen-to-Doustdar transition in August 2025 was abrupt and produced analyst commentary about whether undisclosed issues drove it. Doustdar's communication since has been notably direct: the "execution, execution, execution" framing at JPM 2026, the explicit acknowledgment in Q1 2026 that this is a "turnaround situation," the willingness to call the Wegovy price cuts a strategic decision rather than dressing them up. The 9,000-person layoff (11% of headcount, ~5,000 in Denmark) targeting DKK 8B in annual run-rate savings by end-2026 is the most concrete operational discipline Novo has executed in years. The Q1 2026 results — 32% constant-currency revenue growth, 65% operating profit growth, guidance narrowed from "−5% to −13%" to "−4% to −12%" — give Doustdar his first piece of validation. This is far too early to call him a great capital allocator, but the early signal is consistent with what was promised: candid framing of the problem, concrete cost actions, sharper commercial execution.

**Governance structure.** The Foundation's permanent 77% voting control through Class A shares is the defining feature. Positives: the Foundation has demonstrated it will change management (Jorgensen replacement) when performance disappoints; it has a multi-century time horizon; it eliminates activist disruption and quarterly distortion. Negatives: minority B-shareholders have no governance voice; the Foundation could in principle make decisions that prioritize its scientific mission over shareholder returns; the dual-class structure is permanent and cannot be unwound. For an investor whose interests align with the Foundation's mandate (long-term scientific franchise in metabolic disease), this structure is a feature. For an investor expecting standard governance rights, it is a permanent ceiling.

**Owners or politicians?** Doustdar talks like an operator. The Q1 2026 call language ("turnaround," "execution," "consumer-driven market," explicit acknowledgment of the Lilly competitive challenge) is direct and granular. The 9,000-person layoff is the kind of decision a politician would defer and an operator makes immediately. The Foundation's stewardship is paternalistic in structure but has produced a 100-year compounding record. On balance: closer to owners than politicians, with the caveat that 9 months of evidence is not 5 years of evidence.

**Q1 2026 vs the week15 view.** The week15 write-up scored this 6/10 with capital allocation discipline as the main concern. Two meaningful updates since: (i) Q1 2026 results show the cost-cutting and commercial pivot are starting to produce evidence (32% constant-currency revenue growth, 65% operating profit growth, guidance raise); (ii) oral Wegovy at 2× consensus revenue in its first quarter is the first piece of evidence that the manufacturing capex buildout produced a real commercial return. Neither of these resolves the capital allocation question, but together they shift the balance modestly toward the bull reading.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A 100+ year Foundation-controlled franchise where (a) management was changed quickly when performance slipped, (b) the new CEO has executed concrete cost discipline (9,000 layoffs, DKK 8B run-rate savings), and (c) Q1 2026 results show the commercial machine is responding — a structurally aligned long-term steward acting like one.
- **Bear case:** Capex of DKK 90B in FY2025 (6× FY2022), debt of DKK 131B (5× FY2022), FCF collapse from DKK 70B to DKK 29B, near-zero direct executive ownership, and permanent dual-class disenfranchisement of minority holders — none of which the Q1 beat fully resolves.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The Foundation structure is high-confidence aligned; the new CEO has 9 months of evidence pointing the right way but not enough yet to call a track record; the capex cycle's wisdom will not be fully visible for 2–3 more years.

## Red Flags

- Executive direct ownership is effectively zero relative to market cap; insider transactions in 2025 were net sales, not purchases.
- Total debt rose 5× in two fiscal years (DKK 25.8B → DKK 131B); FCF conversion fell from 84% to 28% over the same period.
- Dual-class voting structure permanently removes minority shareholder governance influence — this is unchangeable.
- Buybacks collapsed at exactly the moment shares became cheaper — a function of the capex cycle constraint, but it means Novo did not aggressively use depressed prices to compound per-share value during the FY2024–2025 share-price decline.
- The CEO transition was abrupt and produced analyst commentary about possible undisclosed issues; the new CEO has only 9 months of evidence behind him.

## Score: 7 / 10

Novo Nordisk's stewardship structure is genuinely above average for global pharma. The Foundation's permanent 77% voting control provides a multi-century time horizon and has demonstrated willingness to change management; the new CEO is communicating directly, executing concrete cost actions, and just delivered a Q1 beat plus guidance raise. The capex pivot is still unresolved — DKK 90B in a single year is a bet that won't be fully visible for 2–3 more years — but the early evidence (oral Wegovy doubling consensus, Wegovy injectable +12% YoY, guidance narrowed) is consistent with the bull reading. A score of 7 (one notch above week15's 6) reflects that the new management's first hard data point landed on the right side of the line, while keeping room for the capital allocation question to be properly settled.
