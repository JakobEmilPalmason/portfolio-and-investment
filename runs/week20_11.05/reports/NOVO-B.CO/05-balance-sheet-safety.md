## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure — Five-Fold Increase, Still Conservative on a Cash-Flow Basis.** Total debt rose from DKK 27B (end-FY2023) to DKK 131B (end-FY2025), with long-term debt of DKK 112B and short-term debt of approximately DKK 19B. Net debt sits at DKK 95.9B after subtracting DKK 27B in cash and short-term investments. The DKK 104B incremental debt funded the Catalent acquisition (roughly DKK 75B of the equity-acquired assets attributable to Novo Nordisk's portion) and the ongoing greenfield buildout in Clayton, Kalundborg, and Chartres. Funding is through the Euro Medium Term Note programme listed on Euronext Dublin (updated May 2025), with known issuances at modest fixed coupons in the 3.0-3.5% range. Despite the absolute increase, Debt/EBITDA remains 0.8x and Net Debt/EBITDA 0.6x — levels at which most CFOs would describe the balance sheet as conservative, not leveraged. Debt/Equity rose from 25% to 67%, a meaningful shift but still far from stressed pharma peers (Bristol Myers, AbbVie, Bayer all carry multi-times higher leverage on a similar revenue base).

**Interest Coverage — Down From Extraordinary to Merely Strong.** EBIT/Interest stands at 32x for FY2025, down from 194x in FY2023 but far above any concern threshold. Interest expense rose from DKK 542M to DKK 4.2B in two years — a real increase, but DKK 4.2B against DKK 128B of operating income is barely visible. As a fraction of EBITDA, interest is 2.7%; as a fraction of operating cash flow, it is 3.5%. Even under the company's own 2026 bear scenario (sales -12%, gross margin compression, R&D held flat), operating income would still be DKK 90-100B and interest coverage well above 20x. The cost of debt is low: the quant model uses a 3.2% pre-tax cost of debt (2.5% after-tax), consistent with the AA/Aa3 credit standing. Importantly, the debt is predominantly fixed-rate, so the company is not exposed to rising rates on outstanding obligations during the very period in which earnings are reset.

**Liquidity — Adequate, Bolstered by OCF Generation Capacity.** Cash and short-term investments of DKK 27B at FY2025 are a modest absolute buffer but more than adequate given the company's operating cash flow profile (DKK 119B annually). The current ratio of 0.8x and negative working capital of DKK 43B are structural features of large pharma economics — substantial accrued rebates, deferred revenue, and managed payables sit on the current liability side without representing a liquidity risk. The Q1 2026 cash flow statement shows the company generated DKK 12.8B of FCF in a single quarter while simultaneously returning DKK 37.7B to shareholders — a configuration only possible because the operating engine prints DKK 100B+ of cash annually and the balance sheet provides a working buffer. Undrawn credit facilities are typical for an Aa3-rated issuer but specific facility sizes were not disclosed in the publicly reviewed materials.

**Refinancing Risk — Effectively None.** Moody's Aa3 (positive outlook, upgraded January 2025) and S&P AA (stable outlook, affirmed June 2025) place Novo Nordisk among the highest-rated pharmaceutical companies in the world — only Johnson & Johnson and a small handful of others sit at this level. At these ratings, capital markets access is essentially assured under any realistic stress, and the EMTN programme provides standing infrastructure to issue across maturities and currencies efficiently. The debt maturity profile is laddered (a EUR 3.375% 2034 bond is one known tranche), and no major bunched maturity has been identified. The risk that Novo Nordisk would face a refinancing crunch even under a severe earnings reset is implausible — the cash generation profile alone would amortize the current net debt in roughly 18-24 months if management chose to. The more relevant question is direction of travel: ratings could plausibly stabilize or weaken from "positive outlook" to "stable" if 2026 results disappoint, but a downgrade below AA is multiple stress scenarios away.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Capital lease obligations are approximately DKK 8-9B (modest), pension obligations for a Danish company are typically well-funded given Denmark's pension architecture, and Novo Nordisk has historically maintained minimal net pension liabilities. Contingent liabilities include semaglutide biosimilar patent litigation in select markets, ongoing pricing disputes, and the residual unknown liability from the US 340B program (the Q1 2026 results included a positive provision reversal on 340B, suggesting prior reserves were conservative). No off-balance-sheet financing vehicles, structured guarantees, or significant unconsolidated joint ventures were identified. The Catalent acquisition was a direct asset purchase, not a sale-leaseback or PIK-financed deal — conservative structure.

**Capital Allocation Reversal — A Strong Positive Signal.** The most informative balance-sheet development since the week15 view is the resumption of capital returns. Buybacks collapsed from DKK 30B (FY2023) to DKK 1.4B (FY2025) as management prioritized capex. In February 2026, the company announced a new DKK 15B buyback program for 2026, with the first DKK 3.8B tranche completed by early May 2026 at an average price of DKK 257. The 2025 final dividend of DKK 7.95/share (total 2025 dividend DKK 11.70) was approved, and Q1 2026 total cash returns to shareholders reached DKK 37.7B — full year is guided above DKK 60B. This is the strongest available signal that the CFO views the balance sheet as restored to flexibility: a company genuinely worried about leverage during a revenue decline does not announce a buyback and return DKK 37.7B in a single quarter. It is consistent with the capex peak having passed.

**Stress Test — 30% Revenue Decline, Two Years.** If revenue dropped 30% from DKK 309B to roughly DKK 216B and stayed there, with gross margin compressing further to 75% (allowing for input cost stickiness and mix), gross profit would be approximately DKK 162B. With R&D and SG&A reduced 15% (DKK 130B combined down to ~DKK 110B), operating income would be approximately DKK 52B. Interest coverage at that level would still be approximately 12x against current interest of DKK 4.2B. Net Debt/EBITDA (using a stressed EBITDA of ~DKK 65B) would rise to roughly 1.5x — comfortable. The company could continue servicing debt, maintaining the dividend, and funding maintenance capex (~DKK 25B) all from operating cash flow, with the buyback as the obvious adjustable lever. The stress confirms the central conclusion: Novo Nordisk does not need the market to stay open to survive — Buffett's exact test of balance sheet adequacy.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Capex peak is behind, FCF is recovering (DKK 12.8B in Q1 2026 alone), the company restarted DKK 15B+ buybacks, and the highest credit ratings in pharma (Aa3/AA) provide unlimited optionality on debt issuance — leverage will trend down from here.
- **Bear case:** Net debt nearly 14x'd in two years during a period of peak earnings, and a deeper-than-guided 2026 revenue decline combined with sticky R&D could push Net Debt/EBITDA from 0.6x toward 1.2-1.5x — still safe, but a meaningful step down from "near-zero" two years ago, with credit ratings drifting from "positive outlook" toward "stable."
- **Confidence:** High — The absolute debt levels are trivial relative to cash generation, the credit profile is among the strongest in healthcare, the stress test confirms survival under severe conditions, and the resumption of large-scale shareholder returns is direct CFO confirmation that the balance sheet is flexible.

## Red Flags

- Net debt rose from DKK 7B to DKK 96B in two years — the most aggressive leverage build in company history, executed at peak earnings just before a structural pricing reset
- Interest expense quadrupled from FY2024 (DKK 1.6B) to FY2025 (DKK 4.2B); a similar step-up is possible in FY2026 as additional debt funds Clayton and Kalundborg completion
- Negative working capital of DKK 43B means current obligations exceed current assets — typical for pharma but a tighter buffer than a fortress balance sheet would show
- Credit ratings (Aa3 positive, AA stable) are at multi-year highs precisely as leverage rises and revenue declines — direction of travel could be flat to slightly down over the next 12-24 months
- The DKK 15B buyback was announced before the full impact of MFN pricing has flowed through; if 2026 disappoints, the buyback can be paused but the signaling cost would be real
