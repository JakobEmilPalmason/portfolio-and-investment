## Detailed Analysis

**What the company sells, and to whom.** Novo Nordisk is a focused metabolic disease company. Its commercial portfolio is concentrated in two therapy areas: diabetes care and obesity care. Diabetes products include the injectable Ozempic and the oral Rybelsus (both semaglutide), plus a still-meaningful insulin franchise built over a century. Obesity products include injectable Wegovy and, since early 2026, an oral Wegovy formulation that became the first FDA-approved oral GLP-1 for weight loss and produced roughly DKK 2.26B of revenue in its first full quarter. Customers are patients (millions globally) prescribed the drugs by physicians, but the economic counterparties are PBMs, commercial insurers, Medicare/Medicaid, and European national health systems — they negotiate the net price.

**How it makes money.** The revenue model is classic branded pharma. Novo manufactures the API and fill-finish in-house at 14 sites globally (including the three integrated Catalent facilities), sells through wholesale and retail pharmacy channels, and earns revenue when prescriptions are filled. Gross margin was 81.0% in FY2025, down from 84.7% in FY2024 — a small but real signal of price compression. Operating margin was 41.3%. Net margin was 33.1%. The DKK 52B annual R&D spend (17% of revenue) funds the pipeline. The DKK 90B capex spend is the controversial line — it nearly doubled in two years and reflects a binding-constraint thesis on manufacturing capacity (more on that under Umbrella 3).

**The 2–4 key drivers.** Three variables explain almost everything:
1. **GLP-1 prescription volume** — a function of obesity/diabetes prevalence (the addressable population is 800M+ for obesity alone), reimbursement breadth, and physician adoption. Novo's Wegovy commands 65% of US new prescriptions of branded GLP-1s, but Eli Lilly's Zepbound/Mounjaro holds 60%+ of total US market share (Lilly grew faster off a smaller obesity base).
2. **Net realized price** — list prices are largely fiction; what matters is the net price after rebates, IRA negotiation, and now the $245/month MFN agreement for Medicare. Net price per Ozempic prescription has been falling and will fall again in 2027.
3. **Pipeline execution** — CagriSema (FDA decision expected end-2026), oral semaglutide for obesity (already approved and selling), amycretin (entering Phase 3 in 2026), and Wegovy HD. Each readout resets competitive positioning.

**Revenue predictability.** Short-term predictability is genuinely mixed. The Q1 2026 result was a beat — sales grew 32% constant currency, EPS topped estimates, and management raised the 2026 guidance range from "−5% to −13%" to "−4% to −12%." That is still a forecast revenue decline, the first since 2017, driven by US net-price pressure and Ozempic loss of exclusivity dynamics in 2026–2027. But the trajectory beneath the guidance is improving: the oral Wegovy launch doubled expectations, International Operations are growing, and the company is now talking about "turnaround." Longer-term predictability is high in direction (chronic disease, structural demand growth) but moderate in magnitude (competitive share, net price, pipeline outcomes).

**Customer concentration.** At the prescriber level, concentration is negligible. At the payer level, concentration is meaningful and increasing. CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx still gate formulary access for most US commercial lives. Medicare added Ozempic to the IRA-negotiated list with a 71% list-price discount (Maximum Fair Price of $274 effective 2027), then Novo agreed in November 2025 to a $245/month MFN price covering all doses and indications — undercutting even the IRA price. This is the single most important economic shift of the past 12 months: the US payer side is asserting its monopsony power, and Novo is choosing to lean into volume rather than fight on price.

**Can you explain it in two minutes?** Yes — easily. Novo Nordisk makes injectable and oral drugs for diabetes and weight loss. The two big ones are Ozempic and Wegovy, both based on semaglutide. They are sold on prescription worldwide, with the US as the largest market. Revenue grows when more patients get prescriptions and falls when payers cut prices. The company spends heavily on manufacturing and R&D, faces one main competitor (Eli Lilly), and has a pipeline of next-generation drugs (CagriSema, amycretin) it needs to deliver. The business model itself is simple. The complexity sits in pricing dynamics and the head-to-head efficacy race with Lilly — not in how the company makes money.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A chronic-disease franchise with recurring demand in a massive underpenetrated obesity market, only 2–3 variables to track, and recent earnings showing the trough is shallower than feared — about as understandable as branded pharma gets.
- **Bear case:** Payer concentration is now actively compressing US net price (IRA + MFN deal), and the path of the business depends on next-generation pipeline outcomes that won't be fully visible for 18–24 months.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is clear and Q1 2026 results made the next 12 months more legible, not less; what's uncertain (competitive share, pricing) is uncertainty about outcomes, not about how the business works.

## Red Flags

- FY2026 guidance still implies a revenue decline (−4% to −12% on adjusted basis), even after the post-Q1 upgrade — the first down year in nearly a decade.
- The US Medicare $245/month MFN price effective 2027 sets a precedent: a government-anchored reference price below Novo's negotiated IRA price, which other payers will use as a benchmark.
- Two-product concentration: Ozempic + Wegovy (both semaglutide) drive the franchise. Loss-of-exclusivity timing on the underlying composition patents (early 2030s) is closer than it looks once one accounts for generic compounding pressure.

## Score: 9 / 10

The business is among the most understandable in global pharma: two molecules, two diseases, a clear demand curve, and a single major competitor. The slight deduction reflects the fact that net pricing is no longer something a long-term investor can take for granted in the US — but understanding *that* dynamic does not require complex analysis, just an honest read of who has leverage over whom.
