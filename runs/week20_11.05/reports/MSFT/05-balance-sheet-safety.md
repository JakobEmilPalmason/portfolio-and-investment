## Detailed Analysis

**Leverage is essentially symbolic on the headline metrics.** Total debt of $60.6B at FY25 against $160.2B of EBITDA gives a Debt/EBITDA of 0.4x — vs. a "comfortable" threshold of 2.0x and a "concerning" line at 4.0x. Net debt is $12.9B, or roughly 8% of one year's EBITDA. Debt/Equity has fallen from 36.8% (FY22) to 17.6% (FY25) as equity has compounded faster than debt. Interest expense was $2.4B against EBIT of $128.5B — 52.8x coverage, vs. the "distress" line at 1.5x. There is no plausible scenario in which Microsoft's existing debt is a survival issue.

**The debt stack is shrinking, not growing — and that's striking.** Even as capex tripled from $24B to $65B and forward calendar-2026 capex guidance is $190B, Microsoft is *paying down* long-term debt: $42.0B (FY23) → $42.7B (FY24) → $40.2B (FY25) → $31.4B (FY26 Q3). Microsoft has not been a net debt issuer to fund the AI build — they are funding it from operating cash flow. The S&P AAA / Moody's Aaa rating is intact. Outstanding bonds are senior unsecured, ranked equally with other obligations, with semiannual interest payments. There is a series maturing August 8, 2026 — modest size and trivially refinanceable given the rating. There is no refinancing wall in any forward year that would meaningfully strain cash.

**Liquidity is comfortable but not as fortress-like as the headline suggests.** Cash and short-term investments fell from $111.3B (FY23) peak to $94.6B (FY25) as buybacks ($18.4B in FY25), dividends, and capex absorbed cash. Current ratio is 1.4x (FY25), down from 1.8x (FY23) — still healthy, but the working-capital cushion has compressed. The mental stress test is informative: if revenue fell 30% for two years (a deeply implausible scenario for a software company with this revenue mix), operating cash flow would still likely exceed $50–70B per year — enough to fund maintenance capex (~$30B), the dividend (~$25B), and interest with no need to access markets. Microsoft passes the "could you survive 2–3 bad years without raising money" test by a wide margin.

**The real balance-sheet question is off-balance-sheet — operating leases and AI build commitments.** Microsoft spent $11.1B on data-center leases *in a single quarter* (FY26 Q1), with the rest of capex going to GPUs and long-lived assets. Operating-lease right-of-use assets and the corresponding lease liabilities are now on-balance-sheet under ASC 842, but the *commitment* tail (long-dated take-or-pay for power, fiber, colocation) is growing rapidly. The $80B disclosed Azure backlog that Microsoft cannot fulfill due to power constraints implies an equally large incoming commitment stack. None of this rises to "distress" — Microsoft is the most creditworthy non-financial in the US — but the simple Debt/EBITDA number understates the true leverage of the AI strategy. A more honest "all-in" obligations measure (debt + lease liabilities + purchase commitments) would be materially higher than $60B, though still very manageable relative to EBITDA.

**Pension and contingent liabilities are not a material issue.** Microsoft has no defined-benefit pension overhang of meaningful size, no underfunded post-retirement health obligations of note, and no significant litigation reserves that would change the credit picture. The regulatory tail (FTC, EU on M365 bundling, Activision integration) is real but a P&L risk, not a balance-sheet risk.

**Stress-test verdict: Microsoft survives anything short of an existential, multi-year demand collapse without raising capital.** The combination of $94.6B in cash/investments, $50B+/year of true free operating cash flow before growth capex, AAA rating, and trivial near-term maturities makes the survival question moot. The reason this is not a 10/10 is the rapidly growing off-balance-sheet obligations stack — a deliberate decision by management, but one that takes Microsoft from "fortress with no obligations" toward "very strong balance sheet with rising contractual commitments."

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** AAA-rated fortress with shrinking debt, $94B in cash, and 52x interest coverage — Microsoft can keep self-funding the AI build from operating cash flow indefinitely, never needs the bond market, and could redirect the dividend or buyback to absorb any surprise.
- **Bear case:** Off-balance-sheet operating lease and data-center commitment growth quietly compounds; if AI revenue disappoints by FY28, the cost stack proves stickier than the demand, and Microsoft draws down cash from $94B toward $50B while still paying the dividend — uncomfortable, but not solvency-threatening.
- **Confidence:** High — the data is unambiguous on the headline numbers; the only uncertainty is the size of the contractual-commitment tail, which is large but well within Microsoft's cash-generation capacity.

## Red Flags

- Operating-lease and data-center commitment growth is the true leverage story — headline Debt/EBITDA of 0.4x understates it. Worth monitoring the 10-K commitments footnote each year.
- Cash and short-term investments have fallen from $111.3B (FY23) to $94.6B (FY25) as buybacks, dividends, and capex consume liquidity. Still very strong, but the trajectory is down, not up.
- Current ratio compressed from 1.8x (FY23) to 1.4x (FY25) — minor but worth noting as another sign that working-capital cushion is being deployed.
- If the AI capex cycle continues past calendar 2027 at $200B/yr, Microsoft will eventually need to either issue debt, slow buybacks materially, or both. The AAA rating gives them an enviable cost of debt if/when that happens.

## Score: 9 / 10

A near-fortress balance sheet — AAA rating, 0.4x leverage, 52x interest coverage, shrinking debt stack, and ample liquidity. The only reason this isn't a 10 is the rapidly growing off-balance-sheet obligation tail from the AI infrastructure build, which is large enough to deserve explicit acknowledgment even though it is comfortably affordable.
