## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** TXN carries $14.0B in total debt ($13.5B long-term), against $16.3B in stockholders' equity, producing a debt/equity ratio of 86.3%. This is elevated relative to TI's own history (the company carried minimal debt through the 2010s) but entirely explained by the strategic decision to partially debt-fund the 300mm fab buildout. The weighted average coupon of approximately 4.0% is very manageable for an A1/AA- rated issuer. TI has been disciplined about maturity management — the most recent issuance (May 2025) placed $550M at 4.50% due 2030 and $650M at 5.10% due 2035, deliberately avoiding near-term maturities. Management has stated explicitly that the debt strategy avoids concentrated maturities to maintain strategic flexibility. With $14B in debt on a business generating $7.2B in operating cash flow, TI has roughly 2x debt/OCF coverage — well within safe territory for a capital-intensive manufacturer.

**Interest Coverage and Debt Serviceability.** Interest expense has risen from $214M (FY2022) to $543M (FY2025), reflecting the debt buildup. Interest coverage of 11.5x operating income remains very healthy — this is a company earning $6.1B in operating income against $543M in interest. Even on an EBITDA basis, coverage is over 15x. The directional trend (coverage declining from 47.9x to 11.5x over three years) looks concerning in isolation, but the absolute level is far from stressed. As capex normalizes and FCF inflects upward, TI is likely to stabilize or modestly reduce total debt, which should stabilize interest coverage in the 10–14x range going forward. There is no scenario where interest payments threaten TXN's operational flexibility at current debt levels.

**Liquidity Position.** TXN's liquidity is robust: $4.9B in cash, a current ratio of 4.4x, and operating cash flow of $7.2B annually. The company also has access to commercial paper programs and revolving credit facilities typical of an A1-rated issuer, though it has not needed to draw on them. The CHIPS Act provides additional liquidity: TI received $75M in direct CHIPS funding in FY2025, with a $555M milestone payment in Q1 2026 and additional disbursements tied to future capex milestones. The investment tax credit contributed ~$670M in FY2025 cash benefits, and with the ITC rate now at 35% (up from 25%), this will continue to provide meaningful cash support as long as qualifying capex continues. Total CHIPS Act support is estimated at $1.6B in direct funding plus $6–8B in ITC over the program's life.

**Refinancing and Maturity Risk.** TI's deliberate approach to staggering maturities is a key balance sheet strength. No single year has a concentrated maturity wall. The company has access to investment-grade credit markets at attractive rates (most recent issuances in the 4.5–5.1% range), and its A1/AA- credit ratings provide ample refinancing capacity. The risk of a credit downgrade is minimal given the improving FCF trajectory. If anything, as capex drops to $2–3B in FY2026 and beyond, TI's credit metrics will improve naturally — net debt/EBITDA could decline below 1.0x within 2–3 years without active deleveraging.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** Assume revenue drops 30% from FY2025's $17.7B to $12.4B and stays there for two years. At a stressed gross margin of ~48% (below even FY2025 levels, reflecting deleverage on the fixed cost base), gross profit would be ~$5.9B. Operating expenses (R&D + SG&A) of ~$3.0B and interest of ~$550M would leave operating income of ~$2.4B. With depreciation of ~$2.0B adding back, EBITDA would be ~$4.4B, and maintenance capex of ~$1.5B would leave FCF of roughly $1.5–2.0B — still positive. Debt/EBITDA would rise to ~3.2x, interest coverage would fall to ~4.5x. Uncomfortable but well within investment-grade territory. TXN would not face a solvency crisis, would not need to access emergency funding, and could continue paying its dividend (currently ~$5.2B annually, though it would likely be reduced in a severe scenario). The $4.9B cash buffer provides an additional 2+ years of cushion even if FCF temporarily went negative. This is a fortress balance sheet that can withstand severe cyclical stress.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Considerations.** TXN's off-balance-sheet obligations are typical for a semiconductor manufacturer: operating leases (modest, as TI owns most of its manufacturing facilities), purchase obligations for equipment and materials, and pension/post-retirement obligations. None of these represent material hidden liabilities. The largest contingent commitment is the ongoing fab buildout, but with capex now guided down to $2–3B and CHIPS Act offsets, this is a manageable and declining obligation.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TXN's balance sheet is at its "worst" point in the cycle — as capex normalizes and FCF inflects to $8+ per share, leverage ratios will improve rapidly without any active deleveraging needed.
- **Bear case:** If TI needs to accelerate LFAB2 spending or if end-market recovery stalls, debt could remain elevated longer, and the company may need additional issuance to fund both capex and its $5B+ annual shareholder return commitment.
- **Confidence:** High — TI is an A1-rated company with $7B+ OCF, no near-term maturity risk, strong CHIPS Act support, and a management team with a multi-decade track record of conservative balance sheet management.

## Red Flags
- Total debt has grown from ~$7B to $14B in four years; while manageable, this is a meaningful change in the capital structure
- Interest expense more than doubled; if rates stay elevated, refinancing maturing tranches will be more expensive than historical averages
- Shareholder returns ($6.5B in FY2025 via dividends + buybacks) exceeded FCF ($2.6B), funded partly by debt — this is unsustainable long-term if FCF doesn't recover as guided
- Dividend commitment of ~$5.2B/year is large relative to current FCF; a severe downturn could force a trim, though management has never cut the dividend

## Score: 7 / 10
TXN's balance sheet is conservatively managed with well-laddered debt, ample liquidity, and strong credit ratings, but the doubling of total debt during the fab buildout and the gap between shareholder returns and current FCF warrant a modest deduction from an otherwise strong safety profile.
