## Detailed Analysis

**Capital allocation philosophy.** TI's capital allocation framework is among the most clearly articulated in the semiconductor industry. The company has publicly stated for over a decade that its objective is to "maximize long-term growth of free cash flow per share." This is not vague corporate-speak — it is an explicit, measurable commitment that management reports against quarterly. The framework prioritizes: (1) investing in the business (R&D and manufacturing); (2) returning all free cash flow to shareholders over time through dividends and buybacks. In FY2025, TI invested $4.6B in CapEx and $1.7B in R&D while returning $6.5B to shareholders. The fact that returns exceeded FCF ($2.6B) reflects management's willingness to use the balance sheet during a known peak-CapEx period — a deliberate, communicated choice, not reckless behavior.

**The CapEx cycle — bold and well-communicated.** The defining capital allocation decision of this management team has been the $30+ billion, six-year manufacturing investment in 300mm analog capacity. This was a counter-cyclical bet: building capacity during a downturn to be ready when demand recovers. The February 2026 Capital Management update confirmed the investment phase is winding down — FY2026 CapEx is guided to $2-3B (down from $4.6B in FY2025 and the $5.1B peak in FY2023), with a target of $8+ FCF per share as depreciation growth moderates while revenue recovers. Management has delivered the buildout "on time" and "on budget" per their own reporting — SM1 Sherman began production in December 2025, RFAB2 is ramping, and LFAB (Lehi) is adding products. If the demand recovery materializes, FCF will inflect sharply upward. This is Buffett-style capital allocation: invest heavily in durable competitive advantages when the market is pessimistic, harvest cash when the cycle turns.

**Shareholder returns track record.** TI has raised its dividend for 22 consecutive years. The current annual dividend of $5.68/share represents a 2.93% yield. The payout ratio versus trailing earnings (~103% of FCF in FY2025) is elevated but manageable given the known CapEx peak — as CapEx declines, FCF will normalize well above the dividend burden. On buybacks, TI has been steady but measured: share count declined from ~926M in FY2022 to ~910M in FY2025, a ~1.7% total reduction over three years. This is deliberately conservative during the heavy investment phase. Management has $15B in buyback authorization remaining. As FCF inflects higher in FY2026-FY2027, buyback acceleration is likely.

**Leadership and governance.** Haviv Ilan became CEO in April 2023 after 26 years at TI, including leadership of the Analog business and COO. He became chairman in January 2026, succeeding Rich Templeton who retired after 45 years. The transition was orderly — Ilan had been on the board since 2021 and was groomed for the role over years. His direct ownership ($12.2M) is modest for a company of this size, which is a minor concern, though his compensation is significantly performance-linked (salary + bonus + profit sharing + equity, with the committee raising bonuses 10% in FY2025 based on 13% revenue growth and 34.1% operating margins). TI's board has a standard governance structure with no dual-class shares or poison pills.

**Candor and consistency.** TI management communicates with unusual clarity. The Capital Management presentation — delivered annually — lays out long-term CapEx plans, FCF targets, and return-of-capital priorities with specific numbers. When margins compressed during the CapEx build, management did not hide behind adjusted metrics — they explained that depreciation was rising as new fabs ramped and that this was temporary. The $8+ FCF/share target for the post-investment period is a concrete, trackable commitment. This is the language of owners, not politicians. One area of concern: TI chose not to break out end-market revenue in granular detail starting in 2024, instead providing directional commentary. This is a mild step backward in transparency, though the company still discloses more than most semiconductor peers.

**Compensation design.** Executive compensation at TI is a mix of base salary, annual cash bonus, profit sharing, and equity awards. The compensation committee targets equity grants around market medians and ties bonuses to financial performance. The 10% bonus increase in FY2025 was tied to concrete results (13% revenue growth, 34.1% operating margin). There are no obvious red flags in compensation structure — no excessive perks, no repriced options, no unusual one-time awards. SBC of $419M in FY2025 represents ~2.4% of revenue, which is reasonable for a semiconductor company.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TI's management has executed a $30B+ counter-cyclical manufacturing investment on time and on budget, and the company is now entering a multi-year FCF inflection as CapEx drops from $4.6B to $2-3B while revenue recovers — the best of the capital allocation story is ahead.
- **Bear case:** CEO insider ownership is modest, the dividend payout temporarily exceeds FCF, and the share count reduction over three years has been minimal (~1.7%) — management has prioritized CapEx over direct shareholder returns during a period of compressed earnings.
- **Confidence:** High — TI's capital allocation framework is the most transparent and consistently communicated in the semiconductor industry, with a trackable multi-decade record of execution.

## Red Flags
- CEO direct ownership of only $12.2M (0.007%) is low for a $177B company — alignment relies heavily on equity compensation rather than meaningful personal stake
- Dividend payout exceeded FCF in FY2025 ($5.2B dividends vs $2.6B FCF) — sustainable only if FCF recovers as CapEx declines
- Share count reduction of only 1.7% over 3 years is modest; net dilution from SBC partially offsets buybacks
- Reduced end-market revenue disclosure starting FY2024 is a mild transparency step backward
- Heavy CapEx commitments were made under prior CEO Templeton — Ilan inherits execution risk on the ramp

## Score: 8 / 10
TI's management team operates with a clear, owner-oriented capital allocation framework backed by a 22-year dividend growth streak and a well-executed $30B+ manufacturing investment — modestly dinged by low CEO ownership and temporarily elevated payout ratios, but the FCF inflection ahead should validate the strategy.
