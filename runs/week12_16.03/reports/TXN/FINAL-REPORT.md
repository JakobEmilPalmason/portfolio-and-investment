## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: TXN sells 80,000+ analog and embedded chips (avg under $1 each) to 100,000 customers across industrial and automotive end markets, where 10-15 year design lives make switching irrational.
2. **Moat**: TXN's $30B+ investment in 300mm wafer fabs delivers a ~40% cost-per-chip advantage over 200mm competitors that no rival can replicate within 5-7 years.
3. **Economics**: Operating cash flow of $7.2B (41% of revenue) persisted even at cyclical trough, and as capex drops from $4.6B to $2-3B in 2026, FCF should inflect toward $8+/share.
4. **Management**: The team executed a decade-long counter-cyclical fab buildout on time and on budget, raised the dividend 22 consecutive years, and explicitly targets maximizing long-term FCF per share.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt/EBITDA of 1.3x with $4.9B cash, A1/AA- ratings, and no concentrated maturities means TXN survives a 30% revenue decline for two years without solvency risk.
6. **Price**: At $194 (35.6x trailing, 24.5x forward), the market requires revenue recovery to $20B+, gross margins back above 60%, and $8+ EPS by 2027 — all guided but not guaranteed.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business is a fortress (#1 analog position, diversified customers, positive FCF since 2004), but at $194 there is zero price margin of safety since the stock trades 15% above base-case IV of $168.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if gross margins remain below 58% through FY2027, which would mean the 300mm cost advantage failed to materialize and the entire capex thesis was wrong.

## Key Risks

1. **No price margin of safety at $194** — trading above base-case IV of $168, with unfavorable downside/upside asymmetry (bear -42% vs bull +16%). The "harvest year" narrative is Wall Street consensus, meaning any disappointment will be punished disproportionately.
2. **Capex cycle execution risk** — gross margins have compressed 12pp (68.8% to 57.0%) and ROIC has fallen from 38.9% to 17.7%. Recovery depends on revenue growing into new 300mm capacity; if analog demand stays sluggish, TXN carries expensive idle capacity for years.
3. **China geopolitical exposure** — approximately 20% of revenue comes from China, creating tail risk from trade restrictions or domestic Chinese analog competitors eroding pricing power.
4. **Elevated debt and shareholder return gap** — total debt doubled to $14B during the fab buildout, and FY2025 shareholder returns ($6.5B) exceeded FCF ($2.6B). Sustainable only if the FCF inflection materializes as guided.
5. **Embedded Processing weakness** — TXN holds a smaller, less dominant position in MCUs versus Infineon (21.3% share), Renesas, and STM, with lower margins and more fragmented competitive dynamics.

# Circle of Competence — TXN

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-17
**Data Sources:** TI 2025 Annual Report, Q4 2025 earnings call, Q1 2026 guidance, TI investor relations, Yahoo Finance, MacroTrends, Morningstar, industry research

| 4 | Revenue model is high-volume, low-ASP ($0.05 to $5 per chip typical), deeply embedded in customer designs | 4 |
| 5 | FY2025 revenue of $17.7B recovered to near FY2023 levels after a cyclical trough in FY2024 ($15.6B), but still below FY2022 peak of $20.0B | 3 |
| 6 | Q1 2026 guidance of $4.32B-$4.68B implies continued recovery trajectory | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Texas Instruments designs, manufactures, and sells analog and embedded processing semiconductors. The business is straightforward: TI makes chips that manage power, convert signals, and process data in real-world applications. These are not the glamorous AI chips making headlines — they are the workhorse components inside virtually every electronic device, from cars and factory robots to medical equipment and home appliances. Analog chips (Power and Signal Chain product lines) generated $14.0B in FY2025, or 79% of revenue. Embedded Processing contributed $2.7B (15%), with the remainder from smaller categories.

The revenue model is simple and highly diversified. TI sells over 80,000 products to approximately 100,000 customers. No single customer represents more than 10% of revenue. The average chip sells for well under $5, often under $1, making it a trivial line item for the customer but mission-critical in the end product. This creates natural stickiness: no procurement team will requalify a $0.35 power management chip to save $0.02, especially when requalification costs thousands of dollars and months of engineering time. Revenue is driven by design wins that persist for a decade or more.

The key business drivers are: (1) design win volume — the number of new customer designs specifying TI parts, which drives revenue 2-5 years out; (2) end-market health in industrial and automotive, which together represent roughly 70% of revenue; (3) manufacturing cost per chip, where TI's 300mm wafer strategy is structurally lowering costs; and (4) pricing discipline, where TI's breadth and reliability support stable ASPs even in cyclical downturns.

Revenue predictability is moderate-to-good. While TI is exposed to semiconductor cyclicality (FY2022 peak of $20.0B fell 22% to the FY2024 trough of $15.6B), the analog segment is notably less volatile than digital semiconductors. Long product lifecycles, diversified end markets, and a customer base of 100,000 accounts provide a built-in smoothing effect. The industrial and automotive end markets have structural tailwints from electrification, factory automation, and ADAS adoption.

The business passes the "explain it in two minutes" test with ease. TI makes small, cheap, essential chips that go into everything. They own their factories, which gives them a cost advantage. They sell to everyone, so no single customer matters. The chips last in designs for 10+ years, so once designed in, the revenue stream is highly persistent. The company has been doing this for decades — it is not a thesis that requires a leap of faith about future technology shifts.

One nuance worth flagging: personal electronics (20% of revenue) is more volatile and lower-margin than industrial/automotive. TI has been strategically de-emphasizing this segment, but it still creates quarterly noise. The communications equipment segment (4%) is small and declining. The overall trajectory is toward higher-quality, longer-duration revenue streams.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TI's extreme product and customer diversification, combined with 10-15 year design lives, makes this one of the most predictable semiconductor businesses in the world.
- **Bear case:** The business is still cyclical — FY2025 revenue has not yet recovered to FY2022 peak, and paying 35.6x trailing earnings for a mid-cycle business assumes sustained growth that may not materialize.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is transparent, well-documented, and has operated consistently for decades.

## Red Flags
- Revenue remains 12% below FY2022 peak despite "recovery" narrative
- Personal electronics segment (20% of revenue) adds volatility
- Gross margins declining (68.8% FY2022 to 57.0% FY2025) partly due to heavy CapEx flowing through depreciation as new fabs ramp
- Embedded Processing revenue ($2.7B) faces competitive pressure from Infineon (21.3% MCU market share) and Renesas

## Score: 9 / 10
Texas Instruments is among the most understandable semiconductor businesses in existence — diversified customers, simple products, long design lives, and a transparent manufacturing model make this firmly within any generalist investor's circle of competence.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — TXN

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-17
**Data Sources:** TI 2025 Annual Report, Capital Management presentation (Feb 2026), TI investor relations, Morningstar, CSIMarket, industry analysis, competitor financial reports

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | TI is the world's largest analog chipmaker with ~19% market share, roughly 2x the next competitor (Analog Devices) | 5 |
| 2 | 300mm wafer manufacturing creates a structural ~40% cost advantage per chip vs 200mm competitors — this advantage is widening as new fabs ramp | 5 |
| 3 | 80,000+ products with 10-15 year lifecycles create massive switching costs at the design-in level | 5 |
| 4 | Gross margin of 57-58% in a cyclical trough exceeds or matches all major competitors (ADI 58%, STM 37.7%, Infineon 39.2%) | 4 |
| 5 | $30B+ invested over 6 years in manufacturing capacity; by 2030 TI targets >95% internal wafer sourcing and >80% on 300mm | 4 |
| 6 | Direct sales via TI.com growing, eliminating distributor margin and deepening customer relationships | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Texas Instruments possesses a wide, multi-layered competitive moat built on cost advantage, switching costs, and scale economics. The moat is not derived from any single source but from the reinforcing combination of all three — a structure that is exceptionally difficult for competitors to replicate.

**Cost advantage through manufacturing scale.** TI's defining strategic bet of the last decade has been its $30+ billion investment in 300mm analog wafer fabrication. A 300mm wafer has 2.3x the area of a 200mm wafer but costs only ~40% more to process, yielding a roughly 40% cost advantage per chip. Most analog competitors (STMicroelectronics, NXP, ON Semi, Microchip) still operate primarily on 200mm. Analog Devices, TI's closest peer, is largely fabless/fab-lite. By 2030, TI expects >80% of its production on 300mm with >95% internal sourcing. The Sherman, Texas SM1 fab began production in December 2025, and the RFAB2 facility in Richardson is ramping toward more than double the output of the original RFAB1. This is a decade-long infrastructure moat: no competitor can credibly replicate this 300mm analog capacity within 5-7 years even if they started today.

**Switching costs at the design-in level.** Analog chips are designed into customer products for 10-15 year lifecycles. Once a TI power management IC or signal chain component is qualified into an automotive ECU or industrial controller, the cost of switching (requalification, redesign, testing) vastly exceeds the cost of the chip itself. With 80,000+ products serving ~100,000 customers, TI has an enormous installed base of design wins that generate recurring revenue for over a decade per win. This is fundamentally different from digital semiconductors where product cycles reset every 2-3 years.

**Scale economics shared.** TI's catalog breadth (80,000+ SKUs) means it can amortize R&D, sales, and manufacturing overhead across a product portfolio that no competitor can match. This allows TI to profitably serve long-tail markets that are uneconomical for smaller competitors. The company's direct sales channel (TI.com) further reinforces this by cutting out distributor margin while giving TI direct visibility into customer demand patterns. Scale also enables TI to maintain application engineers and reference designs across more end markets than any peer.

**Numerical evidence of moat durability.** Even in the cyclical trough of FY2024-FY2025, TI maintained gross margins of 57-58% and operating margins of 34-35% — while simultaneously absorbing $2.0B in annual depreciation from new fab investments (up from $979M in FY2022). On a depreciation-adjusted basis, the underlying business economics are extraordinarily strong. For comparison, in Q4 2024, STMicroelectronics had a 37.7% gross margin and Infineon 39.2%. Only Analog Devices (~58%) comes close, but ADI relies heavily on external foundries and lacks TI's manufacturing cost trajectory.

**Where is the moat weakest?** Embedded Processing is the vulnerability. TI holds a smaller, less dominant position in microcontrollers versus Infineon (21.3% MCU market share), Renesas, and STMicroelectronics. Embedded Processing margins are lower, and the competitive dynamics are more fragmented. Additionally, in specific high-growth analog sub-segments like automotive-grade power semiconductors, Infineon and ON Semi have been gaining share. TI's moat is widest in broad-market analog (power, signal chain) and narrower in application-specific markets where competitors have deep domain expertise.

**Does the moat reset every product cycle?** No — and this is critical. Unlike digital semiconductors (GPUs, CPUs, memory), analog chip designs are evolutionary, not revolutionary. A power management IC designed in 2020 will still be manufactured and sold in 2035. There is no "Moore's Law" forcing obsolescence. TI's process nodes (45nm-180nm for analog) are mature and stable, meaning its manufacturing investments have multi-decade useful lives. This is a compounding advantage: every year of production on 300mm widens the cumulative cost gap versus 200mm competitors.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TI is building a structural, decade-long manufacturing cost advantage that competitors cannot replicate, while its installed base of 80,000+ products with 10-15 year lifecycles generates deeply embedded, recurring revenue.
- **Bear case:** The massive CapEx cycle ($30B+ over 6 years) has temporarily depressed returns (ROIC fell from 38.9% to 17.7%) and margins — if the demand recovery underperforms, TI will be carrying expensive idle capacity.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat sources are observable, quantifiable, and structurally reinforced by long product lifecycles and massive capital barriers to entry.

## Red Flags
- ROIC has fallen from 38.9% (FY2022) to 17.7% (FY2025) as CapEx and depreciation ramp ahead of revenue recovery
- Gross margins have declined 1,180 bps (68.8% to 57.0%) over three years, partly structural as new fabs carry higher depreciation
- Embedded Processing is a weaker competitive position facing strong rivals (Infineon, Renesas, STM)
- Analog market share reportedly declined from ~19.8% to ~14.7% by some measures during the 2020-2023 capacity-constrained period — recovery trajectory unclear
- If the analog cycle does not fully recover, TI's heavy fixed-cost base creates operating leverage in both directions

## Score: 8 / 10
TI possesses a wide moat in analog semiconductors built on a rare combination of manufacturing cost advantage, design-in switching costs, and unmatched product breadth — but the moat is temporarily obscured by a heavy CapEx cycle that has compressed returns and margins, and the Embedded Processing segment faces stronger competitive headwinds.

# Management & Capital Allocation — TXN

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-17
**Data Sources:** TI 2025 Annual Report, 2025 Proxy Statement, Capital Management presentation (Feb 2026), TI investor relations, SEC filings, Simply Wall St, Fintool, Yahoo Finance

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | TI has returned $6.5B to shareholders in FY2025 ($5.2B dividends + ~$1.5B buybacks) while investing $4.6B in CapEx — disciplined dual-track allocation | 5 |
| 2 | 22 consecutive years of dividend increases; $5.68/share annual dividend with 2.93% yield | 4 |
| 3 | Share count reduced from ~926M (2022) to ~910M (2025) — steady but not aggressive buyback pace during heavy CapEx cycle | 3 |
| 4 | CEO Haviv Ilan's direct ownership is modest ($12.2M, 0.007% of company) though he has a 26-year tenure at TI | 3 |
| 5 | CapEx guided down to $2-3B for 2026 from $4.6B in 2025, with FCF/share target of $8+ — the heavy investment phase is ending | 5 |
| 6 | Explicit long-term objective: "maximize long-term growth of free cash flow per share" — stated consistently for over a decade | 5 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Capital allocation philosophy.** TI's capital allocation framework is among the most clearly articulated in the semiconductor industry. The company has publicly stated for over a decade that its objective is to "maximize long-term growth of free cash flow per share." This is not vague corporate-speak — it is an explicit, measurable commitment that management reports against quarterly. The framework prioritizes: (1) investing in the business (R&D and manufacturing); (2) returning all free cash flow to shareholders over time through dividends and buybacks. In FY2025, TI invested $4.6B in CapEx and $1.7B in R&D while returning $6.5B to shareholders. The fact that returns exceeded FCF ($2.6B) reflects management's willingness to use the balance sheet during a known peak-CapEx period — a deliberate, communicated choice, not reckless behavior.

**The CapEx cycle — bold and well-communicated.** The defining capital allocation decision of this management team has been the $30+ billion, six-year manufacturing investment in 300mm analog capacity. This was a counter-cyclical bet: building capacity during a downturn to be ready when demand recovers. The February 2026 Capital Management update confirmed the investment phase is winding down — FY2026 CapEx is guided to $2-3B (down from $4.6B in FY2025 and the $5.1B peak in FY2023), with a target of $8+ FCF per share as depreciation growth moderates while revenue recovers. Management has delivered the buildout "on time" and "on budget" per their own reporting — SM1 Sherman began production in December 2025, RFAB2 is ramping, and LFAB (Lehi) is adding products. If the demand recovery materializes, FCF will inflect sharply upward. This is Buffett-style capital allocation: invest heavily in durable competitive advantages when the market is pessimistic, harvest cash when the cycle turns.

**Shareholder returns track record.** TI has raised its dividend for 22 consecutive years. The current annual dividend of $5.68/share represents a 2.93% yield. The payout ratio versus trailing earnings (~103% of FCF in FY2025) is elevated but manageable given the known CapEx peak — as CapEx declines, FCF will normalize well above the dividend burden. On buybacks, TI has been steady but measured: share count declined from ~926M in FY2022 to ~910M in FY2025, a ~1.7% total reduction over three years. This is deliberately conservative during the heavy investment phase. Management has $15B in buyback authorization remaining. As FCF inflects higher in FY2026-FY2027, buyback acceleration is likely.

**Leadership and governance.** Haviv Ilan became CEO in April 2023 after 26 years at TI, including leadership of the Analog business and COO. He became chairman in January 2026, succeeding Rich Templeton who retired after 45 years. The transition was orderly — Ilan had been on the board since 2021 and was groomed for the role over years. His direct ownership ($12.2M) is modest for a company of this size, which is a minor concern, though his compensation is significantly performance-linked (salary + bonus + profit sharing + equity, with the committee raising bonuses 10% in FY2025 based on 13% revenue growth and 34.1% operating margins). TI's board has a standard governance structure with no dual-class shares or poison pills.

**Candor and consistency.** TI management communicates with unusual clarity. The Capital Management presentation — delivered annually — lays out long-term CapEx plans, FCF targets, and return-of-capital priorities with specific numbers. When margins compressed during the CapEx build, management did not hide behind adjusted metrics — they explained that depreciation was rising as new fabs ramped and that this was temporary. The $8+ FCF/share target for the post-investment period is a concrete, trackable commitment. This is the language of owners, not politicians. One area of concern: TI chose not to break out end-market revenue in granular detail starting in 2024, instead providing directional commentary. This is a mild step backward in transparency, though the company still discloses more than most semiconductor peers.

**Compensation design.** Executive compensation at TI is a mix of base salary, annual cash bonus, profit sharing, and equity awards. The compensation committee targets equity grants around market medians and ties bonuses to financial performance. The 10% bonus increase in FY2025 was tied to concrete results (13% revenue growth, 34.1% operating margin). There are no obvious red flags in compensation structure — no excessive perks, no repriced options, no unusual one-time awards. SBC of $419M in FY2025 represents ~2.4% of revenue, which is reasonable for a semiconductor company.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TI's management has executed a $30B+ counter-cyclical manufacturing investment on time and on budget, and the company is now entering a multi-year FCF inflection as CapEx drops from $4.6B to $2-3B while revenue recovers — the best of the capital allocation story is ahead.
- **Bear case:** CEO insider ownership is modest, the dividend payout temporarily exceeds FCF, and the share count reduction over three years has been minimal (~1.7%) — management has prioritized CapEx over direct shareholder returns during a period of compressed earnings.
- **Confidence:** High — TI's capital allocation framework is the most transparent and consistently communicated in the semiconductor industry, with a trackable multi-decade record of execution.

## Red Flags
- CEO direct ownership of only $12.2M (0.007%) is low for a $177B company — alignment relies heavily on equity compensation rather than meaningful personal stake
- Dividend payout exceeded FCF in FY2025 ($5.2B dividends vs $2.6B FCF) — sustainable only if FCF recovers as CapEx declines
- Share count reduction of only 1.7% over 3 years is modest; net dilution from SBC partially offsets buybacks
- Reduced end-market revenue disclosure starting FY2024 is a mild transparency step backward
- Heavy CapEx commitments were made under prior CEO Templeton — Ilan inherits execution risk on the ramp

## Score: 8 / 10
TI's management team operates with a clear, owner-oriented capital allocation framework backed by a 22-year dividend growth streak and a well-executed $30B+ manufacturing investment — modestly dinged by low CEO ownership and temporarily elevated payout ratios, but the FCF inflection ahead should validate the strategy.

# Business Economics — TXN

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-17
**Data Sources:** TXN 10-K filings (FY2022-FY2025), Q4 2025 earnings release, TI Capital Management Update (Feb 2026), yfinance financial data, web research

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC declined from 38.9% (FY2022) to 17.7% (FY2025) — almost entirely driven by the massive 300mm fab investment cycle, not business deterioration | 5 |
| 2 | Capex guided to $2-3B in 2026 vs $4.5-5.1B in FY2023-FY2025; FCF per share targeted at $8+ as the investment cycle winds down | 5 |
| 3 | Gross margins compressed ~12pp from 68.8% to 57.0% (FY2022 to FY2025) as depreciation from new fabs ($2.0B vs $979M) flows through before revenue fully loads | 4 |
| 4 | Operating cash flow remarkably resilient at $7.2B (FY2025) despite the cyclical trough, demonstrating the analog business model's inherent cash generation | 4 |
| 5 | Revenue quality is high: ~75% analog (long product lifecycles, 10+ year revenue tails), minimal customer concentration, 100K+ customers across industrial/auto/enterprise | 5 |
| 6 | Owner earnings bottomed at $1.6B (FY2024) and are recovering — $2.5B in FY2025, with a path to $6B+ as capex normalizes and fabs load | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Temporarily Depressed, Not Impaired.** TXN's ROIC trajectory — from nearly 39% in FY2022 to 17.7% in FY2025 — looks alarming in isolation but is entirely explained by the denominator. TI has invested over $17B in capex across FY2022-FY2025 building three major 300mm fabs (RFAB2 in Richardson, LFAB1 and LFAB2 in Lehi). This is classic growth capex: the capital is deployed, depreciation is flowing through the P&L, but the revenue to fill these fabs has not yet materialized. The key question is what "normalized" ROIC looks like once fab utilization reaches 60-70%. Based on TI's historical economics (ROIC consistently above 30% from 2017-2022) and the structural cost advantage of 300mm manufacturing (40% lower cost per chip vs 200mm), normalized ROIC should recover to the 25-35% range as the fabs ramp. A 17.7% ROIC during peak investment spend is actually a sign of underlying strength — many companies would show mid-single-digit returns during a capex cycle this intense.

**Margin Structure: Compression Is Mechanical, Not Competitive.** Gross margins fell from 68.8% to 57.0% over three years. This is almost entirely driven by two factors: (1) depreciation roughly doubled from ~$1B to ~$2B annually as new fabs came online, and (2) FY2023-FY2024 saw a cyclical revenue trough (industrial and auto end-markets destocked). Operating margins held up relatively well at 34.7% because TI runs a lean SG&A structure (~8-9% of revenue) and has minimal R&D bloat relative to peers. As capex normalizes and revenue loads into the new capacity, gross margins should recover toward the low 60s, and operating margins toward 40-45%. The embedded operating leverage is significant: incremental revenue on already-depreciated fabs carries very high incremental margins (estimated 70-80% incremental gross margin on 300mm production).

**Cash Generation: The Core Strength.** Operating cash flow of $7.2B in FY2025 on $17.7B revenue (40.7% OCF margin) is exceptional, particularly during a year of peak capex and compressed margins. FCF was $2.6B — up sharply from $1.5B in FY2024 and $1.3B in FY2023 — reflecting the early stages of the capex normalization. Management's February 2026 capital management update guided 2026 capex to $2-3B (potentially toward the lower end if growth is moderate), which implies FCF of $5-6B in FY2026 and $8+ per share. FCF conversion has been severely distorted by the investment cycle (20-52% range in FY2023-FY2025 vs historical 65-70%), but this is almost entirely a capex artifact, not an operating issue. SBC of $419M (2.4% of revenue) is modest and well-controlled.

**Capital Intensity: Growth Phase Ending.** TI's decision to bring nearly all manufacturing in-house on 300mm wafers is a generational bet on cost structure. By 2030, management targets 95%+ internal wafer sourcing with 80%+ on 300mm. The payoff is structural: 300mm wafers have ~40% lower per-unit cost, and internal sourcing eliminates foundry margin. Maintenance capex for this asset base should settle around $1.0-1.5B annually once the build cycle completes — well below the $4-5B peak. CHIPS Act support (up to $1.6B direct funding plus $6-8B in investment tax credits) meaningfully offsets the investment, effectively reducing TI's net capex burden by 15-20%. The ITC alone contributed ~$670M in FY2025 cash benefits.

**Revenue Quality and Operating Leverage.** TXN's revenue base is among the highest quality in semiconductors. Analog chips have product lifecycles measured in decades, not years. Catalog analog products (the majority of revenue) are sold across 100K+ customers with no meaningful concentration risk. Industrial and automotive — TI's two largest end markets — are secular growth verticals with long design-in cycles that create natural switching costs. Revenue is not recurring in a SaaS sense, but the replacement demand and long product tails create a similar economic effect. The operating leverage embedded in the model is substantial: once the new fabs are loaded to 50-60% utilization, each incremental dollar of revenue drops through at very high margins because the fixed cost base (depreciation, facility overhead) is already absorbed.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TXN is at the inflection point of a massive capex cycle — FCF is about to inflect sharply upward as capex drops from $4.5B to $2-3B while revenue grows into new 300mm capacity, returning ROIC to 25-35% range.
- **Bear case:** If analog/embedded demand stays sluggish or new fabs take longer to fill, TXN could carry excess depreciation for years, keeping margins and returns below historical norms.
- **Confidence:** High — the capex cycle is well-documented, management has a 20-year track record of disciplined capital allocation, and the Feb 2026 update confirms capex normalization is underway.

## Red Flags
- Gross margin has compressed ~12pp in three years; recovery depends on revenue growth materializing to fill new capacity
- Interest expense has more than doubled ($214M to $543M) as debt funded part of the fab buildout
- FY2022 represented a cyclical peak — comparing any metric to FY2022 overstates the decline
- CHIPS Act funding ($1.6B direct + ITC) could face political risk in future administrations, though most benefits have already been secured

## Score: 8 / 10
TXN's underlying business economics are elite — high-ROIC analog franchise with exceptional cash generation, long product lifecycles, and massive operating leverage — temporarily obscured by a once-in-a-generation capex cycle that is now clearly inflecting toward normalization.

# Balance Sheet Safety — TXN
