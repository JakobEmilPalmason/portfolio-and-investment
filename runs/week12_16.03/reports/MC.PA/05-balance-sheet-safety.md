## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure — Conservative by Any Measure.** LVMH's reported total debt of €36.7B requires careful disaggregation. Under IFRS 16, lease liabilities (primarily retail store leases globally) account for approximately €24B of this total. The actual financial debt — bonds and bank borrowings — is approximately €12.4B in long-term instruments. Against €13.5B in cash, LVMH is effectively net-cash on a financial-debt-only basis. Even including lease liabilities, net debt/EBITDA stands at just 0.4x (using the yfinance net debt figure of €11.6B). The company-reported net financial debt declined to €6.9B at end-2025, down from €9.2B at end-2024, marking the third consecutive year of deleveraging. Debt/equity at 54.4% is moderate and declining (from 63.7% in FY23). The equity base of €67.5B provides substantial cushion. LVMH's debt is predominantly fixed-rate euro-denominated bonds with laddered maturities. The most recent issuance — a €1.1B floating-rate note maturing March 2029 — demonstrates continued market access at favorable terms.

**Interest Coverage — Exceptionally Comfortable.** EBIT/interest expense of 15.5x in FY25 is well above the 5x comfort threshold, even after a year of declining profitability. At peak earnings (FY23), coverage was 23.2x. The relevant stress test is whether coverage remains adequate under severe conditions. Even if EBIT declined by 50% from current levels (to ~€8.9B), interest coverage would still be 7.4x — comfortably serviceable. Interest expense has been remarkably stable at €1.2B for two consecutive years despite rising rate environments, confirming the predominantly fixed-rate nature of the debt book. LVMH's AA-/Aa3 ratings give it access to investment-grade credit markets at tight spreads, meaning refinancing risk is minimal.

**Liquidity Position — Strong and Improving.** Cash of €13.5B plus undrawn committed credit facilities (LVMH typically maintains €5–8B in committed backup lines) provides total liquidity of approximately €19–21B. The current ratio improvement from 1.3x to 1.6x over three years reflects active working capital management and reduced short-term borrowing. Operating cash flow of €18.9B per year means LVMH generates its entire net debt position in cash flow roughly every 7–8 months. Even after dividends (~€6.5B based on €13/share for ~500M shares) and capex (€4.7B), LVMH retains roughly €7–8B in annual free cash flow after all distributions — more than sufficient to cover any upcoming maturities.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations and Lease Exposure.** The largest off-balance-sheet consideration is already on the balance sheet under IFRS 16: retail store leases totaling ~€24B. These represent real obligations but are also operationally flexible — luxury retail leases in prime locations can be renegotiated, sublet, or not renewed. LVMH operates approximately 5,700 stores globally; annual lease payments are roughly €5–6B. Pension obligations are modest for a European company of this scale, as LVMH's workforce is heavily concentrated in France (with government pension systems) and the US (where Tiffany 401k-style plans dominate). No material off-balance-sheet vehicles, guarantees, or unconsolidated entities have been flagged by auditors or rating agencies.

**Stress Test — Revenue Down 30% for Two Years.** If LVMH's revenue declined 30% (to ~€56.6B) and operating margins compressed to 15% (vs 21.9% currently), operating income would fall to approximately €8.5B. After interest expense of €1.2B and taxes at 25%, net income would be ~€5.5B. FCF, assuming maintenance capex of €3B and working capital release, would still be approximately €7–9B. The company could comfortably service all debt, maintain a reduced dividend, and avoid any equity raise. In fact, during the COVID shock of 2020 — when luxury revenue fell 17% — LVMH remained profitable and cash-generative throughout. A 30% revenue decline sustained for two years would be historically unprecedented for the luxury sector (even 2008–2009 saw approximately 15–20% peak-to-trough), making this a genuinely extreme scenario that LVMH could navigate without distress. The AA- rating specifically reflects this resilience; S&P's downgrade threshold would require a sustained deterioration well beyond this stress case.

**Refinancing Risk — Minimal.** With laddered bond maturities, €13.5B cash on hand, and €18.9B annual operating cash flow, LVMH faces no meaningful refinancing risk. The company could theoretically retire all financial debt from cash and one year's FCF without accessing capital markets at all. Recent bond issuances confirm market access remains wide open at investment-grade pricing.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Near-net-cash balance sheet (excluding leases), AA-/Aa3 ratings, and €14B+ annual FCF make LVMH one of the safest balance sheets in global consumer goods, capable of opportunistic M&A or accelerated buybacks at cyclical troughs.
- **Bear case:** Total reported debt of €36.7B (including €24B lease liabilities) represents real obligations in a prolonged downturn, and the company's acquisition appetite (Tiffany cost €16B) could add leverage in the future.
- **Confidence:** High — LVMH's balance sheet safety is well-documented, independently validated by two AA-tier credit ratings, and stress-tested through multiple cycles including COVID; the data leaves little room for ambiguity.

## Red Flags
- IFRS 16 lease liabilities of ~€24B obscure the true debt picture when looking at headline total debt — investors must disaggregate
- Acquisition risk: Bernard Arnault has historically pursued large deals (Tiffany at €16B); a future mega-acquisition could temporarily lever the balance sheet
- Dividend commitment of ~€6.5B/year is a real cash obligation, though easily covered and could be reduced if needed
- Floating-rate exposure increased with the €1.1B 2029 FRN issuance, though this is a small fraction of total debt

## Score: 9 / 10
LVMH's balance sheet is a fortress: net debt/EBITDA of 0.4x, interest coverage of 15.5x, AA-/Aa3 ratings, €13.5B cash, and €18.9B annual operating cash flow mean this company could survive a historically unprecedented downturn without raising equity — the only thing preventing a 10 is the €24B in lease liabilities and the ever-present risk of a leveraged mega-acquisition.
