## Detailed Analysis

**Owner Earnings Estimate.** LVMH generated owner earnings of approximately €14.2B in FY25, consistent with the €14.2–15.2B range over the past four years. This stability through a meaningful revenue downturn (€86.2B to €80.8B) is striking and reflects the inherent operating leverage in luxury brands with dominant market positions. Current owner earnings per share stand at approximately €28.60 (€14.2B / 496.1M shares). Looking forward, a conservative 3–5% annual growth in owner earnings is reasonable given LVMH's pricing power, geographic diversification, and brand portfolio depth. This would imply owner earnings of €16.5–18.1B by Year 5 (FY30).

**Scenario Analysis — Bear / Base / Bull.** In the Bear case, prolonged China weakness, tariff escalation, and aspirational consumer pullback limit organic growth to 0–1% annually. Owner earnings stay flat at ~€14B by Year 5. Applying a compressed 16x multiple (trough luxury valuation) and discounting back at 10% yields an intrinsic value of approximately €280 per share. In the Base case, luxury demand normalizes with low-to-mid single-digit organic growth. Owner earnings grow to ~€17B by Year 5. A 20x multiple (below LVMH's historical average) discounted at 10% yields approximately €540 per share. In the Bull case, China recovery accelerates, LVMH gains share from weaker competitors (Kering, Burberry), and margin expansion resumes. Owner earnings reach ~€20B by Year 5. A 23x multiple discounted at 10% yields approximately €720 per share.

**Multiples in Context.** LVMH's current trailing P/E of 21.4x is meaningfully below its 10-year average of ~27x. EV/EBITDA at 12.5x compares to a 10-year median of ~14x and a range of 9–28x. Among luxury peers, Hermes trades at ~54x P/E (reflecting its extreme brand scarcity premium), Richemont at ~22x, and Kering at ~30x (distorted by depressed earnings). LVMH's P/FCF of 19.9x on €14.2B FCF is attractive for a business of this quality. The stock is trading at valuations last seen during the 2020 COVID panic and the 2022 China lockdown fears — both proved to be excellent entry points.

**Implied Expectations.** At €468.55 per share and owner earnings of ~€28.60/share, the market is paying 16.4x owner earnings. For this price to deliver a 10% annual return over 5 years, LVMH would need to generate cumulative owner earnings of roughly €165/share over 5 years and exit at a 20x multiple on modestly higher earnings. This implies only ~2% annual growth — well below even the Bear case organic growth assumption. The market appears to be pricing in a permanent impairment to LVMH's earning power that seems inconsistent with the business's track record.

**Currency and Structural Considerations.** LVMH reports in EUR but generates roughly 25–30% of revenue in USD and 25–30% in Asia (largely CNY/JPY). The weak USD environment has been a headwind for reported results. Any normalization in USD/EUR or recovery in Asian currencies would provide a tailwind not reflected in current consensus estimates. Additionally, LVMH's selective retail model and vertical integration provide structural inflation protection that supports real earnings growth over time.

**Dividend and Capital Return.** The 2.75% dividend yield at current prices provides a meaningful return floor. LVMH has consistently grown dividends and supplements with occasional buybacks. Total shareholder yield including buybacks has historically been 3–4%.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** At 16x owner earnings, the market prices near-zero growth for a dominant luxury franchise — any normalization in China or stabilization of aspirational demand could drive 50%+ upside to fair value.
- **Bear case:** Structural shift away from logo-driven luxury consumption, sustained China consumer weakness, and tariff-driven margin compression could justify a further 20% de-rating to trough multiples.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Owner earnings stability through a downturn provides a solid valuation anchor, but the timing of recovery in China and the magnitude of tariff impacts introduce meaningful uncertainty around the base case.

## Red Flags
- Revenue has declined for two consecutive years (FY23 peak to FY25), and FY26 consensus growth of only low-single-digits suggests the trough may not yet be confirmed
- Operating margins compressed from 26.4% (FY23) to 21.9% (FY25) — the trajectory matters more than the level
- ROIC declined from 21.3% to 13.3% over two years, a sharp deterioration even if still adequate
- Hermes is taking share at the ultra-high end, raising questions about LVMH's relative brand positioning
- €36.7B total debt is substantial, though 1.4x Debt/EBITDA and 15.5x interest coverage are comfortable

## Score: 7 / 10
LVMH trades at a meaningful discount to its own historical averages and to intrinsic value under base and bull scenarios, with owner earnings implying the market expects near-zero growth — an unreasonably low bar for a diversified luxury conglomerate with demonstrated pricing power, though timing of cyclical recovery remains uncertain.
