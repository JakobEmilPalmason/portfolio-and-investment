## Detailed Analysis

LVMH is the world's largest luxury goods conglomerate, generating EUR 80.8 billion in FY2025 revenue across six business segments: Fashion & Leather Goods (EUR 37.8B), Selective Retailing (EUR 18.3B, primarily Sephora and DFS), Watches & Jewelry (EUR 10.5B, including Tiffany, Bulgari, TAG Heuer), Perfumes & Cosmetics (EUR 8.2B), Wines & Spirits (the remainder, including Moet, Hennessy, Dom Perignon), and Other Activities. The core value proposition is simple: LVMH owns brands with decades or centuries of heritage, manufactures high-quality goods, and sells them at substantial premiums through tightly controlled distribution channels. The customer base is global affluent consumers, with no single customer or customer group representing a concentration risk.

The revenue model is easy to explain in two minutes. LVMH acquires or develops luxury brands that carry cultural cachet and pricing power. It invests heavily in craftsmanship, marketing (approximately EUR 10B annually), and retail stores (6,280+ worldwide). Products are sold primarily through owned retail (direct-to-consumer), which gives LVMH full control over pricing, inventory, and brand experience. The company explicitly avoids discounting to preserve brand equity. This vertically integrated luxury retail model is well-understood and has been proven over three decades.

The key revenue drivers are limited and identifiable. First, global aspirational demand: the number of individuals with disposable income sufficient for luxury goods, particularly in China, the US, and Europe, drives topline growth. Second, pricing power: Louis Vuitton, Dior, and Tiffany regularly raise prices above inflation, and customers accept this because the products carry social signaling value. Third, geographic and category mix: the balance between mature markets (Europe, US) and growth markets (Asia) determines organic growth rates. Fourth, Sephora's mass-prestige retail model provides a steadier, less cyclical revenue stream than hard luxury.

Revenue predictability is moderate. Luxury spending is discretionary and correlated with consumer confidence, wealth effects (especially equity and real estate markets), and tourism flows. The FY23-to-FY25 decline from EUR 86.2B to EUR 80.8B illustrates this cyclicality, driven primarily by a normalization of post-COVID revenge spending and a slowdown in Chinese luxury demand. However, the cyclicality is dampened by LVMH's diversification across price points (Sephora cosmetics vs. high jewelry), geographies, and categories. Owner earnings remained resilient at EUR 14.2B in FY25 despite the revenue decline, demonstrating the inherent stability of the underlying cash generation.

Customer concentration is essentially zero. LVMH sells to millions of individual consumers worldwide. No single retailer, wholesale partner, or geography dominates to a dangerous degree — the most concentrated exposure is 26% to any single region. The shift toward direct retail (owned stores and e-commerce) has further reduced any dependence on third-party distributors.

The business is highly understandable to anyone who has observed consumer behavior around luxury goods. The only complexity comes from the sheer number of brands (75) and the conglomerate structure, which can make it harder to assess which individual brands are gaining or losing momentum. However, the financial reporting by segment is clear, and the dominant brands (Louis Vuitton, Dior, Tiffany, Sephora, Hennessy) are well-known. This is not a business that requires specialized technical knowledge to evaluate.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** LVMH's business model — own heritage brands, control distribution, charge premium prices to global affluent consumers — is one of the most understandable and proven models in global business.
- **Bear case:** The conglomerate structure with 75 brands introduces complexity, and luxury spending cyclicality makes short-term revenue less predictable than subscription or staples businesses.
- **Confidence:** High — LVMH's business model is transparent, well-documented, and has been operating successfully for over 30 years with consistent strategic logic.

## Red Flags
- Revenue declined 6.3% from FY23 to FY25 peak-to-trough, highlighting luxury demand cyclicality
- Wines & Spirits segment continues to face structural headwinds (down 8.6% YoY in FY25), raising questions about long-term relevance within the portfolio
- Japan revenue under pressure with double-digit H2 2025 declines, likely driven by yen dynamics and tourism shifts
- 75 brands makes it difficult to assess individual brand health without management disclosure

## Score: 8 / 10
LVMH's business is fundamentally clear — buy/build heritage luxury brands, control distribution, charge premium prices — with limited key drivers and no customer concentration, but the 75-brand conglomerate structure and luxury cyclicality prevent a perfect score.
