# Compact Checklist — MC.PA

**Date:** 2026-03-18

1. **Business**: LVMH sells luxury goods through 75 heritage brands (led by Louis Vuitton, Dior, and Tiffany) across 6,280+ owned stores, and customers keep buying because these brands carry centuries of cultural cachet and social signaling value that makes them Veblen goods.
2. **Moat**: Irreplicable brand heritage (Louis Vuitton founded 1854, Hennessy 1765), vertically controlled distribution that prevents any discounting, and EUR 10B annual marketing spend create a compounding moat no competitor can replicate — evidenced by 66%+ gross margins sustained through a two-year revenue decline.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of 13.3% at cyclical trough (down from 21.3% peak) still exceeds cost of capital, and FCF expanded to EUR 14.2B in FY25 despite revenue falling 6.3% from peak, confirming the cash engine holds even in tough conditions.
4. **Management**: Bernard Arnault is a generational capital allocator who crossed 50% ownership in February 2026 with USD 440M in open-market purchases at depressed prices, backed by a 30-year track record that includes doubling Tiffany's operating profit since the $15.8B acquisition.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt/EBITDA of 0.4x, EUR 13.5B cash covering all financial debt, 15.5x interest coverage, and AA-/Aa3 credit ratings mean LVMH could survive a 30% revenue decline for two years without raising equity or cutting the dividend.
6. **Price**: At EUR 468 (21.4x trailing P/E vs 10-year average of 27x), the market must believe LVMH's owner earnings grow at only 2% annually for the next five years — an unreasonably low bar for a franchise with demonstrated mid-single-digit pricing power.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is the margin of safety — fortress balance sheet, EUR 14.2B FCF, and diversified brand portfolio — but the stock trades 67% above conservative IV of EUR 280, so a 15-20% price decline to the EUR 380-400 range is needed for true asymmetric risk-reward.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Louis Vuitton and Dior lose pricing power (same-store sales decline alongside price cuts for two consecutive quarters), leverage exceeds 3x Debt/EBITDA, or a succession crisis fragments strategic direction.
