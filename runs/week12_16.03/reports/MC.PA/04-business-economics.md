## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital — Strong but Cyclically Compressed.** LVMH's ROIC trajectory tells the story of the luxury cycle: 20.6% in FY22, peaking at 21.3% in FY23, then contracting to 16.2% in FY24 and 13.3% in FY25. ROE followed the same pattern, falling from 26.1% to 16.1% over the same period. These are still respectable numbers — a 13% ROIC places LVMH above most global consumer companies — but the direction is clearly negative. The key question is whether trough ROIC stabilizes around 12–14% (which would be consistent with prior luxury downturns) or deteriorates further. The capital base has expanded meaningfully through the Tiffany acquisition integration and store buildout, so the denominator effect matters. On a normalized basis, mid-cycle ROIC is likely 16–18%, which is excellent for a business of this scale.

**Margin Structure — Resilient Architecture with Cyclical Flex.** The margin cascade reveals LVMH's quality. Gross margins at 66.2% reflect the power of brands like Louis Vuitton, Dior, and Tiffany to command extreme pricing relative to COGS. The 260bps compression from FY23's 68.8% is notable but modest given the revenue decline. Operating margin contracted more sharply — from 26.4% (FY23) to 21.9% (FY25) — reflecting operating leverage working in reverse as fixed costs (store leases, personnel, marketing) remained elevated against lower volumes. Fashion & Leather Goods at 35% operating margin is the crown jewel, and its resilience even in a downturn confirms this is one of the highest-quality consumer business segments globally. Wines & Spirits is the weak link, with operating margin collapsing as cognac volumes cratered from US-China trade tensions. Selective Retailing (Sephora) improved margin from ~7.5% to 9.7%, a structural positive.

**Cash Generation — The Engine Holds.** FCF of €14.2B in FY25 is actually the highest absolute level in the four-year window, despite lower earnings. The explanation is capex discipline: LVMH cut capital expenditures from €7.8B (FY23) to €4.7B (FY25), a reduction of nearly €3B. Operating cash flow held flat at €18.9B for two consecutive years despite the earnings decline, demonstrating strong working capital management. Owner earnings (€14.2B) are effectively equivalent to reported FCF, confirming minimal quality-of-earnings concerns. FCF conversion at 130.6% (FCF/net income) is elevated because net income includes non-cash charges that depress the denominator. The more meaningful metric is FCF/EBITDA at 55%, which is solid. D&A of €8.0B is substantial — largely driven by right-of-use asset amortization (IFRS 16 lease accounting) and Tiffany goodwill amortization — and exceeds maintenance capex, meaning reported FCF genuinely overstates the cash available after maintaining the asset base only modestly.

**Capital Intensity and Revenue Quality.** LVMH sits in an unusual position: it is more capital-intensive than a pure asset-light brand licensor (it owns its stores, manufactures leather goods, and runs vineyards) but captures far more value because of that vertical integration. Capex at 5.7% of revenue is the long-term average, with FY23's 9% spike being the outlier (Tiffany integration, store expansion). The revenue mix is high-quality: Fashion & Leather Goods (47% of revenue) is essentially recurring luxury consumption driven by aspirational purchasing and brand loyalty rather than contractual subscriptions. Selective Retailing (23%) benefits from Sephora's loyalty program with 30M+ members. Wines & Spirits (7%) is the most cyclical. Revenue is diversified geographically (Asia ~30%, Europe ~25%, US ~25%, rest of world ~20%) and by category, which dampens volatility. Pricing power is demonstrated by the ability to take mid-single-digit price increases annually without volume collapse.

**Operating Leverage — Double-Edged.** The FY23-to-FY25 period illustrates how operating leverage works both ways at LVMH. Revenue declined ~6.3% from peak, but operating income fell 22.4% (€22.8B to €17.7B), implying an operating leverage ratio of roughly 3.5x. This is typical for a business with high fixed costs (luxury retail stores, brand marketing commitments, haute couture ateliers). On the upside, when revenue recovers, profit growth will significantly outpace top-line improvement. The question is timing: luxury cycles historically last 2–3 years, and LVMH's H2 2025 showed sequential stabilization with 1% organic growth, suggesting the trough may be forming.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Mid-cycle ROIC of 16–18% and €14B+ FCF generation from the world's strongest luxury portfolio positions LVMH to compound at double digits once the luxury cycle turns, with operating leverage amplifying any revenue recovery.
- **Bear case:** ROIC has halved from peak and margin compression may persist if Chinese and US luxury demand remains subdued, with the structurally higher cost base from Tiffany integration and store expansion keeping returns below historical norms.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The business economics are structurally excellent but the cyclical downturn is real and the timing of recovery is uncertain; the data clearly shows a high-quality engine operating below its potential.

## Red Flags
- ROIC contraction of 800bps in two years is the steepest decline since the GFC period
- Wines & Spirits division profit down 33% with cognac exposed to geopolitical risk
- Operating leverage of ~3.5x means further revenue softness disproportionately hits earnings
- Capex "savings" may partly reflect deferred investment that will need to catch up
- Gross margin compression for two consecutive years, though still above 66%

## Score: 7 / 10
LVMH's business economics are structurally strong — 66%+ gross margins, €14B FCF, and mid-teens ROIC even at cyclical trough — but the two-year deterioration in ROIC, margins, and revenue growth prevents a higher score; on a normalized basis this is an 8–9 business temporarily operating as a 7.
