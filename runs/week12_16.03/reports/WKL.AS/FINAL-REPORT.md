## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Wolters Kluwer sells mission-critical compliance and workflow software on subscription (83% recurring) to tax, legal, health, and audit professionals who face regulatory mandates to use such tools.
2. **Moat**: Deeply embedded workflow tools (CCH Axcess, UpToDate, OneSumX) with 90%+ renewal rates create high switching costs, though Morningstar downgraded the moat to Narrow in 2026 on AI disruption risk.
3. **Economics**: ROIC expanded from 18.1% to 24.6% over four years with FCF conversion above 100% and operating margins rising 430bps to 27.5% — textbook compounder economics.
4. **Management**: McKinstry delivered two decades of disciplined bolt-on M&A (51 deals, no mega-mergers), EUR 1B+ annual buybacks shrinking shares 12%, and smooth internal CEO succession to a 30-year veteran.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt/EBITDA of 1.7x with 15x interest coverage and A-/A3 credit ratings means the company survives even a 50% EBIT decline with coverage still at 7.5x.
6. **Price**: At 9.7x EV/EBITDA — half the 5-year median of 20.1x and below every peer — the market must assume revenue declines of 3-5% annually or permanent margin compression to sub-15%, neither of which is supported by FY2025's record results.
7. **Margin of safety**: The current EUR 65.44 price sits below even the bear-case IV of EUR 80, with 100% of 10,000 Monte Carlo simulations producing IV above the current price, delivering roughly 4:1 upside/downside asymmetry.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if organic revenue growth turns negative for two consecutive quarters, confirming that AI alternatives are actually displacing Wolters Kluwer's embedded workflow tools rather than just scaring investors.

## Key Risks

1. **AI disruption to compliance workflows** — Foundation model companies (Anthropic, OpenAI) are actively targeting legal, tax, and regulatory workflows. If generic AI tools replicate CCH/UpToDate/TeamMate functionality at lower cost, pricing power and renewal rates could erode over 3-5 years. The Anthropic Cowork legal plugin announcement in February 2026 already triggered a 13% single-day decline.

2. **Morningstar moat downgrade signals structural concern** — The downgrade from Wide to Narrow is rare and data-driven. Even if the financial evidence supports a strong moat today, the AI disruption vector introduces genuine uncertainty about the moat's durability over a multi-year horizon.

3. **Prolonged multiple compression** — The market may permanently re-rate information services companies lower due to AI overhang. If peers (RELX, Thomson Reuters) also de-rate, the discount may not close through multiple expansion alone, leaving the investor dependent on earnings compounding.

4. **Rising debt and hollowed equity base** — Net debt nearly doubled from EUR 2.0B to EUR 3.8B (FY2022-2025), largely funding buybacks at much higher prices. The equity base has shrunk to EUR 798M. While leverage metrics remain comfortable, the reduced buffer leaves less margin for error if cash flows deteriorate.

5. **CEO transition risk** — New CEO Stacey Caywood is untested at the group level. The internal promotion suggests continuity, but the company may need more radical AI-era transformation than an insider is inclined to deliver.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - Continued 5-6% organic revenue growth through H1 2026, confirming AI fears are not translating into customer defection
  - Price at or below EUR 65 with bear-case IV of EUR 80+ providing 18%+ margin of safety
  - AI product launches (CCH Axcess Expert AI, UpToDate AI) showing measurable customer adoption and engagement

- **Sell triggers**:
  - Organic revenue growth turns negative for two consecutive quarters, indicating actual (not feared) customer loss to AI alternatives
  - Stock re-rates above EUR 150 (approaching base-to-bull IV), where upside/downside asymmetry becomes unfavorable
  - Independent benchmarks show foundation model tools reliably match Wolters Kluwer's accuracy in regulated compliance tasks

## Detailed Analysis

Wolters Kluwer is a professional information services company that sells specialized software, data, and workflow tools to professionals who operate in heavily regulated environments. Its five divisions — Health, Tax & Accounting, Financial & Corporate Compliance, Legal & Regulatory, and Corporate Performance & ESG — each serve a distinct vertical where regulatory complexity creates persistent demand. The products range from clinical decision support tools used at the point of care (UpToDate, Lexicomp) to tax preparation and audit workflow platforms (CCH Axcess) to regulatory reporting and compliance engines for banks and corporations. The common thread is that these are not optional purchases — professionals need them to do their jobs correctly and avoid regulatory penalties.

The business model is highly subscription-oriented. In H1 2025, 84% of total revenues were recurring, with non-recurring revenue (one-time sales, print) declining 4% organically. Within recurring revenue, cloud software represented 21% of total revenue and grew 15% organically, reflecting the ongoing shift from on-premise to cloud delivery. Expert solutions — the core content and workflow bundles — accounted for 59% of revenue and grew 6% organically. This mix provides exceptional revenue visibility: management can forecast with high precision because the installed base renews at rates typically above 90%.

The key drivers are straightforward. First, regulatory complexity — as governments worldwide add rules (tax codes, healthcare mandates, financial reporting standards, ESG disclosure requirements), the demand for compliance tools grows structurally. Second, retention and price: once a law firm or hospital embeds Wolters Kluwer's workflow tools, switching is painful and rare, allowing steady annual price increases of 3-5%. Third, cloud migration and AI upsell: the transition from legacy on-premise to cloud platforms (and now AI-enhanced "Expert AI" products) drives incremental revenue per customer. Revenue grew from EUR 5.5B in FY2022 to EUR 6.1B in FY2025, a steady mid-single-digit compounder.

Customer concentration risk is negligible. Wolters Kluwer serves hundreds of thousands of professional firms, hospitals, banks, and corporations globally. No single customer represents a material share of revenue. Geographic concentration is moderate — over 60% of revenue and profit comes from North America, with the remainder split across Europe and Asia-Pacific. This is a business that can be explained in under two minutes: it sells must-have compliance and workflow software on subscription to professionals in regulated industries.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** One of the most predictable, subscription-driven business models in global equities, with 84% recurring revenue and structural tailwinds from rising regulatory complexity.
- **Bear case:** Print revenue continues to decline, and the core organic growth rate (5-6%) is not exceptional for the valuation historically assigned to the stock.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is transparent, well-documented, and has been consistent for over a decade.

## Red Flags
- None identified. The business model is among the easiest to understand in the professional services sector.

## Score: 9 / 10
Wolters Kluwer is a textbook "understandable business" — subscription-based, mission-critical, with limited key drivers, minimal customer concentration, and highly predictable revenue streams. Near-perfect clarity.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — WKL.AS

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Morningstar moat analysis (March 2026), Wolters Kluwer FY2025 Full-Year Report, Seeking Alpha analysis, Finimize asset snapshot, Yahoo Finance, context/WKL.AS/financials.md, context/WKL.AS/quant-valuation.md

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Morningstar downgraded Wolters Kluwer's moat from Wide to Narrow in early 2026, citing AI disruption potential to switching-cost-based moats | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margins expanded from 71.1% (FY2022) to 73.5% (FY2025) and ROIC from 18.1% to 24.6% — evidence of pricing power and operating leverage despite AI fears | 5 |
| 3 | Switching costs remain the primary moat source: products are deeply embedded in professional workflows (tax prep, clinical decisions, compliance reporting) with 90%+ renewal rates | 5 |
| 4 | AI is both a threat and an opportunity — Wolters Kluwer is investing EUR 724M in R&D (12% of revenue) and plans to increase to 12-13% in 2026 to embed AI into existing platforms | 4 |
| 5 | The weakest moat vector is in content/research where generic LLMs could replicate some informational value, though regulatory-validated and jurisdiction-specific content is harder to replicate | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Wolters Kluwer's competitive advantage rests primarily on switching costs, with secondary support from scale economics and regulatory entrenchment. The switching cost moat operates at multiple levels. At the workflow level, products like CCH Axcess (tax), UpToDate (clinical), and OneSumX (regulatory reporting) are embedded in daily professional routines — tax preparers build their entire season around CCH, clinicians consult UpToDate at the point of care, and bank compliance teams run regulatory reports through OneSumX. Ripping out these systems means retraining staff, migrating years of historical data, and risking errors during the transition in domains where errors carry regulatory or patient-safety consequences. This produces renewal rates consistently above 90%.

The durability question now centers on AI disruption. Morningstar downgraded both Wolters Kluwer and Thomson Reuters from Wide to Narrow moats in early 2026, arguing that generative AI and LLMs could erode switching costs by making it easier for new entrants to build competing workflow tools. The concern is legitimate: generic AI models can already summarize legal documents, answer clinical questions, and interpret tax codes. If a competitor can deliver "good enough" answers powered by frontier AI models at a fraction of the price, some customers — particularly smaller firms — might switch.

However, the numbers tell a more nuanced story. Gross margins have expanded every year from 71.1% (FY2022) to 73.5% (FY2025), and operating margins jumped from 23.2% to 27.5% over the same period. ROIC climbed from 18.1% to 24.6%. These are not the metrics of a company losing competitive position — they reflect increasing pricing power and operating leverage. The explanation is that Wolters Kluwer's products are not just information providers; they are workflow engines that integrate with customer ERPs, generate regulatory filings in jurisdiction-specific formats, and maintain audit trails. A ChatGPT wrapper cannot file a 1040, submit a regulatory report to the ECB, or update a hospital's drug interaction database in real-time.

Wolters Kluwer is investing aggressively to embed AI into its own moat rather than be disrupted by it. R&D spending reached EUR 724M in FY2025 (12% of revenue), up from EUR 541M in FY2022. The company launched CCH Axcess Expert AI (agentic automation for tax/audit) in October 2025 and plans to increase product development to 12-13% of revenue in 2026. AI-powered solutions now represent approximately 70% of digital revenues. The strategy is to use proprietary, domain-specific training data — decades of curated legal, tax, and clinical content — to build AI features that generic models cannot replicate.

The moat is weakest in pure information/research products where generic LLMs can provide similar answers. It is strongest in integrated workflow platforms where the product is the system of record and where regulatory accuracy is non-negotiable. On balance, the moat is real but narrower than five years ago, and the next 2-3 years will be decisive in determining whether Wolters Kluwer successfully integrates AI as a moat-widener or watches it erode its switching cost premium.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Expanding margins, rising ROIC, and 90%+ renewal rates prove the moat is intact operationally; AI investment positions the company to embed generative capabilities into its own walled gardens.
- **Bear case:** Morningstar's moat downgrade reflects a real structural risk — AI could lower barriers to entry in compliance software, particularly for smaller customers and information-only products.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The financial evidence supports a strong moat today, but the AI disruption vector introduces genuine 3-5 year uncertainty that prevents a High confidence rating.

## Red Flags
- Morningstar downgraded the moat from Wide to Narrow specifically on AI disruption risk — this is a credible, data-driven research firm that rarely changes moat ratings.
- Print revenue continues declining, indicating one legacy advantage (physical distribution) is gone.
- Net debt has risen from EUR 2.0B (FY2022) to EUR 3.8B (FY2025), partly funding the defensive R&D/AI investment push.

## Score: 7 / 10
Wolters Kluwer retains a strong moat anchored in switching costs and workflow entrenchment, evidenced by expanding margins and ROIC. However, the Morningstar downgrade and genuine AI disruption uncertainty warrant a deduction from what would otherwise be an 8-9. The moat is real but under structural pressure for the first time in decades.

# Management & Capital Allocation — WKL.AS

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Wolters Kluwer FY2025 Full-Year Report, Wolters Kluwer investor relations, Simply Wall St, Tracxn acquisition data, Wikipedia (Nancy McKinstry), Yahoo Finance, context/WKL.AS/financials.md, context/WKL.AS/quant-valuation.md

## Detailed Analysis

Nancy McKinstry led Wolters Kluwer for over two decades (2003-2026) and transformed it from a sprawling publishing conglomerate into a focused professional information and software company. Under her tenure, the company divested non-core assets, shifted decisively from print to digital (now ~90%+ digital), expanded into cloud and SaaS delivery, and built the current five-division structure. Revenue grew from roughly EUR 3.4B in 2003 to EUR 6.1B in 2025, while margins expanded materially. This is an exceptional track record of strategic execution. In February 2026, Stacey Caywood — a 30-year Wolters Kluwer veteran who led the Health division since 2020 — succeeded McKinstry as CEO. The internal succession signals strategic continuity and deep institutional knowledge, though it also means the new CEO inherits a company that may need more radical AI-era transformation than an insider might deliver.

Capital allocation has been a clear strength. The company returns the vast majority of free cash flow to shareholders through buybacks and dividends, while maintaining disciplined bolt-on M&A. Buybacks have been substantial and consistent: approximately EUR 1.0-1.1B per year from 2022 through 2025, reducing the diluted share count from roughly 256M to 225M over that period — a cumulative reduction of about 12%. This is not vanity buying at any price; the accelerated 2025 buyback (completed two months early) and the 2026 program of EUR 500M (smaller, reflecting the lower stock price and higher R&D investment plans) suggest management pays attention to price and opportunity cost. The total dividend for FY2025 was EUR 2.52 per share, up 8% year-over-year, representing a 3.85% yield at the current price.

M&A discipline is evident across Wolters Kluwer's history. The company has completed 51 acquisitions, overwhelmingly small bolt-ons in the EUR 20-100M range that tuck into existing divisions. The largest acquisition in recent years was Registered Agent Solutions Inc. (RASi) for $415M in March 2025, and the most recent was Standard Fusion for $37M in January 2026 (GRC software for the compliance division). Management has explicitly stated they avoid transformational mega-deals: "We often do a few smaller acquisitions which we then integrate into existing activities. The one thing we don't do is change the entire company with one mega deal." Annual M&A spend averages EUR 200-300M. This is textbook Buffett-style allocation — small, accretive, and integrated.

On compensation, McKinstry drew criticism in the Netherlands for having the highest CEO-to-average-employee pay ratio (137x) among Dutch listed companies, and received a EUR 12M bonus in 2020 — the highest among AEX 27 companies. The 2024 remuneration report passed its advisory vote at the 2025 AGM, but executive pay at Wolters Kluwer is rich by European standards. The new CEO's compensation package has not yet been fully disclosed but will likely remain in a similar range given the governance structure. Insider ownership data for management is not prominently disclosed, which is common for Dutch companies but reduces alignment visibility compared to US-listed peers.

Governance follows the standard Dutch two-tier board structure with a separate Executive Board and Supervisory Board. This provides appropriate oversight but is inherently less transparent than a single-board US structure. The company has a strong track record of candor in investor communications — guidance has been reliable, targets consistently met or exceeded, and management discusses risks (including AI disruption) openly.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Two decades of disciplined capital allocation — consistent buybacks, conservative M&A, margin expansion — combined with a smooth internal CEO succession from a 30-year veteran.
- **Bear case:** Executive compensation is high by European standards with limited visible insider ownership; the new CEO is untested as group leader and may default to continuity when transformation is needed.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The capital allocation track record is objectively excellent; the CEO transition introduces modest uncertainty but the internal promotion suggests continuity of philosophy.

## Red Flags
- McKinstry's compensation ratio (137x average employee) was the highest in the Netherlands — excessive by European norms even if defensible by US standards.
- Insider ownership is not prominently disclosed, reducing confidence in management-shareholder alignment.
- Net debt increased from EUR 2.0B to EUR 3.8B (FY2022-2025), partially reflecting aggressive buybacks funded with debt — manageable at 1.7x net debt/EBITDA but trending upward.

## Score: 8 / 10
Wolters Kluwer's capital allocation under McKinstry was among the best in European equities — disciplined M&A, consistent and value-accretive buybacks, and steady dividend growth alongside margin expansion. The compensation controversy and limited insider ownership disclosure prevent a 9, but the 20-year track record speaks for itself. The CEO transition is a modest risk factor but the internal promotion from a deeply experienced successor mitigates concern.

# Business Economics — WKL.AS

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Wolters Kluwer 2025 Full-Year Results (Feb 2026), Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), quant-valuation.json, company investor relations, web search for current data

| 6 | Minimal SBC (~26M, <0.5% of revenue) means FCF is not inflated by hidden equity dilution | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

### Returns on Capital

Wolters Kluwer's capital efficiency is exceptional and improving. ROIC has risen from 18.1% in FY2022 to 24.6% in FY2025, comfortably above the company's roughly 5% WACC and well into top-decile territory for information services businesses. The company's own adjusted ROIC metric (which differs slightly from our calculation) guided 18-19% for 2025, and actual results exceeded that range. ROE of 111.7% is distorted by the shrinking equity base from aggressive buybacks, but even ROA at 13.6% (up from 10.8% in FY2022) confirms improving asset productivity. Critically, this is not leverage-driven return inflation — the underlying NOPAT margins are genuinely expanding. Over a longer horizon, Wolters Kluwer has compounded returns on capital above 15% for over a decade, placing it in the same tier as Verisk, MSCI, and S&P Global among professional information peers.

### Margin Structure

The margin trajectory tells a story of a business transitioning from legacy publishing to high-margin digital subscriptions and cloud software. Gross margins have expanded 240bps over four years (71.1% to 73.5%), reflecting the mix shift toward software and digital services which carry structurally higher margins than print and one-time transactions. Operating margins have improved even more dramatically — from 23.2% (FY2022) to 27.5% (FY2025), a 430bps expansion. The adjusted operating profit margin (the company's preferred metric) hit 27.5% in 2025, up 40bps year-over-year. EBITDA margins of 36.4% in FY2025 are robust. Net margins at 21.4% in FY2025 (up from 18.8% in FY2022) confirm profitability is improving at every level of the P&L, not just operationally.

### Cash Generation

Free cash flow generation is a standout characteristic. FCF of 1.4B in FY2025 represents a 22.3% FCF margin. FCF conversion (FCF/Net Income) has averaged approximately 119% over four years, meaning the company consistently converts more than 100% of its reported earnings into cash. This is rare and reflects: (1) negative working capital dynamics (customers prepay subscriptions, the company pays suppliers later), (2) modest real capex requirements relative to D&A, and (3) negligible stock-based compensation dilution (26M in FY2025 vs. 1.3B net income). Owner earnings of 1.5B in FY2025 (NI + D&A - CapEx) corroborate the cash flow quality. The company reported adjusted free cash flow up 10% in constant currencies year-over-year.

### Capital Intensity

The business is genuinely asset-light. Total capex of 305M against D&A of 463M indicates capex is running well below depreciation, meaning much of the D&A relates to amortization of acquired intangibles rather than required maintenance spending. The quant model estimates maintenance capex at approximately 5% of revenue (303M), which aligns closely with actual capex — suggesting virtually all capex is maintenance, with growth investment flowing through the R&D line (724M in FY2025, up 5% year-over-year). This is the hallmark of a software-dominant model: invest through R&D (expensed), collect through subscriptions (recurring), with minimal physical asset requirements.

### Revenue Quality

Revenue quality is among the best in the professional information sector. Recurring revenues comprise 83% of total revenues, up from 82% in FY2024, and grew 7% organically. Within recurring, digital and service subscriptions grew 7% organically, while cloud software revenues (21% of total) surged 15% organically. Non-recurring revenues (17%) declined 1%, but this is largely intentional as the company shifts customers to subscription models. While Wolters Kluwer does not disclose specific net revenue retention rates, the combination of 83% recurring mix, 6% organic growth, and the mission-critical nature of its compliance/regulatory/tax software suggests gross retention rates well above 90%. The company serves professionals (lawyers, accountants, doctors, compliance officers) who face regulatory mandates to use such tools — this is not discretionary spend.

### Operating Leverage

Operating leverage is clearly demonstrated: over the FY2022-FY2025 period, revenue grew approximately 11% cumulatively while operating income grew approximately 31%, delivering nearly 3x the profit growth rate. This is the signature of a subscription business reaching scale — incremental revenue drops to the bottom line at very high margins as the fixed-cost base (R&D, infrastructure) is already built. The 430bps operating margin expansion confirms the business has structural operating leverage that should continue as the digital/cloud mix increases. Nearly 65% of revenues and adjusted operating profit are generated in North America, providing geographic earnings stability.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** ROIC above 24% and rising, 83% recurring revenue with expanding margins, and >100% FCF conversion make this one of the highest-quality business economics profiles in European equities.
- **Bear case:** Revenue growth of 6% organic is solid but not explosive; if the digital transition stalls or regulatory tailwinds moderate, margin expansion could plateau.
- **Confidence:** High — Four years of consistent data show clear, improving trends across every business economics metric; the underlying dynamics (recurring revenue, asset-light, operating leverage) are structural, not cyclical.

## Red Flags
- Net debt has risen from 2.0B (FY2022) to 3.8B (FY2025), largely funding buybacks; while manageable given cash generation, this is a trend to monitor.
- ROE of 111.7% is mathematically distorted by near-zero equity and should not be taken at face value as a return metric.

## Score: 9 / 10
Wolters Kluwer exhibits consistently high and improving ROIC (24.6%), exceptional FCF conversion (>100%), expanding margins at every level, 83% recurring revenue, and genuine operating leverage — falling just short of a 10 only because organic growth, while steady, is not accelerating.

# Balance Sheet Safety — WKL.AS

**Analyst Role:** Balance Sheet Safety Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Wolters Kluwer 2025 Full-Year Results (Feb 2026), Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), Wolters Kluwer investor relations debt maturity page, S&P/Moody's credit ratings, web search for current bond issuances and capital structure

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Interest coverage of 15.1x provides a very wide safety buffer against earnings volatility | 5 |
| 2 | Net debt/EBITDA of 1.7x is conservative for an investment-grade compounder with 1.4B annual FCF | 5 |
| 3 | A3 (Moody's) / A- (S&P) credit ratings confirm market assessment of strong creditworthiness | 4 |
| 4 | Debt maturities have been extended to ~6 years average through 2025 bond issuances, reducing near-term refinancing risk | 4 |
| 5 | Negative working capital (-1.4B) is structural and beneficial — prepaid subscriptions fund operations — but current ratio of 0.6x could concern traditional analysts | 3 |
| 6 | Stockholders' equity has shrunk from 2.3B to 798M due to buybacks; debt/equity of 618% is optically alarming but reflects capital return policy, not distress | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

### Debt Levels and Structure

Total debt stands at 4.9B as of FY2025, up from 3.6B in FY2022. Long-term debt is 4.0B with the remainder in short-term obligations. Net debt is 3.8B after 932M in cash. The debt/EBITDA ratio of 2.2x and net debt/EBITDA of 1.7x are both within comfortable territory for a business of this quality and predictability. The increase in absolute debt over four years (from 3.6B to 4.9B) has been primarily driven by the company's aggressive share buyback program (approximately 1.1B annually), which deliberately leverages the balance sheet to return capital. This is a conscious strategic choice enabled by the company's highly predictable cash flows, not a deterioration in financial health. For context, the company generated adjusted operating profit of 1.687B in FY2025 — the entire debt stack represents less than three years of operating profit.

### Interest Coverage

EBIT/interest coverage of 15.1x in FY2025 (consistent with FY2024) is exceptionally strong. Even in FY2022 with lower operating income, coverage was 20.3x (reflecting lower absolute debt at the time). Interest expense has risen from 66M (FY2022) to 117M (FY2025) as debt has increased, but operating income growth has kept pace. At 15x coverage, interest payments consume only about 7% of operating income. This leaves enormous headroom for earnings to decline before debt service becomes problematic. A 50% decline in EBIT would still leave coverage at roughly 7.5x — comfortable even under severe stress.

### Liquidity

Cash and short-term investments of 932M provide a substantial liquidity buffer. The company also maintains undrawn committed credit facilities (standard for A-rated European corporates, typically 1-2B). While the current ratio of 0.6x appears weak, this is entirely a function of the negative working capital model inherent to subscription businesses. Current liabilities of 4.1B include deferred revenue (prepaid subscriptions) which are not true obligations requiring cash outlay — they are fulfilled by delivering existing digital services at near-zero marginal cost. This is a feature, not a risk.
