## Detailed Analysis

**Switching costs are the primary and most durable moat.** Insurance brokerage relationships are inherently sticky. A broker manages a client's entire risk profile — understanding their operations, claims history, coverage needs, carrier relationships, and loss-control programs. Switching brokers means re-educating a new intermediary on years of institutional knowledge, re-negotiating carrier relationships, and risking coverage gaps during transition. For a middle-market company where the CFO or risk manager has limited bandwidth, the friction of switching is substantial relative to any marginal fee savings. This dynamic is confirmed empirically: AJG management has cited improving client retention rates, and the brokerage model's renewal-based revenue has remained highly predictable through multiple economic cycles. Gallagher's improving retention metrics post-enhanced service initiatives further corroborate this structural stickiness.

**Scale advantages compound through the acquisition flywheel.** AJG has perfected a tuck-in acquisition model over four decades, buying small high-performing local agencies and integrating them onto a shared platform. Each acquisition brings not just revenue but data, client relationships, and local expertise. This scale now feeds a data advantage being weaponized through AI — AJG's "Digital Sherpas" leverage decades of proprietary claims and risk data to predict casualty risks more accurately than smaller competitors. The flywheel is clear: more acquisitions yield more data, better analytics yield better client outcomes, higher retention generates cash for more acquisitions. AJG is also a leader in cyber risk consulting for middle-market clients — a high-growth specialty where data and expertise compound against smaller generalist brokers.

**Middle-market positioning creates a defensible niche.** The insurance brokerage market is structurally bifurcated. Marsh McLennan ($24B revenue) and Aon ($15B revenue) dominate the large-enterprise and specialty segments. WTW ($10B revenue) competes across all segments. AJG has deliberately built its fortress in the middle market (100–2,500 employees), where local relationships, industry-specific expertise, and personalized service matter more than global placement capacity. The AssuredPartners acquisition was specifically targeted at this same segment, deepening AJG's dominance rather than stretching into unfamiliar territory. With ~4.6% US market share, there is substantial runway in a $600B+ global commercial insurance distribution market that remains highly fragmented.

**Where is the moat weakest?** Three areas warrant scrutiny. First, the declining ROIC from 9.2% (FY2022) to 5.9% (FY2025) is the most concerning data point. This deterioration reflects the massive goodwill and intangibles accumulation from acquisitions — particularly the $13.5B AssuredPartners deal which added roughly $10B+ in goodwill and intangibles to the balance sheet. ROIC below WACC (estimated at 7.3%) means the company is currently destroying economic value on a reported-returns basis, even if the integration synergies eventually reverse this. Second, the business is sensitive to the insurance pricing cycle. Organic growth partially reflects hard-market pricing tailwinds; a sustained soft market in property insurance (already beginning in 2026) would trim organic growth rates. Third, execution risk at the current acquisition pace is real: 33 mergers in 2025 alone on top of the transformative AssuredPartners deal is a substantial cultural and operational integration challenge.

**Moat rating: Narrow-to-Wide.** The moat sources — switching costs, scale, data, and niche dominance — are structural and will persist for decades. However, the aggressive M&A pace means AJG is spending heavily to maintain and extend its competitive position, and declining ROIC indicates that acquired goodwill is currently outpacing the economic value of that moat. A wide moat designation requires sustained ROIC above WACC; AJG is not there today, though the path to recovery via AssuredPartners synergies is visible. Rating: **Narrow-to-Wide, pending synergy execution**.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Structural switching costs combined with a compounding data/scale flywheel in the most fragmented segment of a growing industry create a moat that widens with each successful acquisition cycle; AssuredPartners synergies could restore ROIC above WACC within 3 years.
- **Bear case:** Declining ROIC and ROE reveal that the company is paying increasingly full prices to maintain its competitive position; the massive debt load from AssuredPartners ($13.5B total debt, 3.7x leverage) creates vulnerability if integration stumbles or markets soften simultaneously.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The moat sources are clear and well-evidenced; the uncertainty is whether the economics of maintaining it recover to historically attractive levels.

## Red Flags

- ROIC declined from 9.2% (FY2022) to 5.9% (FY2025) — currently below estimated WACC of 7.3%
- Debt/EBITDA at 3.7x vs. historical 2.0–2.5x range; interest coverage of only 3.9x
- AssuredPartners synergy realization ($160M by end-2026, $260–280M by 2028) is the single largest execution risk in AJG's history
- Softening property insurance market could reduce organic growth tailwind in 2026

## Score: 7 / 10

AJG possesses a genuine and durable moat built on switching costs, scale, data advantages, and middle-market dominance, but the score is capped at 7 because ROIC is currently below WACC, leverage is elevated above historical norms, and the economic quality of the moat will only be confirmed once AssuredPartners synergies are realized and reported returns recover.
