## Detailed Analysis

### Debt Levels and Structure

AJG's balance sheet underwent a dramatic transformation in late 2024/2025. To fund the $13.45B AssuredPartners acquisition, the company executed a two-pronged capital raise: an $8.5B equity offering (plus overallotment) and a $5.0B senior notes issuance, both in December 2024. The acquisition closed on August 18, 2025. Total debt now stands at $13.5B, comprising public senior notes, private placement notes, and zero drawn on the revolving credit facility. The December 2024 notes were issued across five fixed-rate tranches: $750M at 4.60% due 2027, $750M at 4.85% due 2029, $500M at 5.00% due 2032, $1.5B at 5.15% due 2035, and $1.5B at 5.55% due 2055. This is a deliberately well-laddered structure — no single maturity year presents a refinancing cliff, and the longest tranche extends to 2055, removing any refinancing pressure for decades on that portion. The weighted average coupon on the new issuance is approximately 5.1%, reasonable for investment-grade paper issued in late 2024. Critically, all $5.0B in new notes are fixed-rate, eliminating exposure to interest rate fluctuations on the acquisition-related debt. Notably, S&P upgraded AJG's credit rating to BBB+ (Stable) in connection with or following the transaction — a signal that even rating agencies view the leverage as manageable given the quality of AJG's cash flows.

### Interest Coverage and Debt Metrics

Interest expense rose to $639M in FY2025 (from $381M in FY2024), reflecting the new $5.0B debt load for most of the year plus partial-year AssuredPartners cash flows. Interest coverage (EBIT/interest) fell from 5.9x to 3.9x — the sharpest single-year deterioration in the four-year history provided. On an EBITDA basis, coverage is more comfortable at approximately 5.6x ($3.6B EBITDA / $639M interest), which is a more relevant measure for an acquisition-heavy broker with heavy intangible amortization. Debt/EBITDA stands at 3.7x (FY2025 debt $13.5B / EBITDA $3.6B), and Net Debt/EBITDA at 3.2x. Management's stated target is to return to approximately 3.3x Net Debt/EBITDA by end of 2025 (consistent with prior cycle range of 3–4x), with further deleveraging to historical norms of ~2.5x by late 2027. On the trajectory of $2B+ annual FCF, this is arithmetically achievable if no additional large transactions are pursued. Debt/Equity at 57.8% reflects the balanced funding approach used for AssuredPartners — roughly 60% equity / 40% debt. The maintained (and upgraded) investment-grade rating is operationally significant: large corporate clients and carrier partners require counterparty confidence, and a downgrade would be a meaningful competitive disadvantage.

### Liquidity Position

Liquidity is strong. Cash on hand is $1.4B. The company amended its revolving credit facility in April 2025, extending maturity to April 2030 and increasing the commitment from $1.7B to $2.5B. As of year-end 2025, zero was drawn on this facility, providing the full $2.5B as a backstop. Combined with annual FCF generation north of $1.8B, AJG has approximately $3.9B of available liquidity — more than sufficient to cover any near-term debt maturities (the first significant maturity is $750M in 2027) and continue its tuck-in acquisition program. The current ratio of 1.1x is typical for insurance brokers, where premium trust liabilities inflate current liabilities but are matched by corresponding receivables.

### Refinancing Risk

Refinancing risk is low. The maturity ladder from the December 2024 issuance spans 2027 ($750M), 2029 ($750M), 2032 ($500M), 2035 ($1.5B), and 2055 ($1.5B) — no single year exceeds $1.5B and no maturity wall exists until 2035 at the earliest. The nearest maturity ($750M in 2027) is easily covered by a single half-year of FCF or a modest refinancing at investment-grade rates. By the time the 2029 tranche matures, AJG will likely have generated $6–8B in cumulative post-acquisition FCF, providing ample capacity to retire it with cash rather than rolling it. The private placement debt (~$3B of the $13.5B total) has its own staggered schedule; while specific terms are less public, AJG has historically managed private placements conservatively with staggered maturities. The revolving credit facility extension to April 2030 further insulates the company from near-term market volatility. The S&P BBB+ rating ensures continued access to investment-grade markets at competitive spreads.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations

Insurance brokers carry significant off-balance-sheet items worth monitoring. AJG holds client premium funds in fiduciary trust accounts — these are not AJG's assets or liabilities but represent a significant operational responsibility. Operating lease commitments exist for office space globally but are modest relative to the business scale. The AssuredPartners acquisition likely brought additional contingent consideration (earnouts) tied to performance targets, though the bulk of the $13.8B was paid at close. Total assets of $70.7B include substantial fiduciary assets that inflate the balance sheet but carry no credit risk to AJG.

### Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years

This is the critical safety analysis. Assume revenue drops 30% from FY2025's $13.9B to ~$9.7B and stays there for two years.

**Revenue characteristics mitigate the scenario's severity:** Insurance brokerage revenue is inherently sticky — ~90%+ of revenue comes from renewals that are contractually recurring. A 30% decline would require a combination of (a) massive client losses, (b) a severe hard market turn reducing premiums, and (c) economic collapse reducing insured exposures. Even in the 2008-2009 financial crisis, AJG's revenue declined only ~5%. A 30% decline is an extreme tail scenario.

**Assuming it happens anyway:**
- Revenue: ~$9.7B
- Assume operating margins compress to ~12% (cost cuts lag): operating income ~$1.16B
- Interest expense: ~$640M (fixed-rate, does not decrease)
- Interest coverage: ~1.8x — very thin but still above 1.0x
- FCF would likely be $500M-$800M after capex, still positive
- Cash + undrawn revolver ($3.9B) covers 6+ years of interest expense
- No debt matures in large blocks until 2035 ($1.5B)

**Verdict:** AJG survives a 30% revenue decline with significant discomfort but no solvency crisis. The fixed-rate debt structure, staggered maturities, and large undrawn revolver provide crucial buffers. The company would need to cut M&A spending and potentially reduce dividends, but would not face a liquidity crisis.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Debt is intelligently structured (all new acquisition debt fixed-rate, laddered maturities from 2027 to 2055, no near-term wall), S&P upgraded AJG to BBB+, $3.9B liquidity buffer exists, and $2B+ annual FCF supports credible deleveraging to ~2.5x by late 2027 without requiring asset sales.
- **Bear case:** Interest coverage at 3.9x (EBIT basis) is the lowest in AJG's recent history and leaves limited margin if organic growth slows further or integration costs persist — the balance sheet is carrying more absolute debt than at any point in company history.
- **Confidence:** High — the debt structure is transparent and meticulously constructed; the primary risk is execution risk on the deleveraging timeline, not liquidity or solvency.

## Red Flags

- Interest coverage of 3.9x (EBIT/interest) is at the lower bound of comfort for an investment-grade financial services company; deterioration from 5.9x in one year is the steepest observed in the historical record
- Debt/EBITDA at 3.7x is approximately 50% above AJG's historical 2.0–2.5x range — management credibility is now tied to the deleveraging timeline
- $639M annual interest expense consumes ~35% of GAAP operating income, up from ~17% (FY2022) — every dollar of organic growth shortfall now has a bigger flow-through impact on coverage ratios
- Private placement debt terms (~$3B of the total) are less transparent than the public notes; covenant structures and any financial maintenance tests are not fully public
- Cash balance dropped from $15.0B (FY2024 — pre-close staging) to $1.4B (FY2025) — while expected, it leaves less cushion for unexpected needs

## Score: 6 / 10

The balance sheet is carrying historically elevated leverage following the $13.45B AssuredPartners acquisition, but the debt is intelligently structured (fixed-rate, laddered maturities, no near-term wall), the S&P BBB+ rating reflects credit quality confidence, liquidity is solid at ~$3.9B, and AJG's recurring FCF supports a credible path to deleveraging — a score of 6 reflects temporarily elevated risk with a clear deleveraging roadmap that must be executed over the next 18–24 months.
