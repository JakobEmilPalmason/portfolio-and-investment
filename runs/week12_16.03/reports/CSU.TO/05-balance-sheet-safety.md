## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure**

Total debt grew from $2.3B (FY2022) to $5.8B (FY2025), tracking closely with acquisition activity. The structure is layered across several instruments: (1) the CSI revolving credit facility ($1.085B limit, unsecured, matures November 2026 — historically renewed well in advance); (2) publicly traded debentures totaling C$495M/$361M with the longest maturing in 2040; (3) a $500M promissory note tied to Optimal Blue with no amortization until year five (~2028–2029 first cash payment) maturing 2063; and (4) subsidiary-level debt raised within operating companies for their own acquisitions. The debt structure is intentionally long-dated and diversified across instruments — CSI does not rely on short-term commercial paper or overnight facilities. The most immediate refinancing item is the CSI revolving credit facility maturing November 2026, which functions as working capital/acquisition capacity and has been consistently renewed. At a 2.2x total Debt/EBITDA and 0.9x Net Debt/EBITDA, leverage is moderate and within the company's historical operating band.

**Interest Coverage**

EBIT/interest expense declined from 9.2x (FY2022) to 5.1x (FY2024) to 4.7x (FY2025). The deterioration reflects deliberate leverage addition to fund acquisitions — interest expense grew from $88M (FY2022) to $256M (FY2025) while EBIT grew from $1.0B to $2.1B. At 4.7x, coverage remains above the 3.0x warning threshold, though it has moved materially closer to it than the 9.2x starting point. Importantly, FCF-based coverage is far more comfortable: $2.7B FCF covers $256M in interest at roughly 10.5x, because FCF is far higher than EBIT-based coverage implies. The interest burden is genuinely manageable given the FCF engine.

**Liquidity**

Cash on hand at December 31, 2025 was $3.1B, up $1.1B from $2.0B at FY2024, reflecting strong FCF generation. The $1.085B revolving facility (assumed to be partially drawn or available) provides additional liquidity headroom. With $3.1B in cash against $5.8B total debt, near-term liquidity is robust. The company has demonstrated the ability to run with relatively modest cash balances (as low as $926M at FY2022) without distress, since operating FCF of $2.7B/year ($670M per quarter) provides continuous replenishment. The 12-24 month survival case requires no capital market access — internal FCF more than covers all debt service obligations.

**Refinancing Risk**

The CSI revolving facility (limit $1.085B) maturing November 2026 is the only near-term maturity of consequence. CSI has an unsecured, investment-grade-quality covenant profile. Historically it has renewed its facility ahead of maturity — given the company's financial profile (0.9x net leverage, $2.7B FCF, 20-year track record of uninterrupted growth), there is no credible scenario in which a renewal fails. The debentures (2040 final maturity) and the promissory note (2063) have no near-term maturities. Subsidiary debt may have shorter tenors but is funded by the subsidiary's own FCF. Refinancing risk is low.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

No material pension obligations are disclosed. Operating lease obligations are immaterial relative to FCF — CSI's businesses are software-only with minimal physical footprint. The primary contingent liability is earn-out obligations embedded in acquisition agreements, which can add to near-term cash outflows but are contractually capped and anticipated in the acquisition model. No significant guarantee or indemnification obligations have been flagged in recent filings.

**Stress Test: Revenue Down 30% for Two Years**

FY2025 revenue: $11.6B. A 30% decline would take revenue to approximately $8.1B — roughly equivalent to FY2023 levels. In that scenario:
- At FY2023 FCF margins (~20.7%), FCF would be approximately $1.7B per year.
- Annual interest expense at current debt levels: $256M.
- FCF after interest: approximately $1.4B per year.
- With $3.1B cash on hand, the company enters the stress period with roughly 2.2 years of full interest and operating cost coverage from cash alone, before counting ongoing FCF.
- No covenants are disclosed that would trigger acceleration on a revenue decline alone.

The company survives a 30% revenue decline for two years with substantial margin. The 75%+ recurring revenue base makes a sudden 30% organic revenue drop structurally implausible — churn would have to be extraordinary and sustained. The more realistic stress scenario is a sharp acquisition slowdown (new deal prices spike, management goes conservative), in which case CSI simply retains FCF and strengthens the balance sheet. The acquisition model is optionally deployed capital — it is not required to service existing obligations.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** 0.9x net leverage, $3.1B cash, $2.7B annual FCF, and long-dated debt structure mean the balance sheet will continue to support accelerating acquisition activity without financial distress for the foreseeable future.
- **Bear case:** The revolving facility renewal in late 2026 coincides with a credit tightening environment, and the continued growth of subsidiary debt within operating companies is opaque and potentially under-disclosed.
- **Confidence:** High — the key metrics (leverage, coverage, cash) are directly from audited filings, the debt structure is disclosed in detail, and the FCF cushion is large enough to resolve uncertainty on nearly any stress scenario.

---

## Red Flags

- Credit facility matures November 2026 — requires proactive renewal; if credit markets tighten materially before then, terms could worsen (though a default is essentially inconceivable given the financial profile).
- Interest coverage (EBIT-based) trending toward the 3x warning zone: currently 4.7x vs. 9.2x in FY2022; further leverage addition without EBIT growth would be concerning.
- Subsidiary-level debt is consolidated but not always broken out by entity — some opacity around which operating companies carry how much debt and what their individual covenants look like.
- The $500M Optimal Blue promissory note at 7% is above current market rates for investment-grade credits — suggests this was a seller-financing arrangement, acceptable but not optimal cost of capital.

---

## Score: 7 / 10

Moderate leverage (0.9x net Debt/EBITDA) and enormous FCF coverage of debt service provide genuine fortress-level safety in practice, but the EBIT-based interest coverage declining to 4.7x, near-term revolving facility renewal, and lack of transparency in subsidiary debt structures prevent a higher rating.
