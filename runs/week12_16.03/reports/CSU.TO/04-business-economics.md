## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Real but Declining**

ROIC has moved from 23.5% in FY2023 to 20.5% in FY2024 to 16.5% in FY2025. This requires careful decomposition. The denominator — invested capital — grew from $5.2B to $8.9B (+71%) over two years as CSI deployed capital aggressively into acquisitions. Meanwhile NOPAT (operating income × (1 − effective tax rate)) grew from roughly $1.2B to $1.7B (+42%). The invested capital base is expanding faster than NOPAT because acquired businesses are booked at full purchase price (including goodwill and intangibles), inflating the capital base immediately, while earnings from those acquisitions compound slowly over time. This is a predictable accounting consequence of accelerating the pace of acquisitions — not evidence of declining business quality per se. ROE fell more sharply, from 31.5% (FY2023) to 16.1% (FY2025), partly because equity grew as retained earnings accumulated and partly because the net income figure is depressed by rising amortization. The true economic return — assessed by FCF yield on tangible invested capital — remains high.

**Margin Structure: Steadily Improving**

Gross margins have expanded from 35.0–35.1% (FY2022–2023) to 37.6% (FY2025), reflecting two dynamics: (1) ongoing mix shift toward higher-margin SaaS and subscription products within the portfolio, and (2) Constellation's operational playbook of increasing maintenance fees 3–7% annually across acquired businesses. Operating margins expanded from 15.6% to 18.1% over the same period. Net margins appear to have deteriorated (7.7% in FY2022 to 4.4% in FY2025), but this is entirely attributable to rising D&A from acquired intangibles ($819M → $1.4B) and higher interest expense ($88M → $256M). On an FCF margin basis — which strips D&A and reflects the cash economics — margins have been consistently expanding: 19.0% (FY2022), 20.7% (FY2023), 21.2% (FY2024), 22.9% (FY2025). FCF margin is the more accurate window into earnings power.

**Cash Generation: The Core Thesis**

The FCF conversion ratio of 291–520% over four years is not a distortion — it is the central economic feature of CSI's model. The company acquires mature vertical market software (VMS) businesses, books large intangible amortization charges (non-cash), but the underlying businesses require minimal capital reinvestment. Maintenance capex has been flat at $41–68M across four years while revenue nearly doubled, confirming the asset-light nature. Free cash flow grew from $1.3B (FY2022) to $2.7B (FY2025), a 3-year CAGR of approximately 27%. CSI's own preferred metric — free cash flow available to shareholders (FCFA2S) — was $1.68B for FY2025, up 14% year-over-year, and is the figure management uses for capital allocation decisions and dividend coverage. Owner earnings (NI + D&A − CapEx) are approximately $1.8B in FY2025, somewhat lower than FCF due to working capital movements, but consistently growing.

**Capital Intensity: Exceptionally Low**

Maintenance capex of $68M against $11.6B in revenue represents 0.6% of revenue — a ratio that places CSI in the top decile of capital-light businesses globally. The acquired VMS businesses are software-only with no manufacturing, minimal hardware, and low physical asset requirements. Growth investments take the form of acquisition prices, not internal capex. This means essentially all FCF generated above interest and tax is available for redeployment into acquisitions — a self-reinforcing model where the capital flywheel turns faster as the portfolio grows.

**Revenue Quality: Near-Textbook Recurring**

Approximately 75% of revenue is recurring maintenance and subscription fees. The balance is professional services and licenses, which tend to correlate with new customer wins and implementations. Net revenue retention is reported above 100% — meaning the existing customer base expands organically through price increases and cross-sells before counting new logo growth. Organic maintenance growth on a constant currency basis was 6% in FY2025, driven by annual price escalations and upsells. Total organic growth of 3% (constant currency) is modest but expected — CSI explicitly does not chase high-growth markets; it acquires stable, dominant VMS businesses in unglamorous niches where switching costs are high and customer churn is structurally low.

**Operating Leverage: Present but Masked**

The genuine operating leverage in CSI's model is visible in the FCF margin expansion (19% → 23% over four years) and in operating margin improvement (15.6% → 18.1%). On a per-share basis, FCFA2S compounded at ~14% annually in FY2025. The reported net income line does not show clean leverage because it is penalized by rapidly growing D&A from acquisitions — but this is accounting, not economics. As acquired intangibles roll off over time, reported earnings will converge upward toward FCF. The quant model's sensitivity grid confirms that even in a bear scenario (16% revenue growth, 8.7% WACC), the implied IV is C$3,950 — 58% above current price — which captures the economics more accurately than trailing P/E.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** FCF margin continues to expand as the acquisition engine deploys into larger and more complex VMS targets (Lumine, Perseus, etc.), compounding FCFA2S at 15%+ for a decade.
- **Bear case:** Organic growth stays at 3% and acquisition multiples creep upward as competition for VMS businesses increases, compressing returns on deployed capital toward cost of capital.
- **Confidence:** High — the data is from audited filings, the FCF dynamics are mechanically clear, and the model has been consistent for 15+ years with no material deterioration in the fundamental franchise.

---

## Red Flags

- ROIC declining trend (23.5% → 16.5% over two years) warrants monitoring — if it drops below 12%, the acquisition engine is no longer creating value above WACC.
- Organic growth of 3% constant currency is at the low end of historical ranges; if maintenance pricing power weakens, the 75% recurring base could stagnate.
- D&A of $1.4B now exceeds net income of $512M — this gap will widen with continued acquisitions, making GAAP earnings increasingly uninformative for valuation.

## Score: 8 / 10

An exceptional FCF-generating machine with a structurally asset-light model, expanding margins, and 75%+ recurring revenue — held back from a 9 by the decelerating ROIC trend and low single-digit organic growth that makes the compounding entirely acquisition-dependent.
