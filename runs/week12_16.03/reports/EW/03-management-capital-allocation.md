## Detailed Analysis

Bernard Zovighian became CEO in May 2023 after a decade at Edwards, most recently running the TMTT and Surgical businesses. He is an insider promotion rather than an outside hire, which typically signals continuity. His total compensation of ~$14.6M is structured with 92% in performance-based components — options, performance-based restricted stock units (PBRSUs), and short-term incentive. The long-term incentive mix is 75% options and PBRSUs with minimum three-year vesting, which ties his wealth directly to share price performance over multi-year horizons. The compensation committee removed the FCF metric in 2025 in favor of heavier weighting on revenue (60%) and EPS (40%), which is a slight concern — FCF discipline is arguably more important than revenue growth for a company at Edwards' maturity. Insider ownership data shows Zovighian holds unvested options (334,875) and RSUs concentrated in 2025-2027 vesting, plus the company enforces anti-hedging and anti-pledging policies with ownership retention guidelines. This is a solid but not exceptional insider alignment — he has significant skin in the game through equity compensation, but not the kind of concentrated founder-level ownership you see at owner-operator businesses.

Capital allocation over the past four years has been aggressive and largely sensible. The $4.7B in cumulative buybacks from FY2022-FY2025 has genuinely reduced the share count from roughly 620M to 581M diluted shares — a ~6% reduction that, combined with revenue growth, has amplified per-share economics. Crucially, buybacks have been partially funded by the $4.2B Critical Care divestiture, which was a textbook portfolio pruning: selling a good-but-non-core business at a fair price to concentrate on the structural heart franchise. Edwards does not pay dividends, which is appropriate for a company reinvesting $1.1B annually in R&D and still growing double-digits.

The JenaValve episode, however, is a blemish. Edwards tried to acquire JenaValve for $945M after already purchasing JC Medical ($316M) — both in the aortic regurgitation TAVR space. The FTC challenged the combination on obvious antitrust grounds (Edwards would control the only two AR-TAVR platforms heading toward approval), and a court blocked the deal in January 2026. Edwards reportedly refused the FTC's suggestion to divest JC Medical as a remedy. This sequence suggests either overconfidence in regulatory strategy or a willingness to test antitrust boundaries that ultimately wasted management attention and likely $50M+ in transaction and legal costs. It is not a fatal flaw — the company walked away rather than overpaying for a remedy — but it reveals a management team that can overreach.

On governance, Edwards has an independent board chair (separate from CEO), a majority-independent board, and an independent Compensation & Governance Committee. The ~91% say-on-pay approval signals institutional comfort. The company communicates clearly in earnings calls and investor presentations — Zovighian speaks in concrete product and clinical terms rather than financial abstractions. The investor conference in December 2025 laid out specific 2026 guidance by segment with clinical milestones attached, which is the kind of transparency investors should demand.

R&D spending at $1.1B (18% of revenue) is high but justified for a company whose competitive position depends on next-generation product launches. The TMTT ramp (PASCAL, EVOQUE, SAPIEN M3) is the most important reinvestment bet — Edwards is targeting $2B in TMTT revenue by 2030 from $551M in 2025. If TMTT delivers, it justifies the R&D spend many times over. If it disappoints, Edwards remains a slower-growing TAVR company with high but gradually compressing margins.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Zovighian proves to be a disciplined capital allocator who continues shrinking shares, scales TMTT to $2B, and avoids further M&A missteps — creating significant per-share value growth.
- **Bear case:** Management chases growth through acquisitions after the organic TAVR growth rate decelerates, diluting the focused structural heart thesis and destroying capital.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The track record is solid on buybacks and the Critical Care divestiture, but Zovighian has been CEO for less than 3 years and the JenaValve episode raises questions about M&A discipline.

## Red Flags

- JenaValve acquisition blocked by FTC after Edwards refused to divest JC Medical — reveals a willingness to push antitrust boundaries that backfired.
- Compensation committee removed FCF metric in 2025 incentive plan — could reduce discipline on cash conversion as TMTT investments scale.
- CEO tenure is relatively short (~3 years); the capital allocation track record is still developing under new leadership.
- No dividend — appropriate for now, but if growth slows and buybacks continue at elevated valuations, capital return efficiency could deteriorate.

## Score: 7 / 10

Edwards demonstrates solid capital allocation discipline with genuine share count reduction and a smart divestiture, but the short CEO tenure, JenaValve misstep, and removal of FCF incentive metrics keep this a notch below top-tier stewardship.
