## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Edwards' ROIC trajectory is the most important signal in this analysis. At 20.9% in FY2022, returns were exceptional and reflected the company's dominance in structural heart. The decline to 12.6% in FY2025 is meaningful. Part of this is denominator-driven: the $4.2B Critical Care divestiture in September 2024 flooded the balance sheet with cash that inflates invested capital while the divested segment's earnings disappeared. ROE shows a similar distortion — spiking to 50.2% in FY2024 (driven by a $3B+ gain on the divestiture) before normalizing to 10.6% in FY2025. Adjusting for excess cash, underlying ROIC is likely in the 15–17% range, which is good but no longer elite. The key question is whether ROIC can re-expand as the company deploys its cash pile and TMTT scales toward profitability.

**Margin Structure.** Gross margins in the 78–80% range are among the best in medical devices and reflect Edwards' proprietary valve technology, physician switching costs, and limited direct competition in the TAVR space. The ~5 percentage point decline from FY2022's 83.8% reflects product mix changes (TMTT products carry lower initial margins than mature TAVR) and geographic mix (international TAVR growth at lower ASPs). Operating margins have compressed from 34.5% to 27.0% over the same period, driven by elevated R&D spending ($1.1B in FY2025, up 30% from FY2022) as Edwards invests in TMTT, TAVR next-gen platforms, and the asymptomatic aortic stenosis opportunity. This is deliberate margin compression for growth investment — not structural deterioration.

**Cash Generation.** FY2024's FCF of only $260M was an anomaly: operating cash flow was depressed by working capital movements and discontinuation effects surrounding the Critical Care sale. The FY2025 rebound to $1.3B FCF on $6.1B revenue (22% FCF margin) is much more representative. FCF conversion of 124% (FCF/NI) is excellent and reflects low capital intensity plus favorable working capital. Owner earnings (NI + D&A − CapEx) of ~$970M in FY2025 are somewhat lower because FY2025 net income was depressed by one-time charges. Adjusting SBC ($158M) as a real cost, SBC-adjusted FCF is approximately $1.15B — still strong at ~19% of revenue.

**Capital Intensity and Asset-Lightness.** Edwards is genuinely asset-light for a medical device manufacturer. Total CapEx of $260M on $6.1B revenue is a 4.3% ratio. Maintenance CapEx of only $157M means the business requires minimal reinvestment to sustain current earnings. Growth CapEx of ~$104M funds manufacturing expansion for TMTT and next-gen TAVR. D&A of $157M roughly matches maintenance CapEx, confirming the company is not under-depreciating. The invested capital base of $10.9B is inflated by cash; property/plant/equipment is only ~$1.5B.

**Revenue Quality.** TAVR procedures are not truly "recurring" in the subscription sense, but they are structurally durable: aortic stenosis is age-related, the patient population grows with demographics, and once a hospital adopts the SAPIEN platform, switching costs are high (catheter lab setup, physician training, clinical pathway integration). The TAVR market is expanding through indication creep — asymptomatic patients could double the addressable population if the CMS National Coverage Determination is favorable (expected Q4 2026). TMTT at $550M+ and growing 40%+ adds diversification. The 2026 guidance of 8–10% total revenue growth and $2.90–3.05 EPS signals continued momentum. Revenue concentration in TAVR (75%) is a risk, but the moat width around that revenue is exceptional.

**Operating Leverage.** Operating leverage has been negative over the measurement period — operating margins contracted from 34.5% to 27.0% while revenue grew from $4.5B to $6.1B. This is entirely explained by the ramp in R&D spending and TMTT investment. As TMTT approaches scale (projected $740–780M in 2026), Edwards has guided for "annual operating profit margin expansion thereafter," suggesting the business is at an inflection point where operating leverage should begin to manifest. The underlying TAVR business absolutely has operating leverage given its 78%+ gross margins and relatively fixed manufacturing base.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** TAVR dominance with 75% US share, TMTT inflecting toward profitability, and a potential doubling of the addressable market via asymptomatic AS indication create a path back to 20%+ ROIC and margin expansion.
- **Bear case:** ROIC has halved from 20.9% to 12.6% in three years, margins are compressing, and 75% revenue concentration in a single product category creates binary risk if competitive or regulatory dynamics shift.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The FY2024 distortions from the Critical Care divestiture make trend analysis difficult; FY2025 represents a cleaner baseline but only one year of post-divestiture data.

## Red Flags
- ROIC declining from 20.9% to 12.6% over three years is a meaningful deterioration, even after adjusting for excess cash
- Operating margin compression of 750bps (34.5% to 27.0%) is substantial and not yet reversing
- 75% revenue concentration in TAVR — a single product category
- FY2024 net income of $4.2B is artificially inflated by the ~$3B Critical Care divestiture gain, distorting multi-year trend analysis

## Score: 7 / 10
Edwards possesses genuinely elite gross margins (78%+), an asset-light model, and strong normalized FCF generation, but the declining ROIC trend, temporary margin compression from heavy R&D investment, and extreme revenue concentration in TAVR prevent a higher score until ROIC stabilization and TMTT profitability are demonstrated.
