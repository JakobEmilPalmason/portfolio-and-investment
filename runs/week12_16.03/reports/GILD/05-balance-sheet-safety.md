## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** As of December 31, 2025, Gilead carried $24.9B in total debt, down from $26.7B at year-end 2024 after repaying $1.8B during the year. The debt consists predominantly of fixed-rate senior unsecured notes across a laddered maturity schedule. Short-term debt and current maturities stood at approximately $2.8B, with the remaining ~$22.1B classified as long-term. Debt/EBITDA improved dramatically from 6.0x in FY2024 (distorted by impairment-driven low EBITDA) to 1.8x in FY2025 on a normalized basis. Net debt/EBITDA of 1.3x is conservative by any measure. Gilead maintains investment-grade credit ratings (Baa1/BBB+ from Moody's/S&P), providing favorable access to capital markets. The fixed-rate nature of the debt portfolio means rising interest rates have limited direct impact on debt service costs until individual tranches mature and require refinancing.

**Interest Coverage.** EBIT/interest coverage of 10.6x in FY2025 is well above the comfort threshold of 5x. Even using the more conservative operating cash flow basis, OCF of $10.0B covered interest expense of $1.0B by 10x. The FY2024 anomaly (1.7x coverage on a GAAP basis) was entirely driven by non-cash impairments — operating cash flow that year was actually the highest of the four-year period at $10.8B, which covered interest expense by 11x. This distinction matters: Gilead's cash interest coverage has never been in question, even during its worst GAAP earnings year. The consistency of cash-basis coverage across all four years (7x-11x range) reflects the stability of the underlying HIV franchise.

**Liquidity.** Gilead ended FY2025 with $7.6B in cash and cash equivalents plus $3.0B in marketable debt securities, totaling $10.6B in liquid assets. The current ratio of 1.6x indicates short-term assets ($17.9B) comfortably exceed short-term liabilities. Gilead also maintains a multi-billion dollar revolving credit facility (typically $2.5-3.0B for a company of this size, though the exact current commitment is disclosed in the 10-K). Combined liquid resources likely exceed $13B, covering more than 4 years of near-term debt maturities and interest payments. The company generated $10.0B in operating cash flow in FY2025 alone, adding to liquidity on a rolling basis. There is no near-term liquidity concern.

**Refinancing Risk.** With a laddered maturity profile of senior unsecured notes, Gilead faces manageable refinancing needs spread across multiple years. The $2.8B in current maturities is modest relative to the company's cash position ($10.6B) and annual FCF ($9.5B). Gilead's investment-grade rating ensures access to credit markets at reasonable rates. The key refinancing risk would materialize only in a scenario where multiple large maturities coincided with a severe credit market dislocation and simultaneous business deterioration — a low-probability triple conjunction. The company actively manages its maturity profile, as evidenced by the $1.8B debt repayment in 2025, which reduces future refinancing pressure.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Gilead's off-balance-sheet exposures include operating lease commitments (manufacturing facilities, office space), milestone and royalty obligations from licensing agreements, and potential contingent liabilities from ongoing litigation (patent disputes, product liability). These are typical for large pharma and are disclosed in the 10-K notes. None of these represent existential risks. The company's R&D collaboration commitments create potential milestone payments, but these are generally tied to program success (reducing risk of paying for failed assets). Pension obligations are minimal — Gilead operates defined-contribution plans for US employees. The most material contingent liability is typically litigation risk, but Gilead's patent portfolio on key HIV products is well-defended.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** Under this severe scenario, Gilead's revenue would fall from $29.4B to approximately $20.6B. Assuming gross margins compress to ~73% (some fixed-cost absorption loss) and the company cuts discretionary spending, operating income would likely fall to approximately $4-5B. Interest expense of ~$1.0B would still be covered 4-5x. FCF would decline to roughly $3.5-4.5B, still positive and still sufficient to service debt. With $10.6B in starting liquidity, Gilead could absorb 2-3 years of depressed earnings without accessing external capital. The company would likely suspend buybacks ($1.9B) and could reduce the dividend ($4.0B) if necessary, freeing an additional $4-5B annually. Critical point: a 30% revenue decline in HIV would require a catastrophic event (generic entry across the full portfolio, or a cure) since HIV treatment is medically non-discretionary. A more realistic stress would be a 15-20% decline from oncology/Veklury weakness combined with pricing pressure — under which Gilead would remain strongly cash-flow positive with no equity issuance risk.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** With net debt/FCF under 2x, $10.6B liquidity, 10x+ interest coverage, and a subscription-like HIV revenue base, Gilead's balance sheet is a fortress that can absorb severe shocks while maintaining dividends and buybacks.
- **Bear case:** Absolute debt of $24.9B and debt/equity of 110% are not trivial — a large M&A transaction (as Gilead has done historically) could re-lever the balance sheet materially and reset this safety margin.
- **Confidence:** High — Balance sheet data is from audited FY2025 financials; debt structure is well-disclosed in SEC filings; the durability of HIV cash flows is empirically demonstrated across multiple economic cycles.

## Red Flags

- Absolute debt level of $24.9B is substantial; another large acquisition (as Gilead has done with Pharmasset and Immunomedics) could push leverage to uncomfortable levels
- FY2024 GAAP interest coverage of 1.7x (due to impairments) shows how accounting events can temporarily create misleading signals — investors must monitor for genuine operational deterioration vs. non-cash noise
- Debt/equity of ~110% means a meaningful portion of the balance sheet is creditor-funded; equity cushion is thinner than peers like Johnson & Johnson or Roche
- $2.8B in near-term maturities requires ongoing refinancing attention, though this is small relative to cash reserves
- Historical M&A pattern (Immunomedics at $21B) suggests management is willing to lever up for strategic deals — future capital discipline is not guaranteed

## Score: 8 / 10

Gilead's balance sheet combines conservative net leverage (1.3x net debt/EBITDA), strong interest coverage (10.6x), ample liquidity ($10.6B), and recession-resistant HIV cash flows that would sustain debt service through severe stress scenarios — docked for absolute debt level and historical willingness to lever up for M&A.
