## Detailed Analysis

**What does Gilead sell and to whom?** Gilead Sciences develops and sells antiviral and oncology therapeutics. Its core franchise is HIV, where it sells treatment regimens (primarily Biktarvy, the global #1 single-tablet HIV regimen) and prevention products (Descovy for daily PrEP, Yeztugo/lenacapavir for twice-yearly injectable PrEP). Secondary franchises include liver disease (Livdelzi for primary biliary cholangitis), cell therapy for blood cancers (Yescarta, Tecartus via Kite Pharma), and Trodelvy for breast cancer. Customers are HIV-positive patients and at-risk populations globally, with prescriptions flowing through infectious disease specialists, primary care physicians, and public health programs.

**How does it make money?** Gilead's revenue model is highly recurring. HIV treatment is lifelong — once a patient is stabilized on Biktarvy, switching costs are enormous (both clinical risk and physician reluctance). Prevention (PrEP) is similarly ongoing for at-risk populations. The company manufactures small-molecule pills and now injectable formulations at high gross margins (~78-79%), selling through specialty pharmacy and hospital channels. Veklury (remdesivir) for COVID adds episodic revenue (~$900M in FY2025) that is declining but still material. FY2025 total product sales were $28.9B, with 2026 guidance of $29.6-30.0B.

**Key drivers.** The business hinges on 2-4 trackable variables: (1) Biktarvy treatment share and pricing — it generated $14.3B in FY2025, up 7% YoY, and is protected from US generics until 2036 following patent settlements; (2) Yeztugo launch trajectory — targeting $800M in 2026 with analysts calling this conservative, and peak sales estimates of $5-8B; (3) oncology pipeline execution — Trodelvy has stumbled in expansion indications, but anito-cel (BCMA CAR-T for myeloma, acquired via Arcellx for $7.8B) has an FDA decision expected H2 2026; (4) capital allocation discipline given the large debt load ($24.9B).

**Revenue predictability.** This is one of Gilead's strongest attributes. HIV treatment revenue is among the most predictable in pharma — patients do not stop therapy, switching rates are low, and Biktarvy's dominance is well-established. The base business (ex-Veklury) grew 4% in FY2025. Gilead guided to 6-7% base business growth in 2026. The Veklury tail-off is a known headwind but is becoming immaterial. Oncology revenue is less predictable given clinical trial uncertainty.

**Can you explain this business in 2 minutes?** Yes. Gilead sells HIV medicines that patients take for life. Its flagship pill (Biktarvy) is the most prescribed HIV treatment in the world, protected from generic competition until 2036. It just launched a twice-yearly injectable (Yeztugo) for HIV prevention that could become a multi-billion dollar franchise. It also has a smaller but growing oncology business through cell therapy and antibody-drug conjugates. The company generates ~$9.5B in free cash flow on ~$29B in revenue, pays a growing dividend, and is buying back shares. The main complexity is the oncology pipeline, which has had mixed clinical results, and the large acquisition-driven debt load.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Gilead is a rare pharma company with a dominant, deeply recurring HIV franchise protected until 2036, augmented by a potentially transformative PrEP product (Yeztugo) — the core business is as understandable as any in biotech.
- **Bear case:** The oncology diversification effort has produced expensive acquisitions and clinical setbacks (Trodelvy failures, $7.8B Arcellx deal), adding complexity and execution risk that could obscure an otherwise clean business model.
- **Confidence:** High — The HIV franchise that drives 72% of revenue is highly predictable and easy to understand; oncology adds modest complexity but does not undermine comprehensibility.

## Red Flags

- FY2024 net income collapse to $480M (from ~$5.7B in FY2023) due to acquisition charges and impairments — requires investors to look through GAAP noise
- Oncology pipeline has suffered multiple Phase III failures (Trodelvy in NSCLC, bladder cancer, HR+/HER2- breast cancer), making that segment harder to model
- Veklury revenue declining as COVID recedes — a known but still-active headwind
- Government payer concentration (340B, Medicaid, PEPFAR) creates political/pricing risk that is harder for non-healthcare investors to assess

## Score: 8 / 10

Gilead's core HIV business is one of the most understandable recurring-revenue models in pharma, with clear key drivers and high revenue predictability; the oncology diversification adds some complexity but the business remains well within a generalist investor's circle of competence.
