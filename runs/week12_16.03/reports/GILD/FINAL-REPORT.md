# Investment Analysis: GILD -- Gilead Sciences

**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Verdict:** Own

> Gilead trades at $137, essentially at the floor of its quant bear-case intrinsic value, while its dominant HIV franchise -- protected by Biktarvy patents through 2036 and augmented by the first-in-class twice-yearly injectable PrEP Yeztugo -- generates $9.5B in annual free cash flow with 78%+ gross margins. The market is pricing in none of the four 2026 commercial launches succeeding, creating asymmetric upside of 2:1 or better with a 99.9% Monte Carlo probability that the stock is undervalued. A 3-4% starter position in this defensive compounder (0.37 beta) offers rare combination of downside protection and multi-year growth optionality.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Gilead sells lifelong HIV treatments (Biktarvy, 72% of revenue) and twice-yearly injectable prevention (Yeztugo), creating subscription-like recurring revenue from millions of patients who cannot stop therapy.
2. **Moat**: Biktarvy's patent protection extends to 2036, physician switching costs in HIV are exceptionally high due to viral rebound risk, and Yeztugo is the only approved long-acting injectable PrEP with no competitor in sight.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of 21.7%, gross margins of 78-79%, and $9.5B in annual free cash flow on just $563M capex confirm an asset-light franchise earning well above its 6.2% cost of capital.
4. **Management**: Capital returns are disciplined ($5.9B to shareholders in FY2025, 10-year dividend streak), but the $21B Immunomedics acquisition was value-destructive and the $7.8B Arcellx deal is unproven.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt of $17.4B is covered 1.8x by annual FCF, interest coverage is 10.6x, and $10.6B in cash means Gilead could survive a 30% revenue decline for two years without accessing external capital.
6. **Price**: At $137 and 14.3x forward earnings, the market is pricing Gilead as if none of its four 2026 launches succeed and the HIV franchise stagnates -- all 25 sensitivity grid cells and 99.9% of Monte Carlo simulations show IV above today's price.
7. **Margin of safety**: The $138 bear-case IV sits at essentially the current price, but the business-level protection is strong -- 10 years of patent exclusivity, $9.5B FCF, and a 0.37 beta that limits drawdowns in market selloffs.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Biktarvy faces a credible pre-2036 patent challenge, or if two or more of the four 2026 launches fail AND lenacapavir quarterly revenue stays below $500M by Q3 2026.

## Key Risks

1. **Oncology capital destruction**: The $21B Immunomedics/Trodelvy acquisition has been value-destructive (3 Phase III failures, accelerated approval withdrawn), and the $7.8B Arcellx deal enters a competitive BCMA CAR-T market -- management's appetite for premium-priced pipeline M&A is the single largest risk to shareholder value.

2. **HIV revenue concentration**: Biktarvy alone represents ~47% of total revenue and the broader HIV franchise ~72%. Any disruption -- unexpected patent challenge, government pricing intervention (IRA/340B), or a breakthrough cure/vaccine -- would disproportionately impact Gilead versus diversified pharma peers.

3. **2026 launch execution risk**: Four simultaneous commercial launches (lenacapavir oral combo, Trodelvy 1L TNBC, anito-cel myeloma, bulevirtide) concentrate binary catalysts in a single year. If two or more disappoint, the growth thesis weakens and the multiple could compress back toward trough levels.

4. **Government pricing pressure**: HIV drugs are heavily consumed through government programs (Medicaid, PEPFAR, 340B, VA). IRA drug pricing negotiations and potential 340B expansion represent a slow-moving but real margin risk over the 5-10 year horizon.

5. **Thin price MOS at bear case**: The current price of $137 sits essentially at the bear-case IV of $138, meaning there is zero quantitative cushion if the pessimistic scenario materializes. Downside protection depends entirely on the bear case being too conservative.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) Stock pulls back below $125, creating meaningful cushion below bear-case IV; (2) Q1-Q2 2026 earnings confirm lenacapavir PrEP revenue ramp on track for $800M+ annual run rate; (3) Anito-cel receives FDA approval in myeloma, validating the Arcellx acquisition thesis.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) Biktarvy faces a credible pre-2036 patent challenge or authorized generic entry; (2) Two or more 2026 launches fail AND lenacapavir quarterly revenue stays below $500M by Q3 2026; (3) Net debt exceeds 3x EBITDA from a large acquisition, signaling balance sheet deterioration.

# Circle of Competence -- GILD

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Gilead Q4/FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Gilead investor presentations, FiercePharma reporting on Yeztugo launch and Trodelvy pipeline, Morningstar moat analysis, Yahoo Finance, company financials from context

| 2 | Revenue model is subscription-like: HIV treatment is chronic/lifelong, and the shift to twice-yearly injectable PrEP (Yeztugo) adds a long-duration prevention franchise with multi-billion peak sales potential | 5 |
| 3 | The 2-3 key drivers are straightforward: (1) Biktarvy volume/pricing stability, (2) Yeztugo PrEP ramp, (3) oncology pipeline conversion -- an investor can track these quarterly | 4 |
| 4 | Customer concentration is low at the patient level (millions of HIV patients globally) but high at the payer level -- government programs (Medicaid, PEPFAR, 340B) and a handful of PBMs control reimbursement | 3 |
| 5 | FY2024 net income anomaly ($480M vs $8.5B in FY2025) was driven by the $7.8B Arcellx acquisition write-down and Trodelvy impairments, which is a one-time complexity but does not obscure the underlying business model | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Gilead sell and to whom?** Gilead Sciences develops and sells antiviral and oncology therapeutics. Its core franchise is HIV, where it sells treatment regimens (primarily Biktarvy, the global #1 single-tablet HIV regimen) and prevention products (Descovy for daily PrEP, Yeztugo/lenacapavir for twice-yearly injectable PrEP). Secondary franchises include liver disease (Livdelzi for primary biliary cholangitis), cell therapy for blood cancers (Yescarta, Tecartus via Kite Pharma), and Trodelvy for breast cancer. Customers are HIV-positive patients and at-risk populations globally, with prescriptions flowing through infectious disease specialists, primary care physicians, and public health programs.

**How does it make money?** Gilead's revenue model is highly recurring. HIV treatment is lifelong -- once a patient is stabilized on Biktarvy, switching costs are enormous (both clinical risk and physician reluctance). Prevention (PrEP) is similarly ongoing for at-risk populations. The company manufactures small-molecule pills and now injectable formulations at high gross margins (~78-79%), selling through specialty pharmacy and hospital channels. Veklury (remdesivir) for COVID adds episodic revenue (~$900M in FY2025) that is declining but still material. FY2025 total product sales were $28.9B, with 2026 guidance of $29.6-30.0B.

**Key drivers.** The business hinges on 2-4 trackable variables: (1) Biktarvy treatment share and pricing -- it generated $14.3B in FY2025, up 7% YoY, and is protected from US generics until 2036 following patent settlements; (2) Yeztugo launch trajectory -- targeting $800M in 2026 with analysts calling this conservative, and peak sales estimates of $5-8B; (3) oncology pipeline execution -- Trodelvy has stumbled in expansion indications, but anito-cel (BCMA CAR-T for myeloma, acquired via Arcellx for $7.8B) has an FDA decision expected H2 2026; (4) capital allocation discipline given the large debt load ($24.9B).

**Revenue predictability.** This is one of Gilead's strongest attributes. HIV treatment revenue is among the most predictable in pharma -- patients do not stop therapy, switching rates are low, and Biktarvy's dominance is well-established. The base business (ex-Veklury) grew 4% in FY2025. Gilead guided to 6-7% base business growth in 2026. The Veklury tail-off is a known headwind but is becoming immaterial. Oncology revenue is less predictable given clinical trial uncertainty.

**Can you explain this business in 2 minutes?** Yes. Gilead sells HIV medicines that patients take for life. Its flagship pill (Biktarvy) is the most prescribed HIV treatment in the world, protected from generic competition until 2036. It just launched a twice-yearly injectable (Yeztugo) for HIV prevention that could become a multi-billion dollar franchise. It also has a smaller but growing oncology business through cell therapy and antibody-drug conjugates. The company generates ~$9.5B in free cash flow on ~$29B in revenue, pays a growing dividend, and is buying back shares. The main complexity is the oncology pipeline, which has had mixed clinical results, and the large acquisition-driven debt load.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Gilead is a rare pharma company with a dominant, deeply recurring HIV franchise protected until 2036, augmented by a potentially transformative PrEP product (Yeztugo) -- the core business is as understandable as any in biotech.
- **Bear case:** The oncology diversification effort has produced expensive acquisitions and clinical setbacks (Trodelvy failures, $7.8B Arcellx deal), adding complexity and execution risk that could obscure an otherwise clean business model.
- **Confidence:** High -- The HIV franchise that drives 72% of revenue is highly predictable and easy to understand; oncology adds modest complexity but does not undermine comprehensibility.

## Red Flags

- FY2024 net income collapse to $480M (from ~$5.7B in FY2023) due to acquisition charges and impairments -- requires investors to look through GAAP noise
- Oncology pipeline has suffered multiple Phase III failures (Trodelvy in NSCLC, bladder cancer, HR+/HER2- breast cancer), making that segment harder to model
- Veklury revenue declining as COVID recedes -- a known but still-active headwind
- Government payer concentration (340B, Medicaid, PEPFAR) creates political/pricing risk that is harder for non-healthcare investors to assess

## Score: 8 / 10

Gilead's core HIV business is one of the most understandable recurring-revenue models in pharma, with clear key drivers and high revenue predictability; the oncology diversification adds some complexity but the business remains well within a generalist investor's circle of competence.

# Durable Competitive Advantage -- GILD

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Morningstar wide moat assessment, Gilead Q4/FY2025 earnings and guidance, FiercePharma on Biktarvy patent settlements (Oct 2025), FiercePharma on Yeztugo launch dynamics, Gilead investor presentations, company financials from context

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Biktarvy's US generic competition has been delayed to April 2036 through patent settlements with Lupin, Cipla, and Laurus Labs -- a critical 3-year extension from the prior 2033 estimate, protecting $14.3B/yr in revenue | 5 |
| 2 | Yeztugo (twice-yearly injectable PrEP) is the first and only long-acting HIV prevention option, creating a first-mover advantage in a category with no approved competitors and projected $5-8B peak sales | 5 |
| 3 | Switching costs in HIV treatment are exceptionally high -- physicians are reluctant to change regimens for virally suppressed patients, and Biktarvy's market share continues to grow (7% YoY to $14.3B in FY2025) | 5 |
| 4 | Gross margins of ~78-79% have been sustained for years, reflecting the combination of patent protection, low manufacturing cost (small molecules), and a compact salesforce -- classic wide-moat economics | 4 |
| 5 | Oncology moat is narrow and resetting -- Trodelvy has failed in multiple expansion indications, and cell therapy (Yescarta) faces intensifying competition from other CAR-T and bispecific antibody therapies | 3 |
| 6 | No major loss of exclusivity expected until 2036, providing an unusually long runway of protected cash flows compared to most large-cap pharma peers | 5 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Type of moat.** Gilead's competitive advantage rests on three reinforcing moat sources: (1) **Patent/regulatory protection** -- Biktarvy is shielded from US generics until 2036, and Yeztugo's novel long-acting injectable formulation creates a regulatory barrier that is difficult to replicate (complex manufacturing, years of clinical trials required for any competitor). (2) **Switching costs** -- HIV treatment is a "set and forget" category; once a patient is virally suppressed on Biktarvy, neither the patient nor the physician wants to risk switching. The clinical stakes (viral rebound, resistance) create powerful inertia. (3) **Scale economics in HIV** -- Gilead's dominant market position (~52% of US HIV market projected) allows it to spread R&D and commercial costs across a massive installed base, while requiring only a small specialty salesforce. Morningstar explicitly rates Gilead as a "wide moat" company.

**Durability on a 10-year view.** The moat is unusually durable for pharma. Most large-cap pharma companies face patent cliffs within 5-7 years; Gilead has pushed its primary LOE to 2036. The transition from daily oral PrEP (Descovy) to twice-yearly injectable PrEP (Yeztugo) is a "platform shift" that resets the competitive clock -- even if a competitor develops a long-acting PrEP alternative, Gilead will have years of first-mover advantage, physician familiarity, and real-world efficacy data. The BIC/LEN combination (bictegravir + lenacapavir) expected to launch H2 2026 further extends the franchise by combining treatment and long-acting components. On a 10-year view, Gilead's HIV moat is likely to remain intact through at least 2033-2036, with Yeztugo potentially extending dominance well beyond.

**Evidence in the numbers.** The financial evidence for a wide moat is compelling: gross margins of 78-79% sustained over multiple years; ROIC of 21.7% (FY2025) well above cost of capital (~6.2% WACC); Biktarvy growing 7% YoY despite being a $14B+ product; Descovy growing 31% YoY to $2.8B as PrEP awareness expands; operating margins of 37-40%; and FCF of $9.5B representing ~33% of revenue. The base business (ex-Veklury) has grown consistently at 4-7% annually, which is remarkable for a company of this scale in a mature therapeutic area. Pricing power, while under political scrutiny, has been maintained -- Gilead has been able to implement modest price increases while expanding volume.

**Where is the moat weakest?** The moat is weakest in oncology. Trodelvy has suffered back-to-back Phase III failures in NSCLC, bladder cancer (accelerated approval withdrawn), and first-line HR+/HER2- breast cancer. The cell therapy business (Yescarta, Tecartus) faces growing competition from bispecific antibodies (which don't require the complex logistics of CAR-T manufacturing) and next-generation CAR-T platforms. Gilead spent $7.8B acquiring Arcellx for anito-cel (BCMA CAR-T for myeloma), but this market is already competitive with Johnson & Johnson's Carvykti and others. Oncology represents a significant capital allocation bet where Gilead's advantages are not yet proven.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** This is the critical question for pharma moats, and Gilead's answer is better than most. In HIV, the advantage does NOT reset each cycle -- Biktarvy's switching costs persist, and the lenacapavir platform (Yeztugo, BIC/LEN) extends the franchise rather than replacing it. Gilead is layering new products on top of an entrenched position, not starting from scratch. In oncology, however, advantages do reset with each clinical trial readout, and Gilead has not yet established the kind of durable franchise position it holds in HIV.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Gilead possesses one of the widest moats in pharma -- Biktarvy protected to 2036, Yeztugo as first-in-class long-acting PrEP with no approved competitors, and switching costs that make the HIV franchise nearly unassailable for the next decade.
- **Bear case:** The oncology moat is narrow and expensive to build, with multiple clinical failures and a $7.8B acquisition that may not generate adequate returns in an increasingly competitive cell therapy market.
- **Confidence:** High -- The HIV moat evidence is overwhelming and well-documented in both clinical dominance and financial metrics; oncology weakness is a known but secondary concern given HIV's 72% revenue share.

## Red Flags

- Trodelvy has failed in 3 major expansion indications (NSCLC, bladder, 1L HR+/HER2- breast cancer), eroding confidence in Gilead's oncology thesis
- The $7.8B Arcellx acquisition for anito-cel enters a competitive BCMA CAR-T market with established players
- Political/regulatory risk to HIV pricing -- 340B program, IRA drug pricing negotiations could pressure margins over time
- Gilead's HIV dominance makes it a target for policy intervention -- any government action on HIV drug pricing would disproportionately impact this company
- Cell therapy manufacturing complexity (Yescarta, anito-cel) is a structural disadvantage vs. off-the-shelf bispecific antibodies

## Score: 8 / 10

Gilead's HIV franchise constitutes a textbook wide moat -- patent protection to 2036, powerful switching costs, first-in-class long-acting prevention, and sustained 78%+ gross margins -- offset only by the narrow and unproven oncology moat that consumes significant capital.

# Management & Capital Allocation -- GILD

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Gilead 2025 proxy statement (DEF 14A), Gilead Q4/FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), GuruFocus insider transaction data, Gilead dividend history (investors.gilead.com), FiercePharma on Arcellx acquisition, Barclays conference CFO commentary (Mar 2026), company financials from context

| 2 | CEO Daniel O'Day has explicitly prioritized capital allocation as: (1) growth acquisitions, (2) dividends, (3) buybacks -- a clear and consistently communicated framework since taking the role | 4 |
| 3 | The $7.8B Arcellx acquisition in 2024 was the largest deal under O'Day and carries significant execution risk -- anito-cel FDA decision expected H2 2026 will be a defining test of management's M&A judgment | 5 |
| 4 | 10 consecutive years of dividend increases, with a 3.8% raise announced for Q1 2026 (to $0.82/quarter), reflecting commitment to income investors despite heavy pipeline spending | 3 |
| 5 | CEO O'Day sold 10,000 shares in recent insider transactions, which is notable but modest relative to typical executive compensation-driven sales; 8 of 9 board nominees are independent directors | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Daniel O'Day, who became CEO in 2019 after a long career at Roche, does not have founder-level ownership. Insider selling has been modest -- a recent sale of 10,000 shares was flagged but is typical of scheduled disposition plans for executives compensated heavily in equity. The board structure is sound: 8 of 9 director nominees are independent, with committees (Audit, Compensation and Talent, Nominating/Governance, Science) fully comprised of independent directors. The combined Chairman/CEO role is a governance negative -- O'Day serves as both, though the strong independent board composition partially mitigates this concern. Institutional ownership is high, as expected for a mega-cap pharma name.

**Capital allocation track record.** O'Day has articulated a clear capital allocation hierarchy: growth via bolt-on acquisitions first, dividends second, buybacks third. This is an intentional departure from predecessor John Milligan's era, when O'Day has characterized past buyback spending as "wasted money." The execution has been mixed. On the positive side, Gilead returned $5.9B to shareholders in FY2025 and has increased the dividend for 10 consecutive years. The $6B share repurchase authorization provides flexibility. Share count has been modestly declining -- the 3-year buyback ratio is 0.20, indicating meaningful but not aggressive repurchase activity. On the acquisition front, the $21B Immunomedics deal (2020, for Trodelvy) has been disappointing -- multiple Phase III failures have impaired the asset significantly, contributing to the FY2024 earnings collapse. The $7.8B Arcellx deal (2024, for anito-cel) is too early to judge but enters a competitive market. These large acquisitions represent the primary risk in O'Day's capital allocation record.

**Dividend policy.** The dividend is well-covered and growing. At $3.28/share annualized (post-Q1 2026 raise), the payout ratio against FY2025 EPS of ~$6.80 is approximately 48%, and against FCF of $9.5B is roughly 43%. The 10-year streak of increases and the 2.4% yield make Gilead attractive to income-oriented investors. Management has signaled dividends as a top-2 priority, and coverage ratios suggest ample room for continued increases.

**M&A discipline and track record.** This is the most debatable aspect of Gilead's stewardship. The Pharmasset acquisition (2011, $11B for sofosbuvir/Sovaldi/Harvoni) was transformative and wildly successful. The Kite Pharma acquisition (2017, $12B for Yescarta/CAR-T) has been moderately successful but faces competitive headwinds. The Immunomedics deal (2020, $21B for Trodelvy) has been the weakest -- Trodelvy's multiple clinical failures and the withdrawal of its bladder cancer approval have destroyed significant value. The Arcellx deal ($7.8B) is the most recent large transaction and represents a bet on next-generation CAR-T in myeloma. The pattern shows management willing to pay premium prices for pipeline assets, with mixed results. The total debt of $24.9B (net debt $17.4B) partly reflects this acquisition-heavy strategy, though interest coverage at 10.6x remains comfortable.

**Compensation design.** Based on the 2025 proxy statement, executive compensation is overseen by the independent Compensation and Talent Committee. The structure includes base salary, annual cash incentive, and long-term equity awards. The company is seeking stockholder approval to add 47.75 million shares to the 2022 Equity Incentive Plan (extending to 2036), which represents dilution but is standard for large pharma. A detailed breakdown of the pay-for-performance alignment would require deeper proxy analysis, but the structure appears conventional for the sector. The request for a large equity pool extension warrants monitoring for dilution impact.

**Candor and consistency.** O'Day's communication has been generally straightforward. Management guided to 6-7% base business growth for 2026 ($29.6-30.0B in product sales), which aligns with historical growth rates. The 2026 EPS guidance of $6.75-$7.15 (GAAP) / $8.45-$8.85 (non-GAAP) shows the typical pharma gap between GAAP and adjusted earnings but is within reason. Management has been transparent about Trodelvy setbacks and the competitive challenges in oncology, rather than papering over failures. The CFO's recent commentary at Barclays (March 2026) emphasized "long cycle" growth from Yeztugo and the Arcellx deal, which is consistent with the stated strategy.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Management has a clear capital allocation framework, a 10-year dividend growth streak, and is making bold but potentially transformative pipeline bets (Yeztugo, anito-cel) that could extend Gilead's dominance for another decade.
- **Bear case:** The Immunomedics/Trodelvy acquisition destroyed significant value, the combined Chairman/CEO role is a governance weakness, and the willingness to pay premium prices for pipeline assets creates persistent M&A risk.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- Capital return discipline is strong and communication is candid, but the mixed M&A track record (Trodelvy disappointment, Arcellx unproven) prevents a high-confidence assessment of management quality.

## Red Flags

- $21B Immunomedics acquisition has been a value-destructive deal -- Trodelvy failed in 3 expansion indications and lost its bladder cancer approval
- Combined Chairman/CEO role with no independent lead director explicitly identified
- Request to add 47.75M shares to equity incentive plan represents potential dilution
- CEO insider selling (10,000 shares), though modest and likely programmatic
- Total debt of $24.9B accumulated partly through acquisition spending -- not yet at dangerous levels (10.6x coverage) but limits future M&A flexibility
- FY2024 GAAP earnings collapse ($480M) driven by acquisition write-downs may have been foreseeable at the time of the Immunomedics deal

## Score: 6 / 10

Gilead's management demonstrates disciplined shareholder returns (10-year dividend streak, $5.9B returned in FY2025) and clear strategic communication, but the mixed M&A track record -- particularly the value-destructive Trodelvy acquisition -- and combined Chairman/CEO role prevent a score above average.

# Business Economics -- GILD

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Gilead Sciences FY2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings press release (Feb 10, 2026), Gilead investor relations, Yahoo Finance, GuruFocus ROIC data, MacroTrends financials, context/financials.md

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | HIV franchise ($20.8B, ~72% of product sales) delivers subscription-like recurring revenue with Biktarvy at $14.3B (+7% YoY) | 5 |
| 2 | ROIC averaged ~18-22% over FY2022-FY2025, well above pharma cost of capital (~8-10%), demonstrating genuine economic moat | 5 |
| 3 | FCF generation is remarkably consistent ($7.4B-$10.3B range over 4 years) with minimal capex (~$550M), confirming asset-light model | 4 |
| 4 | Veklury revenue decline (-49% to $911M in FY2025) partially offsets organic growth -- COVID tailwind is largely exhausted | 3 |
| 5 | Operating leverage is moderate: FY2025 revenue grew 2.4% while operating income grew 10.4%, aided by cost discipline on flat R&D spend | 3 |
| 6 | SBC of $894M (FY2025) represents ~9.4% of FCF -- meaningful but not egregious for large-cap biotech | 2 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Gilead's ROIC has been consistently strong, ranging from 15.3% (FY2023) to 21.7% (FY2025), with a four-year average around 18%. The FY2024 dip to 15.7% was driven by large non-cash impairments that depressed net income to $480M while operating cash flow remained robust at $10.8B -- underscoring the importance of looking through GAAP noise to cash economics. ROE of 40.5% in FY2025 is elevated partly by leverage (debt/equity ~110%), but even on an unlevered basis, the business earns well above its cost of capital. This is characteristic of a franchise with pricing power and patent-protected products serving chronic conditions requiring lifetime therapy.

**Margin Structure.** Gross margins have been remarkably stable at 76-79% over the past four years, reflecting the inherent economics of pharmaceutical manufacturing where variable costs are a small fraction of the price. Operating margins expanded from 32.6% (FY2023) to 39.7% (FY2025), driven by a combination of higher-margin HIV sales growth and disciplined SG&A management. R&D spending has been roughly flat at $5.7-5.9B, meaning incremental revenue is falling to the bottom line at high rates. Net margins swing more (1.7% to 28.9%) due to one-time items, but normalized net margins sit comfortably in the 25-30% range. Non-GAAP operating margin for FY2025 was approximately 48%, further illustrating the quality of the underlying business when adjusting for amortization of acquired intangibles.

**Cash Generation and Owner Economics.** Gilead is a free cash flow machine. FY2025 FCF of $9.5B on revenue of $29.4B represents a 32% FCF margin, and owner earnings (adding back SBC, subtracting maintenance capex) were approximately $10.7B. Capex intensity is minimal -- just $563M in FY2025, or ~1.9% of revenue -- confirming the asset-light nature of pharmaceutical manufacturing at scale. FCF conversion from operating income has consistently exceeded 80%. The company returned $5.9B to shareholders in FY2025 ($4.0B dividends + $1.9B buybacks), comfortably covered by FCF with room for continued debt reduction. Cash and marketable securities stood at $10.6B at year-end, providing ample flexibility.

**Revenue Quality.** This is one of Gilead's strongest attributes. The HIV franchise (~72% of product sales) generates subscription-like recurring revenue -- patients on antiretroviral therapy take their medication daily for life. Biktarvy alone at $14.3B is one of the largest drug franchises globally, with 7% growth in FY2025. Descovy contributed $2.8B. The launch of Lenacapavir (Yeztugo) for twice-yearly HIV prevention represents a significant new revenue stream, with 2026 guidance of ~$800M. Liver disease ($3.2B, +6%) provides a secondary stable base. The weakness is oncology -- Trodelvy at $1.4B grew only 6% and cell therapy is expected to decline ~10% in 2026 due to competitive pressures. Veklury's decline from $1.8B to $911M is a headwind but now small enough to be absorbed. Revenue concentration in HIV is both a strength (durability) and a risk (regulatory/competitive disruption).

**Operating Leverage.** Gilead shows moderate positive operating leverage. FY2025 revenue growth of 2.4% translated to operating income growth of 10.4%, a roughly 4:1 leverage ratio. This is driven by the fixed-cost nature of R&D (flat at ~$5.8B) and SG&A discipline. However, the operating leverage is less dramatic than in pure-software businesses because Gilead must maintain substantial R&D spending to defend its franchise and develop next-generation therapies. The company guided FY2026 product sales of $29.6-30.0B with non-GAAP EPS of $8.45-8.85, implying continued margin expansion.
