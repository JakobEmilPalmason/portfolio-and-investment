## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** WCN carries $9.1B in total debt against $8.2B in stockholders' equity, yielding a debt/equity ratio of approximately 1.1x. Net debt stands at $8.8B given just $46M in cash. The debt/EBITDA ratio of 3.1x (on a reported basis) or approximately 2.8x on adjusted EBITDA of $3.13B sits within management's stated target leverage range of 2.5-3.0x. This is moderate leverage for an infrastructure-like business with highly predictable cash flows, but it is not conservative by absolute standards. The debt has grown meaningfully — from roughly $7.3B (implied by FY2022 debt/EBITDA of 3.3x on $2.2B EBITDA) to $9.1B today — reflecting the acquisition-funded growth strategy.

**Maturity Profile and Refinancing Risk.** The maturity schedule is WCN's strongest balance sheet attribute. Based on available data, the company had approximately $1.9B maturing in 2026 (likely revolver borrowings and maturing notes), but has been proactively refinancing: $500M of 5.25% senior notes due 2035 were issued in June 2025, and $600M of 4.80% senior notes due 2036 were priced in March 2026, with proceeds used to pay down the revolving credit facility. The revolver itself does not mature until February 2029, providing a reliable liquidity backstop. The bulk of long-term debt ($4.4B+) matures in 2029 and beyond. This well-laddered profile means no single maturity creates a refinancing cliff, and the company has demonstrated consistent capital market access at investment-grade rates.

**Liquidity Position.** Cash on hand of $46M is negligible, which is standard practice for investment-grade companies with large undrawn revolvers. The revolving credit facility had $581M of remaining borrowing capacity as of mid-2025. Combined with quarterly operating cash flow of approximately $600M, total liquidity is adequate at roughly $1.2B+. However, this model relies entirely on continued capital market access — there is no meaningful cash buffer. In a severe credit market dislocation (2008-style), this would be a vulnerability, though the company's investment-grade rating and essential-service nature mitigate this risk substantially.

**Interest Coverage and Debt Serviceability.** Interest coverage has trended from 6.2x (FY2022) down to 5.2x (FY2025), reflecting both rising debt levels and the higher rate environment. The FY2024 trough of 3.3x was distorted by Chiquita Canyon-related income charges. At 5.2x, coverage is adequate but leaves limited room for error in a stress scenario. The weighted average cost of debt is rising as older, cheaper notes mature and are replaced with new issuances in the 4.8-5.3% range, though these rates remain favorable for investment-grade issuers.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** Waste collection is among the most recession-resistant industries — volumes declined only ~5% during 2008-2009. But testing the extreme: a 30% revenue decline from $9.5B would yield ~$6.6B in revenue. Assuming EBITDA margins compress to 28% (from 33%), EBITDA would be ~$1.9B. At current interest expense of approximately $350M annually, coverage would drop to ~5.4x on EBITDA — still serviceable. However, if we assume interest expense rises to $450M (higher rates on refinanced debt), coverage drops to ~4.2x. The company could survive by cutting capex from $1.2B to maintenance-only (~$700M), suspending dividends/buybacks ($839M in FY2025), and drawing the revolver. Total available cash flow in this stress case would be roughly $1.9B EBITDA - $450M interest - $700M maintenance capex = $750M, more than sufficient to service debt without equity issuance. The company would survive, but net debt/EBITDA would spike to 4.6x, likely triggering covenant pressure and rating agency review.

**Off-Balance-Sheet and Environmental Liabilities.** The Chiquita Canyon landfill closure is a cautionary example — $200M+ in cash flow impact in a single year from environmental remediation. Waste companies carry material closure and post-closure environmental obligations that are partially accrued but represent ongoing tail risk. Operating lease obligations and landfill retirement obligations add to the true leverage picture beyond what headline debt figures show.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Well-laddered maturities, investment-grade access, predictable cash flows from essential services, and a proven ability to refinance at favorable rates make this a manageable capital structure for an infrastructure-like business.
- **Bear case:** $9.1B of gross debt with only $46M cash, declining interest coverage trend, and rising refinancing costs in a higher-rate world leave less margin for error than the headline leverage ratio suggests; environmental liabilities add hidden leverage.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Debt structure is well-documented from public filings and recent capital market activity. The main uncertainty is the full scope of environmental liabilities and off-balance-sheet obligations not visible in summary data.

## Red Flags
- Only $46M cash on hand — entirely reliant on revolving credit facility and capital markets for liquidity
- Interest coverage declining from 6.2x to 5.2x over three years as debt grows faster than earnings
- ~$1.9B of debt maturing in 2026 required active refinancing; future large maturities will need similar proactive management
- Environmental liabilities (Chiquita Canyon precedent: $200M+ single-site impact) represent material off-balance-sheet risk
- Negative working capital of -$813M, though structural for the industry

## Score: 7 / 10
WCN carries meaningful leverage ($9.1B debt, 3.1x debt/EBITDA) but manages it well through a laddered maturity profile, investment-grade market access, and highly predictable cash flows from essential waste services — the balance sheet is conservatively leveraged for this specific business model, though it is not a fortress by any measure, and the declining interest coverage trend warrants monitoring.
