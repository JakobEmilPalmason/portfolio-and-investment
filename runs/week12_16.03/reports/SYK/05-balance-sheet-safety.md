## Detailed Analysis

Stryker's balance sheet reflects the financial profile of a serial acquirer in medtech — meaningfully leveraged but well within the guardrails of investment-grade discipline. Total debt stands at $15.9B against $4.1B in cash, producing net debt of $11.8B. The debt/EBITDA ratio of 2.5x (net debt/EBITDA of 1.9x) is elevated relative to a zero-debt ideal but entirely typical for large-cap medtech acquirers and sits comfortably below the 3.0x threshold that rating agencies generally consider the upper bound for investment-grade single-A territory. The 21.9% year-over-year increase in long-term debt from $12.2B to $14.9B was driven primarily by $3.0B in new senior notes issued to fund the Inari Medical acquisition. Debt-to-equity of 70.7% is manageable given the quality of the underlying cash flows.

The debt maturity profile is well-laddered. Stryker issued multiple tranches of senior unsecured notes with maturities extending from 2027 through 2036, avoiding any single-year concentration of refinancing risk. The company also expanded its revolving credit facility to $3.0B with a maturity date of 2030, which was undrawn as of FY2025 year-end. This structure means Stryker faces no debt wall — annual maturities are spread across the next decade at amounts the company can comfortably refinance or repay from operating cash flow. With $4.3B in annual FCF, even if credit markets were temporarily disrupted, Stryker could fund upcoming maturities from internal cash generation.

Interest coverage of 8.4x (operating income of $5.1B divided by interest expense of $607M) is solid, though notably lower than the 11.5x level implied by FY2024 figures ($4.7B / $409M). The compression reflects the $3.0B in new debt issued at prevailing 2025 interest rates, which are higher than the blended rate on legacy notes. Still, 8.4x coverage provides substantial cushion — interest coverage below 3.0x is generally considered concerning, and Stryker sits nearly three times that threshold. The fixed-rate nature of the senior unsecured notes (as is standard for investment-grade corporate issuers) provides insulation from further rate increases.

Liquidity is robust. The $4.1B cash balance combined with the $3.0B undrawn revolver provides $7.1B in total liquidity. The current ratio of 1.9x indicates the company can cover short-term obligations with ample room. Working capital management appears disciplined, with operating cash flow of $5.0B demonstrating that the business converts its receivables and inventory efficiently. There are no signs of channel stuffing or accounts receivable deterioration in the financial data.

Regarding off-balance-sheet items, Stryker's primary off-balance-sheet obligations include operating lease commitments (now largely on-balance-sheet under ASC 842), purchase obligations for inventory and manufacturing contracts, and standard contingent liabilities from product liability and patent litigation. The company does not use off-balance-sheet financing vehicles, synthetic leases, or unconsolidated variable interest entities that would materially alter the leverage picture. Pension obligations are modest given Stryker's workforce profile.

Stress testing the balance sheet: if revenue declined 30% for two consecutive years (from $25.1B to $17.6B and then $12.3B), operating margins would compress significantly but the business would likely remain profitable given its high gross margin structure (64% gross margins provide substantial absorption capacity). Even at 30% lower revenue in year one ($17.6B), applying a compressed 10% operating margin would yield $1.76B in operating income — still nearly 3x the current $607M interest expense. In a severe two-year downturn, Stryker could suspend dividends ($1.1B annually), reduce capex, and draw on the revolver, providing over $5B in cumulative liquidity headroom beyond debt service. The company would not need to raise equity. This is a business that would survive a severe recession without existential risk, though leverage ratios would temporarily exceed 4x EBITDA.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** With $7.1B in liquidity, well-laddered maturities through 2036, and FCF that covers interest 7x over, Stryker's balance sheet can absorb further M&A or weather significant downturns without equity issuance.
- **Bear case:** Total debt of $15.9B and rising interest expense ($607M, up 48% YoY) reflect the ongoing cost of the acquisition-driven growth model; another large deal before deleveraging could push leverage uncomfortably above 3x EBITDA.
- **Confidence:** High — Debt structure is well-documented in SEC filings, investment-grade rating provides external validation, and FCF trends are consistent and verifiable.

## Red Flags
- Interest expense increased 48% in one year ($409M to $607M), compressing interest coverage from ~11.5x to 8.4x
- Total debt rose 22% YoY to $15.9B; if management pursues another large acquisition before deleveraging, the balance sheet could become strained
- Goodwill and intangibles likely represent a large portion of total assets ($47.8B), meaning tangible book value is substantially lower than reported equity

## Score: 7 / 10
Stryker maintains a disciplined investment-grade balance sheet with 1.9x net debt/EBITDA, well-laddered maturities, and $7.1B in liquidity, but the acquisition-driven leverage increase to $15.9B in total debt and compressed interest coverage of 8.4x prevent a higher rating — this is conservative leverage, not fortress-like.
