# Compact Checklist — LIN

**Date:** 2026-03-17

1. **Business**: Linde separates air into industrial gases and pipes them to factories under 10-20 year take-or-pay contracts, generating 85% recurring revenue with a 94.3% retention rate — customers cannot self-supply without building their own multi-hundred-million-dollar plants.
2. **Moat**: A tight oligopoly (Linde 28-30%, Air Liquide 22-24%, Air Products 12-15%) reinforced by on-site pipeline infrastructure that physically locks in customers, route density cost advantages in merchant delivery, and billions in capital required just to enter the market — no meaningful new competitor has emerged in decades.
3. **Economics**: Operating margins expanded 780bps to 27.2% over three years on just 2% revenue growth, EBITDA reached $13.1B, and SBC dilution is a negligible 2.4% of net income, though ROIC on total capital (including $48B merger goodwill) is a solid but not exceptional 11.5%.
4. **Management**: CEO Lamba bought $999K in shares at near-highs, the company returned $7.4B to shareholders in FY25 while shrinking shares outstanding 12.5% since 2019, and compensation is 93% performance-based tied to return on capital — though the combined CEO/Chairman role since January 2026 removes a governance check.
5. **Debt risk**: With 20.2x interest coverage, 1.7x net Debt/EBITDA, A/A+ credit ratings, and $10.3B annual operating cash flow against well-laddered maturities, Linde could see operating income halved and still cover interest ten times over.
6. **Price**: At $497 (34x trailing, 25.5x forward), the stock requires 8% annual EPS compounding for five years and a 25-27x terminal multiple to justify today's price — achievable given the $10B backlog but leaving no room for disappointment.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is nearly indestructible (essential products, locked-in contracts, no technology disruption risk), but the stock trades 21% above conservative IV of $410 with a 1.3:1 downside-to-upside ratio — wrong price for new money.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if operating margins fall below 23% for two consecutive years, if Debt/EBITDA exceeds 3.5x without clear project justification, or if the $10B backlog conversion consistently delivers returns below cost of capital — any of these would signal the compounding engine has broken.
