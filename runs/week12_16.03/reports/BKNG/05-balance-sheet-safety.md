## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels.** Booking Holdings carries $19.3B in total debt ($16.9B long-term, ~$2.4B current) against $17.2B in cash and investments, resulting in net debt of only $1.5B. Gross Debt/EBITDA is 2.1x, which is moderate for a business of this quality. Net Debt/EBITDA is a negligible 0.2x. The debt is entirely fixed-rate senior unsecured notes — no floating rate exposure, no secured covenants constraining the business. Key maturities include 3.6% notes due June 2026, 1.8% notes due 2027, 4.625% notes due 2030, 3.0% EUR notes due 2030, 3.625% EUR notes due 2035, and additional tranches extending to 2046. The debt has been accumulated partly to fund the massive buyback program ($6.4B in FY2025 alone, $23.3B over FY2022-FY2025), which is why stockholders' equity is negative at -$5.6B. This is financial engineering, not economic distress — the underlying business generates $9.1B in FCF annually.

**Interest Coverage.** EBIT/interest coverage was 5.2x in FY2025, down from 6.6x in FY2024. The decline is driven by $300M of additional interest expense ($1.6B vs $1.3B) from recent debt issuances (including the November 2025 EUR notes). While 5.2x is above the 5x comfort threshold, the downward trend warrants monitoring. EBITDA/interest coverage is more comfortable at approximately 5.8x. Operating cash flow coverage of total debt is 50.8%, which is healthy. The risk is manageable because: (a) all debt is fixed-rate, so rising rates do not affect existing obligations; (b) the cash pile of $17.2B already covers nearly 11 years of interest payments; and (c) FCF of $9.1B dwarfs the $1.6B annual interest burden.

**Liquidity.** The liquidity position is fortress-grade. Cash and investments of $17.2B, current ratio of 1.3x, and working capital of $5.6B provide ample buffer. With annual operating expenses (excluding D&A and interest) of roughly $17.6B, the cash balance alone could fund approximately 12 months of total operating costs with zero revenue. Add in the natural cost flex (performance marketing, which represents a large portion of expenses, can be cut immediately in a downturn), and the survival runway extends to 18-24+ months with no revenue at all. The company also maintains access to undrawn credit facilities, though specific size is not disclosed in available data.

**Refinancing Risk.** Refinancing risk is low. The debt maturity schedule is well-laddered from 2026 through 2046, avoiding any single year with a dangerous concentration of maturities. Booking Holdings maintains investment-grade credit ratings, ensuring continued access to debt capital markets at favorable rates. The June 2026 maturity is the nearest significant refinancing event, but with $17.2B in cash, the company could simply pay it off without accessing capital markets. The November 2025 issuance of EUR-denominated notes (EUR 1.5B total) at 3.0% and 3.625% confirms the company's ability to raise capital at attractive rates across currencies.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Items.** Booking's off-balance-sheet obligations are modest relative to its scale. Operating lease liabilities exist for office space globally but are immaterial relative to the $9.1B FCF stream. There are no significant pension obligations, no material guarantees, and no unconsolidated variable interest entities. The negative equity is entirely a function of Treasury stock accumulated through buybacks, not hidden liabilities. The merchant model creates a float (collecting from travelers before paying hotels), which shows up as deferred merchant bookings on the balance sheet — this is a source of free financing, not a liability risk.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** If revenue dropped 30% (from $26.9B to ~$18.8B), this would approximate a scenario worse than COVID's impact on 2020 revenues ($6.8B, a 55% decline from 2019, though that was an extreme outlier). In a 30% decline scenario: (1) Performance marketing spend (~40% of revenue) would decline roughly proportionally, preserving margins; (2) Fixed costs could be reduced by $500M-$1B through the same playbook management used during COVID; (3) Operating income would likely fall to $4-5B range; (4) FCF would remain in the $4-5B range, still covering the $1.6B interest expense 2.5-3x; (5) The $17.2B cash balance provides an additional multi-year cushion. The company survived a 55% revenue decline in 2020 without issuing equity (it issued debt opportunistically), confirming resilience under severe stress. A 30% decline is well within the survivability envelope.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Net debt of $1.5B on $9.1B FCF, $17.2B cash, well-laddered fixed-rate maturities, and a cost structure that flexes naturally with revenue make this balance sheet essentially self-insuring against all but the most extreme scenarios.
- **Bear case:** Gross debt of $19.3B on negative equity means the balance sheet is technically leveraged; if management continues prioritizing buybacks over debt reduction and a severe downturn materializes simultaneously with a credit market freeze, refinancing could become expensive.
- **Confidence:** High — The combination of massive FCF, large cash balances, fixed-rate long-dated debt, and proven COVID-era resilience makes the downside scenario well-bounded.

## Red Flags

- Interest coverage trending down (6.6x to 5.2x) as debt load has grown to fund buybacks — if this trend continues below 5x, the margin of safety narrows.
- Negative stockholders' equity of -$5.6B means the company is technically insolvent on a book-value basis; while economically benign (driven by buybacks), it could become a constraint if credit agencies tighten standards.
- Gross debt grew 13.9% YoY to fund buybacks — management is using the balance sheet aggressively, which works in good times but reduces dry powder.

## Score: 8 / 10

Booking Holdings has a strong balance sheet with $17.2B cash, negligible net debt (0.2x EBITDA), well-laddered fixed-rate maturities through 2046, and $9.1B FCF providing massive debt-service capacity — docked from fortress status due to declining interest coverage, negative book equity, and an aggressive buyback-funded leverage trajectory that leaves less margin for error than a truly conservative balance sheet would.
