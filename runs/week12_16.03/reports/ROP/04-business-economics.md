## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital — The Goodwill Illusion.** Roper's reported ROIC of 6.4% (FY2025) appears pedestrian at first glance, but this figure is fundamentally misleading. The denominator — invested capital of $29.2B — is inflated by over $25B of goodwill and intangible assets accumulated through Roper's serial acquisition strategy. These represent the purchase premiums paid for businesses, not the operating assets required to generate cash flow. On a tangible capital basis, Roper's returns are extraordinary. The business requires roughly $105M of annual capex to sustain $7.9B in revenue and $2.4B in free cash flow — a return on tangible operating assets that exceeds 100%. The more relevant question for Roper is whether its acquisition-driven capital deployment earns adequate returns, and with EBITDA growing from $2.1B to $3.2B over three years while invested capital grew from $22.7B to $29.2B, the incremental ROIC on newly deployed capital runs in the 15-17% range — well above Roper's 8.2% WACC.

**Cash Generation Machine.** Roper's cash flow profile is among the strongest in the S&P 500. FCF of $2.4B on $7.9B revenue (31% FCF margin) with FCF conversion of 158% of net income in FY2025 is a hallmark of an asset-light, subscription-driven business model. The driver is straightforward: Roper's vertical market software businesses collect recurring subscription fees, require minimal physical infrastructure, and benefit from negative working capital (customers pay upfront, expenses are recognized over time). Working capital was -$1.8B in FY2025, meaning customers effectively finance Roper's operations. D&A of $898M significantly exceeds capex of $105M, reflecting the amortization of acquired intangibles rather than any genuine capital reinvestment need. Owner earnings of $2.3B confirm this — nearly all reported depreciation and amortization is non-cash noise from purchase accounting, not a real economic cost.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** Gross margins have held rock-steady at ~69% for four years, reflecting the inherent economics of software (near-zero marginal cost of serving incremental users) combined with Roper's focus on mission-critical vertical applications where customers have minimal pricing sensitivity. Operating margins at 28% have similarly been stable, though there is arguably room for expansion as newer acquisitions (CentralReach, Procare Solutions, Transact Campus) mature and achieve scale within Roper's decentralized structure. EBITDA margins of 40% in FY2025 underscore the underlying profitability. The stability rather than expansion of operating margins reflects Roper's conscious choice to reinvest in R&D and sales capacity within portfolio companies rather than harvest margins — a sound long-term approach for a compounder.

**Revenue Quality and Durability.** This is where Roper truly excels. Approximately 75% of revenue comes from software, with ~80% of that being recurring (subscriptions, term licenses, maintenance). Net revenue retention rates of 105-115% across most portfolio companies mean Roper grows organically even without adding a single new customer. The three segments — Application Software ($1.16B Q4 revenue), Network Software ($426M Q4), and Technology Enabled Products ($474M Q4) — all serve deeply embedded vertical niches: healthcare administration, legal practice management, government, education, freight matching, food industry compliance. These are markets where switching costs are astronomical (replacing an ERP-like system that runs your daily operations is a multi-year project) and Roper is typically the dominant or sole provider. This creates revenue that behaves almost like a toll — predictable, inflation-linked, and growing.

**Capital Intensity and Allocation.** Roper is one of the least capital-intensive large-cap businesses in existence. Capex of $105M on $7.9B in revenue (1.3%) means the organic business requires almost nothing to sustain. The real capital allocation question is whether management deploys the $2.4B of annual FCF wisely through acquisitions. Roper deployed $3.6B in acquisitions in 2024 and acquired CentralReach for $1.65B in early 2025, funded partly by new debt issuance ($2B across three tranches). The company also initiated buybacks ($500M in FY2025) for the first time, suggesting management sees the stock as undervalued. Stock-based compensation of $166M (FY2025) is manageable at ~7% of FCF, though rising modestly year-over-year.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper is a toll-bridge business generating $2.4B in FCF with 80% recurring revenue, near-zero capex needs, and a long runway to compound through acquisitions at returns well above WACC.
- **Bear case:** Reported ROIC remains low because the acquisition strategy demands ever-larger deals at higher multiples, and organic growth (~7%) alone would not justify the current enterprise value without continued M&A execution.
- **Confidence:** High — The financial data is unambiguous: Roper's cash economics are exceptional and the recurring revenue base provides unusual visibility into future cash flows.

## Red Flags
- SBC rising steadily ($118M to $166M over three years) — still manageable but worth monitoring as a percentage of FCF
- Organic growth of ~7% is decent but not exceptional for a company trading at 14.6x EV/EBITDA; the premium valuation assumes continued successful M&A
- FY2022 net income of $4.5B was inflated by a large gain (likely the TransDigm/CIVCO divestiture), distorting year-over-year comparisons
- Net income flat at $1.5B in FY2024 and FY2025 despite 13% revenue growth, suggesting higher interest expense and acquisition-related costs are eating into bottom-line growth

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper's business economics are genuinely excellent — 69% gross margins, 31% FCF margins, 158% FCF conversion, 80% recurring revenue, and near-zero capital intensity. The reported ROIC of 6% understates the true economics due to acquisition goodwill, with incremental returns on deployed capital running 15-17%. The only reason this is not a 9 is that the acquisition-dependent growth model introduces execution risk and the rising interest expense from deal-financing is compressing net margins.
