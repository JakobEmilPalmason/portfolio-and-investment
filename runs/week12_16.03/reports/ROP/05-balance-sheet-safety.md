## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Roper carries $9.3B in total debt, up from $6.4B just two years ago. This $2.9B increase was primarily driven by acquisition financing — Roper deployed $3.6B on acquisitions in 2024 (including Procare Solutions and Transact Campus) and $1.65B for CentralReach in early 2025. To fund these deals, Roper issued $2B in new senior unsecured notes in 2025 across three tranches: $500M at 4.25% due 2028, $500M at 4.45% due 2030, and $1B at 5.10% due 2035. These proceeds retired near-term maturities (notes due September and December 2025) and repaid revolver borrowings. The remaining debt stack includes older fixed-rate notes at varying maturities. The $3.5B unsecured revolving credit facility had $710M drawn as of Q3 2025, leaving roughly $2.8B of available capacity. All debt is unsecured and investment-grade rated, which is important — Roper has no secured claims ahead of bondholders, and no restrictive covenants that would impair operational flexibility.

**Interest Coverage and Debt Service.** This is where the trend demands attention. Interest coverage has declined from 11.6x in FY2023 to 8.6x in FY2024 to 7.0x in FY2025. The driver is straightforward: interest expense nearly doubled from $165M to $325M as Roper loaded up on acquisition debt. At 7.0x, coverage is still adequate — most credit analysts consider 3-4x the danger zone — but the direction of travel is negative. On an EBITDA basis, Debt/EBITDA has risen from 2.4x (FY2023) to 3.0x (FY2025). Again, manageable for a business with $2.4B in FCF and 80% recurring revenue, but above the 2-2.5x level that would be considered conservative. The saving grace is that Roper's FCF is remarkably stable and predictable — subscription software does not have cyclical revenue swings — which supports a higher leverage ratio than a typical industrial business could safely carry.

**Liquidity Position.** Cash on hand of $297M is modest relative to the debt load, and the current ratio of 0.5x appears dangerously low. However, this metric is misleading for Roper's business model. Current liabilities of $3.7B include significant deferred revenue (prepaid subscriptions that will be recognized as revenue over the next 12 months) — this is not an obligation requiring cash outflow. The real liquidity picture is: $297M cash + $2.8B available on the revolver = ~$3.1B of immediately accessible liquidity, against $2.4B in annual FCF generation. There is no near-term liquidity crisis. The 2025 note issuance pushed out the next significant debt maturity to 2028, giving Roper a comfortable runway.

**Refinancing Risk.** Roper's primary financial risk is refinancing. With $9.3B in total debt and $8.6B in long-term obligations, the company will need to periodically roll maturities. The 2025 issuance at 4.25-5.10% represents a meaningful step-up from older, lower-rate notes, and if interest rates remain elevated, future refinancings will further pressure interest expense. At current rates, a full refinancing of the debt stack at ~4.5% average would imply ~$420M in annual interest expense, up from $325M today. This would compress interest coverage to ~5.5x — still safe but leaving less margin for error. The mitigant is that Roper's $2.4B in annual FCF gives it the option to actively delever rather than roll all maturities.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** Revenue drops from $7.9B to $5.5B. Assuming operating margins compress to 20% (from 28%) due to fixed costs in software businesses, operating income would be ~$1.1B. After $325M interest and ~$200M taxes, net income would be ~$575M. FCF would likely be $800M-$1.0B (lower earnings but still minimal capex). Debt/EBITDA would spike to ~5.5-6.0x, and interest coverage would fall to ~3.4x. This would be uncomfortable and would likely trigger a credit downgrade, but Roper would survive. The subscription revenue model provides a floor — a 30% decline in subscription software is historically unprecedented, as these businesses saw only 5-10% declines even in 2008-2009. A more realistic stress scenario of a 15% decline would be easily absorbed. The $2.8B of revolver availability provides an additional buffer.

**Tangible Book Value and Goodwill.** Stockholders' equity of $19.9B masks the fact that tangible book value is deeply negative. Goodwill and intangible assets exceed $25B, meaning that if acquisitions were impaired, equity would evaporate. This is the structural trade-off of Roper's acquisition model — the balance sheet is an accumulation of purchase premiums, not hard assets. For a going-concern analysis this matters less (the cash flows justify the goodwill), but in a forced liquidation scenario, recovery values would be poor. This is common among serial acquirers (Danaher, Constellation Software) and is accepted by the market as long as the underlying businesses continue performing.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper's $2.4B in predictable, recurring FCF comfortably services $9.3B in well-laddered, investment-grade debt, and the company can actively delever if it pauses acquisitions for even one year.
- **Bear case:** Debt has risen 45% in two years, interest coverage is trending in the wrong direction, and continued large acquisitions funded by debt could push leverage to levels that constrain financial flexibility.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The current balance sheet is clearly serviceable, but the trajectory of rising leverage introduces uncertainty about where the debt ceiling lies if M&A continues at the current pace.

## Red Flags
- Interest coverage declined from 11.6x to 7.0x in just two years — a rapid deterioration even if the absolute level is still adequate
- Debt/EBITDA of 3.0x is at the upper end of what is comfortable for an acquisition-driven model; another large debt-funded deal could push this toward 3.5x+
- Net debt of $9.0B against only $297M in cash means Roper is operating with minimal cash reserves, relying on the revolver for liquidity
- Tangible book value is deeply negative — the entire equity base is goodwill and intangibles from acquisitions
- The $500M in buybacks (FY2025) while simultaneously adding $2B+ in debt raises questions about capital allocation discipline
- Current ratio of 0.5x, while structurally explainable, leaves zero margin for unexpected cash demands

## Score: 6 / 10
Roper's balance sheet is adequate but not strong. The $9.3B debt load is serviceable given $2.4B in annual FCF and 80% recurring revenue, and the well-laddered maturity profile with no near-term walls provides comfort. However, leverage has risen materially over two years (Debt/EBITDA from 2.4x to 3.0x, interest coverage from 11.6x to 7.0x), and the trend is clearly negative. This is a business that could comfortably carry this debt if management paused acquisitions and delevered — but the strategy calls for continued M&A, which means leverage is unlikely to decline. A score of 6 reflects "moderate leverage, manageable but not a fortress."
