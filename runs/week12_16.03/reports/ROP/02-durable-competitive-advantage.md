## Detailed Analysis

Roper's moat is built on a single dominant mechanism: switching costs that are economic and operational, not merely technical. When a law firm runs its entire practice — time tracking, billing, matter management, compliance — on Aderant, the cost of switching to a competitor is not the license fee differential. It is the 6-12 months of disrupted operations, the retraining of every attorney and paralegal, the migration of decades of client data, and the risk of compliance failures during transition. The same logic applies across the portfolio: Deltek for government contractors, Sunquest for hospital laboratories, Vertafore for insurance agencies. In each case, the software is not a tool the customer uses — it is the operating system of the customer's business.

The numerical evidence supports the moat thesis convincingly. Gross retention above 95% means fewer than 5% of customers leave in any given year, and those departures are overwhelmingly due to customer business failures (bankruptcy, acquisition) rather than competitive losses. Net revenue retention of 105-120% means the installed base is growing its spending even without new customer acquisition. Gross margins at a stable 69% for four years confirm that Roper is not buying revenue through discounting or facing pricing pressure from competitors. Operating margins at 28% and EBITDA margins at 40% in the Application Software segment further demonstrate that the moat translates into sustained economic profit.

A secondary moat exists in two portfolio companies with network effects. DAT Solutions operates the largest digital freight marketplace in North America — the more carriers and shippers that use it, the more valuable it becomes to all participants. ConstructConnect aggregates construction project data and bid information, creating a similar network dynamic. These businesses benefit from a virtuous cycle that new entrants cannot easily replicate.

The weakest point of the moat is the potential for AI disruption. Generative AI tools are emerging that target specific workflows within Roper's verticals. Harvey AI for legal work, various insurtech AI platforms, and AI-powered medical diagnostics represent credible threats to individual portfolio companies. The bear case is that AI-native competitors could build purpose-built tools that are 10x better at specific tasks, gradually unbundling the integrated software suites that Roper's switching costs protect. RBC downgraded Roper in early 2026 specifically on AI uncertainty. However, several factors limit this risk in the near term: (1) Roper's software holds the compliance and regulatory data that AI tools need to access, making it more likely to be the platform AI plugs into rather than the thing AI replaces; (2) customers in regulated industries (legal, healthcare, government contracting) are slow to adopt unproven technology; and (3) Roper itself is integrating AI features across its portfolio, which could expand rather than erode its moat.

The moat does not meaningfully reset each cycle. These are not cyclical businesses where market share is re-contested annually. Once a hospital installs Sunquest or an insurance agency deploys Vertafore, the switching decision is revisited perhaps once a decade — and usually only when the current vendor fails catastrophically. The durability of these advantages over a 10-year horizon is strong, with the caveat that AI represents a genuine wild card that bears monitoring through net revenue retention trends.

Roper's portfolio approach also creates a meta-moat: diversification across 28 niche markets means that even if one or two portfolio companies face competitive disruption, the overall business remains resilient. No single subsidiary represents more than 15% of total revenue.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A portfolio of 28 businesses with 95%+ retention, 69% gross margins, and system-of-record status in regulated verticals constitutes one of the widest and most durable moats among technology-adjacent companies.
- **Bear case:** AI-native competitors could gradually unbundle Roper's integrated vertical software suites, eroding switching costs one workflow at a time — and the holding company structure may be too slow to respond across 28 fronts simultaneously.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat is empirically measurable through retention and margin data, though AI risk introduces genuine medium-term uncertainty that warrants ongoing monitoring.

## Red Flags
- AI-native startups are specifically targeting several of Roper's verticals (legal, insurance, healthcare) with purpose-built tools
- RBC downgraded ROP in early 2026 citing AI disruption uncertainty
- Technology Enabled Products segment (~22% of revenue) has weaker switching costs than the software segments — hardware products face more traditional competitive dynamics
- Decentralized model means AI response strategy may be inconsistent across portfolio companies
- Rising debt levels (Debt/EBITDA from 2.4x to 3.0x in two years) could constrain the M&A engine that feeds the moat's expansion

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper possesses a wide, multi-layered moat built on deep switching costs, network effects in select businesses, and niche market dominance — docked for the emerging AI disruption risk that, while not imminent, represents the most credible long-term threat to the switching cost architecture.
