## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Roper owns ~28 niche vertical software businesses (legal, healthcare, freight, government) that serve as the operational backbone of their customers' daily workflows, producing 80%+ recurring revenue with 95%+ gross retention.
2. **Moat**: Switching costs are enormous because replacing system-of-record software (court management, healthcare compliance, freight logistics) requires 6-12 months of disruption, data migration, and retraining — confirmed by 95%+ retention and stable 69% gross margins for four consecutive years.
3. **Economics**: FCF of $2.4B on $7.9B revenue (31% margin) with only $105M capex (1.3% of sales) and 158% FCF-to-net-income conversion makes this one of the most capital-efficient large-cap businesses in the S&P 500.
4. **Management**: A 20-year track record of deploying ~$25B in acquisitions that transformed a cyclical industrial into an 80% recurring-revenue software portfolio, with 96% at-risk CEO compensation, though insider ownership at ~0.4-3% is disappointingly low.
5. **Debt risk**: Debt/EBITDA of 3.0x with $9.3B total debt is serviceable given $2.4B annual FCF and well-laddered maturities, but interest coverage has deteriorated from 11.6x to 7.0x in two years due to acquisition-funded borrowing.
6. **Price**: At $354 (15x forward earnings vs 5-year average of 32x), the market is pricing in permanent 2-3% organic growth and zero accretive M&A — a structural impairment thesis that contradicts the 80% recurring revenue base and $6B deployment capacity.
7. **Margin of safety**: The stock trades 14% below the quant bear-case IV of $411 and below every cell in the 25-scenario sensitivity grid (floor $583), with a 4:1 upside/downside ratio and near-zero permanent capital loss risk across 28 diversified software businesses.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if net revenue retention drops below 100% for two consecutive years, signaling that AI-native competitors are successfully unbundling Roper's vertical software moats and eroding the switching-cost architecture that underpins the entire thesis.

## Key Risks

1. **AI disruption of vertical software moats.** AI-native startups (Harvey for legal, insurtech platforms, AI diagnostics) are targeting several of Roper's verticals. If horizontal AI platforms replicate niche functionality, the switching-cost architecture erodes. RBC downgraded ROP in early 2026 specifically on this concern.

2. **Rising leverage and declining interest coverage.** Debt has grown from $6.4B to $9.3B in two years, pushing Debt/EBITDA from 2.4x to 3.0x and compressing interest coverage from 11.6x to 7.0x. Further acquisition-funded borrowing could constrain financial flexibility.

3. **Concurrent segment headwinds.** Deltek (government contracting slowdown/DOGE risk), Neptune (water meter technology transition), and DAT (freight market weakness) face simultaneous cyclical pressure. Management excluded recovery from 2026 guidance.

4. **Acquisition execution risk at scale.** With $6B+ deployment capacity and $3.3B deployed in 2025 alone, a single poorly executed large deal could impair 15-20% of enterprise value. The bigger Roper gets, the harder it is to find needle-moving targets without overpaying.

5. **Low insider ownership.** At ~0.4-3%, management's personal downside exposure is limited, reducing the "eating your own cooking" alignment preferred in a Buffett-style framework.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - Organic growth recovers above 7% as Deltek/Neptune/DAT headwinds fade, confirming the deceleration is cyclical rather than structural.
  - A well-executed acquisition at reasonable multiples deploys $2B+ of the $6B capacity, demonstrating continued capital allocation discipline.
  - Net revenue retention holds above 105% for two consecutive quarters, disproving the AI disruption narrative.

- **Sell triggers**:
  - Net revenue retention drops below 100% for two consecutive years, signaling AI-native competitors are successfully unbundling vertical software moats.
  - Organic growth turns negative for two consecutive years, indicating structural impairment rather than cyclical headwinds.
  - Debt/EBITDA exceeds 4.0x with no credible deleveraging plan, or a single failed acquisition destroys capital allocation credibility.

# Circle of Competence — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release and call transcript, Yahoo Finance financials, ropertech.com, Seeking Alpha, MatrixBCG, PitchGrade Research, quant-valuation model output

## Detailed Analysis

Roper Technologies is a serial acquirer and operator of vertical market software businesses. The two-minute explanation: Roper buys small-to-midsize software companies that make mission-critical tools for specific industries — practice management for law firms (Aderant), cost accounting for government contractors (Deltek), freight matching for truckers (DAT), laboratory information systems for hospitals (Sunquest), and similar niche applications. Once acquired, these businesses run with high autonomy under a decentralized model. Roper provides capital allocation at the top and leaves operations to the subsidiary management teams.

The economic engine is straightforward. Each portfolio company sells software that becomes the operational backbone of its customers' daily workflows. Because the software manages core processes — billing, compliance, logistics, patient records — customers almost never leave. That stickiness produces recurring revenue north of 80% of the total, with gross retention rates above 95%. Revenue grows organically through price increases, upselling additional modules, and natural expansion as customers grow. On top of that, Roper deploys its prodigious free cash flow into new acquisitions, compounding the revenue base.

The key drivers are few and identifiable: (1) organic revenue growth rate across the software portfolio (guided at 5-6% for 2026), (2) acquisition volume and quality (measured by management's proprietary cash return on investment metric), and (3) retention rates, which serve as the leading indicator of moat health. If net revenue retention starts declining below 100%, that would signal competitive erosion.

Revenue predictability is among the best in the industrial/software space. The subscription model, combined with multi-year contracts in many verticals and the mission-critical nature of the software, means revenue visibility extends 12-18 months with reasonable confidence. There is no meaningful cyclicality in the software segments, though the Technology Enabled Products segment (Neptune water meters, medical imaging) has some project-based lumpiness. Customer concentration is minimal — the portfolio is diversified across dozens of end markets with no single customer representing a material share.

The business is genuinely understandable. It is a holding company for sticky software businesses in boring niches. The complexity comes not from any single business unit but from the sheer number of them — roughly 28 operating companies. An investor does not need to understand each subsidiary in detail but does need to trust that management's acquisition process and decentralized operating model continue to function. The core thesis is simple: buy mission-critical niche software, retain customers at 95%+, compound free cash flow, redeploy into more acquisitions.

One nuance worth noting: the reported ROIC of ~6% looks unimpressive, but this is an artifact of the massive goodwill and intangibles on the balance sheet from serial acquisitions. Management's preferred metric — cash return on investment — strips out acquired goodwill and shows much higher incremental returns on deployed capital. This is a common feature of serial acquirers (Danaher has the same dynamic) and does not indicate poor capital efficiency.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A diversified portfolio of mission-critical vertical software businesses with 80%+ recurring revenue, 95%+ retention, and 31% FCF margins is among the most predictable and understandable business models in the market.
- **Bear case:** The holding company structure means you are trusting management's acquisition judgment across 28 businesses you cannot individually track — if the M&A engine stalls or overpays, the thesis breaks.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is well-documented, the revenue streams are visible, and the key drivers are few and measurable.

## Red Flags
- Reported ROIC of ~6% can mislead investors who do not adjust for acquisition-driven goodwill inflation
- Technology Enabled Products segment (22% of revenue) is less sticky and more cyclical than the software segments
- Organic growth of 5-6% is solid but not exceptional — the compounding story depends heavily on continued M&A execution
- 28 operating companies means limited transparency into any single business unit's performance

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper's business model is highly understandable — a capital-light holding company for sticky vertical software — with few key drivers and exceptional revenue predictability, docked only for the opacity inherent in managing 28 subsidiaries across diverse niches.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings call, Seeking Alpha (moat analyses), PitchGrade Research, MatrixBCG competitive landscape, BeyondSPX, quant-valuation model output, Yahoo Finance

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Switching costs are the primary moat — Roper's software functions as system-of-record infrastructure in verticals where migration cost (data, retraining, compliance risk) dwarfs annual license fees | 5 |
| 2 | Gross retention rates of 95%+ and net revenue retention of 105-120% provide hard numerical evidence that the moat is functioning | 5 |
| 3 | Gross margins of ~69% have been rock-stable for four consecutive years, indicating sustained pricing power with no competitive margin erosion | 4 |
| 4 | AI-native startups (Harvey for legal, insurtech platforms) represent a credible but not yet existential threat to specific portfolio companies | 4 |
| 5 | Network effects in DAT (freight matching) and ConstructConnect (construction bid network) create a second moat layer in those businesses | 3 |
| 6 | Niche market leadership in fragmented verticals means Roper rarely faces a well-capitalized direct competitor in any single market | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Roper's moat is built on a single dominant mechanism: switching costs that are economic and operational, not merely technical. When a law firm runs its entire practice — time tracking, billing, matter management, compliance — on Aderant, the cost of switching to a competitor is not the license fee differential. It is the 6-12 months of disrupted operations, the retraining of every attorney and paralegal, the migration of decades of client data, and the risk of compliance failures during transition. The same logic applies across the portfolio: Deltek for government contractors, Sunquest for hospital laboratories, Vertafore for insurance agencies. In each case, the software is not a tool the customer uses — it is the operating system of the customer's business.

The numerical evidence supports the moat thesis convincingly. Gross retention above 95% means fewer than 5% of customers leave in any given year, and those departures are overwhelmingly due to customer business failures (bankruptcy, acquisition) rather than competitive losses. Net revenue retention of 105-120% means the installed base is growing its spending even without new customer acquisition. Gross margins at a stable 69% for four years confirm that Roper is not buying revenue through discounting or facing pricing pressure from competitors. Operating margins at 28% and EBITDA margins at 40% in the Application Software segment further demonstrate that the moat translates into sustained economic profit.

A secondary moat exists in two portfolio companies with network effects. DAT Solutions operates the largest digital freight marketplace in North America — the more carriers and shippers that use it, the more valuable it becomes to all participants. ConstructConnect aggregates construction project data and bid information, creating a similar network dynamic. These businesses benefit from a virtuous cycle that new entrants cannot easily replicate.

The weakest point of the moat is the potential for AI disruption. Generative AI tools are emerging that target specific workflows within Roper's verticals. Harvey AI for legal work, various insurtech AI platforms, and AI-powered medical diagnostics represent credible threats to individual portfolio companies. The bear case is that AI-native competitors could build purpose-built tools that are 10x better at specific tasks, gradually unbundling the integrated software suites that Roper's switching costs protect. RBC downgraded Roper in early 2026 specifically on AI uncertainty. However, several factors limit this risk in the near term: (1) Roper's software holds the compliance and regulatory data that AI tools need to access, making it more likely to be the platform AI plugs into rather than the thing AI replaces; (2) customers in regulated industries (legal, healthcare, government contracting) are slow to adopt unproven technology; and (3) Roper itself is integrating AI features across its portfolio, which could expand rather than erode its moat.

The moat does not meaningfully reset each cycle. These are not cyclical businesses where market share is re-contested annually. Once a hospital installs Sunquest or an insurance agency deploys Vertafore, the switching decision is revisited perhaps once a decade — and usually only when the current vendor fails catastrophically. The durability of these advantages over a 10-year horizon is strong, with the caveat that AI represents a genuine wild card that bears monitoring through net revenue retention trends.

Roper's portfolio approach also creates a meta-moat: diversification across 28 niche markets means that even if one or two portfolio companies face competitive disruption, the overall business remains resilient. No single subsidiary represents more than 15% of total revenue.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A portfolio of 28 businesses with 95%+ retention, 69% gross margins, and system-of-record status in regulated verticals constitutes one of the widest and most durable moats among technology-adjacent companies.
- **Bear case:** AI-native competitors could gradually unbundle Roper's integrated vertical software suites, eroding switching costs one workflow at a time — and the holding company structure may be too slow to respond across 28 fronts simultaneously.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat is empirically measurable through retention and margin data, though AI risk introduces genuine medium-term uncertainty that warrants ongoing monitoring.

## Red Flags
- AI-native startups are specifically targeting several of Roper's verticals (legal, insurance, healthcare) with purpose-built tools
- RBC downgraded ROP in early 2026 citing AI disruption uncertainty
- Technology Enabled Products segment (~22% of revenue) has weaker switching costs than the software segments — hardware products face more traditional competitive dynamics
- Decentralized model means AI response strategy may be inconsistent across portfolio companies
- Rising debt levels (Debt/EBITDA from 2.4x to 3.0x in two years) could constrain the M&A engine that feeds the moat's expansion

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper possesses a wide, multi-layered moat built on deep switching costs, network effects in select businesses, and niche market dominance — docked for the emerging AI disruption risk that, while not imminent, represents the most credible long-term threat to the switching cost architecture.

# Management & Capital Allocation — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings call transcript, 2024 DEF 14A proxy statement, ropertech.com executive team page, Salary.com executive compensation data, Yahoo Finance insider holdings, WallStreetZen ownership data, MacroTrends share count history, Seeking Alpha

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Roper has deployed ~$25B in acquisitions since 2002, transforming from a cyclical industrial company into an 80% recurring-revenue software portfolio — one of the most successful strategic pivots in industrial history | 5 |
| 2 | CEO Neil Hunn's total compensation of $23.7M is heavily performance-weighted (96% at-risk), with PRSUs that are 100% performance-based and 3-year vesting on all equity | 4 |
| 3 | Insider ownership is low at ~0.4-3% depending on measurement, typical for a $38B company but not the owner-operator alignment an investor prefers | 4 |
| 4 | First-ever share buyback program ($3B authorized) launched in late 2025 with $500M deployed, signaling management believes the stock is undervalued and that the M&A pipeline alone cannot absorb all FCF | 3 |
| 5 | Debt has risen from $6.4B to $9.3B in two years (Debt/EBITDA from 2.4x to 3.0x) to fund acquisitions — still manageable but trending in the wrong direction | 4 |
| 6 | Management guides conservatively and communicates clearly — 2026 guidance of 5-6% organic growth and $21.30-$21.55 adjusted DEPS is a narrow, credible range | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Roper's capital allocation story over the past two decades is genuinely exceptional. Under former CEO Brian Jellison (who passed away in 2018) and current CEO Neil Hunn, the company executed a deliberate transformation from a collection of cyclical industrial businesses into a portfolio of high-margin, recurring-revenue vertical software companies. The strategy was to acquire mission-critical niche software businesses at reasonable multiples, retain the existing management teams under a decentralized model, and redeploy the growing free cash flow into more acquisitions. This flywheel has taken revenue from ~$2B to ~$8B and free cash flow from negligible to $2.4B annually. A $100 investment in Roper at the end of 2001 was worth $1,159 by the end of 2017, versus $322 for the S&P 500. The track record speaks for itself.

Neil Hunn took over as CEO in 2018 after joining Roper in 2011 as Group VP of the medical segment. He has continued the Jellison playbook faithfully. In 2025 alone, Roper deployed $3.3B toward acquisitions including CentralReach (behavioral health software) and Subsplash (church management software). These are classic Roper targets: niche verticals with mission-critical workflows, high recurring revenue, and limited competition. The acquisition cadence has not slowed, and management has indicated $6B+ of capacity for M&A and buybacks in 2026. The discipline question is whether management can maintain acquisition quality at this scale — the bigger the company gets, the harder it is to find targets that move the needle without overpaying.

Compensation structure is well-designed for alignment. CEO compensation of $23.7M is high in absolute terms but reasonable for a $38B market cap company. The critical detail is the structure: 96% of target direct compensation is at-risk/performance-based. Performance-based restricted stock units (PRSUs) are 100% tied to performance metrics with no pure time-based vesting. All equity has 3-year vesting. The Say-on-Pay vote exceeded 89% approval in 2024, indicating institutional investors are satisfied with the pay-for-performance linkage. This is a well-calibrated compensation plan that rewards long-term value creation.

The insider ownership picture is the weakest element of the stewardship analysis. At roughly 0.4-3% insider ownership (depending on whether you measure officers alone or include directors), management does not have meaningful personal wealth at stake relative to the company's size. This is common for large-cap companies but stands in contrast to true owner-operators. The board has a 10x cash retainer stock ownership guideline for directors ($600,000 minimum), and all directors are in compliance. Hunn owns shares but his stake is not large enough to create the kind of "eating your own cooking" dynamic that Buffett-style investors prefer.

The introduction of the first-ever buyback program in late 2025 is a notable capital allocation evolution. Roper had historically deployed all excess capital into acquisitions, paying only a modest dividend (1% yield). The $3B buyback authorization, with $500M already executed, signals either that management sees the stock as significantly undervalued at current prices or that the M&A pipeline cannot fully absorb the cash generation. Given that the stock is trading at 14% of its 52-week range and 40% below its highs, the buyback timing appears shrewd. However, this also raises a question: is the M&A opportunity set narrowing, or is this simply opportunistic capital return?

The debt trajectory warrants monitoring. Total debt has grown from $6.4B in FY2023 to $9.3B in FY2025, pushing Debt/EBITDA from 2.4x to 3.0x. Interest coverage has declined from 11.6x to 7.0x over the same period. These levels are still comfortable for a business generating $2.4B in free cash flow, but the trend is moving in the wrong direction. If acquisition multiples in vertical software remain elevated and Roper continues to debt-fund deals, leverage could become a constraint on future capital deployment flexibility.

Management communication style is straightforward and consistent. Earnings calls are crisp, guidance ranges are narrow, and management does not overpromise. The 2026 guide of 5-6% organic growth and ~8% total growth is deliberately conservative — management noted they are "not assuming market recoveries" in the outlook. This is the language of operators, not promoters.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A two-decade track record of transformative capital allocation, disciplined M&A in sticky niches, well-structured performance-based compensation, and a new buyback program deployed at favorable prices make this one of the strongest stewardship stories in industrial-tech.
- **Bear case:** Low insider ownership means management's personal downside is limited, rising debt to fund acquisitions at potentially peak software multiples could impair returns, and the M&A engine must continuously find high-quality targets at scale to justify the premium.
- **Confidence:** High — the capital allocation track record is empirically verifiable and the compensation structure is well-aligned, though insider ownership is a genuine gap in the stewardship profile.

## Red Flags
- Insider ownership of ~0.4-3% is low for a Buffett-style stewardship assessment
- Debt/EBITDA has risen from 2.4x to 3.0x in two years, with interest coverage dropping from 11.6x to 7.0x
- Current ratio of 0.5x and negative working capital indicate reliance on continuous cash generation to service near-term obligations
- $23.7M CEO compensation, while performance-weighted, is high in absolute terms
- The first-ever buyback could signal that the M&A opportunity set is becoming constrained at current scale
- Forward EPS guidance of $21.30-$21.55 is below trailing EPS of $14.18 on an adjusted vs. GAAP basis — investors must track which metric management is optimizing

## Score: 7 / 10
Roper's capital allocation track record over two decades is genuinely outstanding, and compensation is well-structured for performance alignment, but low insider ownership, rising leverage, and the inherent question of whether M&A quality can be maintained at $3B+ annual deployment prevent a top-tier stewardship score.

# Business Economics — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies FY2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release, Yahoo Finance (yfinance) financial data, quant valuation model output, company investor presentations, SEC filings

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ~75% of revenue is software with ~80% recurring (subscription/term license), creating an exceptionally predictable revenue base with 105-115% net revenue retention | 5 |
| 2 | Reported ROIC of ~6% is distorted by $25B+ of acquisition goodwill/intangibles; cash-on-cash returns on incremental capital and organic ROIC are dramatically higher | 5 |
| 3 | FCF conversion averaged 148% of net income over FY2023-2025, driven by an asset-light model requiring only ~$105M capex on $7.9B revenue (1.3% of sales) | 5 |
| 4 | Gross margins stable at ~69% for four consecutive years, with operating margins locked in at 28%, demonstrating pricing power and disciplined cost management | 4 |
| 5 | Revenue grew from $5.4B (FY2022) to $7.9B (FY2025) — a 46% increase over three years — with both organic growth (~7% in recent quarters) and acquisitions contributing | 4 |
| 6 | Owner earnings of $2.3B (FY2025) with maintenance capex at 100% of total capex ($105M) confirm virtually zero growth capex requirement from the existing business base | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital — The Goodwill Illusion.** Roper's reported ROIC of 6.4% (FY2025) appears pedestrian at first glance, but this figure is fundamentally misleading. The denominator — invested capital of $29.2B — is inflated by over $25B of goodwill and intangible assets accumulated through Roper's serial acquisition strategy. These represent the purchase premiums paid for businesses, not the operating assets required to generate cash flow. On a tangible capital basis, Roper's returns are extraordinary. The business requires roughly $105M of annual capex to sustain $7.9B in revenue and $2.4B in free cash flow — a return on tangible operating assets that exceeds 100%. The more relevant question for Roper is whether its acquisition-driven capital deployment earns adequate returns, and with EBITDA growing from $2.1B to $3.2B over three years while invested capital grew from $22.7B to $29.2B, the incremental ROIC on newly deployed capital runs in the 15-17% range — well above Roper's 8.2% WACC.

**Cash Generation Machine.** Roper's cash flow profile is among the strongest in the S&P 500. FCF of $2.4B on $7.9B revenue (31% FCF margin) with FCF conversion of 158% of net income in FY2025 is a hallmark of an asset-light, subscription-driven business model. The driver is straightforward: Roper's vertical market software businesses collect recurring subscription fees, require minimal physical infrastructure, and benefit from negative working capital (customers pay upfront, expenses are recognized over time). Working capital was -$1.8B in FY2025, meaning customers effectively finance Roper's operations. D&A of $898M significantly exceeds capex of $105M, reflecting the amortization of acquired intangibles rather than any genuine capital reinvestment need. Owner earnings of $2.3B confirm this — nearly all reported depreciation and amortization is non-cash noise from purchase accounting, not a real economic cost.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** Gross margins have held rock-steady at ~69% for four years, reflecting the inherent economics of software (near-zero marginal cost of serving incremental users) combined with Roper's focus on mission-critical vertical applications where customers have minimal pricing sensitivity. Operating margins at 28% have similarly been stable, though there is arguably room for expansion as newer acquisitions (CentralReach, Procare Solutions, Transact Campus) mature and achieve scale within Roper's decentralized structure. EBITDA margins of 40% in FY2025 underscore the underlying profitability. The stability rather than expansion of operating margins reflects Roper's conscious choice to reinvest in R&D and sales capacity within portfolio companies rather than harvest margins — a sound long-term approach for a compounder.

**Revenue Quality and Durability.** This is where Roper truly excels. Approximately 75% of revenue comes from software, with ~80% of that being recurring (subscriptions, term licenses, maintenance). Net revenue retention rates of 105-115% across most portfolio companies mean Roper grows organically even without adding a single new customer. The three segments — Application Software ($1.16B Q4 revenue), Network Software ($426M Q4), and Technology Enabled Products ($474M Q4) — all serve deeply embedded vertical niches: healthcare administration, legal practice management, government, education, freight matching, food industry compliance. These are markets where switching costs are astronomical (replacing an ERP-like system that runs your daily operations is a multi-year project) and Roper is typically the dominant or sole provider. This creates revenue that behaves almost like a toll — predictable, inflation-linked, and growing.

**Capital Intensity and Allocation.** Roper is one of the least capital-intensive large-cap businesses in existence. Capex of $105M on $7.9B in revenue (1.3%) means the organic business requires almost nothing to sustain. The real capital allocation question is whether management deploys the $2.4B of annual FCF wisely through acquisitions. Roper deployed $3.6B in acquisitions in 2024 and acquired CentralReach for $1.65B in early 2025, funded partly by new debt issuance ($2B across three tranches). The company also initiated buybacks ($500M in FY2025) for the first time, suggesting management sees the stock as undervalued. Stock-based compensation of $166M (FY2025) is manageable at ~7% of FCF, though rising modestly year-over-year.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper is a toll-bridge business generating $2.4B in FCF with 80% recurring revenue, near-zero capex needs, and a long runway to compound through acquisitions at returns well above WACC.
- **Bear case:** Reported ROIC remains low because the acquisition strategy demands ever-larger deals at higher multiples, and organic growth (~7%) alone would not justify the current enterprise value without continued M&A execution.
- **Confidence:** High — The financial data is unambiguous: Roper's cash economics are exceptional and the recurring revenue base provides unusual visibility into future cash flows.

## Red Flags
- SBC rising steadily ($118M to $166M over three years) — still manageable but worth monitoring as a percentage of FCF
- Organic growth of ~7% is decent but not exceptional for a company trading at 14.6x EV/EBITDA; the premium valuation assumes continued successful M&A
- FY2022 net income of $4.5B was inflated by a large gain (likely the TransDigm/CIVCO divestiture), distorting year-over-year comparisons
- Net income flat at $1.5B in FY2024 and FY2025 despite 13% revenue growth, suggesting higher interest expense and acquisition-related costs are eating into bottom-line growth

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper's business economics are genuinely excellent — 69% gross margins, 31% FCF margins, 158% FCF conversion, 80% recurring revenue, and near-zero capital intensity. The reported ROIC of 6% understates the true economics due to acquisition goodwill, with incremental returns on deployed capital running 15-17%.
