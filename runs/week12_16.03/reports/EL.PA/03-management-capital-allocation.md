## Detailed Analysis

The management and governance picture at EssilorLuxottica is a study in contrasts: excellent operational execution under CEO Francesco Milleri, coupled with a multi-year succession crisis at the controlling shareholder level that has created real uncertainty for investors. Understanding both sides is essential.

On the operational side, Milleri has been an effective steward. Since becoming CEO in December 2020, he has navigated the complex post-merger integration of Essilor and Luxottica (two companies with deeply different cultures — French precision engineering vs. Italian fashion entrepreneurship), completed the EUR 7.1B GrandVision acquisition, launched the Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses partnership, and grown revenue from approximately EUR 20B to EUR 28.5B. The adjusted operating margin of 16.0% in FY2025 reflects ongoing margin expansion as integration synergies are realized. These are the results of a capable operator. However, Milleri's direct equity ownership is minimal (approximately EUR 309K in shares), which is a governance concern. His compensation of EUR 23M is heavily weighted toward stock and options (91%), which provides meaningful alignment, but the base holding is small relative to the company's EUR 90B market cap. He does not talk like an owner-operator in the Buffett sense — his communication style is corporate and strategic rather than candid and shareholder-focused.

Capital allocation has been generally rational. The company generates approximately EUR 5.6-6.0B in annual operating cash flow (expected through 2026 per Moody's), against which it deploys: (1) approximately EUR 1.5B in maintenance capex, (2) approximately EUR 500M in bolt-on M&A annually (Heidelberg Engineering, Supreme brand in 2024; 4 more acquisitions in 2025), (3) EUR 600-650M in cash dividends (reduced from EUR 1.2B due to increased use of stock dividends), and (4) an active share buyback program. The buyback program is consistent — 1.5M+ shares repurchased in April 2025 alone — and signals management confidence in intrinsic value, though the share count has been approximately flat over recent years (460.5M shares outstanding), suggesting buybacks are largely offsetting stock-based compensation dilution rather than meaningfully shrinking the float.

The M&A track record is mixed but improving. The original 2018 Essilor-Luxottica merger was contentious, with public governance disputes between the two founding families that took years to resolve. The GrandVision acquisition (2021, EUR 7.1B) was strategically sound — adding 7,200+ retail stores primarily in Europe — but integration took longer than planned. More recent bolt-on deals (Heidelberg Engineering for optical instruments, Supreme for fashion brand expansion) are smaller and more targeted. The company is not empire-building at this stage; acquisitions are filling specific capability or distribution gaps within the existing integrated model.

The elephant in the room is the Delfin succession crisis. Leonardo Del Vecchio, the founder, died in June 2022 and split Delfin ownership among his six children, his widow, and a stepson. For nearly three years, the heirs have been unable to agree on governance, dividend policy, or strategic direction. Dividends from Delfin were frozen at 10% of net profit — a fraction of what the holding company could distribute. This stalemate has weighed on the stock and created uncertainty about whether the controlling shareholder would act cohesively. The most significant recent development is that Leonardo Maria Del Vecchio (the founder's son and EssilorLuxottica's Chief Strategy Officer) is nearing a deal — reportedly valued at approximately EUR 14B, backed by bank financing — to buy out siblings Luca and Paola's stakes, which would increase his personal holding from 12.5% to approximately 37.5% of Delfin. If completed, this would resolve the succession overhang, unfreeze dividend flows, and give a single aligned family member effective control of the 32% Delfin stake. This is a material positive catalyst if it closes.

Board governance at EssilorLuxottica itself is adequate but not exemplary. The board has 16 members, including representatives of both the French (Essilor) and Italian (Luxottica) heritage. Milleri serves as both Chairman and CEO — a dual role that reduces board independence. There is no dual-class share structure at the EssilorLuxottica level, but the Delfin controlling block effectively concentrates power. Independent directors exist but do not dominate the board composition.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Resolution of the Delfin succession dispute would remove the largest governance overhang, restore full dividend capacity, and allow management to execute long-term strategy with a stable, aligned controlling shareholder.
- **Bear case:** If the Del Vecchio buyout fails, the family stalemate could persist for years, suppressing dividends and creating ongoing governance uncertainty that deters institutional investors.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Operational management is strong but the Delfin succession overhang is real and unresolved as of March 2026; the reported buyout deal is promising but not yet closed.

## Red Flags
- CEO Milleri holds Chairman + CEO roles simultaneously, reducing board independence
- Milleri's direct equity ownership is negligible relative to company size, despite stock-heavy compensation
- Delfin succession dispute has frozen dividends and blocked governance decisions for ~3 years — a clear stewardship failure at the controlling shareholder level
- The reported EUR 14B leveraged buyout of sibling stakes would add significant debt to the Delfin holding structure, with unknown implications for EssilorLuxottica's own capital allocation flexibility
- Share buybacks appear to largely offset SBC dilution rather than creating per-share value accretion
- Total CEO compensation of EUR 23M is high by European standards, though the stock-heavy structure provides alignment

## Score: 6 / 10
Operational management under Milleri has been competent with disciplined capital allocation and strong revenue growth, but the unresolved Delfin succession dispute, combined with the CEO's minimal direct ownership and dual Chairman/CEO role, prevents a higher stewardship score — resolution of the family buyout would warrant a re-rating to 7-8.
