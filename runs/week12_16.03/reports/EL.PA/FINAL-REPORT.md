## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: EssilorLuxottica sells prescription lenses, branded eyewear frames, and smart glasses through 18,000+ owned stores and wholesale channels, driven by non-discretionary vision correction demand that grows with aging demographics and rising global myopia.
2. **Moat**: Vertical integration across lenses, frames, and retail at a scale no competitor can replicate, combined with optician switching costs on Essilor lens technology and the world's strongest eyewear brand portfolio (Ray-Ban, Oakley, 20+ luxury licenses), produces 60%+ gross margins and ~27% global market share.
3. **Economics**: Tangible ROIC is 15-20% once you strip EUR 30B of merger goodwill, FCF conversion runs above 140% of net income, and capex at 5.4% of revenue confirms asset-light compounding -- though gross margin fell 280bps in FY2025 from smart glasses mix dilution.
4. **Management**: CEO Milleri has executed well operationally (11.2% constant-currency growth, margin expansion roadmap to 19-20%), but he holds minimal equity, serves as dual Chairman/CEO, and the Delfin controlling shareholder has been paralyzed by a three-year succession dispute among the founder's eight heirs.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt/EBITDA of 1.1x, A2/A credit ratings, EUR 3.5B cash on hand, and EUR 3.8B annual FCF mean EssilorLuxottica would remain FCF-positive even in a 30% revenue decline scenario -- this balance sheet is a competitive weapon, not a liability.
6. **Price**: At EUR 195 and a 22.5x forward P/E (versus a 5-year average of 30-35x), the market is pricing in an immediate growth collapse to low-single-digits and permanent multiple compression, which contradicts record 11.2% organic growth and Q4 acceleration to 18.4%.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business quality moat is fortress-grade and Monte Carlo shows 99.9% probability of undervaluation, but the price sits 10% above the quant bear IV of EUR 176, so a truly conservative buyer would prefer to enter closer to EUR 175 for maximum downside protection.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if adjusted ROIC (ex-goodwill) falls below 8% for two consecutive years, signaling the vertically integrated moat is eroding rather than compounding.

## Key Risks

1. **Delfin succession crisis** -- The founder's 8 heirs have been unable to agree on governance for 3 years, freezing dividends and creating strategic uncertainty at the controlling shareholder level. The reported EUR 14B buyout by Leonardo Maria Del Vecchio would resolve this, but is not yet closed.
2. **Smart glasses margin dilution** -- Ray-Ban Meta contributed 4+ ppts of FY2025 growth but carries lower margins than traditional eyewear; gross margin fell 280bps in a single year. If this category grows without margin improvement, the business economics deteriorate.
3. **Tariff and FX headwinds** -- ~40% of revenue is US-derived and EUR-reported; tariff escalation on EU goods and EUR/USD volatility could compress reported earnings for multiple quarters.
4. **Apple smart glasses competition** -- Apple's announced 2027 entry into AI-powered eyewear directly challenges the Ray-Ban Meta growth narrative and first-mover advantage.
5. **Rising debt and declining interest coverage** -- Total debt up 18% in three years while interest coverage halved from 20x to 11x; continued debt-funded M&A could prevent deleveraging.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) Price falls to EUR 175 or below (at or below bear IV), providing a traditional margin of safety; (2) Delfin succession resolved with Leonardo Maria gaining effective control, removing governance overhang; (3) Smart glasses margins shown improving toward corporate average in FY2026 results.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) Adjusted ROIC (ex-goodwill) falls below 8% for two consecutive years, indicating moat erosion; (2) Stock reaches EUR 380-400 (bull IV territory, >20x forward EV/EBITDA); (3) Serial value-destroying acquisitions beyond Supreme that signal strategic drift from core optical competence.

## Detailed Analysis

EssilorLuxottica is straightforward to explain: the company designs, manufactures, and sells eyeglasses and sunglasses -- both the lenses and the frames -- and owns the retail stores where many of them are sold. The core demand driver is human vision, which deteriorates predictably with age and is increasingly impaired at younger ages due to screen time (myopia prevalence). Over 4 billion people globally need vision correction, and that number grows with demographics and lifestyle. This is as close to non-discretionary demand as consumer goods get.

The company makes money through two channels. The Professional Solutions segment sells prescription lenses (Varilux progressives, Transitions photochromics, Stellest myopia-management), lens coatings, optical instruments, and lab equipment to independent opticians and retail chains worldwide. This is a high-margin, recurring B2B business -- opticians need a steady supply of lenses for every patient, and Essilor's technology and brand trust create strong pull-through demand. The Direct-to-Consumer segment operates approximately 18,000 retail locations (LensCrafters, Sunglass Hut, Pearle Vision, GrandVision stores across Europe) plus a growing e-commerce channel led by Ray-Ban.com. This channel captures the full retail margin on both frames and lenses.

The key drivers are few and predictable. First, global vision care demand -- driven by population aging and rising myopia rates, particularly in Asia. This is a secular tailwind measured in decades, not quarters. Second, premiumization -- the shift from basic lenses to value-added products (progressives, coatings, photochromics) and from generic frames to branded ones (Ray-Ban, Oakley, Prada-licensed). Each step up the value chain carries meaningfully higher margins. Third, distribution density -- the number and quality of retail touchpoints, both owned and wholesale. The GrandVision acquisition (2021, 7,200+ stores) significantly expanded owned retail. A newer fourth driver has emerged: smart eyewear via the Meta partnership. The Ray-Ban Meta glasses sold 7 million units in 2025, tripling prior-year volumes, and contributed over 4 percentage points of group revenue growth -- but this remains supplementary to the core vision care engine.

Revenue predictability is high. People do not stop needing glasses in a recession -- they may delay a purchase by a quarter or trade down slightly, but the replacement cycle (every 2-3 years for prescriptions) reasserts itself. FY2020 (COVID) was the only meaningful revenue disruption in the past two decades, and recovery was swift. The subscription-like nature of vision correction, combined with an aging global population, makes this one of the more forecastable large-cap revenue streams in consumer health. The business model can be explained in under two minutes to any lay investor, which is the hallmark of a high circle-of-competence score.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Non-discretionary vision care demand with secular demographic tailwinds makes this one of the most predictable large-cap business models globally.
- **Bear case:** The smart glasses narrative introduces technology execution risk and competitive uncertainty (Apple entering in 2027) that could distort the core simplicity story.
- **Confidence:** High -- The core lens + frame + retail business is among the most understandable in global equities; the Meta/smart glasses layer adds a small variable but does not change the fundamental clarity.

## Red Flags
- Smart glasses (Ray-Ban Meta) contributed 4+ ppts of FY2025 growth -- if this category faces competition or consumer fatigue, the growth profile reverts to mid-single digits
- Reported operating margin of 11.9% seems low for such a dominant franchise; adjusted operating margin of 16.0% suggests heavy intangible amortization from M&A (Luxottica merger, GrandVision) that masks true economics
- Currency complexity: EUR-reporting company with ~50% USD revenue exposure creates translation noise

## Score: 8 / 10
EssilorLuxottica is a highly understandable business with few key drivers, non-discretionary demand, and minimal customer concentration -- the only deduction is the emerging smart glasses layer which introduces modest technology complexity to an otherwise crystal-clear business model.

# Durable Competitive Advantage -- EL.PA

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** EssilorLuxottica FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Moody's credit opinion (Sep 2025), context/EL.PA/financials.md, context/EL.PA/quant-valuation.md, Rijnberk Invest Insights moat analysis, GrowthShuttle industry research, CNBC and Bloomberg reporting

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | EssilorLuxottica commands ~27% global eyewear market share, more than 2x the next competitor, with vertical integration spanning lenses, frames, and retail that no competitor can replicate at scale | 5 |
| 2 | The lens business enjoys deep switching costs: opticians invest in Essilor equipment, training, and workflows; Varilux/Transitions/Crizal are specified by eye care professionals and demanded by patients | 5 |
| 3 | Brand portfolio moat: Ray-Ban (world's best-selling eyewear brand), Oakley, plus 20+ licensed luxury brands (Chanel, Prada, Armani) create a brand fortress that generates pricing power and retail pull-through | 4 |
| 4 | 47 production facilities + ~600 prescription labs worldwide create a manufacturing and distribution scale advantage that suppresses competitor margins and raises barriers to entry | 4 |
| 5 | Gross margin of 60.6% sustained over multiple years demonstrates real pricing power; competitors in lenses (Hoya, Carl Zeiss Vision) and frames (Safilo, Marchon) operate at structurally lower margins | 4 |
| 6 | The Meta smart glasses partnership gives EssilorLuxottica a first-mover position in AI-powered eyewear, but Apple's announced 2027 entry means this advantage could be contested | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

EssilorLuxottica possesses a multi-layered moat that is rare in consumer goods. The most durable layer is the lens technology and optician switching costs. Essilor's progressive lens technology (Varilux), photochromic technology (Transitions), and lens coatings (Crizal) are the industry standards. Eye care professionals are trained on Essilor systems, use Essilor lab equipment and surfacing technology, and specify Essilor products to patients. Switching to a competitor lens system means retraining staff, replacing equipment, and risking patient satisfaction -- a cost that few independent opticians are willing to bear. This creates an installed base dynamic similar to enterprise software: once embedded, the customer relationship is sticky for years. Essilor holds thousands of patents on lens design and coating technology, and its R&D spend of 1.5-2.0% of revenue (approximately EUR 450-570M annually) continuously refreshes this technology lead.

The brand moat is formidable and self-reinforcing. Ray-Ban is the world's most recognized eyewear brand, generating consumer pull that drives foot traffic to both owned retail (Sunglass Hut, LensCrafters) and wholesale channels. Oakley dominates performance/sport eyewear. Beyond proprietary brands, EssilorLuxottica manufactures frames under license for over 20 luxury houses -- Chanel, Prada, Giorgio Armani, Burberry, Ralph Lauren, Versace, Tiffany, and others. These licensing relationships are durable because EssilorLuxottica offers a unique combination of manufacturing quality, global distribution reach, and design capability that luxury brands cannot easily replicate with smaller manufacturers. The licensing model also creates a moat-within-a-moat: each luxury brand's eyewear customers become part of the EssilorLuxottica ecosystem.

The scale and vertical integration moat is the structural backbone. No other company in the world simultaneously designs lenses, manufactures frames, and operates 18,000+ retail stores. This vertical integration allows EssilorLuxottica to capture margin at every step -- from the raw lens blank to the finished, fitted eyeglasses sold at retail. The 47 manufacturing plants and ~600 prescription labs create a logistics network that enables next-day delivery of custom prescription lenses in most developed markets. A new entrant would need to invest tens of billions of dollars over a decade-plus to approximate this infrastructure, and even then would lack the brand portfolio and optician relationships.

The moat's weakest point is in the fashion/sunglasses segment, where brand cycles can shift and direct-to-consumer disruptors (Warby Parker, etc.) have captured some share in lower price tiers. However, Warby Parker's market share remains negligible at global scale, and the premium/luxury tier where EssilorLuxottica dominates has proven resilient. The smart glasses category introduces a new variable: Apple's planned 2027 entry into AI-powered eyewear could contest EssilorLuxottica's first-mover position with Meta, though this remains speculative and EssilorLuxottica's manufacturing expertise in eyewear form factors is a genuine advantage even against big tech.

The numerical evidence for moat durability is strong. Gross margins have held at 60-62% for multiple years. The company has grown revenue at a high-single-digit CAGR organically, demonstrating consistent market share gains. The lens business, in particular, shows no signs of commoditization -- value-added lenses (progressives, myopia management, photochromics) continue to command premium pricing and grow faster than basic lenses.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The triple moat of lens technology switching costs, iconic brand portfolio, and unmatched vertical integration creates a competitive position that would take a decade and tens of billions to approximate -- and even then, the installed base advantage would persist.
- **Bear case:** Smart glasses competition from Apple/Google could erode the premium on EssilorLuxottica's Meta partnership, and DTC disruptors continue to nibble at lower price points, potentially pressuring the retail channel margins over time.
- **Confidence:** High -- The core moat (lenses + brands + scale) has been strengthening for 20+ years and there is no credible challenger to the integrated model; smart glasses risk is real but supplementary to the core franchise.

## Red Flags
- Apple's announced 2027 entry into smart glasses directly challenges the Ray-Ban Meta growth narrative
- Luxury brand licensing relationships, while durable, do have renewal risk -- loss of a major brand (e.g., Chanel) would be reputationally meaningful even if financially modest
- DTC competitors (Warby Parker, Zenni) continue to expand in the value segment; while small at global scale, they signal that the retail moat is not impregnable at lower price points
- Vertical integration can become a liability if consumer preferences shift toward channels EssilorLuxottica does not control (e.g., online-first vision care)

## Score: 8 / 10
EssilorLuxottica possesses a wide, multi-layered moat built on lens technology switching costs, the world's strongest eyewear brand portfolio, and unrivaled vertical integration -- the only deductions are emerging smart glasses competition and modest DTC pressure at the value end, neither of which threatens the core franchise within a 10-year horizon.

# Management & Capital Allocation -- EL.PA

**Date:** 2026-03-23

## Detailed Analysis

The management and governance picture at EssilorLuxottica is a study in contrasts: excellent operational execution under CEO Francesco Milleri, coupled with a multi-year succession crisis at the controlling shareholder level that has created real uncertainty for investors. Understanding both sides is essential.

On the operational side, Milleri has been an effective steward. Since becoming CEO in December 2020, he has navigated the complex post-merger integration of Essilor and Luxottica (two companies with deeply different cultures -- French precision engineering vs. Italian fashion entrepreneurship), completed the EUR 7.1B GrandVision acquisition, launched the Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses partnership, and grown revenue from approximately EUR 20B to EUR 28.5B. The adjusted operating margin of 16.0% in FY2025 reflects ongoing margin expansion as integration synergies are realized. These are the results of a capable operator. However, Milleri's direct equity ownership is minimal (approximately EUR 309K in shares), which is a governance concern. His compensation of EUR 23M is heavily weighted toward stock and options (91%), which provides meaningful alignment, but the base holding is small relative to the company's EUR 90B market cap. He does not talk like an owner-operator in the Buffett sense -- his communication style is corporate and strategic rather than candid and shareholder-focused.

Capital allocation has been generally rational. The company generates approximately EUR 5.6-6.0B in annual operating cash flow (expected through 2026 per Moody's), against which it deploys: (1) approximately EUR 1.5B in maintenance capex, (2) approximately EUR 500M in bolt-on M&A annually (Heidelberg Engineering, Supreme brand in 2024; 4 more acquisitions in 2025), (3) EUR 600-650M in cash dividends (reduced from EUR 1.2B due to increased use of stock dividends), and (4) an active share buyback program. The buyback program is consistent -- 1.5M+ shares repurchased in April 2025 alone -- and signals management confidence in intrinsic value, though the share count has been approximately flat over recent years (460.5M shares outstanding), suggesting buybacks are largely offsetting stock-based compensation dilution rather than meaningfully shrinking the float.

The M&A track record is mixed but improving. The original 2018 Essilor-Luxottica merger was contentious, with public governance disputes between the two founding families that took years to resolve. The GrandVision acquisition (2021, EUR 7.1B) was strategically sound -- adding 7,200+ retail stores primarily in Europe -- but integration took longer than planned. More recent bolt-on deals (Heidelberg Engineering for optical instruments, Supreme for fashion brand expansion) are smaller and more targeted. The company is not empire-building at this stage; acquisitions are filling specific capability or distribution gaps within the existing integrated model.

The elephant in the room is the Delfin succession crisis. Leonardo Del Vecchio, the founder, died in June 2022 and split Delfin ownership among his six children, his widow, and a stepson. For nearly three years, the heirs have been unable to agree on governance, dividend policy, or strategic direction. Dividends from Delfin were frozen at 10% of net profit -- a fraction of what the holding company could distribute. This stalemate has weighed on the stock and created uncertainty about whether the controlling shareholder would act cohesively. The most significant recent development is that Leonardo Maria Del Vecchio (the founder's son and EssilorLuxottica's Chief Strategy Officer) is nearing a deal -- reportedly valued at approximately EUR 14B, backed by bank financing -- to buy out siblings Luca and Paola's stakes, which would increase his personal holding from 12.5% to approximately 37.5% of Delfin. If completed, this would resolve the succession overhang, unfreeze dividend flows, and give a single aligned family member effective control of the 32% Delfin stake. This is a material positive catalyst if it closes.

Board governance at EssilorLuxottica itself is adequate but not exemplary. The board has 16 members, including representatives of both the French (Essilor) and Italian (Luxottica) heritage. Milleri serves as both Chairman and CEO -- a dual role that reduces board independence. There is no dual-class share structure at the EssilorLuxottica level, but the Delfin controlling block effectively concentrates power. Independent directors exist but do not dominate the board composition.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Resolution of the Delfin succession dispute would remove the largest governance overhang, restore full dividend capacity, and allow management to execute long-term strategy with a stable, aligned controlling shareholder.
- **Bear case:** If the Del Vecchio buyout fails, the family stalemate could persist for years, suppressing dividends and creating ongoing governance uncertainty that deters institutional investors.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- Operational management is strong but the Delfin succession overhang is real and unresolved as of March 2026; the reported buyout deal is promising but not yet closed.

## Red Flags
- CEO Milleri holds Chairman + CEO roles simultaneously, reducing board independence
- Milleri's direct equity ownership is negligible relative to company size, despite stock-heavy compensation
- Delfin succession dispute has frozen dividends and blocked governance decisions for ~3 years -- a clear stewardship failure at the controlling shareholder level
- The reported EUR 14B leveraged buyout of sibling stakes would add significant debt to the Delfin holding structure, with unknown implications for EssilorLuxottica's own capital allocation flexibility
- Share buybacks appear to largely offset SBC dilution rather than creating per-share value accretion
- Total CEO compensation of EUR 23M is high by European standards, though the stock-heavy structure provides alignment

## Score: 6 / 10
Operational management under Milleri has been competent with disciplined capital allocation and strong revenue growth, but the unresolved Delfin succession dispute, combined with the CEO's minimal direct ownership and dual Chairman/CEO role, prevents a higher stewardship score -- resolution of the family buyout would warrant a re-rating to 7-8.

# Business Economics -- EL.PA

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Data Sources:** Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), EssilorLuxottica Q4/FY2025 press release (GlobeNewsWire, Feb 11 2026), EssilorLuxottica H1 2025 Interim Financial Report, Morningstar margin analysis, quant-valuation model output (src/quant)

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Reported ROIC of ~5% is heavily distorted by ~EUR 30B of goodwill/intangibles from the Essilor-Luxottica merger and GrandVision acquisition; tangible ROIC is substantially higher, likely 15-20%+ | 5 |
| 2 | FY2025 adjusted operating margin declined to 16.0% from 16.7% in FY2024, driven by dilutive impact of AI smart glasses (7M+ units sold) -- a deliberate margin trade-off for a new growth vector | 4 |
| 3 | FCF generation is robust and growing: EUR 3.8B in FY2025 (up from EUR 3.2B in FY2022), with FCF conversion consistently above 140% of net income | 5 |
| 4 | Revenue grew 11.2% at constant currency in FY2025, the first-ever year of double-digit organic growth, with Q4 accelerating to 18.4% | 4 |
| 5 | Capital intensity is moderate (capex ~5.4% of revenue) and stable, with the quant model flagging 100% of capex as maintenance -- suggesting organic growth is largely asset-light | 3 |
| 6 | Gross margin declined 280bp to 60.6% in FY2025 from 63.4% in FY2024, warranting monitoring for mix shift versus structural erosion | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

### Returns on Capital: The Goodwill Distortion

EssilorLuxottica's reported ROIC of 5.2% (FY2025) looks deeply unimpressive at face value. However, this is one of the clearest cases of merger accounting masking true economic returns. The 2018 Essilor-Luxottica combination and the 2021 GrandVision acquisition loaded the balance sheet with approximately EUR 30B of goodwill and intangible assets. Invested capital stands at EUR 49.7B, of which roughly 60% is acquisition-related goodwill and purchase-price-allocated intangibles. If we strip goodwill and amortization of acquired intangibles (approximately EUR 910M annually), the tangible return on capital employed is far more impressive -- likely in the 15-20% range. This is consistent with what you would expect from a business that controls the entire eyewear value chain from lens manufacturing through retail distribution, with dominant brand pricing power. The ROE of 5.8% and ROA of 3.6% suffer from the same distortion. For a business generating EUR 3.9B in owner earnings on a tangible asset base of perhaps EUR 20B, the underlying economics are strong.

### Margin Structure: Solid but Under Pressure

The margin trajectory tells an important story. EBITDA margins have been stable at 23-25% for four years, while the adjusted operating margin (which adds back acquisition-related amortization) was 16.0% in FY2025 versus 16.7% in FY2024. The reported GAAP operating margin of 11.9% is lower due to EUR ~910M in annual amortization of acquisition-related intangibles -- a non-cash charge with no economic substance. The gross margin decline from 63.4% to 60.6% in FY2025 is notable and appears driven by the product mix shift toward Meta Ray-Ban smart glasses, which carry lower margins than traditional premium eyewear. Management is deliberately accepting near-term margin dilution to build a potentially transformative new product category. The key question is whether smart glasses margins improve as the product matures and scales, or whether this represents a structural shift toward lower-margin consumer electronics. Historical precedent from the lens business suggests EssilorLuxottica can improve margins over time as it vertically integrates new categories.

### Cash Generation: The Crown Jewel

Free cash flow is the most compelling metric here. FCF grew from EUR 3.2B (FY2022) to EUR 3.8B (FY2025), representing a 13.2% FCF margin. FCF conversion (FCF/Net Income) has been extraordinary, consistently above 140% -- this is because D&A significantly exceeds capex (EUR 3.1B vs EUR 1.5B), a classic sign of a business where past acquisitions are generating cash flows well in excess of the accounting charges against them. Owner earnings of EUR 3.9B are essentially identical to FCF, which is a healthy sign -- there is no meaningful gap between economic and accounting cash flow. At the current market cap of EUR 89.7B, the stock trades at roughly 23x owner earnings. Operating cash flow of EUR 5.3B provides ample coverage for capex, dividends (approximately EUR 1.7B), and modest buybacks (EUR 376M), with surplus cash available for bolt-on M&A (guided at up to EUR 1.5B annually).

### Capital Intensity and Revenue Quality

Capex has been remarkably stable at EUR 1.5-1.6B annually (5.4% of revenue), even as revenue has grown from EUR 24.5B to EUR 28.5B. This is the hallmark of an asset-light compounder that grows primarily through pricing power, volume in essential healthcare products, and operational leverage rather than heavy reinvestment. Revenue quality is high: vision correction is non-discretionary (approximately 4.6 billion people globally need corrective eyewear), replacement cycles are predictable, and the company controls distribution through its ~18,000 retail stores (LensCrafters, Sunglass Hut, GrandVision). This is recurring in nature if not contractually recurring. The addition of smart glasses introduces a more discretionary, technology-upgrade-driven revenue stream that may prove more cyclical.

### Operating Leverage

Revenue has grown from EUR 24.5B to EUR 28.5B (+16.3%) over FY2022-FY2025, while operating income has remained flat at EUR 3.4B and net income essentially flat at EUR 2.3B. This absence of operating leverage is concerning at first glance, but is entirely explained by the rising amortization of acquisition intangibles and the deliberate investment in smart glasses. On an adjusted basis, operating profit has grown faster than revenue. The underlying business model has strong inherent operating leverage -- fixed costs (R&D at EUR 664M, corporate overhead) are spread across a growing revenue base, and the vertically integrated model captures margin at multiple points in the value chain.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Tangible ROIC is 15-20%+, FCF compounds reliably at double digits, and the reported metrics systematically understate the economic quality of this franchise due to merger-era goodwill.
- **Bear case:** Gross margin erosion from smart glasses mix shift may not reverse, and reported ROIC/ROE could remain depressed indefinitely if the company continues making large acquisitions that reload goodwill.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The tangible economics are clearly strong, but the magnitude of the goodwill distortion makes precise ROIC estimation difficult without full balance sheet decomposition; the smart glasses margin impact is still evolving.

## Red Flags
- Gross margin declined 280bp in a single year (60.6% vs 63.4%) -- largest annual drop in available history
- Reported ROIC has been flat-to-declining for 4 years (5.1-5.5%), with no improvement despite revenue growth
