## Detailed Analysis

### Debt Levels and Structure

EssilorLuxottica carries EUR 14.4B in total debt against EUR 6.6B EBITDA, producing a gross Debt/EBITDA ratio of 2.2x. Net of EUR 3.5B in cash, net debt/EBITDA is a comfortable 1.1x. This is conservative leverage for a defensive healthcare/consumer business with predictable cash flows. The debt structure is predominantly fixed-rate Eurobonds, which provides protection against further rate increases. Long-term debt stands at EUR 8.1B, with the remainder in short-term borrowings and commercial paper (approximately EUR 6.3B in current debt, including EUR 2.4B of commercial paper under the USCP program). The company actively manages its maturity profile: in 2025, it issued a EUR 1B Eurobond at 2.625% maturing January 2030 while repaying a EUR 1.5B maturity, demonstrating continued access to attractive financing. Debt/Equity of 37.0% is modest, though it has crept up from 30.5% in FY2023. The equity base of EUR 38.9B provides a substantial cushion, even acknowledging that much of it is goodwill-related.

### Interest Coverage Trajectory

The most notable trend is the decline in interest coverage from 20.4x in FY2022 to 11.2x in FY2025. This is driven by a doubling of interest expense (EUR 156M to EUR 314M) while operating income remained flat at EUR 3.4B. The rising interest bill reflects both the post-2022 rate environment and incremental debt taken on for acquisitions and commercial paper usage. At 11.2x, coverage remains comfortable — this is not a distress signal — but the direction of travel warrants attention. If interest rates remain elevated and the company continues adding debt for M&A, coverage could compress further. On an EBITDA basis, coverage is approximately 21x (EUR 6.6B / EUR 314M), which is very strong.

### Liquidity and Near-Term Maturities

Cash and short-term investments of EUR 3.5B represent the highest cash balance in available history (up from EUR 2.0B in FY2022). Combined with annual FCF of EUR 3.8B, the company has approximately EUR 7.3B in liquidity available over the next twelve months before any refinancing. Debt maturities through year-end 2026 total EUR 3.7B (including EUR 2.4B of commercial paper, which regularly rolls). This is easily covered by existing cash and ongoing cash generation. The current ratio of 1.1x is adequate if not luxurious — this is typical for vertically integrated companies that manage working capital tightly. Working capital has been stable at EUR 0.75B-1.3B, indicating disciplined inventory and receivables management.

### Refinancing Risk

Refinancing risk is minimal. EssilorLuxottica holds A2/A credit ratings from Moody's and S&P respectively, both with stable outlooks. These are upper-medium investment-grade ratings, ensuring the company can access bond markets at favorable rates in virtually any environment. The June 2025 bond issuance (EUR 1B at 2.625%) attracted an order book of EUR 2.5B — 2.5x oversubscribed — confirming strong institutional demand. The company maintains a USCP (US Commercial Paper) program and a Euro-denominated medium-term note program, providing multiple funding channels. The bond maturity profile appears well-laddered based on the pattern of issuances and repayments. There is no covenant risk to speak of at these rating levels.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Items and Contingent Liabilities

As a vertically integrated retailer operating approximately 18,000 stores globally, EssilorLuxottica has substantial operating lease obligations. Under IFRS 16, these are capitalized on the balance sheet and included in the EUR 3.1B D&A figure, which significantly exceeds maintenance capex. Pension obligations exist but are not material relative to the company's scale. The company has guided annual M&A spending of up to EUR 1.5B — while not a balance sheet liability, this represents an ongoing capital commitment that will prevent deleveraging if fully utilized. No material litigation or contingent liabilities were flagged in available disclosures.

### Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years

Starting from FY2025 revenue of EUR 28.5B, a 30% decline would bring revenue to approximately EUR 20.0B. Assuming operating margins compress to 8% (vs 11.9% reported; conservative given high fixed costs in retail and manufacturing), operating income would fall to EUR 1.6B. After interest expense of EUR 314M and taxes, net income would be approximately EUR 1.0B. D&A of approximately EUR 3.1B minus maintenance capex of EUR 1.0B (reduced in line with revenue) produces FCF of approximately EUR 2.1B even in this severe scenario. This is sufficient to cover dividends (which could be cut), service debt, and maintain operations without issuing equity. EssilorLuxottica survived the COVID-19 pandemic (2020) when revenue dropped significantly, and emerged without equity issuance. The non-discretionary nature of vision correction provides a floor — people need to see, even in recessions.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Net debt/EBITDA of 1.1x with A2/A credit ratings and EUR 3.8B annual FCF makes this balance sheet a source of competitive advantage — the company can make opportunistic acquisitions and invest through downturns while competitors retrench.
- **Bear case:** Total debt has grown 18% in three years, interest coverage has halved, and a commitment to EUR 1.5B annual M&A spending could prevent deleveraging; if smart glasses require heavier investment, capex could rise.
- **Confidence:** High — Balance sheet data is well-documented, credit ratings are publicly verified, and the stress test math is straightforward given the defensive nature of the core business.

## Red Flags
- Interest coverage declined from 20.4x to 11.2x in three years — not dangerous but a clear negative trend
- Total debt grew EUR 2.2B (from EUR 12.2B to EUR 14.4B) while EBITDA was flat at EUR 6.6B
- EUR 6.3B in short-term/current debt (including EUR 2.4B commercial paper) relies on continued market access for rolling
- Guided EUR 1.5B annual M&A appetite means management prefers empire-building over deleveraging
- Goodwill of approximately EUR 30B means an impairment charge — while non-cash — could materially reduce equity and spike leverage ratios

## Score: 7 / 10
Conservative net leverage (1.1x), strong investment-grade ratings (A2/A stable), and robust FCF generation provide ample safety, but the trend toward higher debt, declining interest coverage, and a standing commitment to debt-funded M&A prevent a fortress-grade rating.
