**Date:** 2026-03-23

1. **Business**: Berkshire collects $176B in insurance float at negative cost, invests it alongside $44.5B in operating earnings from rail, energy, manufacturing, and consumer businesses — customers stay because GEICO is cheapest, BNSF is the only western railroad, and BHE rates are regulated.
2. **Moat**: A self-reinforcing system of $176B costless insurance float, a physically irreplaceable rail duopoly (BNSF), regulated utility returns, and $373B in crisis-deployment cash that no competitor can replicate at any price.
3. **Economics**: Owner earnings of $59–67B on $717B equity confirm real cash generation, but ROE has fallen from 18.6% to 9.8% in two years as $373B in cash earns 4–5% Treasury yields instead of the 15%+ returns the operating businesses produce.
4. **Management**: Abel bought $15.3M in personal shares his first week, resumed buybacks after a 6-quarter pause, and takes $25M total comp with zero stock options — early signals are textbook owner-operator alignment.
5. **Debt risk**: With $373B cash against $129B debt, 17x interest coverage, ring-fenced subsidiary obligations, and $46B annual operating cash flow, Berkshire could service all debt for 7+ years with zero revenue.
6. **Price**: At $481 (1.45x book, 10x owner earnings), the market assumes flat revenues, no accretive deployment of the $373B cash pile, and a permanent post-Buffett discount — the quant model's bear case requires -4.2% growth at 8.7% WACC to justify this price.
7. **Margin of safety**: The $268/share cash floor caps permanent loss at ~44%, 89% Monte Carlo probability shows IV above current price, and 20 of 25 sensitivity grid cells produce IV above $481.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Abel deploying $50B+ on a value-destructive acquisition, insurance reserves proving inadequate after a mega-catastrophe, or 3+ years of cash hoarding with no meaningful buybacks or deals.
