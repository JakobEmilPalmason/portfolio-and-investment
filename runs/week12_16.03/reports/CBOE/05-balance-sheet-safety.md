## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** As of December 31, 2025, CBOE held $1,442.9M in total debt ($1.4B long-term, minimal short-term), consisting primarily of fixed-rate senior notes. Against this, the company held $2,216.5M in cash and cash equivalents, resulting in a net cash position of approximately $774M. Total debt/EBITDA sits at roughly 0.8x on a gross basis and effectively negative on a net debt basis. The debt-to-equity ratio of 31% is conservative. This is a company that could retire all of its outstanding debt from cash on hand and still have hundreds of millions remaining. The debt that does exist was largely taken on to fund the 2017 BATS Global Markets acquisition and subsequent smaller deals, and the company has been steadily deleveraging since.

**Interest Coverage.** With operating income of $1.5B and interest expense of just $52.3M, CBOE's interest coverage ratio stands at approximately 31x. Even using the more conservative EBIT figure, coverage is exceptionally high. This level of coverage means interest expense is essentially negligible relative to the company's earning power. The fixed-rate nature of the senior notes means there is no floating-rate exposure to worry about in a rising rate environment.

**Liquidity.** The current ratio of 1.9x is healthy, and the $2.3B cash balance is substantial — representing roughly 1.6x total debt outstanding. Additionally, CBOE maintains a $400M revolving credit facility that was undrawn as of the most recent filing, providing a further liquidity backstop. The company amended and restated its credit facility in June 2025 (with Cboe Clear Europe N.V.), ensuring continued access to bank lines. Between operating cash flow (~$1.7B annually), the cash pile, and the revolver, CBOE has multiple layers of liquidity.

**Refinancing Risk.** The senior notes are fixed-rate with staggered maturities, which limits concentration risk around any single refinancing window. Given the company's net cash position and strong free cash flow generation ($1.7B in FY2025), CBOE could choose to retire debt at maturity from internal resources without accessing capital markets at all. Even if it chose to refinance, the company's strong credit profile (investment-grade rated) would ensure favorable terms. Refinancing risk is minimal.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** As a regulated exchange operator, CBOE's primary off-balance-sheet considerations relate to its clearinghouse operations (Cboe Clear Europe). Clearing entities carry guaranty fund obligations and potential default fund contributions. These are standard for exchange/clearing businesses and are ring-fenced from the parent. Operating lease commitments exist for office space and data center facilities but are modest relative to the company's cash flow generation. No material off-balance-sheet concerns have been identified.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** Starting from FY2025 net revenue of $2.4B, a 30% decline would reduce net revenue to approximately $1.7B. Given CBOE's high fixed-cost operating leverage, operating margins would compress — but the fixed cost base (estimated at $700-800M) means the company would still generate roughly $900M-$1.0B in operating income even in this dire scenario. After interest expense ($52M) and taxes (~22% rate), net income would still be approximately $660-740M. Free cash flow, given the minimal capex ($71M), would remain strongly positive at $700M+. With $2.3B in cash and $1.7B+ in cumulative FCF even during the stress period, CBOE could comfortably service all debt, continue dividends, and maintain operations without any need to raise equity. The business would survive a 30% revenue decline with ease. Even a 50% decline — unprecedented for a diversified exchange — would leave the company cash-flow positive with sufficient resources to service debt.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** CBOE operates from a near-fortress balance sheet with net cash, 31x interest coverage, and stress-test durability that makes financial distress virtually inconceivable under any realistic scenario.
- **Bear case:** The only balance sheet risk is a potential large acquisition funded by debt that could materially increase leverage — management has shown willingness to pursue M&A (BATS, digital assets), though the current posture is conservative.
- **Confidence:** High — debt structure is straightforward, cash position is verified, and the stress test math is unambiguous given the low-capex, high-margin business model.

## Red Flags
- Clearinghouse operations (Cboe Clear Europe) carry inherent tail risk if a major clearing member defaults, though guaranty fund structures and regulatory capital requirements mitigate this
- Management could deploy the substantial cash pile on a value-destructive acquisition (as arguably occurred with the digital assets push that led to a $569M goodwill impairment in FY2022)

## Score: 9 / 10
CBOE's balance sheet is a fortress: net cash position of ~$774M, negligible leverage (0.4x net debt/EBITDA), 31x interest coverage, ample liquidity, and stress-test durability that ensures survival even under extreme revenue declines. The only reason this is not a 10 is the theoretical risk of management deploying the balance sheet on a large, potentially value-destructive acquisition, as the digital assets impairment demonstrated can happen.
