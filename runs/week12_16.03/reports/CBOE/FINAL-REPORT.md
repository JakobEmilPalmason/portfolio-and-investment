## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: CBOE runs the only exchange where SPX and VIX options trade, earning a per-contract toll on 3.9 million daily contracts at 67% operating margins with near-zero marginal cost.
2. **Moat**: Exclusive SPX licensing through 2032 and outright VIX ownership, reinforced by a self-compounding liquidity network effect that no competitor has ever successfully replicated in electronic derivatives.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of 17.3%, FCF conversion of 153% on just $71M capex, and EBITDA margins expanding to 68% confirm a capital-light toll bridge that converts almost every incremental revenue dollar to cash.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is a fortress, but the stock trades 85% above bear-case IV ($155) and 30% above base-case IV ($222), offering zero price protection for a new buyer.

## Key Risks

1. **Valuation overstretch**: The stock trades at $287 vs. base-case IV of $222 and bear-case IV of $155. The market is pricing in the bull case as the base case, leaving no room for disappointment and deeply unfavorable downside/upside asymmetry (46% downside to bear vs. 3% upside to bull).

# Circle of Competence — CBOE

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Data Sources:** Cboe Q4 2025 earnings release, Cboe IR filings, Yahoo Finance, Macrotrends, Cboe.com product pages, Disruption Banking, Finance Magnates

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | CBOE operates the largest U.S. options exchange (~31% market share) with exclusive rights to list SPX and VIX options — two of the most liquid derivatives products in the world | 5 |
| 2 | Revenue is driven by three clear segments: Derivatives (the dominant engine, +38% YoY in Q4 2025), Cash & Spot Markets (+27% YoY), and Data Vantage (+9% YoY) | 4 |
| 3 | 0DTE (zero-days-to-expiry) SPX options now represent ~59% of total SPX volume, providing a powerful secular growth driver that is straightforward to understand | 4 |
| 4 | Full-year 2025 net revenue reached $2.4B (organic), up 17% YoY, with adjusted operating margins of ~67% in Q4 — demonstrating extreme operating leverage on incremental volume | 5 |
| 5 | Revenue is transaction-fee-driven and therefore volume-dependent, but volume has grown for six consecutive years and is diversified across institutional hedgers, market makers, and retail participants | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What does CBOE sell, and to whom?** Cboe Global Markets operates electronic exchanges where financial instruments — primarily equity options, index options, and volatility products — are traded. Its customers span three tiers: (1) institutional asset managers and hedge funds that use SPX and VIX options for hedging and portfolio overlay; (2) broker-dealers and market makers who pay access and transaction fees; and (3) data consumers (quants, analytics firms, index providers) who license Cboe's proprietary market data. The company also operates cash equity exchanges in the U.S. (BZX, BYX, EDGX, EDGA) and Europe, plus a global FX platform, though these are secondary to the derivatives franchise.

**How does it make money?** Cboe's revenue model has three pillars. Transaction fees on options and futures contracts are the largest driver — every SPX, VIX, or multi-list option contract that trades generates per-contract revenue. Access and capacity fees are charged to members connecting to Cboe's matching engines. Market data and analytics (branded "Data Vantage") generates recurring, subscription-like revenue from licensing proprietary indices, real-time data feeds, and analytics tools. The mix is shifting toward higher-margin proprietary derivatives as the company exits lower-margin businesses like Australian/Canadian equities and corporate listings.

**Key drivers.** Two to four variables explain most of the business: (1) SPX options average daily volume (ADV), which hit a record 3.9 million contracts in 2025; (2) VIX options ADV (862K contracts); (3) revenue per contract (RPC), which benefits from the premium pricing of proprietary index products vs. commoditized multi-list options; and (4) Data Vantage growth rate, currently mid-to-high single digits. The 0DTE phenomenon — short-dated options used for intraday hedging and speculation — is the most important secular tailwind, and it is specific to Cboe's proprietary SPX franchise.

**Revenue predictability.** Revenue is not subscription-based; it is tied to trading volumes, which correlate with market volatility and participant activity. However, the six-year streak of rising options volumes, the structural shift toward options-based portfolio strategies, and the stickiness of 0DTE adoption give the revenue trajectory more predictability than a typical exchange. Data Vantage adds a quasi-recurring component. Guidance for 2026 calls for mid-single-digit organic net revenue growth, suggesting management sees the volume trend as durable.

**Customer concentration.** No single customer dominates. The top market makers (Citadel Securities, Wolverine, Susquehanna) and broker-dealers (Schwab/TD, Interactive Brokers, Fidelity) are important but numerous. Institutional hedging demand is diffuse. The bigger concentration risk is product-level: SPX options alone represent a disproportionate share of proprietary derivatives revenue. If the S&P 500 lost its benchmark status or a competitor broke the exclusivity arrangement, it would be material.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** Yes. Cboe runs the marketplace where people trade options on the S&P 500 and the VIX. It earns a small fee on every contract, sells access to its technology, and licenses its market data. More volatility and more participants mean more volume, which flows almost directly to the bottom line because the cost of matching one more trade is near zero. The business model is a toll bridge on derivatives activity, with exclusive rights to the two most important products.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Cboe owns an irreplaceable franchise in SPX and VIX derivatives with 67% adjusted operating margins, secular volume growth from 0DTE adoption, and a clean balance sheet — a textbook toll-bridge business.
- **Bear case:** Revenue remains volume-dependent; a sustained low-volatility regime or regulatory intervention on 0DTE options could compress the earnings trajectory.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is simple, transparent, and well-documented in public filings; the key drivers are quantifiable.

## Red Flags
- Product concentration risk: SPX options represent an outsized share of high-margin revenue
- Volume dependency means quarters with low volatility can disappoint
- Regulatory scrutiny of 0DTE options (potential flash-crash concerns) is an emerging risk

## Score: 9 / 10
Cboe's business model is among the most transparent in financial services — a small number of quantifiable drivers, a clear toll-bridge economics structure, and exclusive ownership of the two most important volatility products in the world make it exceptionally easy to understand.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — CBOE

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Data Sources:** Cboe Q4 2025 earnings release, Cboe IR filings, Morningstar, William Blair initiation, Star Tribune/Finterra analysis, Cboe.com insights, PRNewswire, Simply Wall St

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Exclusive license to list S&P 500 index options (SPX) through December 2032 (exclusive) and 2033 (non-exclusive), plus proprietary ownership of VIX — no competitor can replicate these products | 5 |
| 2 | Network effects: liquidity begets liquidity; SPX options have the tightest bid-ask spreads, which attracts more volume, which tightens spreads further — a self-reinforcing cycle that is extremely difficult to disrupt | 5 |
| 3 | Adjusted operating margins reached 67.1% in Q4 2025, demonstrating near-zero marginal cost of incremental volume and massive operating leverage characteristic of a wide-moat exchange | 5 |
| 4 | SPX options captured a record 74% market share of S&P 500 index options (vs. competing SPY options), up from ~60% a few years ago — the moat is widening, not narrowing | 4 |
| 5 | Strategic realignment (exiting Australia, Canada, corporate listings) concentrates the business on its highest-moat, highest-margin derivatives franchise | 3 |
| 6 | Regulatory moat: operating a registered national securities exchange requires SEC/CFTC approval, creating a significant barrier to new entrants | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Moat type.** Cboe benefits from a rare combination of moat sources that reinforce each other. The primary moat is intellectual property and contractual exclusivity: SPX options can only trade on Cboe exchanges under an exclusive license from S&P Dow Jones Indices, running through 2032. VIX is a proprietary index owned by Cboe itself — no license can be revoked. This is not a brand advantage or a cost advantage that can be competed away; it is a legal monopoly on two of the most important financial products in the world. Layered on top is a powerful network effect: the deep liquidity pool in SPX options (3.9 million contracts/day in 2025) creates the tightest spreads, which attracts more participants, which deepens liquidity further. Switching costs are moderate at the broker level (firms can route to other exchanges) but high at the product level (there is no substitute for SPX options with the same contract specifications, tax treatment under Section 1256, and cash-settlement mechanics). Finally, regulatory barriers — the licensing requirements, capital standards, and compliance infrastructure needed to operate a national securities exchange — prevent casual new entrants.

**Durability (10+ year view).** The SPX exclusivity runs through 2032, providing at least six more years of guaranteed monopoly pricing. Even after 2032, the liquidity network effect makes it nearly impossible for a competitor to replicate SPX's trading ecosystem — no options exchange has successfully moved entrenched liquidity from an incumbent in the modern electronic era. VIX is owned outright and faces no expiration risk. The secular shift toward options-based portfolio strategies (including 0DTE for intraday hedging) is structural, not cyclical, and disproportionately benefits the SPX franchise. The biggest long-term risk is regulatory: if the SEC or CFTC were to mandate changes to exchange exclusivity arrangements or restrict 0DTE trading, the moat could narrow. But this would require unprecedented regulatory action against a product class that serves legitimate hedging functions.

**Evidence in numbers.** The financial evidence for a wide moat is overwhelming. Gross margins of 40.9% understate the economics because CBOE's reported revenue includes pass-through liquidity payments; on a net revenue basis, adjusted operating margins are 67%. ROIC of 17.3% on a capital-light balance sheet, with FCF conversion of 153%, demonstrates that Cboe earns returns far above its cost of capital. Revenue per contract on proprietary index options is multiples higher than on commoditized multi-list options. The fact that SPX market share has risen to 74% of all S&P 500 index options even as total volumes have surged shows pricing power coexisting with volume growth — the hallmark of a widening moat.

**Weakest point of the moat.** Product concentration is the Achilles' heel. The SPX franchise generates a disproportionate share of high-margin revenue. If S&P Dow Jones Indices were to renegotiate the license terms dramatically at the 2032 renewal, or if a structural market shift reduced demand for S&P 500 hedging specifically, the moat's economic value would shrink even if the legal protections remained. Additionally, the 0DTE phenomenon — while currently a tailwind — could become a regulatory target if it is associated with a market disruption event. The moat is not diversified across many products; it is deep but narrow.

**Do advantages reset every cycle?** No. Exchange liquidity network effects are among the most persistent competitive advantages in business. The SPX/VIX franchise has been dominant for decades and has only strengthened as electronification, 0DTE innovation, and the growth of options-based ETF strategies have expanded the addressable market. Each market cycle — whether high-volatility or low — reinforces Cboe's position because participants return to the deepest liquidity pool. The moat does not require reinvention.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Cboe possesses the rarest type of moat — a legal monopoly on irreplaceable financial products reinforced by self-strengthening network effects, producing 67% operating margins with a widening market share trajectory.
- **Bear case:** The moat is deep but concentrated in SPX/VIX; an adverse regulatory ruling on 0DTE options or a renegotiation of the S&P license in 2032 could structurally impair the franchise.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat sources are observable, quantifiable, and have been durable over multiple market cycles.

## Red Flags
- SPX exclusivity has a defined expiration (2032/2033) — renewal terms are unknown
- Regulatory scrutiny of 0DTE options is real and could intensify after a market dislocation
- EU PFOF ban in 2026 may reduce retail liquidity in European operations (minor to overall business)
- Product concentration means the moat, while wide, is not diversified

## Score: 9 / 10
Cboe's moat is among the widest in financial services — exclusive contractual rights to the world's most important index options, reinforced by deep liquidity network effects and 67% operating margins. The only reason this is not a 10 is the product concentration risk and the finite (though distant) SPX exclusivity timeline.

# Management & Capital Allocation — CBOE

**Date:** 2026-03-18

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** At 0.51%, insider ownership is low in absolute terms. This is common among large-cap exchange operators — CME, ICE, and Nasdaq all have similarly low insider stakes. It means management's personal wealth is not materially tied to the stock price, which weakens the owner-operator alignment that Buffett-style analysis prizes. However, Donohue's $6M sign-on equity grant and $10.1M annual LTI target ensure his forward compensation is heavily equity-linked, partially offsetting the low legacy ownership. Institutional ownership is high (~90%), and the Vanguard/BlackRock passive complex are the largest holders. There have been modest insider sales ($1.6M over the last 90 days) but no notable insider buying.

**Capital allocation.** Cboe's capital allocation track record is above average. The company has returned significant capital through buybacks — retiring roughly 22% of diluted shares since the program began in 2011, which is meaningful cumulative value creation for a company with minimal reinvestment needs. The current $614M remaining authorization provides runway for continued repurchases. Dividends are modest ($0.72/quarter, ~1% yield) and represent a small fraction of FCF ($1.7B in FY2025), leaving ample room for buybacks and opportunistic investment. The balance sheet is conservatively managed: $2.3B cash vs. $1.6B debt, net debt/EBITDA of ~0.4x, and interest coverage of 31x. SBC of $50.4M against $1.1B net income represents ~4.5% dilution offset — manageable but worth monitoring.

**M&A and reinvestment.** Cboe's M&A history is mixed. The 2017 acquisition of Bats Global Markets was transformative and well-executed, diversifying Cboe from a single-venue options exchange into a multi-exchange, multi-asset global operator. However, several subsequent investments — the digital assets push, the Australian and Canadian equity exchange expansions, and some analytics acquisitions — failed to generate adequate returns. To management's credit, the 2025-2026 realignment demonstrates the intellectual honesty to admit mistakes and exit non-core businesses rather than doubling down. The strategic pruning will reduce net revenue by ~3% but cut adjusted operating expenses by 8-10%, a trade-off that clearly favors margins and focus.

**Compensation design.** Donohue's package is structured with a significant equity component ($10.1M LTI target), which aligns incentives with shareholders over a multi-year horizon. The $6M sign-on grant compensates for forfeited compensation at his prior role, which is standard practice for CEO-level hires. The 150% bonus target on a $1.3M base implies total cash compensation of ~$3.25M in an on-target year, with equity representing the majority of total comp. Performance metrics for the LTI program were not detailed in the public announcements reviewed, which is a mild governance gap — investors should verify that vesting is tied to operating metrics (revenue growth, EPS, ROIC) rather than purely time-based.

**Candor and consistency.** The Tilly resignation in 2023 was a genuine governance failure — the CEO's departure following a board investigation into undisclosed personal relationships with employees raised serious questions about tone at the top. However, the board's response was appropriate: an independent investigation, a forced resignation, and an external search that produced a highly credentialed replacement. Donohue's early strategic decisions (the realignment, cost discipline, focus on derivatives) suggest he communicates like an operator — specific about what he will and will not do. The 2026 guidance of "mid single-digit" organic revenue growth was measured and realistic, not promotional.

**Governance.** The board is independent-majority with relevant financial markets expertise. The separation of Chairman and CEO roles following the Tilly scandal was a positive governance reform. The mixed-shelf registration filed in early 2026 provides financial flexibility but also warrants monitoring — if used for dilutive equity issuance rather than debt, it would be a negative signal. So far, there is no indication of that intent.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** New CEO with deep derivatives expertise is executing a focused realignment toward the highest-moat businesses, backed by a conservatively leveraged balance sheet and a strong track record of buyback-driven value creation.
- **Bear case:** Very low insider ownership, a prior CEO governance scandal, and a compensation structure whose performance metrics are not fully transparent create alignment concerns.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Donohue has been CEO for less than a year, and while early signals are positive, the track record is too short to rate with high confidence.

## Red Flags
- Prior CEO Edward Tilly resigned in 2023 following a board-led investigation — a meaningful governance failure
- Insider ownership of 0.51% is low; no notable insider buying
- Mixed M&A track record — several international and digital asset expansions destroyed value before being reversed
- LTI performance metrics for CEO compensation not fully disclosed in reviewed materials
- $6M sign-on equity grant is large, though standard for this market cap

## Score: 7 / 10
Management earns an above-average score on the strength of disciplined buybacks (22% of shares retired), a conservatively managed balance sheet, and a credible new CEO executing a sensible strategic realignment. The score is capped by low insider ownership, a prior governance scandal, and insufficient tenure to fully evaluate the new leadership team.

# Business Economics — CBOE

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Data Sources:** Cboe Global Markets Q4 2025 & FY2025 earnings release, 10-K filings, yfinance data, MacroTrends, investor presentations

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC of 17.3% in FY2025 with ROE of 23.4%, reflecting strong capital efficiency on a relatively asset-light exchange model | 5 |
| 2 | Adjusted operating EBITDA margin expanded to ~68% on net revenue in FY2025 (+700bps y/y), demonstrating powerful operating leverage | 5 |
| 3 | FCF of $1.7B with 152.9% FCF conversion ratio indicates exceptionally strong cash generation well above reported earnings | 5 |
| 4 | CapEx of just $71M against $4.7B gross revenue (~1.5% of revenue) confirms the business is extremely asset-light | 4 |
| 5 | Data Vantage recurring revenue grew 9-11% y/y with ~90% of growth from new subscriptions and unit sales, providing a durable non-transactional revenue base | 4 |
| 6 | Options segment net revenue surged 22% y/y to $1.5B in FY2025, driven by record VIX and index options volumes — volume-dependent but structurally growing | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** CBOE generated ROIC of 17.3% and ROE of 23.4% in FY2025, both strong figures for a financial exchange. The ROE has been trending upward from the depressed FY2022 level (when a $569M goodwill impairment dragged net income to $235M). Excluding that anomaly, net income has grown from $761M (FY2023) to $765M (FY2024) to $1.1B (FY2025), a compound trajectory reflecting organic volume growth, pricing power, and operating leverage. ROIC in the mid-to-high teens is above the cost of capital and consistent with a business possessing durable competitive advantages, though it sits below the 20%+ tier of the very best capital-light compounders partly because of the significant goodwill from past acquisitions (BATS Global Markets in 2017, EuroCCP, and others).

**Margin Structure.** The margin profile is one of CBOE's strongest attributes. Gross margins of 40.9% on total revenue look modest, but this is misleading because CBOE reports gross revenue inclusive of liquidity payments (rebates paid to market makers), which are essentially pass-through costs. On a net revenue basis ($2.4B in FY2025), the adjusted operating EBITDA margin reaches approximately 68%, up from ~63% in FY2024. This margin expansion on 17% net revenue growth is textbook operating leverage — incremental revenue flows to the bottom line with minimal variable cost. The operating margin on a GAAP basis was 32.1%, but this includes amortization of acquired intangibles from past acquisitions. The gap between GAAP operating margin and adjusted EBITDA margin reflects the asset-light reality of running electronic exchanges where the cost base is largely fixed (technology, regulatory compliance, headcount).

**Cash Generation.** Free cash flow of $1.7B in FY2025 against $1.1B in net income yields an extraordinary FCF conversion ratio of 153%. This is driven by minimal capex requirements ($71M), modest working capital needs, and the fact that depreciation/amortization ($122M) and stock-based compensation ($50M) are non-cash charges flowing through the income statement. Owner earnings (FCF adjusted for SBC and maintenance capex) of $1.2B still represent a robust conversion. The business essentially converts the vast majority of operating income into cash, a hallmark of high-quality exchange businesses.

**Capital Intensity and Asset-Lightness.** CBOE is quintessentially asset-light. CapEx of $71M on $4.7B in gross revenue (1.5%) or $2.4B in net revenue (3%) is trivially small. The primary capital deployed is in technology infrastructure (matching engines, data centers, network connectivity) and acquired intangibles/goodwill from M&A. Maintenance capex is likely $30-40M, with the remainder allocated to growth initiatives (new products, geographic expansion, platform upgrades). This means nearly all free cash flow is genuinely distributable — the business does not need to reinvest heavily to maintain its competitive position.

**Revenue Quality.** CBOE's revenue has two distinct components: (1) transaction-based revenue from trading and clearing fees, which comprised the majority of the $2.4B in net revenue and is inherently volume-dependent, and (2) non-transaction recurring revenue, primarily from the Data Vantage segment (market data subscriptions, index licensing, analytics), which grew 9-11% y/y and provides a high-margin, subscription-based revenue floor. The mix is shifting gradually toward recurring streams. Options net revenue ($1.5B, +22%) drove FY2025 growth, benefiting from structurally rising options market participation (retail + institutional), 0DTE product innovation, and VIX product expansion. While transaction revenue is cyclical, the secular trend in options volumes has been strongly upward for over a decade, and CBOE's proprietary products (SPX options, VIX futures) face no direct competition.

**Operating Leverage.** The 17% net revenue growth translating to 25% adjusted EBITDA growth and 24% adjusted EPS growth confirms significant operating leverage. The fixed cost base (technology, compliance, headcount) grows at low-to-mid single digits, meaning each incremental dollar of net revenue drops through at very high margins. This was demonstrated clearly in FY2025 as record volumes drove outsized profit growth. The company guided for mid-single-digit organic net revenue growth in 2026, which at these margins should still translate to solid earnings growth, though the pace will moderate from the exceptional FY2025.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** CBOE is a toll-booth business on the structurally growing global options and data markets, with ~68% EBITDA margins, minimal capex, and proprietary products that face no direct competition.
- **Bear case:** Transaction revenue (the majority) is volume-dependent and could compress in a prolonged low-volatility environment; ROIC is held back by goodwill from past acquisitions that may not fully earn their cost of capital.
- **Confidence:** High — financial data is comprehensive, the business model is well-understood, and margin/FCF trends are clearly positive.

## Red Flags
- FY2022 goodwill impairment ($569M) on the digital assets business signals management made at least one poor capital allocation decision in recent history
- Revenue still heavily weighted toward transaction fees; a sustained period of low market volatility would compress earnings
- Stock-based compensation of $50M is modest but growing and should be monitored

## Score: 8 / 10
CBOE demonstrates strong business economics with 17%+ ROIC, exceptional FCF conversion (153%), extreme asset-lightness (1.5% capex/revenue ratio), and powerful operating leverage driving margins to ~68% on net revenue. The score misses the 9-10 tier due to ROIC below 20% (dragged by acquisition goodwill) and the inherent cyclicality of volume-dependent transaction revenue.

The stock is 30% above even the base case IV.

### Business Margin of Safety

Where price fails, the business partially compensates. CBOE possesses genuine structural advantages that provide a business margin of safety: (1) exclusive licensing of SPX and VIX index options creates a legal moat that competitors cannot easily replicate, (2) the network effect of deep liquidity pools makes switching costs high for institutional participants, (3) the capital-light model converts over 70% of net revenue to EBITDA and requires minimal reinvestment capex (~$50-70M annually on $2.4B net revenue), and (4) the net cash position means even a severe earnings downturn would not threaten solvency. The asymmetry is deeply unfavorable at current prices. From $287: downside to bear case ($155) = -46%, downside to base case ($222) = -23%, upside to bull case ($295) = +3%. A rational risk-reward framework wants at least 2-3x upside vs downside. Here we have roughly 0.07x — the opposite of what a margin of safety framework demands. Even if we use the base case as a "worst likely outcome," the stock is 30% overvalued.

### Zero Scenarios

Total permanent loss is extremely unlikely for CBOE. The business would survive: (a) a prolonged bear market (volatility actually helps derivatives volumes), (b) a recession (reduced equity volumes partially offset by hedging demand), (c) management turnover (the moat is structural, not personality-dependent). Scenarios that could cause severe impairment (>70% loss) include: (1) loss of exclusive SPX/VIX licensing rights through regulatory or legal action — very low probability but catastrophic, (2) a fundamental shift away from options-based hedging/speculation — unlikely given the secular trend, (3) a technology failure or cyber attack that destroys market confidence in CBOE's platforms — low probability, recoverable.

Early warning: new exchange launches (now ~20 options exchanges), pricing pressure in equity options, declining capture rates. CBOE's revenue is heavily concentrated in U.S. derivatives, and within that, in S&P 500-linked products. The Data Vantage segment provides some diversification (growing mid-to-high single digits) and the company has been expanding internationally (European derivatives, APAC). Geographic concentration is primarily North American, though the company is investing in global expansion.

- **Regulatory:** The SEC has been actively scrutinizing the options market, particularly 0DTE products and market structure. A hostile regulatory environment could cap growth even if it doesn't restrict existing products.
- **Fraud/Accounting:** Low risk. CBOE is a regulated exchange with transparent revenue (transaction fees + data). Revenue recognition is straightforward. No red flags in accounting practices.
- **Technology Disruption:** Decentralized finance (DeFi) and blockchain-based derivatives remain a theoretical long-term threat but have not gained institutional adoption for index options.
