## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Declining from Extraordinary Levels, Still Elite.** Novo Nordisk's ROIC trajectory — 57.4% (FY22), 70.5% (FY23), 55.5% (FY24), 36.0% (FY25) — demands careful decomposition. The numerator (NOPAT) has continued growing: net income rose from DKK 83.7B to DKK 102.4B between FY23 and FY25 even as the company absorbed a mid-single-digit operating profit drag from integrating the three Catalent fill-finish sites. The denominator is the story: total assets expanded from DKK 465.6B to DKK 542.9B in FY25 alone, driven by the USD 11.7B Catalent acquisition (closed December 2024) and ongoing greenfield manufacturing builds across Denmark, the US, France, and Ireland. ROE followed a similar path, compressing from 88.1% to 60.7% as equity expanded. At 36%, Novo's ROIC remains far above its ~5.6% WACC and exceeds most quality compounders (typical range 15-25%). The structural question is whether incremental returns on the DKK 68B+ of growth capex deployed in FY25 will approach the legacy base's economics, or whether diminishing returns have begun. The quant model's sensitivity analysis shows that even at base-case assumptions (7.4% Y1 revenue growth fading to 3%), the DCF yields an intrinsic value of DKK 800+, suggesting the market is pricing in a far more pessimistic return-on-investment outcome than historical data supports.

**Margin Structure: Real Compression, Partially Structural.** Gross margin held in the 83.9-84.7% range from FY22-FY24 — a hallmark of Novo's pricing power in GLP-1 markets. The FY25 decline to 81.0% is the first meaningful crack, driven by three distinct forces: (1) depreciation and amortization from the Catalent fill-finish sites flowing through COGS (D&A nearly doubled from DKK 9.4B in FY23 to DKK 22.0B in FY25), (2) the MFN pricing agreement with the US administration committing Novo to lower realized prices on Ozempic and Wegovy (though notably, the deals do not require list price cuts on existing products, limiting the near-term margin hit), and (3) unfavorable mix as lower-margin Wegovy grows faster than higher-margin Ozempic. Operating margin declined to 41.3% from 44.2%, further compressed by R&D spending rising to DKK 52.0B (16.8% of revenue) as Novo pushes next-generation molecules (oral semaglutide, amycretin) and CagriSema despite the February 2026 trial failure. Net margin fell to 33.1% from 34.8%, with interest expense nearly tripling (DKK 1.6B to DKK 4.2B) as Novo services Catalent acquisition debt. Despite the compression, a 41% operating margin and 33% net margin remain world-class for a scaled pharmaceutical manufacturer.

**Cash Generation: The Growth CapEx Vortex.** This is the headline story of FY25. Operating cash flow was a robust DKK 119.1B, essentially flat YoY despite margin pressure — demonstrating the underlying cash engine is intact. But reported FCF plunged from DKK 69.7B to DKK 29.0B because capex consumed DKK 90.1B, nearly as much as net income. FCF conversion crashed from 69.0% to 28.3%, the lowest in the company's modern history. The quant model's owner earnings decomposition is essential context: maintenance capex (proxied by D&A) was only DKK 22.0B (24% of total capex), meaning DKK 68.1B was growth capex invested in expanding API production, fill-finish capacity, the 170,000 sqm Kalundborg mega-facility, the $4.1B Clayton NC fill-finish plant, the EUR 432M Irish oral GLP-1 facility, and the $2.3B French manufacturing upgrade. Adjusted owner earnings (net income minus maintenance capex) were DKK 102.4B — identical to reported net income. This distinction is critical: the underlying cash engine produces DKK 100B+ per year; the FCF shortfall is a deliberate investment decision. Management guides FY26 FCF of DKK 35-45B on DKK 55B capex, implying some improvement but continued heavy spending. The path back to DKK 70B+ FCF likely requires another 2-3 years of capex normalization.

**Capital Intensity: Structural Transformation.** Novo has undergone the most dramatic capital-intensity shift in major pharma history. Capex as a percentage of revenue rose from 8.4% (FY22) to 17.7% (FY24) to 29.1% (FY25). The company has effectively transformed from a capital-light biotech licensing model into a vertically integrated manufacturer. The investment rationale is sound: GLP-1 demand vastly exceeds supply capacity (the FDA only resolved the semaglutide shortage in February 2025), proprietary manufacturing creates barriers to entry, and the assets being built will serve products with 80%+ gross margins. The risks are equally real: this capacity is being built just as pricing compresses (MFN), competition intensifies (Eli Lilly's tirzepatide achieved 58% US GLP-1 market share in Q3 2025), patent cliffs approach (semaglutide compound patent expires 2032 in the US, 2026 in China with 15+ generic manufacturers preparing launches), and the CagriSema next-generation candidate stumbled (failed non-inferiority vs tirzepatide in February 2026, triggering a 16% single-day stock decline). Management's signal that "capex investments are expected to decline" from 2026 onward provides some comfort, but the asset base is permanently larger.

**Revenue Quality and Concentration Risk.** Revenue grew 75% in three years (DKK 177.0B FY22 to DKK 309.1B FY25), driven almost entirely by GLP-1 volume expansion. Obesity care (Wegovy) grew 26% in DKK terms (31% CER) in FY25. This revenue is high-quality by nature: prescription-based, chronic-therapy, high patient retention, strong switching costs (patients stabilized on GLP-1 therapy rarely discontinue voluntarily). However, concentration risk is acute — semaglutide-based products represent the vast majority of growth and an outsized share of profitability. The 2026 guidance for -5% to -13% CER sales decline introduces a new dynamic: Novo is simultaneously facing MFN-driven US pricing pressure, loss of semaglutide exclusivity in markets covering roughly 40% of world population (China, Brazil, India, Canada, Turkey from 2026), Eli Lilly's competitive gains, and the pipeline setback of CagriSema. The combination of heavy operating leverage built for growth (expanded sales force, manufacturing overhead, R&D pipeline) meeting revenue contraction could amplify margin pressure through 2026-2027.

**Buyback Pause and Capital Return Shift.** Share repurchases collapsed from DKK 29.9B (FY23) to DKK 20.2B (FY24) to DKK 1.4B (FY25), confirming management's capital allocation has pivoted entirely from shareholder returns to physical infrastructure. The 6.70% dividend yield at current prices is notable but reflects the stock's 60% decline over the past 12 months rather than dividend growth. SBC of DKK 2.3B (FY24) is modest at <1% of revenue. The capital return profile will remain subdued until the investment cycle completes and FCF normalizes — likely not before 2028.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Owner earnings of DKK 102B are intact beneath the capex storm; the investment cycle is building proprietary manufacturing capacity for a multi-decade GLP-1 franchise, peak capex is approaching, and once growth investment normalizes, FCF should recover toward DKK 80-100B on an 81% gross margin business that remains far above its cost of capital.
- **Bear case:** Margin compression from MFN pricing, Catalent D&A, and emerging-market genericization may prove structural rather than cyclical; the CagriSema failure weakens the next-generation pipeline story; ROIC may settle at 15-20% as the business becomes permanently capital-heavy; and the first negative growth guidance in a decade signals a competitive inflection point.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Core business economics remain objectively strong (81% gross margin, 41% operating margin, 36% ROIC on a vastly expanded base), but the confluence of negative 2026 guidance, MFN pricing impact, CagriSema trial failure, and a structurally different capital intensity profile creates genuine uncertainty about whether the historic earnings power fully reasserts itself.

## Red Flags
- FCF conversion collapsed from 84% to 28% in two years — even if growth-capex-driven, execution risk on a multi-site global manufacturing buildout is material
- 2026 guidance calls for -5% to -13% revenue and operating profit decline at CER — first negative outlook in over a decade
- Interest expense nearly tripled YoY (DKK 1.6B to DKK 4.2B) and will remain elevated while debt exceeds DKK 100B
- CagriSema trial failure (Feb 2026) removes a key next-generation growth catalyst and strengthens Eli Lilly's competitive position
- Share buybacks effectively paused (DKK 1.4B vs DKK 29.9B two years prior), removing a significant capital return lever
- Semaglutide LOE in markets covering ~40% of world population from 2026, with 15+ Chinese generic manufacturers preparing launches
- Capex/revenue ratio of 29% represents a structural transformation from asset-light pharma to capital-intensive manufacturer

## Score: 7 / 10
Novo Nordisk's underlying business economics remain genuinely strong — 81% gross margin, 36% ROIC on a vastly expanded capital base, DKK 102B owner earnings, and DKK 119B operating cash flow — but the deliberate transformation into a capital-intensive manufacturer, first-ever negative growth guidance, CagriSema pipeline disappointment, and accelerating competitive and pricing headwinds represent a material step down from the 9/10 franchise economics of FY22-FY23, warranting a score that reflects excellence with clear deterioration at the margin.
