# Compact Checklist — UNP

**Date:** 2026-03-22

1. **Business**: Union Pacific moves freight on 32,000 miles of irreplaceable western U.S. rail, charging per carload across ~10,000 customers in bulk, industrial, and intermodal segments, generating $24-25B in stable annual revenue because rail moves a ton of freight 500 miles on one gallon of fuel — 3-4x cheaper than trucking.
2. **Moat**: A natural duopoly with BNSF, built on physically irreplicable infrastructure that would cost $20-30B+ and decades of impossible permitting to reproduce, plus geographic exclusivity where many shippers have access to only one railroad.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of 15-17% consistently exceeds the ~8.5% cost of capital, operating margins hit 40.2% in FY2025 (59.3% operating ratio), and owner earnings of $5.1-5.8B annually convert at 77-87% of net income to real cash.
4. **Management**: Capital allocation is competent but not exceptional — 20 years of dividend growth and 9% share count reduction are offset by procyclical buyback timing ($6.3B at 2022 highs, only $705M at 2023 lows) and an ambitious $85B Norfolk Southern merger pursuit that carries serious execution risk.
5. **Debt risk**: With 8.0x interest coverage, 2.5x Debt/EBITDA, an A credit rating, and stress-tested ability to generate $2-2.5B in FCF even in a 30% revenue decline, UNP survives a severe recession without equity issuance — though the pending Norfolk Southern merger could add $25-30B in combined debt.
6. **Price**: At $235 versus a base IV of $230, the market requires exactly base-case execution — 2% revenue growth, 40%+ margins, 15x exit multiple — with zero margin for error, and only 8 of 25 sensitivity grid cells produce IV above the current price.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business moat is a fortress (duopoly, essential infrastructure, 150-year track record), but there is no price margin of safety — conservative IV of $160 sits 32% below the current price, and Monte Carlo gives only 41% probability of undervaluation.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if operating margins fall below 35% for two consecutive years without a macro excuse, if the Norfolk Southern merger destroys shareholder value through overpayment or integration failure, or if the STB implements forced reciprocal switching that structurally erodes geographic pricing power.
