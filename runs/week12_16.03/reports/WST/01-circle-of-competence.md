## Detailed Analysis

West Pharmaceutical Services makes the components that sit between a drug and the patient. Think of the rubber stopper in a vaccine vial, the plunger in a pre-filled syringe, or the seal on a biologic cartridge. These are elastomeric closures and containment systems — the physical packaging that ensures an injectable drug stays sterile, stable, and deliverable. West also makes self-injection devices (like auto-injectors) and provides contract manufacturing services for pharma companies that need someone to assemble and fill their drug delivery systems. The company was founded in 1923 and has spent a century becoming the dominant supplier of these components globally.

The revenue model is essentially a consumable/component sale attached to drug production volumes. Every time Pfizer makes a batch of vaccines, or Novo Nordisk fills a run of GLP-1 pens, they need West components. Revenue scales with global injectable drug production — not with any single drug's success or failure. The Proprietary Products segment (82% of revenue) sells these components and devices directly; the Contract Manufacturing segment (18%) provides turnkey assembly, filling, and packaging services. Within Proprietary Products, the key sub-mix is critical: HVP (high-value product) components — which are engineered, coated, and validated to higher specifications for biologics — generated $389.8M in Q4 2025 alone, representing 48% of total quarterly revenue and growing at 20.3% YoY. Standard components (legacy stoppers for small-molecule vials) are a slower-growth but stable base.

The 2-4 variables that drive this business are clear. First, global injectable drug volumes — as the pharma industry shifts toward biologics, biosimilars, and GLP-1 therapies, more drugs require West's HVP components. Second, the mix shift from standard to HVP products — HVP carries significantly higher margins and pricing, so as biologics grow as a share of the pharma market, West's revenue per unit and margins expand. Third, the regulatory stickiness of the installed base — once a drug is filed with a West component in its Drug Master File (DMF), switching is prohibitively expensive and takes 2-3 years, so revenue per approved drug is essentially permanent. Fourth, contract manufacturing utilization, particularly for self-injection devices tied to obesity/diabetes drugs.

Revenue predictability is high. This is not a lumpy, project-based business. Drug production is ongoing, and West's components are consumed with every dose manufactured. There is some cyclicality — COVID-era vaccine demand created a surge that normalized in 2023-2024 — but the underlying trajectory is steady mid-single-digit organic growth. FY2025 organic growth was 4.3%, and 2026 guidance calls for 5-7%. The GLP-1 tailwind (Ozempic, Mounjaro, and expanding indications) provides a visible secular growth vector for HVP components over the next 5+ years.

Customer concentration is low. West supplies essentially all of the top 20 global pharma companies. No single customer represents a dominant share of revenue. The customer base spans Big Pharma (Pfizer, Novo Nordisk, Eli Lilly, AbbVie, Roche), biotech, and generics/biosimilar manufacturers worldwide. This diversification is a structural strength — West is not dependent on any single drug franchise or company.

Can I explain this business in two minutes? Absolutely. West makes the rubber and plastic parts that go into injectable drug packaging — stoppers, seals, plungers, and self-injection devices. Every injectable drug needs these components, and once a drug is approved with West's parts, the pharma company is locked in for the life of that drug. West makes money by selling these components at scale to essentially every major pharma company in the world. The more injectable drugs get made, the more West sells. The shift to biologics and GLP-1s means more of those sales are higher-margin HVP products. That is the entire business.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** West is the dominant, regulation-entrenched supplier of consumable components for the fastest-growing segment of pharma (biologics/GLP-1s), with visible multi-year volume and mix tailwinds.
- **Bear case:** A normalization of GLP-1 growth or a shift away from injectable delivery (oral biologics) could slow HVP growth below current expectations.
- **Confidence:** High — This business is straightforward, well-documented, and has a century of operating history. The key drivers are identifiable and trackable.

## Red Flags

- COVID vaccine demand created a temporary revenue surge in 2021-2022 that has since normalized; investors should not extrapolate that era's growth rates
- The SmartDose 3.5mL divestiture to AbbVie ($112.5M, ~4% of sales) suggests some delivery device bets did not achieve scale — worth monitoring whether other device platforms face similar outcomes
- Contract Manufacturing segment growth (1.9% organic in Q4) lags the Proprietary segment meaningfully

This is one of the most understandable businesses in healthcare — a consumable component supplier with regulatory lock-in, diversified customers, and clear volume/mix drivers. A teenager could grasp it.
