## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and structure: Conservative and well-laddered.** At March 2025, total debt stood at GBP 849M with net debt of GBP 426M (excluding IFRS 16 lease liabilities; GBP 536M including them). The debt-to-EBITDA ratio of 1.6x gross and 0.8x net is very conservative for a business of Halma's quality and cash generation. By September 2025 (H1 2025/26), net debt had risen to GBP 641M with gearing at 1.03x EBITDA, reflecting the record GBP 451M of acquisition spending. The debt is principally composed of US Private Placements (long-dated, fixed-rate) and a syndicated revolving credit facility. In April 2024, Halma completed a new GBP 336M Private Placement in USD and EUR tranches, and in May 2024 extended its revolving credit facility maturity to May 2029. The staggered maturity profile across the next ten years means Halma faces no debt wall — maturities come due gradually, reducing refinancing risk substantially.

**Interest coverage: Very comfortable.** EBIT interest coverage of 12.8x in FY2025 is excellent. Using the more conservative EBITDA-based measure, coverage is approximately 16x. Interest expense has risen from GBP 8.3M in FY2022 to GBP 32.7M in FY2025, reflecting both higher debt levels (to fund acquisitions) and higher rates, but the growth in operating earnings has more than kept pace. The Private Placement structure means much of the debt carries fixed rates locked in at issuance, providing insulation from further rate increases. Even if interest costs doubled from here, coverage would remain above 6x — perfectly manageable.

**Liquidity: Strong and improving.** The current ratio of 2.4x is well above the 1.5x threshold that signals comfort. Cash on hand of GBP 313M at FY2025, plus undrawn revolving credit facility capacity, gives Halma substantial liquidity headroom. The business generates GBP 490M+ of operating cash flow annually, so even without external financing, Halma can fund its typical annual acquisition programme (GBP 100-200M in a normal year) entirely from internal cash generation. The GBP 451M record acquisition year in FY2026 was the exception, requiring additional drawdowns, but gearing only ticked up to 1.03x — still very modest.

**Refinancing risk: Minimal.** With committed facilities of GBP 1.25B maturing progressively through 2029 and beyond, and financial covenants set at 3.5x leverage and 4x interest cover (versus actual levels of ~1x and ~13x respectively), Halma has enormous covenant headroom. The company would need to see a catastrophic deterioration in earnings before approaching covenant limits. The mix of US Private Placements (which are long-dated, typically 7-12 year maturities) and the revolving facility (extended to 2029) means there is no near-term maturity cliff. Halma's investment-grade credit quality and long track record give it reliable access to debt markets.

**Off-balance-sheet obligations: Modest.** IFRS 16 lease liabilities of approximately GBP 110M are now on-balance-sheet. Pension obligations are modest — Halma's UK defined benefit scheme is well-funded. The decentralised acquisition model means earnout liabilities exist (contingent consideration on recent deals), but these are typically small relative to group cash flow. No material off-balance-sheet structures, guarantees, or special purpose vehicles were identified.

**Stress test: Revenue drops 30% for two years.** This is a severe scenario for Halma, whose revenue has never declined by more than single digits even in the worst downturns (the business actually grew through 2020). But testing it: FY2025 revenue of GBP 2.25B drops to GBP 1.58B. Assuming operating margins compress to ~14% (from 21.6%), that yields EBIT of approximately GBP 220M. Against GBP 33M of interest expense, coverage is still 6.7x. FCF would likely fall to GBP 150-200M range (lower revenue but reduced capex and working capital release). Annual dividend costs approximately GBP 90M. Even in this stress scenario, Halma generates enough cash to service its debt, maintain dividends, and avoid any equity issuance. The only scenario requiring external capital would be if Halma chose to make large acquisitions during the downturn — but that would be opportunistic, not forced. The business would survive comfortably.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The balance sheet provides a genuine strategic advantage — Halma can deploy capital into acquisitions during downturns when competitors are capital-constrained, and the staggered maturity profile eliminates refinancing risk for the foreseeable future.
- **Bear case:** Continued aggressive acquisition spending pushes net debt toward 2x EBITDA, reducing the margin of safety and limiting future M&A optionality without equity dilution.
- **Confidence:** High — The debt metrics are unambiguous, the maturity profile is well-documented, and the stress test math works clearly in Halma's favour.

## Red Flags

- Net debt increased from GBP 426M to GBP 641M in just six months (H1 2025/26), driven by record acquisition spending — the pace of leveraging up bears watching
- Tangible equity is negligible due to GBP 2B+ of goodwill and intangibles from acquisitions; if impairments materialise, reported equity would erode rapidly
- Rising interest expense (GBP 8M to GBP 33M over three years) reflects the true cost of the acquisition-funded growth model

## Score: 8 / 10

Halma's balance sheet is conservatively managed with net debt/EBITDA of ~1x, 12.8x interest coverage, staggered maturities through 2029+, and enough cash generation to survive a severe downturn without raising equity — a clear "conservative" rating that narrowly misses "fortress" only because of the goodwill-heavy asset base and accelerating acquisition-driven leverage.
