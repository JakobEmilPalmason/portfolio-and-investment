## Detailed Analysis

Sherwin-Williams possesses a wide, multi-layered moat that is among the most durable in the industrial sector. The primary moat source is the company-owned store network — a vertically integrated distribution system with no true parallel in the coatings industry or, arguably, in any other consumer-industrial category. With over 4,800 locations, Sherwin-Williams has placed a store within a short drive of virtually every professional painting contractor in North America. PPG, the next largest competitor, operates roughly 800 stores. No competitor can realistically close this gap. The store network was built over 150+ years, and the real estate footprint, local relationships, and brand trust embedded in each location represent a compounding advantage that grows more valuable with time.

The switching costs for professional painters are genuine and multidimensional. A typical painting contractor does not comparison-shop for paint the way a consumer might. The contractor has an established credit account at a local Sherwin store, with payment terms that function as working capital financing. The contractor knows the store staff, who know the contractor's preferences and can recommend products for specific jobs. The contractor uses Sherwin's color-matching system and digital tools (the Pro Plus app) and has learned to estimate coverage, dry times, and application behavior for specific Sherwin formulations. Switching to a competitor means re-establishing credit, learning new products, risking callbacks from inconsistent finishes, and losing the convenience of a nearby store. These switching costs are not contractual but behavioral and economic — they are sticky precisely because they are woven into the contractor's daily workflow rather than imposed by a contract.

Pricing power is the financial signature of these moat characteristics, and the evidence is compelling. Gross margin expanded nearly 700 basis points from FY2022 (42.1%) to FY2025 (48.8%). While some of this reflects favorable raw material costs (TiO2 and resin prices declined from their 2022 peaks), the magnitude and durability of the expansion — even as the demand environment softened — indicates that SHW retained pricing gains rather than passing savings through to customers. This is the behavior of a company with genuine pricing power, not a commodity producer.

Scale economics further reinforce the moat. As the world's largest coatings company by revenue ($23.6B), SHW has purchasing power advantages in procuring titanium dioxide, resins, and solvents that smaller competitors cannot match. The company's manufacturing footprint and supply chain have been continuously optimized, with recent plant consolidations expected to deliver $80M in annual savings. R&D spending is amortized over a far larger revenue base than any competitor. At the store level, the density of the network means delivery logistics are more efficient — a Sherwin truck can service more stores per route than a competitor with a thinner network.

The moat is weakest in two areas. First, the Consumer Brands Group competes on retailer shelves where Sherwin does not control distribution — Lowe's and other retailers can switch suppliers or push private-label alternatives, eroding CBG's pricing power. Second, in Performance Coatings, the industrial end markets are more competitive and specification-driven; while SHW has global scale post-Valspar, the moat in industrial coatings is narrower than in the domestic architectural paint business. The architectural PSG moat, however, is wide and durable, and it represents the majority of the company's value.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A rare combination of captive distribution, behavioral switching costs, and scale economics produces a wide moat evidenced by expanding margins, ~20% ROIC, and dominant market share — all in a product category with no technological disruption risk.
- **Bear case:** The moat is narrower in Consumer Brands (retailer power) and Performance Coatings (specification-driven competition); a prolonged housing downturn could pressure volume even if the moat itself remains intact.
- **Confidence:** High — The moat is visible in both qualitative competitive dynamics and quantitative financial outcomes (margins, ROIC, market share trajectory).

## Red Flags
- Consumer Brands Group is exposed to retailer bargaining power — Lowe's concentration is a risk if the relationship sours.
- Rising debt ($12.9B total, 2.9x Debt/EBITDA) partially reflects the capital deployed to build the moat (Valspar acquisition, store expansion), but limits flexibility if margins compress.
- Global industrial coatings market is more competitive, with regional players offering comparable products in some categories.

## Score: 8 / 10
Sherwin-Williams has a wide and durable moat in its core Paint Stores Group driven by an unreplicable store network, genuine professional switching costs, and demonstrated pricing power, modestly offset by narrower advantages in Consumer Brands and industrial coatings.
