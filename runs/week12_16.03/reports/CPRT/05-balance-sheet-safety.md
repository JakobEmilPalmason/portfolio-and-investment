## Detailed Analysis

Copart's balance sheet is among the strongest in the entire US equity market. As of Q2 FY2026 (January 31, 2026), the company held $5.1B in cash, cash equivalents, and short-term treasury investments against total debt of approximately $100M (down from $398M five years ago). This is not simply a low-leverage balance sheet — it is a net cash position of roughly $5.0B, or $5.31 per share, representing approximately 16% of the current market capitalization. The debt-to-equity ratio stands at 1.1%, and the company has been systematically deleveraging for years, having fully retired its meaningful long-term debt. Total shareholder equity has doubled from $4.6B (FY2022) to $9.6B (Q2 FY2026), funded entirely by retained earnings rather than equity issuance.

The liquidity position is exceptional. Current assets of $6.2B against current liabilities of $781M produce a current ratio above 8x. Working capital of $5.1B provides a cushion that dwarfs any conceivable short-term obligation. The company added a new $1.25B unsecured revolving credit facility in January 2026 (maturing 2031) — entirely undrawn — which provides additional optionality for opportunistic acquisitions, share repurchases, or catastrophic-event working capital needs without touching the existing cash balance. Total liquidity (cash + undrawn revolver) stands at approximately $6.4B.

Debt structure is a non-issue. The remaining $104M in total debt consists of minor obligations (primarily operating lease liabilities and small facility-related items). There is no material long-term debt, no floating-rate exposure worth measuring, no refinancing risk, no bond covenants to trip, and no off-balance-sheet obligations of concern. Interest expense has been negligible for the past three years and rounds to zero in the most recent filings. Copart is one of the rare large-cap industrials where debt analysis is essentially moot.

The stress test is straightforward. Copart generated $1.8B in operating cash flow in FY2025. If revenue fell 30% (from $4.6B to $3.2B) and we assume operating margins compress to 28% (well below even the weakest historical margin), operating income would be approximately $900M. After taxes (~$185M) and maintenance CapEx (~$220M), the company would still generate roughly $500M in free cash flow per year. Over a two-year stress scenario, Copart would accumulate additional cash — not deplete it. Even in an extreme scenario combining a 30% revenue decline with a cessation of all cash generation, the $5.1B cash pile would cover operating expenses for roughly 7-8 years. There is no plausible scenario in which Copart needs to raise external capital.

The one area worth noting is the capital allocation question that the fortress balance sheet raises. With $5.1B in cash, no debt, and $1.2B in annual FCF, Copart has accumulated far more cash than it needs for operations or even aggressive land expansion ($569M CapEx in FY2025). The company has historically eschewed dividends and buybacks, though the new revolving credit facility and recent signals suggest management may be preparing to deploy capital more aggressively through acquisitions (Purple Wave, Hills Motors) and potentially share repurchases. This is not a balance sheet risk — quite the opposite — but it does raise the question of whether excess cash is being deployed at its highest and best use or simply sitting in treasuries earning sub-equity returns.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A $5.1B net cash position, 8.4x current ratio, zero meaningful debt, and the ability to generate positive FCF even in a severe downturn make Copart's balance sheet virtually impregnable — this company cannot be killed by a recession.
- **Bear case:** The massive cash accumulation with no buybacks or dividends to date suggests potential capital-allocation inefficiency; excess cash earning 4-5% treasury yields dilutes overall return on equity.
- **Confidence:** High — Balance sheet data is unambiguous and verified across multiple sources; the stress test math is conservative and still produces a wide margin of safety.

## Red Flags

- $5.1B in cash with no shareholder return program to date raises questions about capital allocation discipline and opportunity cost
- No dividend and historically no buybacks despite generating $1.2B+ in annual FCF; management's capital deployment priorities remain opaque
- The new $1.25B revolver, while undrawn, could signal a strategic shift toward levered acquisitions — worth monitoring for discipline
- Rising total assets ($5.3B to $10.1B in three years) driven by cash accumulation and land purchases may suppress return-on-asset metrics and mask underlying capital efficiency

## Score: 10 / 10

Copart has a textbook fortress balance sheet: $5.1B net cash, zero meaningful debt, 8.4x current ratio, infinite interest coverage, and the ability to generate positive free cash flow through any plausible stress scenario — this is as safe as a balance sheet gets for a publicly traded industrial company.
