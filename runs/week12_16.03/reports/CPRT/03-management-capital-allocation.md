## Detailed Analysis

Copart's governance begins with its founder. Willis Johnson started the company in 1982 from a single salvage yard in Vallejo, California. Four decades later, he remains Chairman of the Board with an ownership stake estimated at 6-8% of shares outstanding -- roughly $1.8-2.4 billion in personal wealth tied to the stock at current prices. This is not token ownership. Johnson thinks in decades, not quarters, and his continued presence provides institutional memory and cultural continuity. The separation of Chairman (Johnson) and CEO (Liaw) roles is clean governance.

CEO Jeffrey Liaw, who took the helm in 2022, has a compensation structure that is notably aligned with long-term shareholders. His initial package was front-loaded with performance-hurdled stock options (about 50% of value) and RSUs subject to a 10-year lock-up -- meaning he cannot fully cash out his equity grants for a decade. He received no new equity grants until April 2026 at the earliest. This is the kind of structure that filters for patient operators, not short-term stock promoters. Liaw's direct shareholding of ~56,000 shares ($1.8M) is modest relative to the company's size, but the locked-up equity grants substantially increase his effective skin in the game.

Capital allocation has been conservative to a fault. Copart sits on $4.8 billion in cash and equivalents with essentially zero debt (Debt/EBITDA 0.1x). The company pays no dividend. While this fortress balance sheet is admirable from a safety perspective -- and provides catastrophic event capacity (hurricane surges can require massive temporary yard capacity) -- the sheer scale of cash accumulation raises legitimate questions. With $1.2B in annual free cash flow piling onto a $4.8B cash hoard, the balance sheet is becoming inefficient. Management argues the cash provides optionality for land acquisition, international expansion, and M&A. The resumed buyback activity ($218M in H1 FY2026) is a positive signal, but remains modest relative to the cash pile and market cap.

Historical capital allocation has been sound. Since 2003, Copart has repurchased over 458 million shares for $1.38 billion -- much of that at far lower prices, making it genuinely accretive. Growth capex has been directed primarily at yard acquisition and expansion ($351M growth capex in FY2025), which directly strengthens the physical moat. There have been no large, headline-grabbing acquisitions or empire-building moves. International expansion (UK, Germany, Spain, Brazil, Middle East) has been measured and organic. This is a company that grows by doing more of the same thing in more places, not by reinventing itself.

Stock-based compensation deserves special mention because it is strikingly low. At $38M in FY2025 (0.8% of revenue), Copart's SBC is a rounding error compared to peers. For context, many technology companies run SBC at 5-15% of revenue. This means net income is a reliable measure of actual earnings, not a number inflated by ignoring equity dilution. Share count has been roughly stable, reflecting the discipline.

The insider selling pattern is the one area requiring nuance. Over the past 18 months, insiders sold 1.6 million shares with zero purchases. CEO Liaw sold $1M in shares in January 2026. All sales were under pre-planned 10b5-1 programs, which is standard practice and not necessarily bearish -- executives with concentrated positions in a single stock routinely diversify. But the complete absence of open-market buying, at a time when the stock is near its 52-week low, is notable. Founder Johnson has not transacted recently, which is neutral.

On candor and communication, Copart's management has been consistently straightforward. Earnings calls are information-dense without being promotional. Liaw has acknowledged headwinds directly -- noting tariff-driven volatility, the rise in uninsured drivers, and competitive dynamics with IAA. This is not a management team that overpromises or hides behind corporate-speak.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Founder-led governance with minimal SBC, a fortress balance sheet, and a CEO locked in for 10 years via equity structure creates ideal alignment for patient, long-term compounding.
- **Bear case:** The massive cash pile ($4.8B) is earning below cost of capital, and insider selling without any buying at near-52-week-low prices signals that management may not see the stock as a bargain.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Governance structure and compensation design are excellent, but the cash accumulation question and insider selling pattern prevent full confidence.

## Red Flags

- $4.8B cash pile with 0.1x Debt/EBITDA suggests potential capital allocation inefficiency -- cash is earning below cost of equity.
- Net insider selling of 1.6M shares over 18 months with zero open-market purchases, even as the stock declined ~48% from its 52-week high.
- Buyback activity ($218M in H1 FY2026) is modest relative to cash on hand ($5.1B as of Jan 2026) and the stock's significant decline -- a more aggressive repurchase would better signal conviction.

## Score: 8 / 10

Copart's founder-chairman ownership, unusually long-term CEO compensation design, minimal SBC, and clean capital allocation history reflect strong stewardship, docked slightly for the growing cash pile inefficiency and the absence of aggressive buybacks or insider purchases at depressed prices.
