## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Copart runs online auctions for totaled vehicles on behalf of insurance companies, earning fees on both sides of a transaction that recurs because cars will always crash and modern vehicles are increasingly too expensive to repair.
2. **Moat**: A duopoly with IAA controlling ~80% of US salvage auctions, protected by 250+ owned yards that take decades of zoning and permitting to replicate, plus network effects where 1M+ global buyers drive ASPs 3x higher than competitors.
3. **Economics**: Adjusted ROIC on operating assets is 29-32% (headline 16.6% is depressed by $4.8B idle cash), the consignment model requires no inventory risk, and owner earnings hit $1.6B in FY2025 on 45% gross margins.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business cannot go to zero (duopoly, $5.1B cash, $1.2B annual FCF), but there is no price MOS against the bear case IV of $23, so the protection is business quality rather than cheap entry.

# Circle of Competence — CPRT

**Date:** 2026-03-22

## Detailed Analysis

Copart runs online auctions for damaged, salvage, and used vehicles. Think of it as eBay for wrecked cars, but with a critical physical infrastructure layer -- the company owns and operates over 250 storage yards across the US and internationally where it receives, stores, photographs, and auctions vehicles. The sellers are primarily insurance companies who have declared vehicles total losses, along with fleet operators, rental companies, dealers, and financial institutions. The buyers are a global network of dismantlers, rebuilders, used car dealers, and exporters in 185+ countries.

The revenue model is straightforward. When an insurance company totals a car, Copart picks it up, stores it at one of its yards, lists it on its VB3 online auction platform, and sells it to the highest bidder. Copart earns fees from both sides of the transaction: sellers pay for towing, storage, and auction services; buyers pay transaction fees and premiums. Service revenue is the dominant stream and has been growing faster than vehicle sales revenue. In FY2025, total revenue reached $4.6 billion with a 45% gross margin and 37% operating margin -- numbers that reflect a capital-light marketplace layered on top of a physical storage network.

The key drivers are refreshingly simple. First, total loss frequency -- the percentage of insurance claims that result in the car being declared a total loss rather than repaired. This has been rising secularly from 15.6% in 2015 to 24.2% in late 2025, driven by increasingly complex and expensive-to-repair modern vehicles packed with sensors, cameras, and advanced materials. Second, auction volume -- how many units flow through Copart's yards each quarter. Third, average selling price (ASP) per vehicle, which hit all-time highs in FY2025 with US insurance ASPs growing 8%+ year-over-year. These three variables explain the vast majority of Copart's financial performance.

Revenue predictability is reasonably high for a non-subscription business. Insurance companies sign multi-year service agreements, creating a sticky annuity-like relationship. Cars will continue to crash, and the structural trend toward total losses is driven by vehicle complexity -- not something that reverses easily. That said, there is cyclicality: the Q2 FY2026 quarter (ended January 2026) showed revenue declining 3.6% year-over-year, partly from softening used car values and a rise in uninsured drivers reducing claim volumes. Catastrophic weather events (hurricanes, hailstorms) create lumpy volume spikes. But the underlying secular trend is clear and positive.

Customer concentration exists but is manageable. The top insurance carriers are large, sophisticated counterparties. Switching costs are meaningful because Copart is deeply integrated into insurers' claims workflows. No single customer dominates to the point of creating dependency risk. On the buyer side, the global network of 1 million+ registered members across 185 countries provides deep liquidity and competitive auction prices, which in turn attracts more sellers.

Can you explain this business in two minutes? Absolutely. Cars get wrecked. Insurance companies total them. Copart picks them up, stores them, and auctions them online to a global buyer network. Copart earns fees on both sides. More crashes, more complex cars, and more total losses mean more business. It is one of the simplest and most understandable business models in the market.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Rising total loss frequency is a durable secular trend driven by vehicle technology complexity, providing a multi-decade volume tailwind for a business anyone can understand.
- **Bear case:** A sustained downturn in used car values or a meaningful rise in uninsured drivers could compress auction fees and volumes, as the Q2 FY2026 revenue dip (-3.6%) already hints at.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is transparent, the key drivers are few and trackable, and there is minimal technological or regulatory complexity to misunderstand.

## Red Flags

- Q2 FY2026 showed the first meaningful revenue decline (-3.6% YoY), suggesting some near-term cyclical headwinds from softening used car prices and rising uninsured motorists.
- Dependence on insurance industry dynamics (total loss decisions, claim volumes) means regulatory changes to insurance or repair economics could alter the model.

## Score: 9 / 10

Copart's business model is among the most understandable in the market -- a salvage vehicle auction marketplace with only 2-3 key drivers, semi-recurring insurance relationships, and a value proposition explainable in one sentence.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — CPRT

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** context/CPRT/financials.md, context/CPRT/quant-valuation.md, web search (competitive landscape analysis, IAA/RB Global comparisons, industry reports, Q1/Q2 FY2026 earnings), analyst knowledge of auction marketplace dynamics.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Copart and IAA (RB Global) operate a duopoly controlling ~80% of the US salvage auction market, with Copart holding roughly 40% market share. | 5 |
| 2 | Copart's physical yard network (250+ locations, including the 400-acre Hall Ranch in South Florida) is nearly impossible to replicate due to zoning, permitting, and NIMBY barriers. | 5 |
| 3 | Network effects are genuine: more buyers drive higher ASPs, which attract more insurance consignors, creating a self-reinforcing flywheel -- Copart's ASPs outpace competitors by 3x. | 5 |
| 4 | Switching costs are high because Copart is deeply embedded in insurance company claims workflows, with multi-year contracts and integrated technology systems. | 4 |
| 5 | Operating margins of 36-39% and ROIC of 16-24% sustained over multiple years provide strong numerical evidence of moat durability. | 4 |
| 6 | IAA/RB Global is showing signs of competitive resurgence: unit growth +4.7% vs Copart's -2.8%, and Progressive Insurance shifted volume toward IAA. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Copart's moat is multi-layered and reinforcing, which is what makes it particularly durable. Start with the physical infrastructure. Salvage yards are locally unwanted land uses -- nobody wants a junkyard in their neighborhood. Zoning approvals, environmental permits, and community resistance make it extraordinarily difficult for a new entrant to build a national yard network from scratch. Copart has spent four decades acquiring and permitting these locations, including strategic land banks for catastrophic event capacity. This is a regulatory and physical moat that money alone cannot easily overcome.

Layer on top of that the marketplace network effects. Copart's VB3 platform lists roughly 400,000 vehicles daily to a buyer network spanning 185+ countries with over 1 million registered members. More buyers mean more competitive bidding, which pushes ASPs higher. Higher ASPs mean insurance companies recover more from their total-loss vehicles, which makes Copart the preferred auction partner. This flywheel is measurable: in FY2025, Copart's US insurance ASPs grew 8%+ year-over-year, outpacing an industry index and tripling the growth rate of competitors. That pricing premium is the clearest evidence of a functioning network effect.

Switching costs further entrench the moat. Insurance companies do not casually switch salvage auction providers. Copart is integrated into their claims management systems, field operations, and towing logistics. Changing providers means retraining adjusters, rewiring technology integrations, and risking lower auction returns during the transition period. These contracts tend to be multi-year, and the workflow integration creates genuine stickiness.

The financial evidence is compelling. Gross margins have held steady around 45% for four consecutive years. Operating margins of 36-39% are extraordinary for what is essentially a logistics and marketplace business. ROIC has ranged from 16.6% to 24.2%, and while the trend has been declining (from 24.2% in FY2022 to 16.6% in FY2025), this partly reflects Copart's growing cash pile ($4.8 billion) and land investments diluting the return on capital base rather than deteriorating competitive position. A business earning mid-teens ROIC with virtually no debt and a fortress balance sheet is still generating substantial economic profits above its cost of capital.

The weakest point in the moat is the duopoly rival. IAA, now part of RB Global after the 2023 acquisition, has been investing aggressively and showing competitive improvement. Recent data shows IAA unit growth of +4.7% versus Copart's -2.8%, and Progressive Insurance -- a major insurer -- reportedly shifted some volume toward IAA. This is worth monitoring. However, the duopoly structure itself is the moat: two well-capitalized incumbents with massive physical networks and entrenched insurance relationships make the barrier to a third entrant essentially insurmountable. Even within the duopoly, Copart's ASP premium and global buyer network suggest it remains the stronger platform.

Do these advantages reset each cycle? No. The yard network, buyer relationships, and workflow integrations are cumulative assets. Technology refreshes (like VB3) are incremental improvements to an existing platform, not cycle-dependent bets. The moat deepens with each yard acquisition, each new international buyer, and each insurance contract renewed.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The duopoly structure is essentially permanent, and Copart's network effects (ASP premium, global buyer reach) create a widening gap that IAA may not be able to close.
- **Bear case:** IAA/RB Global's renewed investment and volume gains could gradually erode Copart's market share and compress the ASP premium that underpins its economic moat.
- **Confidence:** High — The multi-layered moat (physical, network, switching costs) has been evident in the financials for over a decade, and the duopoly structure has no credible third-party threat.

## Red Flags

- IAA/RB Global unit growth (+4.7%) is outpacing Copart (-2.8%) in recent quarters, suggesting the competitive dynamic may be shifting at the margin.
- Progressive Insurance reportedly shifted volume toward IAA, indicating that even large, sticky relationships are not perfectly locked in.
- Declining ROIC trend (24.2% to 16.6% over three years) warrants monitoring, even if partly explained by cash accumulation.

## Score: 9 / 10

Copart possesses a wide, multi-layered moat combining irreplaceable physical infrastructure, genuine marketplace network effects, and deep workflow switching costs within a duopoly that effectively blocks new entrants -- the recent IAA competitive gains are worth watching but do not fundamentally threaten the moat's durability.

## Detailed Analysis

Copart's governance begins with its founder. Willis Johnson started the company in 1982 from a single salvage yard in Vallejo, California. Four decades later, he remains Chairman of the Board with an ownership stake estimated at 6-8% of shares outstanding -- roughly $1.8-2.4 billion in personal wealth tied to the stock at current prices. This is not token ownership. Johnson thinks in decades, not quarters, and his continued presence provides institutional memory and cultural continuity. The separation of Chairman (Johnson) and CEO (Liaw) roles is clean governance.

CEO Jeffrey Liaw, who took the helm in 2022, has a compensation structure that is notably aligned with long-term shareholders. His initial package was front-loaded with performance-hurdled stock options (about 50% of value) and RSUs subject to a 10-year lock-up -- meaning he cannot fully cash out his equity grants for a decade. He received no new equity grants until April 2026 at the earliest. This is the kind of structure that filters for patient operators, not short-term stock promoters. Liaw's direct shareholding of ~56,000 shares ($1.8M) is modest relative to the company's size, but the locked-up equity grants substantially increase his effective skin in the game.

Capital allocation has been conservative to a fault. Copart sits on $4.8 billion in cash and equivalents with essentially zero debt (Debt/EBITDA 0.1x). The company pays no dividend. While this fortress balance sheet is admirable from a safety perspective -- and provides catastrophic event capacity (hurricane surges can require massive temporary yard capacity) -- the sheer scale of cash accumulation raises legitimate questions. With $1.2B in annual free cash flow piling onto a $4.8B cash hoard, the balance sheet is becoming inefficient. Management argues the cash provides optionality for land acquisition, international expansion, and M&A. The resumed buyback activity ($218M in H1 FY2026) is a positive signal, but remains modest relative to the cash pile and market cap.

Historical capital allocation has been sound. Since 2003, Copart has repurchased over 458 million shares for $1.38 billion -- much of that at far lower prices, making it genuinely accretive. Growth capex has been directed primarily at yard acquisition and expansion ($351M growth capex in FY2025), which directly strengthens the physical moat. There have been no large, headline-grabbing acquisitions or empire-building moves. International expansion (UK, Germany, Spain, Brazil, Middle East) has been measured and organic. This is a company that grows by doing more of the same thing in more places, not by reinventing itself.

Stock-based compensation deserves special mention because it is strikingly low. At $38M in FY2025 (0.8% of revenue), Copart's SBC is a rounding error compared to peers. For context, many technology companies run SBC at 5-15% of revenue. This means net income is a reliable measure of actual earnings, not a number inflated by ignoring equity dilution. Share count has been roughly stable, reflecting the discipline.

The insider selling pattern is the one area requiring nuance. Over the past 18 months, insiders sold 1.6 million shares with zero purchases. CEO Liaw sold $1M in shares in January 2026. All sales were under pre-planned 10b5-1 programs, which is standard practice and not necessarily bearish -- executives with concentrated positions in a single stock routinely diversify. But the complete absence of open-market buying, at a time when the stock is near its 52-week low, is notable. Founder Johnson has not transacted recently, which is neutral.

On candor and communication, Copart's management has been consistently straightforward. Earnings calls are information-dense without being promotional. Liaw has acknowledged headwinds directly -- noting tariff-driven volatility, the rise in uninsured drivers, and competitive dynamics with IAA. This is not a management team that overpromises or hides behind corporate-speak.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Founder-led governance with minimal SBC, a fortress balance sheet, and a CEO locked in for 10 years via equity structure creates ideal alignment for patient, long-term compounding.
- **Bear case:** The massive cash pile ($4.8B) is earning below cost of capital, and insider selling without any buying at near-52-week-low prices signals that management may not see the stock as a bargain.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Governance structure and compensation design are excellent, but the cash accumulation question and insider selling pattern prevent full confidence.

## Red Flags

- $4.8B cash pile with 0.1x Debt/EBITDA suggests potential capital allocation inefficiency -- cash is earning below cost of equity.
- Net insider selling of 1.6M shares over 18 months with zero open-market purchases, even as the stock declined ~48% from its 52-week high.
- Buyback activity ($218M in H1 FY2026) is modest relative to cash on hand ($5.1B as of Jan 2026) and the stock's significant decline -- a more aggressive repurchase would better signal conviction.

## Score: 8 / 10

Copart's founder-chairman ownership, unusually long-term CEO compensation design, minimal SBC, and clean capital allocation history reflect strong stewardship, docked slightly for the growing cash pile inefficiency and the absence of aggressive buybacks or insider purchases at depressed prices.

# Business Economics — CPRT

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Copart FY2025 10-K, Q1/Q2 FY2026 10-Q filings, Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), quant-valuation model output, Seeking Alpha, Simply Wall St, GuruFocus, MacroTrends, StockAnalysis.com

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC has declined from 24.2% (FY2022) to 16.6% (FY2025) on a calculated basis, though this understates true returns because the denominator is inflated by $4.8B of cash earning treasury yields rather than deployed in operations; adjusted ROIC on operating assets remains in the high-20% to low-30% range | 5 |
| 2 | The service-fee model (80%+ of revenue) is quasi-recurring and capital-light — vehicles pass through on consignment, generating fees without Copart taking inventory risk on most units | 5 |
| 3 | Operating margins compressed from 39.3% (FY2022) to 36.5% (FY2025) and 34.7% in Q2 FY2026, driven by heavy land/facility investment and softer insurance volumes, though net margins have actually expanded from 31.1% to 33.4% over the same period thanks to investment income on the cash pile | 4 |
| 4 | FCF grew from $839M (FY2022) to $1.2B (FY2025) despite CapEx nearly doubling from $337M to $569M, demonstrating the cash-generative power of the platform at scale | 4 |
| 5 | Growth CapEx ($351M in FY2025, 62% of total) is predominantly land acquisition to build a "density moat" — 14 new sites opened in FY2025 — creating a durable physical barrier to entry that compounds over time | 4 |
| 6 | Revenue growth decelerated to <1% YoY in Q1 FY2026 ($1.155B) as insurance total-loss volumes declined 9%, though average selling prices hit all-time highs (+7% globally) and net income still grew 11.5% | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Copart operates one of the most elegant business models in industrial services. The company runs a two-sided digital marketplace for damaged, salvage, and used vehicles, primarily serving insurance carriers who declare vehicles total losses. Over 80% of vehicle volume comes from insurance companies, and Copart earns fees on both the seller and buyer side of each transaction without taking title to the vast majority of units. This consignment structure means Copart collects revenue with minimal inventory risk, creating a naturally asset-light income stream on a per-transaction basis. The recurring nature of insurance total losses — driven by rising vehicle complexity, repair costs, and weather events — provides a structural tailwind that is largely non-discretionary for Copart's customers.

The returns-on-capital picture requires nuance. The headline ROIC figure from financial statements has declined from 24.2% in FY2022 to 16.6% in FY2025, but this is misleading. Copart has accumulated $4.8B in cash and treasuries on its balance sheet — capital that inflates the invested-capital denominator but earns investment income rather than operating returns. When measured on operating assets alone (excluding excess cash), Copart's ROIC remains in the high-20% to low-30% range, consistent with third-party estimates of 29.9%–32.5%. This is exceptional by any standard and reflects the platform's pricing power, network density, and operating leverage.

Margin trends tell two stories. Operating margins have compressed roughly 280 basis points over four years (39.3% to 36.5%) as Copart absorbs the cost of aggressive land acquisition and facility buildout — 14 new sites in FY2025 alone, with CapEx nearly doubling since FY2022. This is deliberate: management is building physical density that competitors cannot replicate without billions in land spend and decades of permitting. Meanwhile, net margins have actually expanded from 31.1% to 33.4% because investment income on the massive cash balance ($300M+ annually at current rates) flows through below the operating line. In Q1 FY2026, gross profit rose 4.9% and net income grew 11.5% even on essentially flat revenue, demonstrating latent operating leverage when volume headwinds ease.

Free cash flow generation is robust and accelerating. FCF grew from $839M in FY2022 to $1.2B in FY2025, a 43% increase, even as CapEx rose 69% over the same period. FCF conversion (FCF/NI) improved to 79.3% in FY2025. The quant model separates maintenance CapEx ($218M, proxied by D&A) from growth CapEx ($351M), yielding adjusted owner earnings of $1.6B — a figure that better represents the cash Copart could retain if it stopped expanding. This distinction matters: the heavy CapEx is a choice, not a necessity, and it is building a physical moat rather than simply maintaining the status quo.

Revenue quality is high but not without cyclical exposure. The near-term softness — Q1 FY2026 revenue grew just 0.72% as insurance volumes fell 9% — reflects mild weather patterns and consumers dropping coverage. However, Copart offset this through pricing: average auction selling prices hit all-time highs, up 7% globally, driven by the company's "open all night" global buyer network that creates competitive bidding across time zones. The structural trend toward higher total-loss rates (driven by ADAS sensor costs, labor inflation, and EV battery damage complexity) remains intact and should reassert itself as weather normalizes and the vehicle fleet continues to age.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Adjusted ROIC north of 30%, $1.6B in owner earnings, and a density moat funded by discretionary growth CapEx position Copart as a compounding machine whose near-term volume softness obscures exceptional long-term economics.
- **Bear case:** Declining headline ROIC, operating margin compression, and sub-1% revenue growth in the latest quarter suggest the business may be entering a period of diminishing marginal returns as it saturates its domestic land footprint.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model economics are transparent, the cash generation is verified across multiple years, and the ROIC distortion from excess cash is well-understood.

## Red Flags

- Headline ROIC declining four consecutive years (24.2% to 16.6%) could mask genuine return-on-capital erosion if land investments don't generate proportionate returns
- Operating margin compression (39.3% to 36.5% over four years) not yet showing signs of stabilizing
- Insurance volume decline of 9% in Q1 FY2026 is a meaningful cyclical headwind; prolonged mild weather could extend the soft patch
- CapEx intensity rising — $569M in FY2025 vs. $337M in FY2022 — with no explicit ROI disclosure on land investments
- No buybacks or dividends despite $4.8B cash pile raises capital-allocation discipline questions (though the new $1.25B revolver signals potential shift)

## Score: 8 / 10

Copart's underlying economics — 30%+ adjusted ROIC, $1.6B owner earnings, 80%+ fee-based recurring revenue, asset-light consignment model — are clearly strong-to-excellent, docked from a 9 due to the four-year operating margin compression trend, near-term volume softness, and the open question of whether aggressive land CapEx will generate commensurate returns.
