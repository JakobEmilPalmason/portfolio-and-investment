## Detailed Analysis

**Holding period suitability: excellent for 5-10+ years.** Copart's competitive advantages are structural and durable. The company controls roughly 40% of the U.S. auto salvage auction market in a duopoly with IAA. Its moat comes from physical yard locations (200+ facilities, mostly owned), network effects (more buyers attract more sellers and vice versa), and deep insurance company relationships built over decades. These are not advantages that erode in 2-3 years. The secular trend is favorable: vehicles are becoming more complex and expensive to repair, which means a growing percentage of damaged cars are declared total losses and flow through Copart's platform. International expansion into Europe and the Middle East provides a multi-decade growth runway. This is a business where compounding alone — even without a catalyst — justifies ownership.

**Volatility tolerance and drawdown history.** The stock has fallen 48.5% from its May 2025 high of $63.85 to the current $32.87, sitting just 2% above its 52-week low. Historically, Copart has experienced significant drawdowns: roughly 50% during the 2020 COVID crash, ~35% during the 2018-2019 correction, and multiple 20-30% pullbacks over its history. Each time, the stock recovered and reached new highs within 12-24 months because the underlying business continued to compound. The current drawdown is driven by a combination of multiple compression (from ~40x to ~21x P/E), a Q2 earnings miss, and the DOJ overhang. If the business is intact — and the duopoly structure, margin profile, and cash generation suggest it is — this drawdown is the type where patient investors add rather than panic. The question is whether this is a temporary earnings hiccup or a structural change. The Q2 revenue decline of 3.6% is the most concerning data point and deserves monitoring over the next 1-2 quarters.

**Catalysts.** Several identifiable catalysts could re-rate the stock: (1) Q3/Q4 FY2026 earnings showing revenue growth resumption — expected reporting in May 2026, (2) resolution or clarity on the DOJ investigation — any settlement or dismissal would remove a major overhang, (3) continued share buybacks at depressed prices — Copart repurchased $898.7M in shares after Q2, which is aggressively accretive at current valuations, and (4) international expansion milestones, particularly in Germany where Copart has been building out yard capacity. The timeline for these catalysts ranges from 3 months (next earnings) to 2+ years (DOJ resolution), which suits a patient investor.

**Position sizing.** The Monte Carlo data supports a measured position. With P(IV > Price) at 76.9%, the probability-weighted outcome is favorable, supporting a real allocation rather than just a watchlist entry. However, the sensitivity range of $29.49 (P5) to $43.45 (P95) and the analyst-adjusted bear-to-bull spread of $23-$50 suggest meaningful uncertainty. A starter position of 2-3% is appropriate, with a plan to add toward 4-5% if (a) the next earnings report shows revenue stabilization, or (b) the DOJ situation gains clarity. The stock's near-zero debt and massive cash position ($4.8B) mean that even a worst-case scenario does not threaten permanent capital loss, which reduces the risk of a starter position.

**Correlation and portfolio fit.** Copart is unique in a portfolio context. It is classified as Industrials/Specialty Business Services, but its actual business drivers — auto insurance claims, total loss frequency, and salvage vehicle values — are largely uncorrelated with typical industrial cycle factors. It has no meaningful overlap with technology, financial, or consumer holdings. The main correlation risk would be with other insurance-adjacent or specialty marketplace businesses (e.g., Verisk, ICE). Copart adds genuine diversification to a quality-focused portfolio. Currency exposure is primarily USD with growing but still small international revenue.

**Process discipline.** Entry plan: a starter position at current levels ($32-33) is justified by the 77% Monte Carlo probability and the 40%+ discount to historical multiples. Add on confirmation of revenue growth resumption (next 1-2 earnings reports) or DOJ clarity. Sell rules: (1) thesis break — if the duopoly structure is disrupted (e.g., a well-funded new entrant with a digital-first model), (2) ROIC falls below 12% for two consecutive years without a clear reinvestment payoff, (3) DOJ outcome that structurally impairs the business model (not just a fine). Review cadence: quarterly around earnings, with particular focus on revenue growth rate, operating margin, and any DOJ updates in 10-Q filings.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Classic Buffett setup — a high-quality compounder trading at a multi-year low valuation due to temporary headwinds, with a 5-10 year holding period that allows compounding to do the heavy lifting.
- **Bear case:** The Q2 revenue decline is the beginning of a structural slowdown, and the DOJ investigation creates ongoing headline risk that keeps the multiple depressed for years.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The business quality and long-term fit are clearly strong; the main uncertainty is whether the current price represents "good enough" value or whether patience for a lower entry would be rewarded.

## Red Flags

- DOJ investigation timeline is uncertain — could take 1-3 years to resolve, testing investor patience
- No dividend creates zero income return while waiting for business compounding
- Q2 revenue decline needs confirmation as temporary, not structural — next 1-2 quarters are critical
- The stock could remain cheap for an extended period if the market continues to de-rate quality compounders
- Growth capex is elevated — $350M/year of growth capex needs to eventually show returns in new markets

## Score: 7 / 10

Copart is a strong fit for a patient, owner-oriented approach. The duopoly moat, fortress balance sheet, and secular tailwinds make it a hold-forever business. The current valuation at a multi-year trough offers a reasonable entry point but not a screaming bargain. A starter position with a disciplined add-on plan matches the risk/reward profile — high enough conviction to own, not enough clarity to go full size.
