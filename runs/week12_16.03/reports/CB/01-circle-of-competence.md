## Detailed Analysis

**What does Chubb sell and to whom?** Chubb sells property and casualty insurance (commercial and personal), life insurance, accident and health (A&H) coverage, reinsurance, and agricultural insurance. Its customers span the full spectrum: large multinational corporations needing complex commercial coverage, middle-market businesses, high-net-worth individuals (its Masterpiece personal lines product), and mass-market consumers in Asia and Latin America. Chubb operates through six reportable segments: North America Commercial P&C, North America Personal P&C, North America Agricultural, Overseas General Insurance, Global Reinsurance, and Life Insurance. North America accounts for roughly 60% of premiums, Asia approximately 20%, Europe around 15%, and Latin America the remainder.

**How does it make money?** Chubb earns money two ways. First, underwriting profit: it collects premiums and pays out claims, keeping the difference when claims and expenses are less than premiums collected. Chubb's FY2025 P&C combined ratio of 85.7% (a record) means it earned roughly 14 cents of underwriting profit on every dollar of premium — yielding $6.5B in P&C underwriting income. Second, investment income: Chubb holds $272B in total assets, with a substantial investment portfolio generating steady returns on the "float" (premiums collected but not yet paid out as claims). Together these produced $10.3B in net income for FY2025.

**Key drivers (2-4 variables).** The business is governed by: (1) underwriting discipline / combined ratio — the single most important metric, reflecting pricing accuracy and claims management; (2) premium growth — driven by rate adequacy in hard/soft markets and geographic expansion; (3) investment income — a function of portfolio size, duration, and interest rates; and (4) catastrophe losses — hurricanes, earthquakes, and other natural disasters create earnings volatility. All four are well-understood insurance fundamentals.

**Revenue predictability.** Insurance premiums are contractual and renew annually or on multi-year terms. Retention rates in commercial lines typically exceed 85-90%. Investment income is largely predictable (dominated by fixed income). The main source of unpredictability is catastrophe losses, which can swing quarterly results but are manageable over full-year periods for a diversified global insurer. Chubb's geographic and product diversification further dampens volatility. Revenue grew from $43.0B (FY2022) to $60.1B (FY2025) — a steady 12% CAGR with no down years.

**Customer concentration.** There is no meaningful customer concentration risk. Chubb serves millions of policyholders across 54 countries. No single client, broker, or geography dominates. The broker distribution model (Marsh, Aon, Willis, etc.) means Chubb accesses clients through intermediaries rather than depending on any single relationship.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** Yes. Chubb collects insurance premiums from businesses and individuals, invests that money, and pays out when claims occur. It makes money when it prices risk accurately (underwriting profit) and earns returns on its investment portfolio. The business is conceptually simple — collect more than you pay out — though execution requires deep actuarial expertise and disciplined underwriting culture. This is a textbook "circle of competence" business for a Buffett-style analyst.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The insurance business model is one of the most understandable in finance, and Chubb's diversified, global operation with six clear segments makes it straightforward to analyze and monitor.
- **Bear case:** Catastrophe risk and the long-tail nature of certain P&C liabilities introduce genuine uncertainty that can be difficult to model precisely.
- **Confidence:** High — Insurance is a well-understood industry with over a century of data; Chubb's financials are transparent and its drivers are clearly identifiable.
