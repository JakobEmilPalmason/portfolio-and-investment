## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Insider ownership at Chubb stands at approximately 1.14% of outstanding shares. CEO Evan Greenberg personally holds roughly 626,000 shares worth approximately $181M as of early 2025. While this is a meaningful dollar amount ($181M is significant personal wealth tied to the stock), it is a low percentage of the $126B market cap. This is somewhat typical for large-cap financial institutions where founder-led ownership is rare. Greenberg did sell 15,060 shares ($5.0M) in February 2026 — a routine transaction representing a small fraction of his holdings, likely related to tax planning or portfolio diversification. Institutional ownership is dominant at 88.9%, with Berkshire Hathaway being a notable long-term holder (Warren Buffett took a significant position in 2023-2024).

**Capital allocation.** Chubb's capital allocation under Greenberg has been exemplary across all channels. Organic reinvestment is the priority: Chubb has expanded from a primarily U.S. commercial insurer to a 54-country operation with strong positions in Asia (22.2% premium growth in 2024), Latin America ($3.5B in premiums), and Europe ($8.1B in premiums). M&A has been disciplined and transformative — the 2016 ACE-Chubb merger ($28.3B) created the world's largest P&C insurer, and Greenberg has since made targeted acquisitions in high-growth markets (Thailand, Vietnam, 85.5% stake in China's Huatai Insurance Group). Buybacks have been substantial and well-timed: $3.7B in FY2025 at an average price of $282.57 (below the current $322.58), $1.8B in FY2024, $2.4B in FY2023, and $2.9B in FY2022. The share count has declined from approximately 420M in 2022 to 390M currently — a cumulative 7% reduction. The dividend has grown for 33 consecutive years with a proposed increase to $4.08/share in 2026, while maintaining a conservative 14.6% payout ratio. Book value per share grew 18.0% and tangible book value per share grew 25.7% in FY2025 — outstanding results that reflect both earnings power and disciplined capital deployment.

**Compensation design.** Greenberg's 2024 total compensation was $30.1M, comprising $1.6M salary, $9.5M cash bonus, $17.35M in equity awards, and $1.69M in other compensation. The compensation structure is well-designed from a shareholder alignment perspective: approximately 95% of total direct compensation is variable ("at-risk"). The equity component is 100% performance-based (performance share units/awards) with vesting tied to relative tangible book value per share growth (70% weight) and P&C combined ratio (30% weight). These are precisely the right metrics — BVPS growth captures long-term value creation and combined ratio captures underwriting discipline, the two core drivers of value in insurance. The absolute dollar figure is high and the 477x median employee pay ratio is notable, but given Chubb's $126B market cap and $10.3B in net income, the pay-for-performance alignment is reasonable.

**Candor and consistency.** Greenberg's annual shareholder letters and earnings commentary are notably candid and operationally detailed. He consistently discusses market conditions, competitive dynamics, and specific segment performance with a level of granularity unusual among large-cap CEOs. His recent acknowledgment that the market is "growing incrementally more competitive" demonstrates willingness to flag headwinds rather than spin positive narratives. The company has delivered on its commitments: the guidance for "excellent 2026 with strong growth in operating earnings and double-digit growth in EPS and tangible book value" follows a consistent pattern of meeting or exceeding targets.

**Governance structure.** Chubb operates with a combined Chairman/CEO role held by Greenberg, which is a governance weakness in principle. This is mitigated by an empowered independent Lead Director (Michael Connors) and independent board committees. The board is largely independent. The Swiss domicile provides certain governance differences from U.S.-incorporated companies but is standard for global insurers.

**Shareholder letter tone.** Greenberg's communications are operationally focused, data-driven, and forward-looking without being promotional. He consistently frames strategy around underwriting discipline, geographic expansion, and long-term value creation — language that aligns well with Buffett-style ownership principles. The tone is confident but measured, with appropriate acknowledgment of risks.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Greenberg is one of the most accomplished insurance CEOs of his generation, having compounded book value and earnings for 21 years while consistently returning capital through buybacks and dividends — the FY2025 buyback at $282.57 average price looks especially well-timed.
- **Bear case:** The combined Chairman/CEO role, high absolute compensation, and modest insider ownership percentage create theoretical agency risks, and eventual CEO succession after 21+ years is a legitimate concern.
- **Confidence:** High — The 21-year track record, consistent capital allocation philosophy, and well-designed compensation structure provide ample evidence of management quality.

## Red Flags
- Combined Chairman/CEO role is a governance weakness, partially mitigated by an independent Lead Director.
- CEO compensation at 477x median employee pay is high, though structure (95% at-risk, tied to BVPS and combined ratio) is well-aligned.
- Eventual CEO succession risk after 21+ years of Greenberg leadership — no public succession plan has been detailed.

## Score: 8 / 10
Greenberg's 21-year track record of compounding value, disciplined M&A, consistent buybacks at reasonable prices, and 33 years of dividend growth make Chubb's capital allocation among the best in insurance. The score is 8 rather than 9 due to the combined Chairman/CEO role, high absolute compensation, modest insider ownership percentage, and eventual succession risk.
