# Circle of Competence — CB

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Chubb Q4/FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Chubb 10-K filing, Yahoo Finance financials, Chubb investor relations, Insurance Journal, Macrotrends

| 3 | Key drivers are underwriting discipline (combined ratio), premium rate adequacy, investment income on float, and catastrophe loss experience — all well-understood variables. | 5 |
| 4 | Revenue is recurring and predictable: insurance premiums renew annually with high retention rates, and investment income on $272B in assets provides a steady second earnings stream. | 4 |
| 5 | No meaningful customer concentration — Chubb serves millions of commercial and personal lines clients globally through broker distribution. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Chubb sell and to whom?** Chubb sells property and casualty insurance (commercial and personal), life insurance, accident and health (A&H) coverage, reinsurance, and agricultural insurance. Its customers span the full spectrum: large multinational corporations needing complex commercial coverage, middle-market businesses, high-net-worth individuals (its Masterpiece personal lines product), and mass-market consumers in Asia and Latin America. Chubb operates through six reportable segments: North America Commercial P&C, North America Personal P&C, North America Agricultural, Overseas General Insurance, Global Reinsurance, and Life Insurance. North America accounts for roughly 60% of premiums, Asia approximately 20%, Europe around 15%, and Latin America the remainder.

**How does it make money?** Chubb earns money two ways. First, underwriting profit: it collects premiums and pays out claims, keeping the difference when claims and expenses are less than premiums collected. Chubb's FY2025 P&C combined ratio of 85.7% (a record) means it earned roughly 14 cents of underwriting profit on every dollar of premium — yielding $6.5B in P&C underwriting income. Second, investment income: Chubb holds $272B in total assets, with a substantial investment portfolio generating steady returns on the "float" (premiums collected but not yet paid out as claims). Together these produced $10.3B in net income for FY2025.

**Key drivers (2-4 variables).** The business is governed by: (1) underwriting discipline / combined ratio — the single most important metric, reflecting pricing accuracy and claims management; (2) premium growth — driven by rate adequacy in hard/soft markets and geographic expansion; (3) investment income — a function of portfolio size, duration, and interest rates; and (4) catastrophe losses — hurricanes, earthquakes, and other natural disasters create earnings volatility. All four are well-understood insurance fundamentals.

**Revenue predictability.** Insurance premiums are contractual and renew annually or on multi-year terms. Retention rates in commercial lines typically exceed 85-90%. Investment income is largely predictable (dominated by fixed income). The main source of unpredictability is catastrophe losses, which can swing quarterly results but are manageable over full-year periods for a diversified global insurer. Chubb's geographic and product diversification further dampens volatility. Revenue grew from $43.0B (FY2022) to $60.1B (FY2025) — a steady 12% CAGR with no down years.

**Customer concentration.** There is no meaningful customer concentration risk. Chubb serves millions of policyholders across 54 countries. No single client, broker, or geography dominates. The broker distribution model (Marsh, Aon, Willis, etc.) means Chubb accesses clients through intermediaries rather than depending on any single relationship.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** Yes. Chubb collects insurance premiums from businesses and individuals, invests that money, and pays out when claims occur. It makes money when it prices risk accurately (underwriting profit) and earns returns on its investment portfolio. The business is conceptually simple — collect more than you pay out — though execution requires deep actuarial expertise and disciplined underwriting culture. This is a textbook "circle of competence" business for a Buffett-style analyst.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The insurance business model is one of the most understandable in finance, and Chubb's diversified, global operation with six clear segments makes it straightforward to analyze and monitor.
- **Bear case:** Catastrophe risk and the long-tail nature of certain P&C liabilities introduce genuine uncertainty that can be difficult to model precisely.
- **Confidence:** High — Insurance is a well-understood industry with over a century of data; Chubb's financials are transparent and its drivers are clearly identifiable.

## Red Flags
- None identified. The business model is highly transparent and well-documented.

## Score: 9 / 10
Chubb's business model is crystal clear: collect premiums, invest float, pay claims. The key drivers are few and well-understood, revenue is recurring and predictable, customer concentration is nonexistent, and the entire model can be explained in under two minutes.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — CB

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Chubb Q4/FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Chubb 10-K, Insurance Journal, S&P Global Market Intelligence, J.D. Power 2025 study, Simply Wall St, Beanvest moat analysis, company investor presentations

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Chubb's FY2025 P&C combined ratio of 85.7% is a company record and among the best in the global P&C industry, demonstrating sustained underwriting superiority. | 5 |
| 2 | Scale advantage: $54.8B in net premiums written — more than double most competitors — provides superior data, diversification, and expense leverage. | 5 |
| 3 | High switching costs in commercial lines: embedded risk engineering, multinational servicing across 54 countries, bespoke policy wordings, and claims expertise create deep client relationships. | 4 |
| 4 | Brand/trust moat in high-net-worth personal lines: J.D. Power ranks Chubb highest in homeowner property claims satisfaction (2025). | 4 |
| 5 | ROE has been consistently strong at 15.0% (FY2025 and FY2024), with ROIC at 14.7% — well above cost of capital, confirming the moat generates real economic value. | 4 |
| 6 | The insurance market is cyclical and becoming incrementally more competitive, per CEO Greenberg, which could pressure margins in a soft market. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Moat type identification.** Chubb possesses a multi-layered moat built on several reinforcing advantages. First, switching costs: in complex commercial lines (which represent ~60% of premiums), Chubb embeds itself through risk engineering services, multinational program coordination across 54 countries, bespoke policy language, and deep claims expertise. Replacing Chubb as a commercial insurer is not simply a matter of finding a lower quote — it requires replicating an entire risk management infrastructure. Second, scale economics: with $54.8B in net premiums written, Chubb is roughly 2x the size of competitors like Travelers or AIG in global P&C. This scale provides data advantages for better risk pricing, geographic diversification that smooths catastrophe volatility, and expense leverage. Third, brand and trust: in high-net-worth personal lines (Masterpiece product), Chubb's brand commands premium pricing. The J.D. Power 2025 top ranking in homeowner claims satisfaction reinforces this. Fourth, regulatory barriers: insurance is a heavily regulated industry requiring licenses in every jurisdiction, and Chubb's 54-country operating footprint represents decades of accumulated regulatory approvals that would be extremely costly to replicate.

**Durability — will the moat exist in 10 years?** Very likely yes. Insurance is a centuries-old industry where the fundamental value proposition (risk transfer) does not change. Chubb's advantages — underwriting culture, global scale, regulatory licenses, and client relationships — are not susceptible to technological disruption in the way that, say, retail or media businesses are. Insurtechs have nibbled at distribution but have not demonstrated an ability to replicate Chubb's underwriting discipline or multinational capabilities. The Greenberg-led underwriting culture has been in place for 20+ years and is deeply institutional. AI adoption, where Chubb is investing aggressively, is more likely to reinforce Chubb's scale advantage than erode it.

**Evidence in numbers.** The combined ratio tells the story: Chubb's FY2025 P&C combined ratio of 85.7% means it earns nearly $0.14 of underwriting profit per premium dollar. The industry average combined ratio typically runs 95-100%, meaning most insurers barely break even on underwriting. Chubb's P&C underwriting income grew from $5.85B (FY2024) to a record $6.5B (FY2025), up 11.6%. Net margins expanded from 12.2% (FY2022) to 17.2% (FY2025). ROE has stabilized at 15.0%, and ROIC of 14.7% substantially exceeds WACC of 6.8%. These are not the metrics of a commodity business — they demonstrate sustained pricing power and operational superiority.

**Where is the moat weakest?** The moat is weakest in commoditized, price-sensitive personal lines and small commercial segments where switching costs are lower and price comparison is easier. Chubb mitigates this by targeting the high-net-worth personal lines segment (where brand and service matter more than price) and complex commercial accounts (where switching costs are highest). The life insurance segment in Asia, while growing rapidly (up 15.1% in FY2025), operates in markets with different competitive dynamics and may not carry the same moat as the core P&C franchise. Additionally, CEO Greenberg has noted the commercial P&C market is "growing incrementally more competitive quarter by quarter," which could pressure margins in a cyclical softening.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** No. Insurance does not have "product cycles" in the technology sense. Policies renew annually, but the underlying risk engineering relationships, actuarial databases, regulatory licenses, and underwriting culture persist and compound over time. Unlike tech companies that must reinvent every 3-5 years, Chubb's competitive position strengthens with each year of accumulated data and client relationships.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Chubb possesses the widest moat in global P&C insurance — a combination of underwriting culture, scale, switching costs, and regulatory barriers that has produced record combined ratios and consistently above-cost-of-capital returns.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged soft market cycle could compress margins, and the expanding Asia life insurance business may not carry the same moat characteristics as the core P&C franchise.
- **Confidence:** High — The moat is demonstrable in the financials (combined ratio, ROIC, margin trends) and reinforced by structural industry characteristics that change slowly.

## Red Flags
- CEO acknowledges market becoming "incrementally more competitive" — normal cyclicality but worth monitoring for combined ratio deterioration.
- Combined Chairman/CEO role concentrates strategic risk in one individual (mitigated by succession depth, but worth noting under moat durability).

## Score: 8 / 10
Chubb has a wide, multi-layered moat built on underwriting discipline, global scale, high switching costs in commercial lines, and regulatory barriers. The record combined ratio of 85.7% and ROIC of 14.7% (vs. 6.8% WACC) provide hard evidence. The score is 8 rather than 9 because insurance is inherently cyclical, the market is becoming more competitive, and the rapidly growing Asia life segment carries a less proven moat.

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Insider ownership at Chubb stands at approximately 1.14% of outstanding shares. CEO Evan Greenberg personally holds roughly 626,000 shares worth approximately $181M as of early 2025. While this is a meaningful dollar amount ($181M is significant personal wealth tied to the stock), it is a low percentage of the $126B market cap. This is somewhat typical for large-cap financial institutions where founder-led ownership is rare. Greenberg did sell 15,060 shares ($5.0M) in February 2026 — a routine transaction representing a small fraction of his holdings, likely related to tax planning or portfolio diversification. Institutional ownership is dominant at 88.9%, with Berkshire Hathaway being a notable long-term holder (Warren Buffett took a significant position in 2023-2024).

**Capital allocation.** Chubb's capital allocation under Greenberg has been exemplary across all channels. Organic reinvestment is the priority: Chubb has expanded from a primarily U.S. commercial insurer to a 54-country operation with strong positions in Asia (22.2% premium growth in 2024), Latin America ($3.5B in premiums), and Europe ($8.1B in premiums). M&A has been disciplined and transformative — the 2016 ACE-Chubb merger ($28.3B) created the world's largest P&C insurer, and Greenberg has since made targeted acquisitions in high-growth markets (Thailand, Vietnam, 85.5% stake in China's Huatai Insurance Group). Buybacks have been substantial and well-timed: $3.7B in FY2025 at an average price of $282.57 (below the current $322.58), $1.8B in FY2024, $2.4B in FY2023, and $2.9B in FY2022. The share count has declined from approximately 420M in 2022 to 390M currently — a cumulative 7% reduction. The dividend has grown for 33 consecutive years with a proposed increase to $4.08/share in 2026, while maintaining a conservative 14.6% payout ratio. Book value per share grew 18.0% and tangible book value per share grew 25.7% in FY2025 — outstanding results that reflect both earnings power and disciplined capital deployment.

**Compensation design.** Greenberg's 2024 total compensation was $30.1M, comprising $1.6M salary, $9.5M cash bonus, $17.35M in equity awards, and $1.69M in other compensation. The compensation structure is well-designed from a shareholder alignment perspective: approximately 95% of total direct compensation is variable ("at-risk"). The equity component is 100% performance-based (performance share units/awards) with vesting tied to relative tangible book value per share growth (70% weight) and P&C combined ratio (30% weight). These are precisely the right metrics — BVPS growth captures long-term value creation and combined ratio captures underwriting discipline, the two core drivers of value in insurance. The absolute dollar figure is high and the 477x median employee pay ratio is notable, but given Chubb's $126B market cap and $10.3B in net income, the pay-for-performance alignment is reasonable.

**Candor and consistency.** Greenberg's annual shareholder letters and earnings commentary are notably candid and operationally detailed. He consistently discusses market conditions, competitive dynamics, and specific segment performance with a level of granularity unusual among large-cap CEOs. His recent acknowledgment that the market is "growing incrementally more competitive" demonstrates willingness to flag headwinds rather than spin positive narratives. The company has delivered on its commitments: the guidance for "excellent 2026 with strong growth in operating earnings and double-digit growth in EPS and tangible book value" follows a consistent pattern of meeting or exceeding targets.

**Governance structure.** Chubb operates with a combined Chairman/CEO role held by Greenberg, which is a governance weakness in principle. This is mitigated by an empowered independent Lead Director (Michael Connors) and independent board committees. The board is largely independent. The Swiss domicile provides certain governance differences from U.S.-incorporated companies but is standard for global insurers.

**Shareholder letter tone.** Greenberg's communications are operationally focused, data-driven, and forward-looking without being promotional. He consistently frames strategy around underwriting discipline, geographic expansion, and long-term value creation — language that aligns well with Buffett-style ownership principles. The tone is confident but measured, with appropriate acknowledgment of risks.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Greenberg is one of the most accomplished insurance CEOs of his generation, having compounded book value and earnings for 21 years while consistently returning capital through buybacks and dividends — the FY2025 buyback at $282.57 average price looks especially well-timed.
- **Bear case:** The combined Chairman/CEO role, high absolute compensation, and modest insider ownership percentage create theoretical agency risks, and eventual CEO succession after 21+ years is a legitimate concern.
- **Confidence:** High — The 21-year track record, consistent capital allocation philosophy, and well-designed compensation structure provide ample evidence of management quality.

## Red Flags
- Combined Chairman/CEO role is a governance weakness, partially mitigated by an independent Lead Director.
- CEO compensation at 477x median employee pay is high, though structure (95% at-risk, tied to BVPS and combined ratio) is well-aligned.
- Eventual CEO succession risk after 21+ years of Greenberg leadership — no public succession plan has been detailed.

## Score: 8 / 10
Greenberg's 21-year track record of compounding value, disciplined M&A, consistent buybacks at reasonable prices, and 33 years of dividend growth make Chubb's capital allocation among the best in insurance. The score is 8 rather than 9 due to the combined Chairman/CEO role, high absolute compensation, modest insider ownership percentage, and eventual succession risk.

# Business Economics — CB

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Data Sources:** Chubb Q4 2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Chubb 10-K FY2025, Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), quant-valuation model output, Insurance Journal, Reinsurance News

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Record P&C combined ratio of 85.7% in FY2025 (81.2% in Q4), demonstrating elite underwriting discipline and cost-free float generation | 5 |
| 2 | Core operating ROE of 13.7% and return on tangible equity of 20.5% in FY2025, with per-share tangible book value growing 25.7% YoY | 5 |
| 3 | Owner earnings grew from $5.5B (FY2022) to $10.6B (FY2025), a 93% increase in three years driven by underwriting + investment income leverage | 5 |
| 4 | Adjusted net investment income rose ~9% to nearly $7B in FY2025, reflecting disciplined float deployment into investment-grade fixed income | 4 |
| 5 | Net premiums written grew 6.6% consolidated (P&C +5.4%, Life +15.1%), showing pricing power and geographic diversification | 4 |
| 6 | FCF conversion averaged 163% over FY2022-FY2025, though FY2025 dipped to 124% — still exceptionally strong for a $60B revenue insurer | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Chubb's ROE has stabilized at 15.0% in both FY2024 and FY2025, a meaningful improvement from 10.4% in the catastrophe-heavy FY2022. The more telling metric for insurers is return on tangible equity, which reached 20.5% in FY2025 — comfortably above Chubb's cost of equity (~7.2% per CAPM). ROIC is difficult to compute cleanly for insurers because operating income is not reported in standard form (yfinance returns n/a), but core operating income of $9.95B on invested capital of ~$85B (average) implies a mid-teens return. These are not software-company-level returns, but for a global P&C insurer managing $272B in assets, they are best-in-class. Importantly, returns have been remarkably stable: ROE has not dipped below 10% even in a historically bad catastrophe year (FY2022), demonstrating Chubb's underwriting resilience.

**Margin Structure.** Traditional gross/operating margin metrics do not apply cleanly to insurers. The correct analog is the combined ratio — the sum of loss ratio and expense ratio. Chubb posted an all-time-low P&C combined ratio of 85.7% for FY2025 (Q4: 81.2%), meaning for every $1 of premium collected, only $0.857 was spent on losses and expenses. This leaves $0.143 as underwriting profit before investment income. For context, a combined ratio below 90% is considered excellent in the industry; below 85% is rare. The current accident year combined ratio hit a record low of 80.4% in Q4 2025, suggesting the underlying book is even stronger than the headline number. Net margins of 17.2% (FY2025) are solid for a diversified insurer, up from 12.2% in FY2022. The expansion reflects both improved underwriting and higher investment yields in the post-rate-hike environment.

**Cash Generation and Owner Earnings.** Chubb is a cash machine. Operating cash flow was $12.8B in FY2025, essentially equal to free cash flow since insurance companies have negligible physical capex (D&A of just $301M against $60B in revenue). Owner earnings reached $10.6B in FY2025, up from $5.5B in FY2022. The FCF-to-net-income conversion ratio has averaged 163% over the past four years, a function of the float model: premiums are collected upfront and claims paid later, creating a persistent working capital tailwind. FY2025's 124% conversion is the lowest in this window but still far above 100%, indicating strong underlying cash economics. The gap between operating cash flow ($12.8B) and net income ($10.3B) is healthy and structural — not an accounting artifact.

**Capital Intensity.** This is as asset-light as a $272B-asset business can be. Chubb's physical capex is essentially zero. The "capital" required is regulatory capital to support insurance reserves and the investment portfolio backing those reserves. Growth capex comes in the form of reserve growth (supporting more premium volume) and acquisitions. Chubb's $3.7B in share buybacks in FY2025 (up from $1.8B in FY2024) signals management confidence that excess capital is being generated beyond what the business needs to grow. Total capital return (buybacks + dividends) likely exceeded $5B in FY2025.

**Revenue Quality.** Insurance premiums are not truly "recurring" in the SaaS sense, but Chubb's commercial lines — which constitute the majority of its book — have very high retention rates (typically 85-90%+ for specialty and excess lines). The company writes across 54 countries with no single market concentration. Net premiums written of $54.8B grew 6.6% in FY2025, with life insurance growing 15.1% and P&C at 5.4%. Growth is driven by both rate increases (pricing power in a hard market) and new business, a healthy mix. Revenue is not lumpy or project-based; it accrues monthly as policies renew.

**Operating Leverage.** Chubb demonstrates meaningful operating leverage. Net income grew 11.2% in FY2025 on 7.1% revenue growth, and core operating income grew 8.9%. Over the FY2022-FY2025 period, net income nearly doubled (+98%) while revenue grew 40% — a clear demonstration that profits grow faster than premiums when underwriting discipline is maintained and investment income scales with the growing float. The FY2022 base was depressed by elevated catastrophe losses, so the comparison is somewhat flattering, but even FY2024 to FY2025 shows leverage: 7.1% premium growth translating to 11.2% earnings growth.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Chubb generates $10B+ in annual owner earnings with a sub-86% combined ratio and 20%+ return on tangible equity — a Buffett-quality float machine still growing premiums mid-single digits.
- **Bear case:** ROE at 15% is strong but not exceptional for the equity base required; if the P&C pricing cycle turns soft, combined ratios could revert toward 90%+, compressing margins and returns.
- **Confidence:** High — Chubb's financial track record is among the most transparent and consistent in global insurance, and FY2025 results are fully reported.

## Red Flags
- Commercial auto combined ratio of 126.2% in FY2025 indicates a persistent trouble spot in one sub-segment, though it is small relative to the total book.
- FY2025 operating cash flow ($12.8B) declined from FY2024 ($16.2B), a $3.4B drop that deserves monitoring — likely timing-related but worth tracking.

## Score: 8 / 10
Chubb delivers best-in-class underwriting economics with a record combined ratio, 20%+ return on tangible equity, and $10.6B in owner earnings. ROE of 15% is strong but not in the 20%+ territory that would warrant a 9, and the insurance model inherently requires a large asset base. This is an elite insurer, not quite an asset-light compounder.
