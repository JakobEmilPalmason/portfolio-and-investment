# Compact Checklist — MSFT

**Date:** 2026-03-22

1. **Business**: Microsoft sells recurring enterprise software (M365, 400M+ users) and cloud infrastructure (Azure, $75B+ revenue growing 34-40%) to millions of organizations, with AI copilots as the next upsell layer — customers stay because rip-and-replace costs years and millions of dollars.
2. **Moat**: Deep switching costs from enterprise-wide identity, workflow, and data lock-in across M365/Azure/Dynamics, reinforced by an ecosystem network effect no competitor can replicate across productivity, cloud, developer tools, and professional networking simultaneously.
3. **Economics**: ROIC has held at 30-34% for four consecutive years on a growing $387B capital base, with 69% gross margins and 46% operating margins, though the $120B FY2026 capex cycle is compressing FCF conversion from 84% to 70% and the true payback remains unproven.
4. **Management**: Nadella tripled revenue to $282B and expanded operating margins from 32% to 46% over a decade, but insider ownership is negligible at 0.04% and the $130B+ annual capex bet on AI infrastructure is the largest unproven capital allocation decision in tech history.
5. **Debt risk**: AAA-rated with 0.1x net debt/EBITDA, $95B cash, and 53x interest coverage — Microsoft could lose 98% of operating income and still service its debt, though growing off-balance-sheet data center lease commitments deserve monitoring.
6. **Price**: At $382 and 20x forward earnings, the market prices roughly 10-11% sustained growth — base-case execution breaks even, and you need Azure AI monetization to re-accelerate above 12% for the stock to be genuinely cheap.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business fortress (30% ROIC, $95B cash, $60-70B owner earnings even if AI fails entirely) protects against permanent capital loss, but the price sits 45-53% above conservative IV of $250-264, offering zero price cushion.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if ROIC falls below 20% sustainably, operating margins compress below 35% without offsetting revenue growth, or the OpenAI partnership fractures with no credible in-house AI alternative — any of these signals the capex cycle destroyed value rather than created it.
