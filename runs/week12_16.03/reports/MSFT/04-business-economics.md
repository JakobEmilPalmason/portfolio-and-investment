## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Microsoft's ROIC has ranged from 30.0% to 33.5% over the past four fiscal years — a level that very few businesses of this scale sustain. ROE at 33–37% tells a similar story. These returns are not an artifact of financial engineering; the invested capital base has grown from roughly $217B to $387B over the period while returns stayed high, meaning Microsoft is deploying ever-larger sums at elite rates. The spread between ROIC (~30%) and WACC (~10.4%) is roughly 20 percentage points, which is the hallmark of a wide-moat business creating massive economic value.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** Gross margins have been steady near 69%, reflecting the asset-light software/cloud model where incremental revenue carries very high incremental gross profit. Operating margins have expanded from ~42% (FY2022) to ~46% (FY2025), driven by cloud mix shift and pricing power. The Q2 FY2026 result (47% operating margin) shows the trend continuing. EBITDA margins at 57% confirm the business generates enormous pre-capex cash. The key dynamic is operating leverage: profits grow faster than revenue because the cost structure (particularly R&D at ~12% of revenue and G&A) scales sub-linearly with revenue. This leverage has been visible in every recent quarter.

**Cash Generation.** FCF was $71.6B in FY2025, but this figure needs context. CapEx has nearly tripled in three years ($23.9B to $64.6B), with guidance pointing to $120B in FY2026 for data center and AI infrastructure. The quant model estimates roughly 53% of capex is maintenance and 47% is growth, producing adjusted owner earnings of $101.8B. Even the "simple" FCF of $71.6B represents a 25% FCF margin — strong for any company, but a compression from 30%+ levels just two years ago. The critical question is whether today's growth capex will generate cloud/AI returns that justify the investment. Azure's 39–40% growth and the $368B RPO backlog suggest it will, but the magnitude of the spending cycle introduces uncertainty about near-term cash returns.

**Capital Intensity Shift.** Microsoft is undergoing a structural shift from an asset-light software business to a capital-intensive cloud/AI infrastructure operator. D&A surged from $13.9B (FY2023) to $34.2B (FY2025), reflecting the growing asset base. The $120B FY2026 capex guide — roughly 38% of expected revenue — makes Microsoft one of the largest industrial-scale capital deployers globally. This is a deliberate bet on AI infrastructure. Management expects cloud/AI demand to vastly exceed current capacity (supply constraints through at least June 2026). If the bet pays off, the installed base generates decades of high-margin consumption revenue. If AI demand disappoints, Microsoft will carry a massive fixed-cost structure.

**Revenue Quality.** This is where Microsoft truly excels. Microsoft 365 (400M+ commercial seats), Azure (growing 39% YoY), Dynamics 365, LinkedIn, and GitHub all operate on recurring subscription or consumption models. Commercial RPO of $368B (with 35% recognized within 12 months) provides exceptional revenue visibility. The business has minimal exposure to one-time or cyclical revenue — even the More Personal Computing segment (Windows, Xbox, devices) has shifted toward services and subscriptions. Net revenue retention rates across commercial cloud products consistently exceed 100%.

**Stock-Based Compensation.** SBC of $12.0B is a real economic cost. It represents share dilution that partially offsets the $18.4B in buybacks. Adjusted for SBC, true free cash flow to equity holders is closer to $60B rather than $72B. This is standard for large-cap tech but should not be ignored in valuation.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A 30%+ ROIC business deploying $100B+ annually into AI/cloud infrastructure where demand exceeds supply, with $368B of contracted future revenue providing downside protection.
- **Bear case:** The capex supercycle compresses FCF for 2–3 years, and if AI monetization disappoints or competition intensifies, Microsoft carries a bloated cost base with returns below historical levels.
- **Confidence:** High — the historical track record is unambiguous, revenue quality is best-in-class, and near-term demand signals (RPO, Azure growth, supply constraints) validate the investment cycle, though the scale of capex warrants monitoring.

## Red Flags
- FCF conversion dropped from 84% to 70% in one year and will likely fall further with $120B FY2026 capex
- D&A tripled in two years ($13.9B to $34.2B), signaling a structurally heavier asset base going forward
- SBC at $12B partially offsets buyback program; real shareholder yield is lower than headline figures suggest
- Concentration risk: Azure/cloud growth is the dominant driver; a slowdown would disproportionately impact the narrative
- Growth capex vs maintenance capex split relies on judgment — the 53/47 ratio could shift unfavorably if AI infrastructure depreciates faster than expected

## Score: 8 / 10
Microsoft's business economics are elite — 30%+ ROIC, 69% gross margins, 46% operating margins, and an overwhelming recurring revenue base — but the unprecedented capex cycle introduces real uncertainty about near-term FCF generation and whether AI infrastructure returns will match historical software economics. A 9 would require more visibility on capex payback.
