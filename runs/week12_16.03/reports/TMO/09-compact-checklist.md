# Compact Checklist — TMO

**Date:** 2026-03-22

1. **Business**: TMO is the world's largest life sciences "picks and shovels" supplier, where 83% of $44.6B revenue comes from recurring consumables and services that labs must rebuy once they standardize on TMO instruments.
2. **Moat**: Validated workflows in regulated pharma labs create 5-12 year switching cycles costing hundreds of thousands of dollars to break, reinforced by unmatched scale (40% larger than Danaher) and the broadest product catalog in the industry.
3. **Economics**: Owner earnings have been rock-stable at $8.0B annually across a revenue swing of $42.9B to $44.9B, but GAAP ROIC of 8.6% merely equals the WACC because $60B+ in acquisition goodwill bloats the capital base.
4. **Management**: CEO Casper has compounded the stock from $40 to $474 over 17 years with 100% performance-based compensation, disciplined buybacks that shrank shares 6% since 2021, and $69B in acquisitions that delivered on synergy targets.
5. **Debt risk**: $39.4B of total debt (3.4x Debt/EBITDA, rising to ~3.2x post-Clario) is serviceable at 6.2x interest coverage with A2/A- ratings, but a 30% revenue stress would squeeze coverage to 3.0-3.5x and force suspension of buybacks and M&A.
6. **Price**: At $474, you need ~10% revenue growth at the current 8.6% WACC to justify the price -- the Monte Carlo gives only a 42.8% probability the stock is undervalued, and the quant base IV of $424-$440 sits below market.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is resilient (stable $8B owner earnings, 600K+ customers, no single customer >3%), but at $474 there is zero price margin of safety -- the math improves decisively at $380-$420.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if organic growth turns negative for two consecutive years, ROIC falls below 7%, or Debt/EBITDA exceeds 4.5x without a clear deleveraging path -- any of these signals the M&A compounding model has broken.
