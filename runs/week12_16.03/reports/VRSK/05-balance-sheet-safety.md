## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Verisk's balance sheet tells a story of two events in 2025. First, in mid-2025, the company raised $1.5B in senior notes and drew on credit facilities to finance the proposed $2.35B AccuLynx acquisition. When the FTC failed to complete its review by the December 2025 deadline, Verisk terminated the deal and immediately moved to redeem the $1.5B acquisition-related notes at 101% of par (per the special mandatory redemption clause). Pro forma for that redemption, leverage drops from the reported 2.9x Debt/EBITDA to approximately 1.9x — well within management's stated 2-3x target range and consistent with historical levels. The remaining debt stack consists of legacy senior notes with staggered maturities plus the new $1B offering from February 2026. All debt is fixed-rate, eliminating floating-rate risk. The debt maturity profile is well-laddered: the February 2026 notes mature in 2031 and 2036, and earlier tranches likely span the intervening years. There is no near-term maturity wall.

**Interest Coverage.** Reported interest coverage fell from 10.9x in FY2024 to 7.9x in FY2025 as interest expense rose from $125M to $171M. This deterioration is almost entirely attributable to carrying the AccuLynx acquisition debt for several months before the deal was terminated. Post-redemption of the $1.5B notes, annual interest expense should decline meaningfully. Even at the FY2025 trough, 7.9x coverage is adequate for an investment-grade data analytics company with 84% recurring revenue. Using FCF instead of EBIT, the FCF/interest coverage ratio is 7.0x ($1.19B / $171M), which provides a more conservative but still comfortable margin.

**Liquidity.** At FY2025 year-end, Verisk held $2.2B in cash against a current ratio of 1.2x. The company also has a $1.25B syndicated revolving credit facility (expanded from its prior size). Combined, this gives Verisk approximately $3.4B in available liquidity — more than sufficient to cover any near-term maturities, working capital needs, or unexpected disruptions. The high cash balance at year-end partly reflects the AccuLynx financing proceeds that had not yet been redeemed; the normalized cash position will be lower but still ample given the FCF generation profile.

**Refinancing Risk.** Verisk is a Baa2/BBB+ rated issuer with consistent access to investment-grade debt markets. The February 2026 offering ($1B at blended ~4.8%) was successfully placed with major underwriters (BofA Securities, Wells Fargo) and was used to refinance short-term facilities into long-dated bonds. The company has no significant maturities creating a "wall" in any single year. Refinancing risk is low: the business generates $1.2B+ in annual FCF, debt markets remain accessible to IG issuers, and the company has demonstrated a willingness to manage maturities proactively.

**The Equity Question.** Book equity of $309M looks alarming, and the D/E ratio of 16.3x would be disqualifying for most companies. However, this is a deliberate capital structure choice: Verisk has repurchased approximately $6B+ in shares since 2020, systematically returning capital well in excess of earnings. For an asset-light data business with $1.2B in annual FCF, minimal tangible asset needs, and no risk of physical asset impairment, thin book equity is a feature of capital efficiency rather than a sign of distress. The relevant leverage metric is Debt/EBITDA (1.9x pro forma), not D/E. That said, the near-zero equity cushion means any large impairment or unexpected loss would technically create negative book equity — an optical risk if not a functional one.

**Stress Test: Revenue Drops 30% for 2 Years.** This is a severe scenario for a business where 84% of revenue is contractual subscriptions with 92% retention. A 30% revenue decline would imply mass cancellations across the insurance industry, which is nearly inconceivable given Verisk's regulatory entrenchment. But assuming it happens: Revenue drops to ~$2.15B. At a stressed 45% EBITDA margin (vs. current 56%), EBITDA would be ~$968M. Against ~$200M in interest expense (post-refinancing), coverage would be 4.8x — still serviceable. FCF would fall to roughly $600-700M, still positive and sufficient to cover debt service. The company would need to suspend buybacks but would not need to raise equity. The $2.2B cash position and $1.25B revolver provide a further multi-year runway. Verdict: Verisk survives a 30% revenue decline for 2 years without raising equity, though buybacks and dividends would be suspended.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Pro forma leverage of 1.9x EBITDA is conservative, all debt is fixed-rate with staggered maturities, $1.2B FCF comfortably covers all obligations, and the business model's recurring revenue base makes severe stress scenarios extremely unlikely.
- **Bear case:** Management has demonstrated a willingness to lever up aggressively for acquisitions (AccuLynx) and buybacks ($1.5B ASR funded by new debt), and book equity is essentially zero — a future large acquisition financed by debt could push leverage above 3x and stress coverage ratios.
- **Confidence:** High — debt figures come from audited financials and SEC filings; the AccuLynx note redemption is confirmed; the February 2026 refinancing terms are public; FCF data is well-established.

## Red Flags
- Debt/EBITDA temporarily reached 2.9x in FY2025 due to AccuLynx acquisition financing — management was willing to lever up to the high end of its range for a deal that ultimately fell through
- Book equity of $309M ($16.3x D/E) creates optical vulnerability and eliminates any tangible book value cushion
- Interest expense rose 37% YoY ($125M to $171M), compressing net income despite revenue growth — a direct consequence of the debt-funded capital return strategy
- The February 2026 $1B note offering was used partly to fund a $1.5B accelerated share repurchase — the company is borrowing to buy back stock at a time when shares trade well below 52-week highs, which could be value-accretive but adds balance sheet risk
- AccuLynx dispute is unresolved — AccuLynx claims the termination is invalid, which could result in litigation costs or settlement

## Score: 7 / 10
Verisk's balance sheet is sound but not a fortress: pro forma leverage of 1.9x EBITDA is conservative, all debt is fixed-rate and well-laddered, FCF covers interest 7x over, and the business easily survives severe stress scenarios — but the near-zero equity base, willingness to lever up for M&A, and borrowing-to-buyback strategy warrant a one-notch deduction from what would otherwise be a higher score for this caliber of business.
