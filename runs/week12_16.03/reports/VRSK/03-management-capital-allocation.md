## Detailed Analysis

**CEO and Strategic Direction.** Lee Shavel became CEO in 2022 after serving as CFO, bringing a disciplined financial lens to the role. His signature accomplishment has been the "Focus and Efficiency" strategy — the divestiture of Wood Mackenzie ($3.1B to Veritas Capital) and the financial services business ($515M to TransUnion), transforming Verisk from a diversified data conglomerate into a focused insurance analytics pure-play. This is exactly the kind of capital allocation decision Buffett would applaud: selling businesses where Verisk had no structural advantage to concentrate on the core franchise where the moat is widest. The divestitures were executed at attractive prices and the proceeds have been returned to shareholders through buybacks.

**Capital Allocation Framework.** Management has committed to returning at least 75% of free cash flow to shareholders through dividends and buybacks. In practice, the return has been even higher. FY2025 saw $624M in buybacks on $1.19B in FCF (52%), plus dividends. FY2024 saw $1.1B in buybacks. In February 2026, Verisk authorized a new $2.5B share repurchase plan and announced a $1.5B accelerated share repurchase program — a massive commitment representing roughly 9% of the current market cap. The quarterly dividend was raised to $0.50/share. This is disciplined, owner-oriented capital allocation: the business generates substantial FCF with limited reinvestment needs (SBC is only $54M, remarkably low for a tech/data company), and management returns the excess rather than empire-building.

**Share Count Reduction.** The share count has declined from approximately 163 million diluted shares in 2020 to roughly 138 million in 2025 — a 15% reduction over five years. This is meaningful per-share value creation at a compounding rate of approximately 3% annually. Combined with 6-7% organic revenue growth and margin expansion, the per-share economics are compounding at a mid-teens rate. The "cannibal" label from institutional investors is well-earned. Importantly, SBC dilution is negligible at $54M against a $28B market cap, so the buybacks represent genuine shrinkage rather than merely offsetting stock-based compensation.

**Compensation Design.** CEO Shavel's FY2024 total compensation of $12.1M breaks down as: $1M base salary, $1.53M cash bonus, $1.9M stock options, $7.6M restricted stock/performance shares, and $45K other. The heavy equity weighting (~79% of total comp) aligns management with shareholders. The base salary of $1M is modest for a company of this size. Performance-based equity likely ties to revenue growth, EBITDA margins, and TSR metrics, though specific vesting conditions require further proxy statement analysis. There are no obvious red flags like excessive perquisites, guaranteed bonuses, or change-of-control golden parachutes that would suggest misalignment.

**Governance.** The board structure is sound: 11 directors, 10 of whom are independent. Bruce Hansen serves as independent board chair, creating separation from CEO Shavel. All six standing committees (Audit, Compensation, Executive, Finance and Investment, Governance/Sustainability/Nominating, and Risk) are 100% independent. Four of 11 directors are women. Recent board additions (Christopher Perry from Broadridge, Sabra Purtill from AIG) bring relevant insurance and financial services expertise. The CEO does not sit on any board committees, which is appropriate governance practice.

**Candor and Communication Style.** Based on earnings call language, management communicates in a straightforward, numbers-driven style. Shavel, as a former CFO, leads with metrics rather than narrative. The divestitures were explained clearly as strategic focus decisions, not dressed up as "portfolio optimization." The failed AccuLynx acquisition (abandoned in late 2025) and subsequent debt redemption show willingness to walk away from deals that don't meet the bar — a sign of discipline, not weakness. Management talks like operators, not promoters.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Shavel has demonstrated exceptional capital allocation discipline — divesting non-core businesses at good prices, aggressively repurchasing shares, maintaining low SBC, and keeping the board independent and aligned.
- **Bear case:** Insider ownership levels appear modest in absolute terms (management owns a small fraction of the $28B market cap), and the aggressive buyback program at historically elevated multiples could destroy value if the stock mean-reverts.
- **Confidence:** High — the capital allocation track record over 2022-2026 is objectively strong, governance is clean, compensation is well-structured, and the 75%+ FCF return commitment provides a clear framework.

## Red Flags
- Buybacks have been executed across a wide price range including periods when VRSK traded near all-time highs (~$290-320) — not all repurchases have been at attractive prices
- Total debt of $4.9B (Debt/EBITDA 2.9x) is elevated for a company returning 75%+ of FCF; leverage is manageable but not conservative
- Insider ownership in absolute dollar terms is not disclosed as exceptionally high — management is more "professional steward" than "owner-operator"
- M&A pace is accelerating (4 acquisitions in 2025) after the divestiture phase, raising the question of whether discipline will hold as the company seeks growth

## Score: 8 / 10
Management under Lee Shavel has demonstrated strong stewardship: transformative divestitures to focus the business, aggressive and genuine share buybacks (15% reduction in 5 years with minimal SBC offset), clean governance, and a clear capital return framework — docked one point for elevated leverage, modest insider ownership, and buybacks at rich valuations.
