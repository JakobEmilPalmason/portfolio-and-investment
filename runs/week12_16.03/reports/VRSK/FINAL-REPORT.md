## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Verisk sells subscription data, standardized forms, and catastrophe models to ~90% of U.S. P&C insurers, who keep buying because the data is embedded in their regulatory filings, underwriting workflows, and claims processes.
2. **Moat**: Verisk is the legally designated statistical agent in all U.S. states with a 30B+ record contributory data flywheel and switching costs so high that migration would require insurers to rebuild years of regulatory filings, retrain staff, and risk compliance failures.
3. **Economics**: ROIC has averaged ~24% over four years, EBITDA margins hit 56%, and $1.19B in FCF was generated on just $244M of capex — a 4.9x FCF-to-capex ratio with 131% FCF conversion.
4. **Management**: CEO Shavel divested $3.6B of non-core businesses to focus the franchise, reduced the share count 15% in five years with minimal SBC dilution ($54M), and commits 75%+ of FCF to shareholder returns.
5. **Debt risk**: Pro forma leverage is 1.9x EBITDA with all fixed-rate debt, $1.2B annual FCF covers interest 7x over, and even a 30% revenue decline still produces positive FCF — the business survives any plausible recession.
6. **Price**: At $204 and 24x forward earnings, the market must believe Verisk grows like a utility at 6-7% with zero margin expansion — any re-rating toward its historical 30x+ multiple on consensus 20% EPS growth delivers 30%+ upside.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is the margin of safety — 85% recurring revenue, 92% retention, and a quasi-regulatory monopoly that has never experienced a meaningful revenue disruption in 55 years — though the price offers no discount to the $195 bear-case IV.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Organic revenue growth below 4% for two consecutive quarters or customer retention falling below 85% would signal the moat is eroding and the compounding thesis is broken.

## Key Risks

1. **No price margin of safety.** At $204, the stock trades 55% above the quant model's conservative IV of $132 and ~5% above the AI-estimated bear case of $195. The entire thesis rests on business quality and growth execution rather than a valuation cushion. If the multiple compresses further or growth disappoints, there is meaningful downside.

2. **Elevated debt and aggressive financial engineering.** Total debt spiked to $4.9B (2.9x EBITDA) from the failed AccuLynx acquisition financing. Pro forma leverage normalizes to ~1.9x, but management has demonstrated willingness to lever up for both M&A and buybacks. The $1.5B accelerated share repurchase funded by new debt at elevated rates adds financial risk. Book equity is essentially zero ($309M).

3. **Single-industry concentration.** Approximately 80% of revenue comes from U.S. P&C insurance. While the customer base is diversified across thousands of insurers, a structural shift in the insurance model (parametric insurance, regulatory reform of the statistical agent framework) would be existential and impossible to offset quickly.

4. **International moat is materially narrower.** Outside the U.S., Verisk lacks regulatory embeddedness and must compete on product merit. Growth from international expansion may disappoint relative to the domestic franchise quality.

5. **Buybacks at rich valuations.** Share repurchases have been executed across a wide price range including near all-time highs ($290-320). Not all repurchases have been value-accretive, and the current accelerated buyback program adds financial leverage.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: Price drops below $185 providing genuine margin of safety vs AI bear case; FY2026 Q1 earnings confirm 20%+ EPS growth with continued margin expansion; Debt/EBITDA normalizes below 2.0x as acquisition-related debt is fully retired

## Detailed Analysis

**What Verisk Sells and To Whom.** Verisk Analytics is a data analytics and technology company that serves almost exclusively the property and casualty (P&C) insurance industry. Through its legacy ISO (Insurance Services Office) subsidiary, Verisk provides standardized policy forms, rating rules, loss cost data, and statistical agent services to insurers across all U.S. states and territories. It also provides catastrophe modeling (AIR Worldwide), claims analytics (anti-fraud, property estimating via Xactware/XactAnalysis), and underwriting decision-support tools. Its customers are insurance carriers, reinsurers, brokers, and regulators. The company divested its non-insurance businesses (Wood Mackenzie sold to Veritas Capital for $3.1B; financial services sold to TransUnion for $515M), making it a pure-play insurance data company since 2023.

**How It Makes Money.** Verisk operates a subscription and long-term agreement model. Approximately 83% of insurance revenues come from recurring subscriptions. Insurers pay annual fees for access to ISO forms, loss costs, and statistical services (Underwriting segment, $2.18B in FY2025). They also pay for claims workflow tools, anti-fraud databases, and property estimating solutions (Claims segment, $893M). Pricing is typically tied to insurer premium volume or usage, creating organic growth even without new customer wins. Transaction-based revenue from claims estimating and certain data products provides a smaller but growing complement.

**Key Revenue Drivers.** The business is governed by a small number of predictable variables: (1) annual subscription price increases, which Verisk has consistently pushed through given its mission-critical, non-discretionary role; (2) net customer additions and deeper penetration within existing accounts (new product cross-sell); (3) catastrophe and weather event frequency, which drives demand for risk modeling; and (4) new product launches such as the upcoming Verisk Synergy Studio cloud-native cat modeling platform (planned 2026 launch) and generative AI underwriting tools. FY2025 organic constant-currency revenue grew 6.6%, with subscription revenue growing 10.6% in Q1 2025 alone.

**Revenue Predictability and Customer Concentration.** This is one of the most predictable revenue streams in all of software/data analytics. The 92% retention rate, subscription-dominant model, and regulatory embeddedness create a near-utility-like revenue profile. No single customer represents a meaningful share of total revenue — the client base spans the entire U.S. P&C industry (top 100 insurers all use Verisk), plus expanding international and life/annuity segments. The business is not cyclical in any traditional sense; insurance is a necessity and Verisk's tools are embedded in regulatory compliance workflows.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Verisk is one of the simplest, most predictable businesses in the market — a subscription data monopoly embedded in regulatory infrastructure, explainable in under two minutes.
- **Bear case:** The single-industry focus (P&C insurance) means any structural disruption to the insurance model itself could reduce Verisk's relevance, though this is a very low-probability tail risk.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is transparent, the financials are clean, the revenue streams are few and well-documented, and the company is now a focused pure-play after divestitures.

## Red Flags
- Near-total dependence on one industry (P&C insurance) — diversification is minimal
- Recent acquisitions (SuranceBay for $162.5M, Nasdaq Risk Modelling for Catastrophes, Opta, Pruvan) add complexity; integration risk is non-zero
- Failed AccuLynx acquisition in late 2025 suggests some M&A discipline but also ambition creep

## Score: 9 / 10
Verisk is an exceptionally clear business: two revenue streams, subscription-dominant, embedded in regulatory infrastructure, serving a well-defined customer base with 92% retention — this is among the easiest businesses to understand in the market.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — VRSK

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Data Sources:** Verisk 10-K (FY2025), Morningstar (wide moat rating), Verisk.com, Wikipedia (ISO history), Alpine Software (ISO history), Verisk investor relations, Waver.one, ainvest.com, beanvest.com

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Verisk/ISO is the designated statistical agent in all U.S. states and territories — a quasi-regulatory monopoly position established over 50+ years | 5 |
| 2 | Switching costs are among the highest in enterprise software: insurers build products, filings, and compliance workflows around ISO forms and loss costs; migration would take years | 5 |
| 3 | Network effects from "give-to-get" contributory data model — 30B+ statistical records improve as more insurers participate, creating a self-reinforcing data flywheel | 5 |
| 4 | Operating margins of 42-44% and ROIC of 20-31% over the past four years provide quantitative evidence of pricing power and competitive insulation | 4 |
| 5 | 92% customer retention and 83% subscription revenue demonstrate the stickiness of the embedded relationship | 4 |
| 6 | Morningstar assigns a "wide moat" rating, reflecting durable competitive advantages expected to persist for 20+ years | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Moat Type: Regulatory Embeddedness + Switching Costs + Network Effects + Scale.** Verisk's moat is not a single competitive advantage but a layered fortress. At its foundation is the regulatory role: ISO was formed in 1971 as the insurance industry's advisory and rating organization, and its subsidiary ISO Data Inc. serves as the designated statistical agent across all U.S. jurisdictions. This means insurers are legally required to report data through ISO for regulatory compliance. Some 2,000 regulatory filings are processed annually, supported by 240 experts and specialized lawyers who maintain ISO's standardized forms, rules, and loss costs. This regulatory infrastructure took decades to build and cannot be replicated by a startup or tech entrant — it would require state-by-state regulatory approval, industry-wide data contribution agreements, and decades of actuarial credibility.

**Switching Costs.** The switching costs are structural, not merely contractual. Insurance companies build their entire product architecture — policy forms, underwriting rules, pricing models, claims workflows, and regulatory filings — around Verisk's ISO standards. Migrating away would require rewriting policy language, refiling with state regulators, retraining thousands of employees, migrating decades of historical data, and risking regulatory non-compliance. This is a multi-year, multi-hundred-million-dollar undertaking with no guarantee of success. The result is near-zero voluntary churn among core customers. Verisk processes approximately 3,000 data quality checks on contributed data, and its statistical databases represent the actuarial foundation upon which the entire U.S. P&C industry prices risk.

**Network Effects and Data Flywheel.** Verisk operates a "give-to-get" contributory data model: insurers contribute their claims and loss data in exchange for access to the aggregated, anonymized dataset. With over 30 billion statistical records spanning 100+ countries, the dataset's value increases with each additional contributor. New entrants face a chicken-and-egg problem — they cannot build a competitive dataset without participation, and insurers have no incentive to participate in a dataset that lacks critical mass. This network effect compounds over decades and is virtually impossible to disrupt.

**Evidence in Numbers.** Gross margins of 68-70%, operating margins of 42-44%, and ROIC of 20-31% are not the profile of a commodity business competing on price. These margins have been stable or expanding over the past five years, even as Verisk divested lower-margin non-insurance segments. FCF conversion of 131% in FY2025 (relative to net income) reflects the asset-light, high-incremental-margin nature of a data business. Annual subscription price increases are passed through with minimal pushback because the cost of Verisk's services is a rounding error relative to the premiums and claims the data helps manage.

**Durability: 10-Year View.** The moat is as durable as any in the market. Insurance will still need standardized forms, loss costs, catastrophe models, and claims analytics in 2036. Regulatory requirements will not disappear. The contributory data model has no viable alternative. AI and machine learning are additive to Verisk's capabilities (the company is investing in generative AI underwriting tools and cloud-native cat modeling), not threats to them — Verisk has the data upon which any AI model must be trained. The primary risk is a hypothetical regulatory restructuring that eliminates the statistical agent role, which has not been seriously proposed in the 55-year history of the current framework.

**Weakest Point.** The weakest point is international expansion: outside the U.S., Verisk lacks the same regulatory embeddedness and must compete more on product merit. The Opta acquisition (Canadian property intelligence) and international cat modeling are steps toward building similar positions abroad, but the moat is narrower outside North America.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Verisk possesses one of the widest and most durable moats in the market — a quasi-regulatory monopoly reinforced by switching costs, network effects, and scale that would take decades and billions to replicate.
- **Bear case:** The moat is strongest in U.S. P&C insurance; international expansion and new verticals (life, annuity) lack the same structural advantages, and growth could disappoint if these areas underperform.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat sources are observable, quantifiable, and have been tested over 50+ years; Morningstar's wide moat designation and stable 40%+ operating margins corroborate the thesis.

## Red Flags
- International moat is significantly narrower than domestic — Verisk is not the statistical agent outside the U.S.
- Potential for large insurers to in-source analytics using their own data science teams and AI, though this has not materialized despite years of speculation
- Acquisition-driven expansion (SuranceBay, Opta, Pruvan) moves into adjacencies where Verisk's structural advantages are weaker
- AI disruption risk is low but non-zero — a transformative AI approach to insurance pricing could theoretically reduce dependency on historical statistical data

## Score: 9 / 10
Verisk's moat is among the widest in the market: regulatory embeddedness as the designated statistical agent, extreme switching costs from decades of integrated workflows, a self-reinforcing data flywheel with 30B+ records, and 40%+ operating margins that have proven durable over multiple cycles — this is a franchise that would take a generation and billions of dollars to replicate.

# Management & Capital Allocation — VRSK

## Detailed Analysis

**CEO and Strategic Direction.** Lee Shavel became CEO in 2022 after serving as CFO, bringing a disciplined financial lens to the role. His signature accomplishment has been the "Focus and Efficiency" strategy — the divestiture of Wood Mackenzie ($3.1B to Veritas Capital) and the financial services business ($515M to TransUnion), transforming Verisk from a diversified data conglomerate into a focused insurance analytics pure-play. This is exactly the kind of capital allocation decision Buffett would applaud: selling businesses where Verisk had no structural advantage to concentrate on the core franchise where the moat is widest. The divestitures were executed at attractive prices and the proceeds have been returned to shareholders through buybacks.

**Capital Allocation Framework.** Management has committed to returning at least 75% of free cash flow to shareholders through dividends and buybacks. In practice, the return has been even higher. FY2025 saw $624M in buybacks on $1.19B in FCF (52%), plus dividends. FY2024 saw $1.1B in buybacks. In February 2026, Verisk authorized a new $2.5B share repurchase plan and announced a $1.5B accelerated share repurchase program — a massive commitment representing roughly 9% of the current market cap. The quarterly dividend was raised to $0.50/share. This is disciplined, owner-oriented capital allocation: the business generates substantial FCF with limited reinvestment needs (SBC is only $54M, remarkably low for a tech/data company), and management returns the excess rather than empire-building.

**Share Count Reduction.** The share count has declined from approximately 163 million diluted shares in 2020 to roughly 138 million in 2025 — a 15% reduction over five years. This is meaningful per-share value creation at a compounding rate of approximately 3% annually. Combined with 6-7% organic revenue growth and margin expansion, the per-share economics are compounding at a mid-teens rate. The "cannibal" label from institutional investors is well-earned. Importantly, SBC dilution is negligible at $54M against a $28B market cap, so the buybacks represent genuine shrinkage rather than merely offsetting stock-based compensation.

**Compensation Design.** CEO Shavel's FY2024 total compensation of $12.1M breaks down as: $1M base salary, $1.53M cash bonus, $1.9M stock options, $7.6M restricted stock/performance shares, and $45K other. The heavy equity weighting (~79% of total comp) aligns management with shareholders. The base salary of $1M is modest for a company of this size. Performance-based equity likely ties to revenue growth, EBITDA margins, and TSR metrics, though specific vesting conditions require further proxy statement analysis. There are no obvious red flags like excessive perquisites, guaranteed bonuses, or change-of-control golden parachutes that would suggest misalignment.

**Governance.** The board structure is sound: 11 directors, 10 of whom are independent. Bruce Hansen serves as independent board chair, creating separation from CEO Shavel. All six standing committees (Audit, Compensation, Executive, Finance and Investment, Governance/Sustainability/Nominating, and Risk) are 100% independent. Four of 11 directors are women. Recent board additions (Christopher Perry from Broadridge, Sabra Purtill from AIG) bring relevant insurance and financial services expertise. The CEO does not sit on any board committees, which is appropriate governance practice.

**Candor and Communication Style.** Based on earnings call language, management communicates in a straightforward, numbers-driven style. Shavel, as a former CFO, leads with metrics rather than narrative. The divestitures were explained clearly as strategic focus decisions, not dressed up as "portfolio optimization." The failed AccuLynx acquisition (abandoned in late 2025) and subsequent debt redemption show willingness to walk away from deals that don't meet the bar — a sign of discipline, not weakness. Management talks like operators, not promoters.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Shavel has demonstrated exceptional capital allocation discipline — divesting non-core businesses at good prices, aggressively repurchasing shares, maintaining low SBC, and keeping the board independent and aligned.
- **Bear case:** Insider ownership levels appear modest in absolute terms (management owns a small fraction of the $28B market cap), and the aggressive buyback program at historically elevated multiples could destroy value if the stock mean-reverts.
- **Confidence:** High — the capital allocation track record over 2022-2026 is objectively strong, governance is clean, compensation is well-structured, and the 75%+ FCF return commitment provides a clear framework.

## Red Flags
- Buybacks have been executed across a wide price range including periods when VRSK traded near all-time highs (~$290-320) — not all repurchases have been at attractive prices
- Total debt of $4.9B (Debt/EBITDA 2.9x) is elevated for a company returning 75%+ of FCF; leverage is manageable but not conservative
- Insider ownership in absolute dollar terms is not disclosed as exceptionally high — management is more "professional steward" than "owner-operator"
- M&A pace is accelerating (4 acquisitions in 2025) after the divestiture phase, raising the question of whether discipline will hold as the company seeks growth

## Score: 8 / 10
Management under Lee Shavel has demonstrated strong stewardship: transformative divestitures to focus the business, aggressive and genuine share buybacks (15% reduction in 5 years with minimal SBC offset), clean governance, and a clear capital return framework — docked one point for elevated leverage, modest insider ownership, and buybacks at rich valuations.

# Business Economics — VRSK

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Data Sources:** Verisk Q4 2025 earnings release, FY2025 10-K filing data, investor presentations, Yahoo Finance, StockTitan, Investing.com, company newsroom

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC averaged ~24% over FY2022-2025 (25.6% in FY2025), consistently exceeding WACC by ~20 percentage points — textbook economic value creation | 5 |
| 2 | Subscription revenue is 84% of total and grew 7.7% OCC in FY2025, with ~92% client retention — extremely high-quality, recurring revenue base | 5 |
| 3 | FCF surged 30% to $1.19B in FY2025 with 131% FCF conversion (FCF/net income), demonstrating superior cash generation relative to reported earnings | 5 |
| 4 | Adjusted EBITDA margin expanded 200bps to 56.1% in FY2025, showing strong operating leverage on mid-single-digit revenue growth | 4 |
| 5 | CapEx/revenue is ~8% ($244M on $3.07B), and the business is fundamentally asset-light — most "assets" are proprietary data and software, not physical infrastructure | 4 |
| 6 | SBC is modest at $54M (1.8% of revenue, ~4.5% of FCF), meaning FCF is not materially overstated by excluding stock comp | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Verisk's ROIC profile is exceptional for a data analytics business. Over FY2022-2025, ROIC has ranged from 19.6% to 31.0%, averaging roughly 24%. The FY2025 figure of 25.6% comfortably exceeds any reasonable cost of capital estimate (8-10%). ROE is optically extreme at 444% due to minimal equity ($309M) after years of aggressive buybacks — this is more a capital structure artifact than an operating metric. ROA of 14.7% on a $6.2B asset base is solid and more representative of underlying economics. The key insight: Verisk earns very high returns on the capital it deploys because its competitive advantages (proprietary data, regulatory entrenchment, switching costs) translate directly into pricing power and low marginal cost of serving additional customers.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** Gross margins have expanded steadily from 67.0% in FY2022 to 69.9% in FY2025, reflecting the increasing mix of higher-margin subscription revenue and the inherent scalability of data products. Operating margins held at 43-44% in FY2023-2025 (down from the 56% level in FY2022, which reflected the one-time gain dynamics around the Wood Mackenzie separation). On an adjusted EBITDA basis, which is more operationally representative, margins expanded 200bps to 56.1% in FY2025 — a very strong level for any software/data company. The incremental margin story is compelling: Verisk grew revenue $193M in FY2025 while adjusted EBITDA grew by roughly $130M, implying incremental EBITDA margins near 67%. This operating leverage is the hallmark of a scaled data business with high fixed costs and low variable costs.

**Cash Generation.** FCF of $1.19B in FY2025 represents a 30% year-over-year increase and 131% FCF conversion (FCF/net income). This conversion ratio consistently above 100% signals that reported earnings understate the true cash-generating power of the business, largely because D&A ($327M) exceeds capex ($244M) — a classic sign of an asset-light compounder where past investments are still generating returns. Owner earnings (net income + D&A - capex, adjusted for working capital) came in at $991M. The gap between FCF ($1.19B) and owner earnings ($991M) is explained by favorable working capital timing, which can fluctuate year to year. Importantly, SBC at $54M is low enough that FCF is not materially inflated by its exclusion — a common concern with tech companies.

**Capital Intensity.** Verisk's capex of $244M (8% of revenue) is primarily spent on software development, platform modernization (the "Core Lines Reimagine" initiative), and next-generation catastrophe models. There is very little physical infrastructure spend. Maintenance capex is likely in the range of $150-180M (roughly in line with D&A on legacy systems), meaning true growth capex is only $60-90M per year. This is an extraordinarily capital-efficient business: it generates $1.19B in FCF on just $244M of reinvestment. The ratio of FCF to capex (4.9x) is among the best in the data analytics space.

**Revenue Quality.** The 84% subscription / 16% transactional revenue mix is highly favorable. Subscription revenue grew 7.7% OCC while transactional revenue declined 6.5% OCC in FY2025, partly due to historically low catastrophe activity reducing property estimating volumes. The 92% client retention rate understates true stickiness — Verisk's data and forms are embedded in regulatory filings, underwriting workflows, and claims processes across 97% of U.S. P&C insurers. Switching costs are enormous because insurers would need to rebuild their entire data infrastructure, retrain staff, and re-file regulatory documentation. This creates revenue that is not just recurring but effectively permanent.

**Operating Leverage.** The business exhibits significant operating leverage. Revenue grew 6.6% OCC in FY2025 while adjusted EBITDA grew 8.5% and FCF grew 30%. The high fixed-cost / low variable-cost structure means each incremental dollar of subscription revenue drops through to EBITDA at a rate above 60%. Management has guided 2026 revenue of $3.19-3.24B and EBITDA of $1.79-1.83B, implying continued margin expansion. The company is also investing in AI capabilities to enhance productivity, which could provide further operating leverage over the medium term.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Verisk is a capital-light, high-ROIC compounder with 84% recurring revenue, 56% EBITDA margins, expanding operating leverage, and a FCF machine generating $1.2B+ annually on minimal reinvestment needs.
- **Bear case:** Transactional revenue is declining, the business is mature with mid-single-digit organic growth, and aggressive capital return (buybacks funded partly by debt) could limit flexibility if the insurance market environment shifts.
- **Confidence:** High — financial data is audited and publicly available; the business model economics are well-established over a long track record; margin and FCF trends are clear and consistent.

## Red Flags
- Net income declined 5.1% YoY in FY2025 ($908M vs $958M) despite revenue growth, partly due to higher interest expense ($171M vs $125M) from debt taken on for the failed AccuLynx acquisition
- Transactional revenue declined 6.5% OCC, exposing vulnerability in the non-subscription segment to catastrophe activity levels
- ROE of 444% is cosmetically impressive but masks a near-zero equity base created by buyback-driven leverage — not a sign of conservative financial management
- The FY2022 operating margin of 56.3% included one-time items from the Wood Mackenzie separation, making trend analysis require adjustment

## Score: 9 / 10
Verisk exhibits textbook elite business economics: 25%+ ROIC sustained over multiple years, 56% EBITDA margins with expansion, $1.2B FCF on $244M capex, 84% recurring revenue with 92% retention, and powerful operating leverage — the only deduction is the mature mid-single-digit growth profile and reliance on capital returns rather than organic reinvestment opportunities.

## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Verisk's balance sheet tells a story of two events in 2025. First, in mid-2025, the company raised $1.5B in senior notes and drew on credit facilities to finance the proposed $2.35B AccuLynx acquisition. When the FTC failed to complete its review by the December 2025 deadline, Verisk terminated the deal and immediately moved to redeem the $1.5B acquisition-related notes at 101% of par (per the special mandatory redemption clause). Pro forma for that redemption, leverage drops from the reported 2.9x Debt/EBITDA to approximately 1.9x — well within management's stated 2-3x target range and consistent with historical levels. The remaining debt stack consists of legacy senior notes with staggered maturities plus the new $1B offering from February 2026. All debt is fixed-rate, eliminating floating-rate risk. The debt maturity profile is well-laddered: the February 2026 notes mature in 2031 and 2036, and earlier tranches likely span the intervening years. There is no near-term maturity wall.

**Interest Coverage.** Reported interest coverage fell from 10.9x in FY2024 to 7.9x in FY2025 as interest expense rose from $125M to $171M. This deterioration is almost entirely attributable to carrying the AccuLynx acquisition debt for several months before the deal was terminated. Post-redemption of the $1.5B notes, annual interest expense should decline meaningfully. Even at the FY2025 trough, 7.9x coverage is adequate for an investment-grade data analytics company with 84% recurring revenue. Using FCF instead of EBIT, the FCF/interest coverage ratio is 7.0x ($1.19B / $171M), which provides a more conservative but still comfortable margin.

**Liquidity.** At FY2025 year-end, Verisk held $2.2B in cash against a current ratio of 1.2x. The company also has a $1.25B syndicated revolving credit facility (expanded from its prior size). Combined, this gives Verisk approximately $3.4B in available liquidity — more than sufficient to cover any near-term maturities, working capital needs, or unexpected disruptions. The high cash balance at year-end partly reflects the AccuLynx financing proceeds that had not yet been redeemed; the normalized cash position will be lower but still ample given the FCF generation profile.

**Refinancing Risk.** Verisk is a Baa2/BBB+ rated issuer with consistent access to investment-grade debt markets. The February 2026 offering ($1B at blended ~4.8%) was successfully placed with major underwriters (BofA Securities, Wells Fargo) and was used to refinance short-term facilities into long-dated bonds. The company has no significant maturities creating a "wall" in any single year. Refinancing risk is low: the business generates $1.2B+ in annual FCF, debt markets remain accessible to IG issuers, and the company has demonstrated a willingness to manage maturities proactively.

**The Equity Question.** Book equity of $309M looks alarming, and the D/E ratio of 16.3x would be disqualifying for most companies. However, this is a deliberate capital structure choice: Verisk has repurchased approximately $6B+ in shares since 2020, systematically returning capital well in excess of earnings. For an asset-light data business with $1.2B in annual FCF, minimal tangible asset needs, and no risk of physical asset impairment, thin book equity is a feature of capital efficiency rather than a sign of distress. The relevant leverage metric is Debt/EBITDA (1.9x pro forma), not D/E. That said, the near-zero equity cushion means any large impairment or unexpected loss would technically create negative book equity — an optical risk if not a functional one.

**Stress Test: Revenue Drops 30% for 2 Years.** This is a severe scenario for a business where 84% of revenue is contractual subscriptions with 92% retention. A 30% revenue decline would imply mass cancellations across the insurance industry, which is nearly inconceivable given Verisk's regulatory entrenchment. But assuming it happens: Revenue drops to ~$2.15B. At a stressed 45% EBITDA margin (vs. current 56%), EBITDA would be ~$968M. Against ~$200M in interest expense (post-refinancing), coverage would be 4.8x — still serviceable. FCF would fall to roughly $600-700M, still positive and sufficient to cover debt service. The company would need to suspend buybacks but would not need to raise equity. The $2.2B cash position and $1.25B revolver provide a further multi-year runway. Verdict: Verisk survives a 30% revenue decline for 2 years without raising equity, though buybacks and dividends would be suspended.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Pro forma leverage of 1.9x EBITDA is conservative, all debt is fixed-rate with staggered maturities, $1.2B FCF comfortably covers all obligations, and the business model's recurring revenue base makes severe stress scenarios extremely unlikely.
- **Bear case:** Management has demonstrated a willingness to lever up aggressively for acquisitions (AccuLynx) and buybacks ($1.5B ASR funded by new debt), and book equity is essentially zero — a future large acquisition financed by debt could push leverage above 3x and stress coverage ratios.
- **Confidence:** High — debt figures come from audited financials and SEC filings; the AccuLynx note redemption is confirmed; the February 2026 refinancing terms are public; FCF data is well-established.

## Red Flags
- Debt/EBITDA temporarily reached 2.9x in FY2025 due to AccuLynx acquisition financing — management was willing to lever up to the high end of its range for a deal that ultimately fell through
- Book equity of $309M ($16.3x D/E) creates optical vulnerability and eliminates any tangible book value cushion
- Interest expense rose 37% YoY ($125M to $171M), compressing net income despite revenue growth — a direct consequence of the debt-funded capital return strategy
- The February 2026 $1B note offering was used partly to fund a $1.5B accelerated share repurchase — the company is borrowing to buy back stock at a time when shares trade well below 52-week highs, which could be value-accretive but adds balance sheet risk
- AccuLynx dispute is unresolved — AccuLynx claims the termination is invalid, which could result in litigation costs or settlement

## Score: 7 / 10
Verisk's balance sheet is sound but not a fortress: pro forma leverage of 1.9x EBITDA is conservative, all debt is fixed-rate and well-laddered, FCF covers interest 7x over, and the business easily survives severe stress scenarios — but the near-zero equity base, willingness to lever up for M&A, and borrowing-to-buyback strategy warrant a one-notch deduction from what would otherwise be a higher score for this caliber of business.

Given Verisk's FCF durability, even the bear case assumes continued profitability — this is not a business at risk of earnings collapse. The more relevant MOS framework here is the gap between current price and base case IV, which is substantial at ~$66 per share.

### Business Margin of Safety

Verisk possesses one of the widest business margins of safety in the data analytics universe. The company is the sole provider of ISO statistical data used by the US property and casualty insurance industry for rate filings. This is not a "nice to have" — insurers are legally required to use actuarial data for rate-setting in most states, and Verisk has been the industry standard for over 50 years. Customer retention exceeds 90%, subscription revenue is ~85% of total, and switching costs are enormous because Verisk data is embedded in underwriting models, claims workflows, and regulatory filings. The business has survived recessions, hard and soft insurance markets, and technology shifts without meaningful revenue disruption.

### Downside vs Upside Asymmetry

The risk-reward setup is asymmetric in the investor's favor. Downside to the bear case is approximately 5% ($204 to $195), while upside to the base case is 32% ($204 to $270) and upside to the bull case is 76% ($204 to $360). Even if the multiple remains compressed at current levels, mid-teens EPS growth over 2-3 years would push the price to $260+ on unchanged multiples. The floor is well-defined because Verisk would generate $1.0B+ in FCF even in a recession, implying a worst-case FCF yield of ~5% at $140 (the absolute floor scenario where the multiple contracts to 12x FCF).

### Zero Scenarios

Permanent capital loss scenarios for Verisk are extremely limited. The business does not depend on a single product, technology cycle, or customer. It does not carry credit risk. It does not manufacture anything physical. The closest zero scenario would be a regulatory dismantling of the ISO statistical agent model — essentially forcing insurers to develop proprietary actuarial data. This is theoretically possible but practically implausible given the cost and complexity involved. A more realistic severe scenario is a hostile M&A attempt at a depressed valuation, which would likely still deliver a premium to the current price.
