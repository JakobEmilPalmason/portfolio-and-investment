## Detailed Analysis

AstraZeneca's balance sheet is conservatively managed and has improved materially since the $39B Alexion acquisition in 2021 temporarily levered up the company. Total debt of $29.1B against EBITDA of $19.5B gives a Debt/EBITDA ratio of just 1.5x (1.1x on a net debt basis after deducting $5.7B cash). This compares favorably to the broader pharma sector where 2-3x net leverage is typical. Moody's upgraded AstraZeneca to A1 (stable outlook) in early 2025, citing strengthening portfolio and pipeline, while S&P rates the company A+ — both solidly in the upper-single-A category. These are ratings that provide access to the cheapest corporate borrowing rates and reflect minimal credit risk.

The debt maturity profile is well-managed. AstraZeneca has bonds maturing across 2026 (1.2% notes), 2027 (4.8% and 3.625% EUR notes), 2028 (4.875% and 1.75% notes), 2030 (4.9% notes), 2032 (3.75% EUR notes), and 2033 (4.875% notes), among others. No single maturity year represents a disproportionate refinancing burden, and the mix of EUR and USD issuances provides natural currency hedging against their geographically diversified revenue base. With A1/A+ ratings and strong cash flow, refinancing maturing tranches is essentially zero-risk — the company could fund its entire 2026 maturity wall from operating cash flow alone without touching the revolving facility.

Liquidity is robust. The $5.7B cash position, combined with the $4.9B undrawn revolving credit facility available until April 2031, provides over $10B of immediately available liquidity. Annual operating cash flow of $14.6B means the company generates enough cash in roughly two years to retire its entire gross debt balance. The current ratio of 0.9x looks optically weak but is typical for large pharma companies with significant payables and accruals; when adjusted for the undrawn credit facility, the effective liquidity position is comfortable. The company also maintains US and Euro commercial paper programs for short-term funding flexibility.

Stress testing the balance sheet: if revenue dropped 30% for two consecutive years (from $58.7B to ~$41B), gross margins would likely compress modestly given the largely fixed-cost drug manufacturing model, but operating income would still be in the $4-6B range given the variable cost of R&D that could be reduced. EBITDA under this severe scenario would likely be $8-12B, keeping Debt/EBITDA at 2.5-3.5x — stressed but manageable and still investment-grade. Interest coverage would remain above 4x even in the stress case. Critically, AstraZeneca would not need to issue equity to survive a prolonged downturn. The $4.9B revolving facility and ability to defer growth CapEx (much of the ~$3.3B guided increase is discretionary manufacturing expansion) provide significant buffers.

Off-balance-sheet obligations are manageable for a company of this scale. AstraZeneca maintains defined benefit pension schemes, primarily in the UK and Sweden, though these have been progressively de-risked and are not a material liability relative to the balance sheet. Operating lease obligations (IFRS 16 recognized) and contingent milestone payments on licensing deals represent the primary off-balance-sheet items. The company's conservative financial policy — modest dividend growth (1.74% yield), limited appetite for transformative M&A after Alexion, and stated commitment to maintaining investment-grade ratings — provides strong governance around leverage discipline.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A1/A+ rated balance sheet with 1.1x net leverage, $10B+ liquidity, and $14.6B annual operating cash flow make AstraZeneca essentially immune to financial distress — management can invest aggressively in the pipeline from a position of strength.
- **Bear case:** Rising CapEx ($50B US investment commitment through 2030) and potential large-scale M&A to fill patent cliff gaps could re-lever the balance sheet, and the current ratio below 1.0 provides limited short-term cushion if commercial paper markets seize.
- **Confidence:** High — The credit ratings, leverage metrics, and liquidity position are all objectively verifiable and leave substantial margin of safety against adverse scenarios.

## Red Flags

- Current ratio below 1.0 (0.9x), though offset by undrawn credit facilities and strong OCF
- $50B US investment commitment through 2030 will absorb significant cash flow and could constrain balance sheet flexibility
- Interest expense of $1.6B is manageable today but represents a step-up from pre-Alexion levels
- Potential for large M&A to address patent cliff could temporarily re-lever the balance sheet (as seen with Alexion in 2021)

## Score: 8 / 10

AstraZeneca's balance sheet is a clear strength — A1/A+ rated, 1.1x net leverage, $10B+ liquidity, and easily passes a 30% revenue stress test without equity issuance — docked from a 9 only because the company is not net-cash and faces a period of elevated capital investment that will prevent further deleveraging.
