## Detailed Analysis

**Switching costs (Strong).** This is Adyen's most powerful moat. Integrating a payment processor into an enterprise's checkout flow across multiple channels (online, in-store POS, mobile), multiple geographies, and multiple payment methods is a deep technical undertaking. Once live, the merchant's fraud rules, reporting dashboards, reconciliation workflows, and financial operations are all built around Adyen's APIs. Ripping this out to switch to a competitor means months of engineering work, risk to conversion rates during migration, and potential revenue loss. The numbers bear this out: volume churn below 1% is exceptional for any B2B software category, and the fact that 80% of growth is organic expansion from existing clients means merchants are choosing to give Adyen more of their business over time, not less.

**Scale and cost advantage (Moderate to Strong).** Adyen built its own technology stack from scratch rather than acquiring and bolting together legacy systems. This single-platform architecture means lower marginal cost per transaction as volume scales, because there is one codebase to maintain rather than dozens. Adyen holds direct acquiring licenses in 40+ countries, meaning it processes transactions "as close to the issuer as possible" rather than routing through intermediaries. This gives it both a cost advantage (fewer middlemen) and a quality advantage (higher authorization rates). The result shows in the numbers: 89% gross margins and 41% operating margins, both expanding.

**Data network effects (Moderate).** Every transaction that flows through Adyen's platform feeds its machine learning models for fraud detection, payment routing optimization, and authorization rate improvement. Adyen's "Uplift" tool reportedly improves payment conversion by 6% for merchants that use it. This is a soft network effect: the more data Adyen accumulates, the better its models perform, making it harder for smaller competitors to match its authorization rates. However, Stripe has comparable data scale, so this advantage is not unique to Adyen.

**Where the moat is weakest: competition from Stripe and commoditization risk.** Stripe holds roughly 25% of the payment management market versus Adyen's 9%. Stripe processed $1.4 trillion in 2024 with 38% volume growth -- faster than Adyen. Stripe is now profitable, has developer-friendly APIs that are considered best-in-class, and is aggressively moving upmarket into enterprise (50% of Fortune 100 use Stripe, including Amazon). In the mid-market, Stripe's brand and developer ecosystem give it an edge. The risk is that payment processing converges toward commodity pricing over time as both platforms achieve comparable scale, authorization rates, and global coverage. If take rates compress toward 10-12 basis points industry-wide, Adyen's margins would come under pressure.

**Durability assessment.** The moat is durable but not invulnerable. The switching cost moat is structural -- it does not reset with each product cycle and only deepens as merchants add channels and geographies. The scale advantage from the single platform is a genuine architectural edge that legacy players (Worldline, FIS, Fiserv) cannot easily replicate without rebuilding from scratch. However, Stripe can and does replicate many of Adyen's capabilities, making this a two-horse race in the modern payments infrastructure category. In 10 years, both Adyen and Stripe are likely to be thriving, but neither will have the market to itself.

**Moat durability risk from regulation.** European regulators have capped interchange fees, which actually benefits acquirers like Adyen (lower costs to pass through). But open banking regulations (PSD2/PSD3) could eventually enable alternative payment rails that bypass traditional card networks entirely, potentially disrupting the economics of card-based payment processing.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Adyen's single-platform architecture and enterprise switching costs create a widening moat as merchants deepen integrations across channels and geographies, with the Platforms business adding a second layer of ecosystem lock-in.
- **Bear case:** Stripe's faster growth, larger market share, and developer mindshare gradually commoditize the enterprise payments market, compressing Adyen's take rates and margins toward industry averages.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High -- the switching cost moat is well-evidenced in the numbers, but the competitive threat from Stripe is real and intensifying, particularly in North America.

## Red Flags

- Adyen's ~9% market share versus Stripe's ~25% means it is the smaller player in a market where scale matters for data advantages and merchant trust.
- FY2025 processed volume growth of 8% reported (21% ex-one large customer) is concerning -- if the headline number reflects a large customer reducing volume, that undermines the churn narrative.
- Asian e-commerce clients (Shein, Temu) represent meaningful volume but may be vulnerable to US tariff policies, adding geopolitical risk to the customer mix.
- Open banking regulation (PSD3) could create alternative payment rails that bypass card networks, potentially disrupting the take-rate economics that underpin Adyen's business.

## Score: 7 / 10

Adyen has a clear moat built on enterprise switching costs, a unique single-platform architecture, and expanding data advantages. However, Stripe is a comparable competitor growing faster with larger market share, preventing this from being a wide-moat 8+. The moat is real and durable, but Adyen must execute well to maintain it in an increasingly competitive duopoly.
