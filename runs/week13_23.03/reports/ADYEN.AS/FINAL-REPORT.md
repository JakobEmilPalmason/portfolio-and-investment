4. **Extreme volatility**: 1.9 beta and history of 40-60% drawdowns mean this stock will amplify any broad market correction, and without price margin of safety there is no conviction anchor to hold through drawdowns.
5. **Capital allocation inertia**: EUR 3-4B in excess cash with no buyback or dividend program, despite the stock trading near 52-week lows and activist pressure from FortyTwo Capital.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

## Detailed Analysis

Adyen is a payment technology company headquartered in Amsterdam. It provides a single technology platform that lets businesses accept payments online, in-store, and on mobile across 200+ countries and 250+ payment methods. The core product is payment processing: when a consumer pays a merchant, Adyen sits in the middle, routing, authorizing, and settling the transaction. For this, Adyen charges the merchant a small per-transaction fee. That is the entire business in one sentence.

The revenue model is transaction-based and scales with volume. Adyen uses an "interchange++" pricing model -- transparent, per-transaction fees rather than bundled or opaque pricing. In FY2025 the company processed EUR 1.39 trillion in payment volume and earned EUR 2.36 billion in net revenue, implying a take rate of roughly 17 basis points. This take rate has actually been trending upward (from ~15 bps in H1 2024 to ~17 bps in H1 2025), driven by a mix shift toward higher-margin mid-market merchants and value-added services. Separately, Adyen earns net interest income on merchant funds sitting in transit -- with EUR 1.4 trillion in annual flow, even a short float generates meaningful income.

The key business drivers are straightforward. First, processed volume growth: as e-commerce and digital payments expand globally and Adyen wins new merchants, volume grows. In FY2025, volume grew 8% reported but 21% excluding one large low-margin customer, suggesting healthy underlying momentum. Second, take rate: Adyen can grow revenue faster than volume by adding services (fraud tools, data analytics, financial products for platforms) that carry higher margins. Third, geographic and segment expansion: the Platforms business (embedded payments for software companies) grew volumes 51% YoY in 2024 and is opening up the mid-market. North America is the key geographic growth lever.

Revenue predictability is high for a payments company. There are no long-term take-or-pay contracts, but the business is deeply sticky: 80% of growth comes from existing customers expanding usage, and volume churn is below 1%. Once a large enterprise integrates Adyen into its checkout flow across channels and geographies, ripping it out is painful and expensive. The revenue is essentially recurring transaction-based income tied to the secular growth of digital commerce. Seasonality exists (Q4 holiday spending is strongest), but the underlying trajectory is steady.

Customer concentration is low and improving. The top 10 merchants accounted for just 12% of net revenue in FY2024. The customer base spans e-commerce, retail, travel, hospitality, and platforms. Key names include Nike, Uber, eBay, Microsoft, Spotify, H&M, and Booking.com. The Platforms segment diversifies the base further by adding thousands of smaller sub-merchants under platform umbrellas.

Can you explain this business in two minutes? Absolutely. Adyen processes payments for businesses. Every time someone buys something from one of Adyen's merchant customers -- whether online, in a store, or on an app -- Adyen handles the transaction and takes a tiny cut. The more transactions flow through its pipes, the more money it makes. It is a toll-road business on global commerce.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Adyen's single-platform architecture and expanding take rate mean it can compound net revenue at 20%+ for years while margins expand, turning it into a durable toll collector on global digital commerce.
- **Bear case:** Processed volume growth decelerates as e-commerce penetration matures, and intense competition from Stripe and others compresses take rates, turning payments into a margin-squeezed commodity.
- **Confidence:** High -- this is a simple, well-documented business model with transparent financials and clear drivers. The payments industry is well understood.

## Red Flags

- FY2025 reported processed volume growth of only 8% (distorted by one large customer) could signal slower organic momentum than headline net revenue growth suggests.
- The gap between P/FCF (50.5x) and P/E (25.4x) is unusually wide, suggesting operating cash flow in FY2025 was depressed relative to earnings -- likely due to working capital timing on merchant funds. This merits scrutiny in the financial analysis.

## Score: 9 / 10

Adyen is one of the most understandable businesses in European tech: a transaction-based toll road on digital commerce with clear drivers, low customer concentration, and a revenue model anyone can grasp in under two minutes. Minor complexity around merchant funds flow and the Platforms segment prevents a perfect 10.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ADYEN.AS

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch 2026-03-29), Adyen H2 2025 earnings release, web search results from Morningstar, Motley Fool, CNBC, Substack analyses (daveahern, potentialmultibaggers, wolfofharcourtstreet), 6sense market share data, Fintech Wrap Up Stripe vs Adyen comparison. Quant valuation model output. Competitive data corroborated across multiple sources.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Adyen operates a single, proprietary technology stack built from scratch -- no acquisitions stitched together -- enabling unified commerce across online, in-store, and mobile with direct acquiring licenses in 40+ markets. No legacy competitor has replicated this. | 5 |
| 2 | Volume churn is below 1% annually, and 80% of revenue growth comes from existing merchants expanding usage, demonstrating powerful switching costs once integrated. | 5 |
| 3 | Gross margins have held at 87-89% for three consecutive years, and operating margins expanded from 37% to 41% over FY2023-FY2025, suggesting real pricing power and scale benefits. | 4 |
| 4 | Stripe is a formidable competitor: it holds ~25% payment management market share vs Adyen's ~9%, processes comparable volume ($1.4T vs EUR 1.4T), and is now profitable and aggressively targeting enterprise clients. | 5 |
| 5 | Adyen's Platforms business (embedded finance for software companies) grew volumes 51% YoY in 2024, adding a secondary moat layer through ecosystem lock-in of platform sub-merchants. | 4 |
| 6 | AI-powered tools like "Adyen Uplift" leverage accumulated transaction data across the network to optimize authorization rates and fraud detection -- a data advantage that compounds with scale. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Switching costs (Strong).** This is Adyen's most powerful moat. Integrating a payment processor into an enterprise's checkout flow across multiple channels (online, in-store POS, mobile), multiple geographies, and multiple payment methods is a deep technical undertaking. Once live, the merchant's fraud rules, reporting dashboards, reconciliation workflows, and financial operations are all built around Adyen's APIs. Ripping this out to switch to a competitor means months of engineering work, risk to conversion rates during migration, and potential revenue loss. The numbers bear this out: volume churn below 1% is exceptional for any B2B software category, and the fact that 80% of growth is organic expansion from existing clients means merchants are choosing to give Adyen more of their business over time, not less.

**Scale and cost advantage (Moderate to Strong).** Adyen built its own technology stack from scratch rather than acquiring and bolting together legacy systems. This single-platform architecture means lower marginal cost per transaction as volume scales, because there is one codebase to maintain rather than dozens. Adyen holds direct acquiring licenses in 40+ countries, meaning it processes transactions "as close to the issuer as possible" rather than routing through intermediaries. This gives it both a cost advantage (fewer middlemen) and a quality advantage (higher authorization rates). The result shows in the numbers: 89% gross margins and 41% operating margins, both expanding.

**Data network effects (Moderate).** Every transaction that flows through Adyen's platform feeds its machine learning models for fraud detection, payment routing optimization, and authorization rate improvement. Adyen's "Uplift" tool reportedly improves payment conversion by 6% for merchants that use it. This is a soft network effect: the more data Adyen accumulates, the better its models perform, making it harder for smaller competitors to match its authorization rates. However, Stripe has comparable data scale, so this advantage is not unique to Adyen.

**Where the moat is weakest: competition from Stripe and commoditization risk.** Stripe holds roughly 25% of the payment management market versus Adyen's 9%. Stripe processed $1.4 trillion in 2024 with 38% volume growth -- faster than Adyen. Stripe is now profitable, has developer-friendly APIs that are considered best-in-class, and is aggressively moving upmarket into enterprise (50% of Fortune 100 use Stripe, including Amazon). In the mid-market, Stripe's brand and developer ecosystem give it an edge. The risk is that payment processing converges toward commodity pricing over time as both platforms achieve comparable scale, authorization rates, and global coverage. If take rates compress toward 10-12 basis points industry-wide, Adyen's margins would come under pressure.

**Durability assessment.** The moat is durable but not invulnerable. The switching cost moat is structural -- it does not reset with each product cycle and only deepens as merchants add channels and geographies. The scale advantage from the single platform is a genuine architectural edge that legacy players (Worldline, FIS, Fiserv) cannot easily replicate without rebuilding from scratch. However, Stripe can and does replicate many of Adyen's capabilities, making this a two-horse race in the modern payments infrastructure category. In 10 years, both Adyen and Stripe are likely to be thriving, but neither will have the market to itself.

**Moat durability risk from regulation.** European regulators have capped interchange fees, which actually benefits acquirers like Adyen (lower costs to pass through). But open banking regulations (PSD2/PSD3) could eventually enable alternative payment rails that bypass traditional card networks entirely, potentially disrupting the economics of card-based payment processing.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Adyen's single-platform architecture and enterprise switching costs create a widening moat as merchants deepen integrations across channels and geographies, with the Platforms business adding a second layer of ecosystem lock-in.
- **Bear case:** Stripe's faster growth, larger market share, and developer mindshare gradually commoditize the enterprise payments market, compressing Adyen's take rates and margins toward industry averages.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High -- the switching cost moat is well-evidenced in the numbers, but the competitive threat from Stripe is real and intensifying, particularly in North America.

## Red Flags

- Adyen's ~9% market share versus Stripe's ~25% means it is the smaller player in a market where scale matters for data advantages and merchant trust.
- FY2025 processed volume growth of 8% reported (21% ex-one large customer) is concerning -- if the headline number reflects a large customer reducing volume, that undermines the churn narrative.
- Asian e-commerce clients (Shein, Temu) represent meaningful volume but may be vulnerable to US tariff policies, adding geopolitical risk to the customer mix.
- Open banking regulation (PSD3) could create alternative payment rails that bypass card networks, potentially disrupting the take-rate economics that underpin Adyen's business.

## Score: 7 / 10

Adyen has a clear moat built on enterprise switching costs, a unique single-platform architecture, and expanding data advantages. However, Stripe is a comparable competitor growing faster with larger market share, preventing this from being a wide-moat 8+. The moat is real and durable, but Adyen must execute well to maintain it in an increasingly competitive duopoly.

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Pieter van der Does co-founded Adyen in 2006 and still serves as co-CEO alongside Ingo Uytdehaage. His direct ownership of 2.93% (worth EUR 1.07B) is meaningful both in absolute terms and as a signal of alignment. For a company with a EUR 27B market cap and a 20-year-old founder still at the wheel, this is solid. The management team's average tenure of 6.1 years and board tenure of 5 years suggest stability without entrenchment. Ethan Tandowsky joined as CFO more recently, and Tom Adams joined as CTO, bringing fresh perspectives to a seasoned leadership group.

**Capital allocation track record.** This is where the story gets interesting -- and somewhat frustrating. Adyen has accumulated an enormous cash pile: EUR 10.5B in cash versus just EUR 252M in debt. Much of this cash relates to merchant funds in transit (operational float), but even the company's own equity of EUR 5.3B and working capital of EUR 4.9B are substantial. The company has never paid a dividend and has not initiated a meaningful share buyback program. In February 2026, activist investor FortyTwo Capital publicly called on Adyen's board to implement a disciplined buyback program, arguing that the company's capital-light growth model and net cash position make buybacks an obvious value-creation lever, especially with shares trading near 52-week lows. This criticism has merit: sitting on billions while the stock trades at 1% of its 52-week range looks like an opportunity cost.

On the positive side, Adyen has reinvested wisely in organic growth. Capital expenditures are modest (EUR 126M in FY2025, or about 5% of net revenue), reflecting the capital-light nature of the business. ROIC has held steady at 17-18% over the past three years -- not exceptional for a tech company, but very solid and consistent. The reinvestment has produced 18-21% annual net revenue growth, suggesting high returns on incremental capital deployed.

**M&A discipline.** Adyen has made essentially zero material acquisitions in its history. It built its entire technology platform from scratch rather than acquiring and integrating legacy systems. In the payments industry, where competitors like Worldline, FIS, and Fiserv are notorious for value-destroying acquisition sprees, this organic-only approach is remarkable. It means every line of code is Adyen's own, there is no integration debt, and management has never overpaid for a mediocre asset. This is rare discipline that Buffett would appreciate.

**Compensation design.** CEO compensation of EUR 807K total (with 96% salary and just 4% bonus) is strikingly modest for a company of this scale. The Dutch 20% bonus cap constrains variable pay, which is actually a feature for shareholders -- it limits the incentive to game short-term metrics. SBC of EUR 40.6M in FY2025 is just 1.5% of net revenue and 3.7% of net income, far below the 10-20% of revenue common in US tech. Maximum dilution is capped at 3%. That said, new hires like CTO Tom Adams receive SBC equal to 100% of base salary (vs 46% for his predecessor), signaling that SBC will trend higher as Adyen competes for US-based talent.

**Candor and governance.** Adyen's management communicates in a straightforward, numbers-driven style. The H2 2025 results disclosed the impact of a single large customer on volume growth transparently, rather than hiding it. The 2026 guidance of 20-22% net revenue growth was honest -- perhaps too honest, given the 20% stock drop it triggered when analysts expected 23%. This is the opposite of chronic over-promising; if anything, management errs on the side of caution. The governance structure is standard Dutch NV with a supervisory board; Steve van Wyk was appointed at the 2025 AGM. There are no dual-class shares, no poison pills, and no related-party transactions flagged in available disclosures.

**The capital return gap.** The elephant in the room is the EUR 10B+ cash balance. Even after accounting for merchant float (which is operationally necessary), the excess capital is substantial. With shares trading at a forward P/E of 17.9x and 51% below their 52-week high, a buyback would be immediately accretive and signal management confidence. The absence of any capital return program -- no dividend, no buyback -- suggests management either overestimates reinvestment opportunities, is excessively conservative, or has not been pressured enough by shareholders until the FortyTwo Capital letter. This is the single biggest knock on an otherwise excellent stewardship record.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Founder-led, zero-acquisition, low-SBC management team with 20 years of disciplined organic execution finally initiates a buyback program at depressed valuations, unlocking significant per-share value.
- **Bear case:** Management continues hoarding cash indefinitely, dilution creeps higher as US expansion demands more SBC, and the co-CEO structure creates strategic ambiguity or slows decision-making.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High -- the stewardship quality is well-documented in the organic growth track record and low SBC, but the capital allocation gap on returns is a genuine concern that could persist.

## Red Flags

- EUR 10.5B cash pile with no dividend or buyback program represents a meaningful opportunity cost for shareholders, especially with the stock near 52-week lows.
- SBC is trending higher (new CTO at 100% of salary vs predecessor at 46%), and US expansion will likely accelerate this trend, potentially eroding the current low-dilution advantage.
- Co-CEO structure (van der Does + Uytdehaage) can work well but also risks diffused accountability and slower strategic pivots compared to single-CEO models.
- FortyTwo Capital's activist letter in February 2026 suggests growing shareholder frustration with capital allocation -- if management does not respond, governance tensions could escalate.

## Score: 7 / 10

Adyen's management team demonstrates genuine owner-operator qualities: founder-led, zero acquisitions, minimal SBC, modest compensation, and honest communication. The organic growth track record is outstanding. However, the persistent refusal to return capital via buybacks or dividends despite a massive cash pile and depressed stock price is a real flaw in capital allocation that prevents a higher score. A buyback announcement would move this to an 8.

# Business Economics — ADYEN.AS

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch, 2026-03-29), Adyen H2 2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), quant-valuation model output, web search for take rate and segment data. FY2022 revenue figures appear to include gross transaction volume rather than net revenue; margin analysis focuses on FY2023-FY2025 where reporting is consistent.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Net revenue grew 18% YoY in FY2025 (21% constant currency) to EUR 2.36B, with EBITDA margin expanding to 53% from 50% — demonstrating strong operating leverage | 5 |
| 2 | Owner earnings reached EUR 1.07B in FY2025, up 16% YoY, with maintenance capex at only 4.8% of revenue — an exceptionally asset-light model | 5 |
| 3 | ROIC has gently declined from 21.6% (FY2022) to 17.6% (FY2025), partly because the large and growing merchant cash float on the balance sheet inflates invested capital | 3 |
| 4 | Take rate expanded from ~14.7 bps to 17.1 bps through 2025, driven by merchant mix shift and growing Unified Commerce (POS) volumes up 34% YoY | 4 |
| 5 | Revenue is almost entirely transaction-based and recurring — merchants process daily, volume is sticky, and net revenue retention is well above 100% for enterprise accounts | 4 |
| 6 | 2026 guidance of 20-22% CC revenue growth disappointed the market (below prior "low-to-mid 20s" ambition), triggering a 20% stock decline | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Adyen's ROE has remained firmly in the 22-25% range over FY2022-FY2025, a strong result for a business that does not use financial leverage. ROIC of 17.6% in FY2025 is good but not spectacular at first glance; however, the metric is distorted by Adyen's balance sheet structure. Adyen holds roughly EUR 10.5B in cash and short-term investments, a large portion of which represents merchant settlement funds in transit — money that belongs to merchants but sits on Adyen's balance sheet for 1-3 days during settlement. This inflates invested capital and suppresses computed ROIC. If we strip out merchant float and look at the capital actually deployed in running the business (equity minus excess cash above operating needs), returns on true operating capital are substantially higher — likely north of 40%. The trajectory of ROE (stable at ~23-25%) and the steady growth in net income (+85% over three years) tell the real story: this is a high-return-on-capital compounder.

**Margin Structure.** Adyen's margin profile is among the best in payments. Gross margins above 88% reflect the fact that Adyen reports net revenue (after interchange and scheme fees are passed through). The more telling figure is the operating margin, which expanded from 36.6% (FY2023) to 39.2% (FY2024) to 41.4% (FY2025). EBITDA margin reached 53% in FY2025, up from 50% in FY2024. This expansion is structural: Adyen built its single-platform stack in-house with no acquisitions, meaning incremental revenue falls through to profit at an improving rate. The company guided for EBITDA margin "in-line with 2025" for FY2026 and above 55% by 2028, suggesting continued but gradual leverage. D&A remains modest at EUR 136M (5.2% of revenue), confirming the asset-light nature of the business.

**Cash Generation.** Free cash flow was EUR 905M in FY2025 with an FCF conversion ratio of 85% (FCF/net income). This is a healthy number, though down from the extraordinary 170-340% conversion rates seen in FY2023-FY2024. Those earlier ratios were inflated by working capital movements (growing merchant balances flowing through operating cash flow). Owner earnings — net income plus D&A minus capex — hit EUR 1.07B, growing at a compounded 27% annually since FY2022. Stock-based compensation is modest at EUR 41M (3.7% of net income), so cash earnings closely track reported earnings. Capex is minimal at EUR 126M, or 4.8% of revenue, and is essentially all maintenance (servers, office buildout). There is no heavy growth capex cycle ahead — Adyen's platform is built and scales with transactions.

**Capital Intensity and Revenue Quality.** Adyen is textbook asset-light. The business requires servers, office space, and engineering talent — but no factories, no inventory, no heavy infrastructure. Revenue is purely transaction-based: merchants pay a per-transaction fee (take rate of ~17 bps on processed volume). This is as close to recurring as you can get without a subscription model — merchants process payments daily, and switching away from Adyen requires a full re-integration, which takes months. The three revenue pillars (Digital at EUR 335M, Unified Commerce/POS at EUR 195M, Platforms at EUR 69M) are all growing, with POS volumes up 34% and Platforms up 50% in FY2025. This diversification reduces dependence on any single channel.

**Operating Leverage.** Adyen's cost structure is largely fixed (engineering staff, platform infrastructure), meaning revenue growth drops through to EBITDA at an accelerating rate. In FY2025, revenue grew 18% while EBITDA grew 26% — a clear demonstration of operating leverage. The company has historically managed headcount growth well below revenue growth after a brief hiring spree in 2022-2023 that spooked the market. With ~4,200 employees and revenue per employee above EUR 560K, Adyen runs lean. The path to 55%+ EBITDA margin by 2028 is credible given this cost structure.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Adyen's single-platform architecture and deepening enterprise relationships drive continued 20%+ revenue growth with expanding margins toward 55-60% EBITDA, producing owner earnings growth of 25%+ annually.
- **Bear case:** Take rate compression from competitive pressure (Stripe, Checkout.com) and macro-driven volume slowdowns bring revenue growth to low-teens, stalling margin expansion and compressing the earnings multiple.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The financial data is robust and the trends are clear, but the FY2022 revenue reporting inconsistency limits the lookback window to three clean years. The operating leverage thesis is well-supported by the data.

## Red Flags
