## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Pieter van der Does co-founded Adyen in 2006 and still serves as co-CEO alongside Ingo Uytdehaage. His direct ownership of 2.93% (worth EUR 1.07B) is meaningful both in absolute terms and as a signal of alignment. For a company with a EUR 27B market cap and a 20-year-old founder still at the wheel, this is solid. The management team's average tenure of 6.1 years and board tenure of 5 years suggest stability without entrenchment. Ethan Tandowsky joined as CFO more recently, and Tom Adams joined as CTO, bringing fresh perspectives to a seasoned leadership group.

**Capital allocation track record.** This is where the story gets interesting -- and somewhat frustrating. Adyen has accumulated an enormous cash pile: EUR 10.5B in cash versus just EUR 252M in debt. Much of this cash relates to merchant funds in transit (operational float), but even the company's own equity of EUR 5.3B and working capital of EUR 4.9B are substantial. The company has never paid a dividend and has not initiated a meaningful share buyback program. In February 2026, activist investor FortyTwo Capital publicly called on Adyen's board to implement a disciplined buyback program, arguing that the company's capital-light growth model and net cash position make buybacks an obvious value-creation lever, especially with shares trading near 52-week lows. This criticism has merit: sitting on billions while the stock trades at 1% of its 52-week range looks like an opportunity cost.

On the positive side, Adyen has reinvested wisely in organic growth. Capital expenditures are modest (EUR 126M in FY2025, or about 5% of net revenue), reflecting the capital-light nature of the business. ROIC has held steady at 17-18% over the past three years -- not exceptional for a tech company, but very solid and consistent. The reinvestment has produced 18-21% annual net revenue growth, suggesting high returns on incremental capital deployed.

**M&A discipline.** Adyen has made essentially zero material acquisitions in its history. It built its entire technology platform from scratch rather than acquiring and integrating legacy systems. In the payments industry, where competitors like Worldline, FIS, and Fiserv are notorious for value-destroying acquisition sprees, this organic-only approach is remarkable. It means every line of code is Adyen's own, there is no integration debt, and management has never overpaid for a mediocre asset. This is rare discipline that Buffett would appreciate.

**Compensation design.** CEO compensation of EUR 807K total (with 96% salary and just 4% bonus) is strikingly modest for a company of this scale. The Dutch 20% bonus cap constrains variable pay, which is actually a feature for shareholders -- it limits the incentive to game short-term metrics. SBC of EUR 40.6M in FY2025 is just 1.5% of net revenue and 3.7% of net income, far below the 10-20% of revenue common in US tech. Maximum dilution is capped at 3%. That said, new hires like CTO Tom Adams receive SBC equal to 100% of base salary (vs 46% for his predecessor), signaling that SBC will trend higher as Adyen competes for US-based talent.

**Candor and governance.** Adyen's management communicates in a straightforward, numbers-driven style. The H2 2025 results disclosed the impact of a single large customer on volume growth transparently, rather than hiding it. The 2026 guidance of 20-22% net revenue growth was honest -- perhaps too honest, given the 20% stock drop it triggered when analysts expected 23%. This is the opposite of chronic over-promising; if anything, management errs on the side of caution. The governance structure is standard Dutch NV with a supervisory board; Steve van Wyk was appointed at the 2025 AGM. There are no dual-class shares, no poison pills, and no related-party transactions flagged in available disclosures.

**The capital return gap.** The elephant in the room is the EUR 10B+ cash balance. Even after accounting for merchant float (which is operationally necessary), the excess capital is substantial. With shares trading at a forward P/E of 17.9x and 51% below their 52-week high, a buyback would be immediately accretive and signal management confidence. The absence of any capital return program -- no dividend, no buyback -- suggests management either overestimates reinvestment opportunities, is excessively conservative, or has not been pressured enough by shareholders until the FortyTwo Capital letter. This is the single biggest knock on an otherwise excellent stewardship record.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Founder-led, zero-acquisition, low-SBC management team with 20 years of disciplined organic execution finally initiates a buyback program at depressed valuations, unlocking significant per-share value.
- **Bear case:** Management continues hoarding cash indefinitely, dilution creeps higher as US expansion demands more SBC, and the co-CEO structure creates strategic ambiguity or slows decision-making.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High -- the stewardship quality is well-documented in the organic growth track record and low SBC, but the capital allocation gap on returns is a genuine concern that could persist.

## Red Flags

- EUR 10.5B cash pile with no dividend or buyback program represents a meaningful opportunity cost for shareholders, especially with the stock near 52-week lows.
- SBC is trending higher (new CTO at 100% of salary vs predecessor at 46%), and US expansion will likely accelerate this trend, potentially eroding the current low-dilution advantage.
- Co-CEO structure (van der Does + Uytdehaage) can work well but also risks diffused accountability and slower strategic pivots compared to single-CEO models.
- FortyTwo Capital's activist letter in February 2026 suggests growing shareholder frustration with capital allocation -- if management does not respond, governance tensions could escalate.

## Score: 7 / 10

Adyen's management team demonstrates genuine owner-operator qualities: founder-led, zero acquisitions, minimal SBC, modest compensation, and honest communication. The organic growth track record is outstanding. However, the persistent refusal to return capital via buybacks or dividends despite a massive cash pile and depressed stock price is a real flaw in capital allocation that prevents a higher score. A buyback announcement would move this to an 8.
