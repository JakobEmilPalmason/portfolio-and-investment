## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Capital Structure.** AMD's balance sheet is a fortress by any conventional measure. Total debt of $3.8B against $63B in equity yields a debt-to-equity ratio of just 6.1%. Long-term debt of $2.3B is minimal for a company generating $7.7B in operating cash flow. The debt-to-EBITDA ratio of 0.5x means AMD could pay off all borrowings from less than six months of EBITDA generation. This conservative posture is a deliberate choice by management — AMD endured years of near-bankruptcy in the 2012-2016 era and has since maintained a clean balance sheet as a strategic priority. The company carries investment-grade credit ratings and faces no meaningful refinancing pressure. The $3.8B debt load is well-laddered with no near-term maturities that would create liquidity stress.

**Liquidity and Cash Reserves.** With $10.6B in cash and short-term investments against a current ratio of 2.9x, AMD has more than ample liquidity to fund operations, invest in growth, and weather any short-term disruption. The net cash position (cash exceeding total debt by ~$6.8B) is a rare luxury in the technology sector. Annual FCF of $6.7B means the cash pile is growing rapidly — even after $1.9B in buybacks, AMD added roughly $4.8B in net cash generation during FY2025. This cash generation trajectory means AMD could self-fund significant acquisitions, accelerate buybacks, or initiate a dividend without touching its balance sheet strength.

**The Goodwill Question.** The balance sheet's one notable structural feature is the ~$43B in combined goodwill and intangible assets from the Xilinx and Pensando acquisitions, representing approximately 56% of total assets. Goodwill alone is ~$24.8B. Under GAAP, goodwill is not amortized but tested annually for impairment. If AMD's Embedded segment (the core Xilinx FPGA business) were to experience a sustained secular decline — not merely cyclical softness — a material goodwill impairment charge would be possible. The intangible assets (~$19B) are amortizing at ~$2.1B annually and will wind down over the next 5-8 years. This is a non-cash expense that depresses GAAP earnings but does not affect cash flow. The goodwill risk is real but manageable: even a hypothetical $5B impairment charge would be a non-cash event that does not affect AMD's liquidity, cash generation, or debt service capacity.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** Under a severe downturn scenario where AMD revenue drops 30% from $34.6B to ~$24.2B and stays there for two years:
- Revenue of $24.2B at a compressed 42% gross margin yields ~$10.2B gross profit
- Assume R&D cuts 15% to ~$6.9B and SG&A holds at ~$2.5B → operating income of ~$800M
- Interest expense of ~$131M is easily covered even in this scenario (6x coverage)
- FCF would compress to ~$1-2B as working capital absorbs the hit, but remains positive
- $10.6B cash reserve provides a 5-7 year runway at even zero FCF
- Total debt of $3.8B could be serviced indefinitely from the cash pile alone
- No debt covenants would be at risk given the minimal leverage

AMD would survive this stress scenario comfortably. The fabless model is the key advantage: unlike Intel, AMD does not carry $20B+ in factory capex obligations. In a downturn, AMD can slash tape-out schedules and wafer orders to TSMC, converting capex flexibility into cash preservation. The 2015-2016 AMD near-death experience occurred at $4B revenue with heavy debt — today's AMD at $24B revenue with net cash is a fundamentally different risk profile.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Net cash position, 0.5x leverage, 32.6x interest coverage, and $6.7B FCF make AMD's balance sheet among the safest in semiconductors — financial distress is essentially impossible at current scale.
- **Bear case:** $43B in goodwill and intangibles from Xilinx create non-trivial impairment risk if the FPGA business fails to grow into its acquisition price, and the 56% intangible asset concentration inflates the apparent book value.
- **Confidence:** High — Balance sheet data is audited and unambiguous; the stress test assumptions are conservative and AMD passes with wide margins.

## Red Flags
- Goodwill of ~$24.8B represents 32% of total assets and has never been impaired — the Embedded segment's sluggish recovery ($3.5B, -3% YoY) warrants monitoring for potential impairment triggers
- Intangible asset amortization of $2.1B/year creates a persistent gap between GAAP and cash earnings that investors must adjust for
- Cash deployment strategy is evolving: $1.9B in buybacks at elevated share prices (~$150-220 range) may not represent optimal capital allocation if shares are trading above intrinsic value
- Off-balance-sheet: AMD has significant purchase commitments to TSMC for wafer supply; in a severe downturn, these take-or-pay arrangements could create short-term cash flow pressure

## Score: 9 / 10
AMD's balance sheet is exceptionally strong — net cash position, minimal leverage (0.5x Debt/EBITDA), fortress-level interest coverage (32.6x), and a fabless model that limits capital intensity. The only deduction is the $43B goodwill/intangible overhang from Xilinx, which creates theoretical impairment risk without affecting actual liquidity or solvency.
