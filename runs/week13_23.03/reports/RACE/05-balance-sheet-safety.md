## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels.** Ferrari's total debt stands at €2.9B with long-term debt of €2.7B and short-term obligations well-covered. However, the headline debt figure overstates the risk significantly. Net industrial debt — which management and rating agencies treat as the core metric — fell to just €32M at year-end 2025. This means Ferrari's operating business is essentially debt-free. The gross debt carries low coupons from bonds issued during the low-rate era. Debt-to-equity of 73.8% has improved from 108% and continues trending down. Debt/EBITDA at 1.0x and net debt/EBITDA at 0.5x are conservative by any standard. Credit agency CRIF rates Ferrari BBB+ stable, with commentary suggesting the company is "close to AA-" and could be upgraded as metrics continue to improve through 2027.

**Interest Coverage.** This is where Ferrari's safety becomes unambiguous. With EBIT of €2.1B against interest expense of just €41.2M, interest coverage stands at 51.1x. This is not a typo — Ferrari earns 51 times its annual interest bill. Even in a severe stress scenario where EBIT halved, coverage would remain above 25x. The interest expense is economically irrelevant to this business. Ferrari could service its entire debt load from a single quarter's operating income.

**Liquidity.** Cash on hand of €1.5B against current liabilities of €1.4B gives a current ratio of 3.4x with working capital of €3.4B. Total current assets of €4.8B provide a massive liquidity buffer. At zero revenue, Ferrari's cash alone would cover approximately 12-13 months of operating expenses (estimated at €120-130M/month in fixed costs). With credit facilities in addition to cash, survival capacity extends well beyond 24 months. The order backlog extending over a year means a zero-revenue scenario is essentially theoretical — customers have already committed.

**Refinancing Risk.** Ferrari repaid €734M in debt during 2025, including a €451M bond that matured in the first half. These repayments were funded entirely from operating cash flow with no new issuance required. The remaining debt has a well-laddered maturity profile. Given that industrial FCF exceeds €1.5B annually and net industrial debt is just €32M, refinancing risk is negligible. The company could theoretically retire all remaining gross debt within two years from cash flow alone if it chose to stop buybacks. Analysts project the adjusted net leverage ratio will turn negative by 2027, meaning Ferrari will be in a net cash position even after continued buybacks of ~€700M/year and regular dividends.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Ferrari's off-balance-sheet exposure is modest. The company operates from owned facilities in Maranello, limiting lease obligations. Pension liabilities are typical for a European manufacturer of this size and are not a material risk. The financial services arm (Ferrari Financial Services) carries securitization debt that is ring-fenced from the industrial business — this is why the "industrial net debt" metric is the appropriate measure. There are no unusual contingent liabilities, guarantees, or structured financing arrangements that would alter the safety assessment.

**Stress Test: Revenue Drops 30% for 2 Years.** In this scenario, revenue would fall from €7.1B to approximately €5.0B. Assuming operating margins compress to ~20% (from 29.3%) due to fixed cost deleveraging, EBIT would be roughly €1.0B. Interest expense remains ~€41M, so coverage stays above 24x. FCF would decline but remain positive at perhaps €400-500M after reduced capex. Net industrial debt of €32M means there is no debt spiral risk. Ferrari would likely pause buybacks (saving €700-800M/year) and trim capex, easily preserving cash. The company would emerge from a two-year downturn with its balance sheet intact and no need for equity issuance. Historically, Ferrari's volumes proved resilient even during 2008-2009 and COVID, with demand recovering quickly due to the extended order backlog.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari operates with essentially zero net industrial debt, 51x interest coverage, and €1.5B+ annual FCF — this is a fortress balance sheet that could survive virtually any economic scenario without external financing.
- **Bear case:** Gross debt of €2.9B still exists and carries refinancing risk if capital markets freeze, though the company's cash generation makes this largely theoretical.
- **Confidence:** High — The numbers are unambiguous: net industrial debt of €32M, 51x coverage, 3.4x current ratio, and improving trajectory toward net cash by 2027. There is no reasonable stress scenario where Ferrari faces financial distress.

## Red Flags

- Gross debt of €2.9B is non-trivial in absolute terms, even if coverage ratios make it safe
- Financial services arm carries separate securitization debt that is excluded from industrial metrics — warrants monitoring if auto loan delinquencies rise
- Share buyback program (€3.5B through 2030) could slow deleveraging if management prioritizes returns over balance sheet strength, though current cash generation easily supports both
- EV transition capex (€4.7B through 2030) is a significant commitment, though projected €8.0B in industrial FCF over the same period provides ample funding

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari's balance sheet is fortress-grade: net industrial debt of just €32M, interest coverage of 51x, current ratio of 3.4x, and a clear trajectory to net cash by 2027 — the company could survive a multi-year revenue collapse without external financing, docked one point only because gross debt of €2.9B still exists on the books.
