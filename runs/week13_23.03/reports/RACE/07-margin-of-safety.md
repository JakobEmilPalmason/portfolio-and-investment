## Detailed Analysis

**Price margin of safety: absent.** The quant model's bear IV of $174 implies that at $317, the stock carries a negative MOS of -82%. Even the base-case IV of $283 sits below the current price, meaning there is no conventional Buffett-style price discount. The sensitivity grid reinforces this: the current price of $317 exceeds all cells except those in the top-left corner (high growth + low WACC). Specifically, $317 is only matched or exceeded at growth rates of 12.9% combined with WACC at or below 7.2%. The Monte Carlo probability of 20.2% means that only about 1 in 5 simulated scenarios produce an IV above the current price. For a value investor, this is a clear "priced for perfection" signal.

**Business margin of safety: substantial.** Where price MOS fails, business MOS partially compensates. Ferrari's structural advantages create a thick buffer against operational adversity. The order backlog extends through end-2027, meaning 2+ years of revenue visibility — a feature almost no other manufacturer enjoys. EBITDA margins of 39% provide ~1,500 basis points of cushion before the business would generate below-average industrial returns. ROIC of 24% is more than 2x the cost of capital. Net debt of ~€1.3B against €2.8B EBITDA (0.5x leverage) means the balance sheet can absorb tariff hits, demand slowdowns, or capex overruns without distress. Even if margins compressed 500 basis points and volumes fell 10%, Ferrari would still generate over €1B in free cash flow.

**Downside-upside asymmetry.** The risk-reward skew is modestly unfavorable at current prices. In a bear scenario (tariff escalation to 25%+, EV launch disappointment, luxury demand softening), the stock could re-rate to 20-22x forward earnings, implying $200-230 — roughly 30-35% downside. In a bull scenario (tariff resolution, successful EV launch boosting the brand narrative, continued margin expansion), the stock re-rates to 35-40x forward earnings, implying $430-500 — roughly 35-58% upside. The upside is slightly larger in percentage terms, but the probability-weighted expected return is close to zero given the Monte Carlo distribution. The asymmetry improves meaningfully below $270, where the risk-reward begins to favor the buyer.

**Zero and permanent loss scenarios.** Ferrari faces no realistic permanent capital loss risk. The brand is 80+ years old, production-constrained (not demand-constrained), carries minimal debt, and serves a customer base that is largely recession-resistant. Scenarios that could impair the franchise permanently — a catastrophic brand scandal, regulatory ban on ICE vehicles with no viable alternative, or nationalization — are tail-tail risks with negligible probability. This is one of the most durable consumer franchises in the world. The question is not whether Ferrari survives, but what price adequately compensates for the risk of temporary overvaluation.

**Key risks.** (1) **Tariff escalation:** The current 15% EU tariff has already prompted 10% price increases on US models. If tariffs escalate to 25-50% (as threatened), Ferrari faces a choice between margin destruction and demand destruction in its largest market. Management has signaled it will pass costs through, but even ultra-wealthy buyers have thresholds. (2) **EV execution risk:** The Ferrari Luce, launching in 2026, is existential for the brand narrative. If it fails to deliver the emotional driving experience Ferrari is known for, or if demand disappoints, it undermines the entire electrification strategy. (3) **Multiple compression:** At 30.7x trailing P/E, the stock is still priced as a luxury compounder. A shift in market regime toward value, rising rates, or a luxury sector selloff could compress the multiple to 20-25x, implying 20-35% downside independent of fundamentals. (4) **China/macro luxury softness:** While Ferrari's exposure to China is lower than peers (~10% of shipments), a global luxury slowdown would pressure the narrative even if direct revenue impact is modest. (5) **Key-person risk:** CEO Benedetto Vigna's strategy of balancing tradition with electrification is central to the investment case; leadership disruption would create uncertainty.

**Concentration risks.** Ferrari has meaningful geographic concentration (US ~30%, Europe ~45%), product concentration (90%+ revenue from cars, with personalizations and brand activities as supplements), and regulatory concentration (subject to EU emissions regulations and US trade policy simultaneously). Customer concentration is low (13,600 units spread across thousands of buyers), but the ultra-high-net-worth customer pool is finite. Currency concentration is notable: costs in EUR, significant revenue in USD and GBP — the tariff issue compounds an existing FX exposure.

**Tail risks.** Litigation risk is low (no major pending lawsuits). Accounting risk is minimal (straightforward revenue recognition, audited by EY). Fraud risk is negligible for a publicly traded company of this profile. Geopolitical risk is moderate (Italian manufacturing base, EU-US trade tensions, potential EU regulatory tightening on luxury vehicles). The most credible tail risk is a tariff war escalation that simultaneously compresses margins and demand, combined with an EV launch stumble — a "perfect storm" scenario that could send the stock to $150-180.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari's business quality provides an enormous intrinsic buffer — even at premium prices, the franchise's pricing power, order visibility, and capital efficiency mean the floor is high and recovery from any drawdown is likely.
- **Bear case:** At $317, there is zero conventional margin of safety; the stock is priced above quant fair value in 80% of simulated scenarios, tariff risk is live, and the EV transition introduces binary outcome risk for the brand narrative.
- **Confidence:** High — The business quality assessment is straightforward and well-supported by data; the price assessment is equally clear — there is no margin of safety at current levels.

## Red Flags

- Negative MOS of -82% against bear IV — price must fall 45% to reach conservative value
- Only 20% Monte Carlo probability of undervaluation
- US tariff headwind is structural (all production in Italy) with no mitigation path beyond price increases
- EV launch in 2026 is a brand-defining binary event
- Sensitivity grid shows current price only justified in most optimistic growth/WACC combinations
- 39% drawdown from peak may feel like a correction but still leaves stock above base-case fair value

## Score: 3 / 10

Ferrari's exceptional business quality provides a strong floor against permanent capital loss, but there is no price margin of safety at $317 — the stock trades well above conservative IV, the Monte Carlo distribution strongly favors overvaluation, and near-term tariff and EV risks add volatility to an already stretched valuation.
