## Detailed Analysis

Ferrari is one of the most understandable businesses in the public markets. The company designs, manufactures, and sells ultra-luxury sports cars and grand tourers to ultra-high-net-worth individuals worldwide. It produced approximately 14,000 vehicles in FY2025, generating EUR 7.1 billion in revenue. The deliberate scarcity — Ferrari could sell far more cars than it produces — is the central mechanism of the business. This is not a volume automaker; it is a luxury goods house that happens to make cars.

The revenue model is clean and transparent. Cars and spare parts (including personalization) account for roughly 85% of revenue. Ferrari's personalization program has become an increasingly important margin lever — buyers routinely spend 20-30% above list price on bespoke features, paint, interiors, and limited configurations. Sponsorship, commercial, and brand revenues (including Formula 1 team income, licensing, fashion, and merchandise) contribute approximately 10%, growing at 22% in 2025. Engine supply to Maserati and other activities make up the remainder. There are no complex financial engineering layers, no recurring subscription ambiguity, and no channel-stuffing risk given the waiting list dynamic.

The key business drivers are unusually few and stable. First, average selling price — driven by model mix (V12 vs V8/V6, special series vs range cars) and personalization uptake. Second, unit volume — which Ferrari deliberately caps well below demand to maintain scarcity premium and resale values. Third, brand monetization — the growing contribution from F1 sponsorship, lifestyle products, and licensing. In FY2025, revenue grew 7% while shipments were roughly flat, confirming that price/mix is the dominant growth lever, not volume. This makes the business highly predictable on a 1-3 year horizon.

Revenue predictability is among the best in any industry. Ferrari's order book typically extends more than two years into the future. When management guides for EUR 7.5 billion in 2026 revenue and at least EUR 2.93 billion in adjusted EBITDA, this is not speculative — it is backed by orders already in hand. The 2026 guidance was raised after FY2025 results, further reinforcing visibility. Customer concentration is essentially zero; Ferrari sells to thousands of collectors and enthusiasts across Europe, the Americas, the Middle East, and Greater China. No single client, dealer, or geography dominates to a degree that creates dependency risk.

Can you explain Ferrari in two minutes? Absolutely. Ferrari makes a small number of very expensive sports cars. Demand permanently exceeds supply. Buyers wait years and pay premiums for personalization. The brand is arguably the most iconic in automotive history, reinforced by Formula 1 and a deliberate scarcity strategy. The company grows revenue primarily through richer mix and higher prices per car, not by making more cars. It converts roughly 88% of operating cash flow to free cash flow. This is a textbook example of a business within almost anyone's circle of competence.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari is among the simplest, most predictable luxury businesses in public markets — few key drivers, 2+ year order backlog, and a revenue model a child could understand.
- **Bear case:** The business is so simple and well-understood that any edge must come from valuation discipline, not informational advantage — the stock rarely trades at a discount.
- **Confidence:** High — Ferrari's business model has been transparent and stable for decades; there is minimal risk of misunderstanding what drives this company.

## Red Flags

- Engine supply to Maserati is a declining revenue stream as that relationship evolves
- The EV transition (Ferrari Luce launching 2026-2027) introduces modest execution complexity to an otherwise simple business model
- Geographic mix shifts (China demand volatility) can create short-term noise in shipment numbers

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari is one of the clearest businesses in the investable universe — a luxury scarcity model with few key drivers, exceptional revenue visibility, zero customer concentration, and a business anyone can explain in under a minute. The only reason this is not a 10 is the modest complexity introduced by the EV transition and evolving brand monetization strategy.
