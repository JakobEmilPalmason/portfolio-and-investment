## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Ferrari's returns on capital are exceptional and improving. ROIC has risen from roughly 18-20% in 2020-2022 to 24.3% in FY2025. ROE stands at 42.9%, partly amplified by leverage but still reflecting genuinely high operating returns. ROA of 16.6% on a €9.6B asset base confirms this is not just a leverage story. The 5-year trajectory is upward and stable — there are no wild swings. These are Hermes-tier returns for what is technically classified as an automaker, placing Ferrari firmly in the luxury goods category economically.

**Margin Structure.** The margin profile is outstanding and expanding. Gross margin of 51.7% is up from 48.0% just three years ago — a 370bps improvement driven almost entirely by richer product mix and personalization attach rates, not volume growth. Operating margin reached 29.3% (up from 24.0% in FY2022), and EBITDA margin hit 38.8%. Net margin of 22.3% is clean. The key insight is that Ferrari shipped roughly the same number of cars in FY2025 as FY2024 (~13,640 units) yet grew revenue 7% and operating profit 12%. This is pricing power in its purest form. Management has stated explicitly that "our pricing power is not at an end." Personalization revenue (~20% of car revenues) carries near-100% incremental margins, functioning as a structural margin escalator.

**Cash Generation.** Operating cash flow reached €2.3B in FY2025, up from €1.9B prior year. Free cash flow was €1.4B on a reported basis, with industrial FCF exceeding €1.5B. FCF conversion (FCF/net income) improved dramatically to 88% from 62%. Owner earnings of approximately €1.3B (after maintenance capex, estimated at 70% of total capex) are real and growing. The company returned over €1.3B to shareholders in FY2025 through buybacks (€785M) and dividends, yet net industrial debt fell to just €32M. This is a genuine cash machine.

**Capital Intensity and Revenue Quality.** CapEx of €943M (13.2% of revenue) is the one area that warrants scrutiny. This is elevated relative to a typical luxury goods business and reflects Ferrari's investment in its EV platform (the electric Ferrari is expected around 2026-2027), new model development, and the e-building factory in Maranello. Management guides €4.7B in cumulative capex through 2030, offset by €8.0B in projected industrial FCF — so growth investment is self-funded with substantial excess. The capex cycle should moderate as EV infrastructure buildout completes. Revenue quality is high: the order backlog extends well over a year, personalization is recurring per vehicle, and the brand licensing/lifestyle segment (sponsorship, merchandise, Ferrari World) is growing. There is minimal one-time revenue.

**Operating Leverage.** Ferrari exhibits strong operating leverage. Revenue grew 7% in FY2025 while EBIT grew 12% — a 1.7x profit multiplier on incremental revenue. This has been the pattern for several years: flat-to-low-single-digit unit growth translates into high-single-digit revenue growth via mix/price, which in turn drives double-digit profit growth. The fixed cost base (the Maranello factory, engineering staff, F1 operations) is largely stable, so each incremental euro of personalization or model-mix upgrade flows through at high margins. The 2026 guidance (revenue ~€7.5B, EBITDA margin ~39%) implies continued operating leverage.

**R&D Spending.** R&D of €919M (12.9% of revenue) is substantial and rising, driven by electrification. This is capitalized as an investment in future competitive positioning — Ferrari cannot afford to be late to the hybrid/EV luxury segment. The spending is manageable relative to cash generation and is better thought of as growth capex protecting the moat rather than maintenance spend.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari is a luxury goods business disguised as an automaker, with 50%+ gross margins, 24% ROIC, pricing power on flat volumes, and a self-funding growth investment cycle — the economics are getting better, not worse.
- **Bear case:** Elevated capex for EV transition could compress returns if the electric Ferrari fails to command traditional pricing premiums, and the 13% capex-to-revenue ratio is high for a company valued as a luxury compounder.
- **Confidence:** High — The financial data is unambiguous: margins are expanding, returns are rising, cash generation is strong, and the growth is price/mix-driven rather than volume-dependent. The only uncertainty is whether EV-era economics will match ICE-era economics.

## Red Flags

- CapEx intensity at 13.2% of revenue is elevated for a luxury business; needs to moderate post-EV buildout
- R&D at 12.9% of revenue is high and rising — justified by electrification but creates near-term earnings drag
- US tariff exposure (~25% of units sold to US) creates headline risk, though Ferrari has demonstrated ability to pass through price increases
- Flat unit shipments in FY2025 were deliberate (model changeover), but any sustained volume decline would pressure fixed cost absorption

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari delivers world-class returns on capital (ROIC 24%, ROE 43%), expanding margins (gross 52%, operating 29%), and strong FCF generation — all on flat unit volumes — making it one of the highest-quality business models in public markets, docked only for elevated near-term capex intensity during the EV transition.
