# Investment Analysis: RACE — Ferrari N.V.

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Verdict:** Watch

> Ferrari is one of the highest-quality franchises in public markets -- a scarcity-model luxury business with 52% gross margins, 24% ROIC, a fortress balance sheet, and a brand moat built over 76 years of racing heritage that no amount of capital can replicate. However, at $317 per share the stock trades above fair value in 80% of quantitative scenarios with zero conventional margin of safety, making this a business to admire and monitor but not to buy at today's price.

## Compact Checklist
1. **Business**: Ferrari sells ~14,000 ultra-luxury sports cars per year at average prices above EUR 350k, with a 2+ year order backlog and revenue growth driven by personalization and mix — not volume — making it one of the most predictable businesses in public markets.
2. **Moat**: 76 years of Formula 1 heritage and deliberate production scarcity create a self-reinforcing flywheel where waiting lists preserve resale values, which deepens aspiration and demand, producing 52% gross margins that no competitor can approach because the brand cannot be replicated with capital.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of 24.3% on rising trajectory, gross margins expanding to 52% on flat unit volumes, and EUR 1.4B in free cash flow confirm Ferrari converts pricing power into real, growing cash returns well above cost of capital.
4. **Management**: The Agnelli family (Exor, 36.5% voting power) provides patient long-term stewardship, returning EUR 1.3B to shareholders in FY2025 via buybacks and dividends while reducing net industrial debt to EUR 32M — though programmatic buybacks at 30x earnings may not always be the best use of capital.
5. **Debt risk**: With net industrial debt of EUR 32M, interest coverage of 51x, and EUR 1.5B annual free cash flow, Ferrari could survive a multi-year revenue collapse without external financing and is on track for a net cash position by 2027.
6. **Price**: At $317, the stock requires ~11% revenue CAGR with 39%+ EBITDA margins sustained for 5+ years — the quant model's Monte Carlo gives only a 20% probability of undervaluation, meaning you must believe Ferrari executes near-perfectly on the EV transition and tariff pass-through to justify today's price.
7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself provides a thick buffer (2-year backlog, 39% EBITDA margins, near-zero debt), but there is zero price margin of safety — the stock trades 12% above base-case IV and 82% above bear-case IV, so your protection comes entirely from franchise durability, not from what you paid.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Ferrari expands annual production above 15,000 units to chase volume growth, because that would dismantle the scarcity model that underpins the entire pricing power and brand premium.

## Key Risks
1. **US tariff escalation** (high likelihood, high impact): 30%+ of revenue from US market subject to 15% EU auto tariffs with potential escalation to 25-50%; all production in Italy with no relocation option, forcing a choice between margin destruction and demand destruction.
2. **EV launch execution** (medium likelihood, high impact): The Ferrari Luce launching in 2026 is a brand-defining binary event -- if it fails to deliver the visceral Ferrari driving experience, it undermines the electrification strategy and could crack the brand moat for the first time in decades.
3. **Valuation compression** (medium likelihood, medium impact): At 30.7x trailing P/E, the stock remains priced as a luxury compounder; a shift toward value or rising rates could compress the multiple to 20-25x, implying 20-35% additional downside.
4. **China/global luxury softness** (medium likelihood, low-medium impact): While Ferrari's direct China exposure is limited (~10% of shipments), a global luxury demand slowdown would pressure the narrative and sentiment.
5. **Capex intensity during EV transition** (low likelihood, medium impact): EUR 4.7B in cumulative capex through 2030 could compress returns if EV models fail to command traditional pricing premiums.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell
- **Buy triggers**: (1) Stock declines to $240-270 range, providing meaningful margin of safety against base-case IV; (2) US-EU tariff resolution or reduction below 15%, removing the largest overhang; (3) Successful Ferrari Luce launch with strong order intake validating the EV strategy.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) Ferrari expands annual production above 15,000 units, dismantling the scarcity model; (2) Brand moat demonstrably impaired through scandal, sustained F1 irrelevance, or volume dilution; (3) Stock re-rates above 40x forward P/E without corresponding earnings acceleration.

# Circle of Competence — RACE

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Ferrari FY 2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Ferrari corporate filings, yfinance financial data, Motley Fool analysis, Untaylored business model breakdown, Nasdaq brand analysis, MacroTrends historical data

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |

| 1 | Ferrari sells ultra-luxury sports cars (~14,000 units/year) at average prices exceeding EUR 350k, deliberately constraining supply below demand | 5 |
| 2 | Revenue model is straightforward: ~85% cars/spare parts/personalization, ~10% sponsorship/brand/F1, ~5% engines/other — all high-margin | 4 |
| 3 | Key drivers are remarkably few: average selling price (mix + personalization), unit volume (deliberately capped), and brand monetization growth | 5 |
| 4 | Revenue visibility is exceptional — order backlog extends 2+ years, providing near-certainty on near-term shipments and pricing | 5 |
| 5 | No meaningful customer concentration; thousands of ultra-high-net-worth individuals globally, no single buyer >1% of revenue | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Ferrari is one of the most understandable businesses in the public markets. The company designs, manufactures, and sells ultra-luxury sports cars and grand tourers to ultra-high-net-worth individuals worldwide. It produced approximately 14,000 vehicles in FY2025, generating EUR 7.1 billion in revenue. The deliberate scarcity — Ferrari could sell far more cars than it produces — is the central mechanism of the business. This is not a volume automaker; it is a luxury goods house that happens to make cars.

The revenue model is clean and transparent. Cars and spare parts (including personalization) account for roughly 85% of revenue. Ferrari's personalization program has become an increasingly important margin lever — buyers routinely spend 20-30% above list price on bespoke features, paint, interiors, and limited configurations. Sponsorship, commercial, and brand revenues (including Formula 1 team income, licensing, fashion, and merchandise) contribute approximately 10%, growing at 22% in 2025. Engine supply to Maserati and other activities make up the remainder. There are no complex financial engineering layers, no recurring subscription ambiguity, and no channel-stuffing risk given the waiting list dynamic.

The key business drivers are unusually few and stable. First, average selling price — driven by model mix (V12 vs V8/V6, special series vs range cars) and personalization uptake. Second, unit volume — which Ferrari deliberately caps well below demand to maintain scarcity premium and resale values. Third, brand monetization — the growing contribution from F1 sponsorship, lifestyle products, and licensing. In FY2025, revenue grew 7% while shipments were roughly flat, confirming that price/mix is the dominant growth lever, not volume. This makes the business highly predictable on a 1-3 year horizon.

Revenue predictability is among the best in any industry. Ferrari's order book typically extends more than two years into the future. When management guides for EUR 7.5 billion in 2026 revenue and at least EUR 2.93 billion in adjusted EBITDA, this is not speculative — it is backed by orders already in hand. The 2026 guidance was raised after FY2025 results, further reinforcing visibility. Customer concentration is essentially zero; Ferrari sells to thousands of collectors and enthusiasts across Europe, the Americas, the Middle East, and Greater China. No single client, dealer, or geography dominates to a degree that creates dependency risk.

Can you explain Ferrari in two minutes? Absolutely. Ferrari makes a small number of very expensive sports cars. Demand permanently exceeds supply. Buyers wait years and pay premiums for personalization. The brand is arguably the most iconic in automotive history, reinforced by Formula 1 and a deliberate scarcity strategy. The company grows revenue primarily through richer mix and higher prices per car, not by making more cars. It converts roughly 88% of operating cash flow to free cash flow. This is a textbook example of a business within almost anyone's circle of competence.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari is among the simplest, most predictable luxury businesses in public markets — few key drivers, 2+ year order backlog, and a revenue model a child could understand.
- **Bear case:** The business is so simple and well-understood that any edge must come from valuation discipline, not informational advantage — the stock rarely trades at a discount.
- **Confidence:** High — Ferrari's business model has been transparent and stable for decades; there is minimal risk of misunderstanding what drives this company.

## Red Flags

- Engine supply to Maserati is a declining revenue stream as that relationship evolves
- The EV transition (Ferrari Luce launching 2026-2027) introduces modest execution complexity to an otherwise simple business model
- Geographic mix shifts (China demand volatility) can create short-term noise in shipment numbers

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari is one of the clearest businesses in the investable universe — a luxury scarcity model with few key drivers, exceptional revenue visibility, zero customer concentration, and a business anyone can explain in under a minute. The only reason this is not a 10 is the modest complexity introduced by the EV transition and evolving brand monetization strategy.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — RACE

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Ferrari FY 2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Ferrari Capital Markets Day 2025 materials, yfinance financial data, Motley Fool analysis, MIT Sloan case study (2025), Substack luxury strategy analysis, ainvest scarcity analysis, MacroTrends historical margins

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Ferrari possesses the strongest brand moat in the automotive industry — 76 years of racing heritage, cultural iconography, and deliberate scarcity create an emotional barrier no competitor can replicate | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margins of 51.7% and EBITDA margins of 38.7% — sustained at luxury-goods levels for over a decade — are the quantitative proof of pricing power and moat durability | 5 |
| 3 | The scarcity model (demand permanently exceeding supply) creates a self-reinforcing flywheel: waiting lists preserve resale values, which reinforces aspiration, which deepens demand | 5 |
| 4 | ROIC of 24.3% sustained over multiple years confirms the moat generates real economic returns well above cost of capital | 4 |
| 5 | The moat's weakest point is the EV transition — Ferrari must prove its emotional and performance identity translates to electric powertrains without diluting brand equity | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

Ferrari's competitive advantage is rooted in brand power of a depth and character that has no close parallel in the automotive world, and arguably few parallels in any industry. The brand was forged through 76 years of Formula 1 racing, beginning with Enzo Ferrari's original vision that road cars existed primarily to fund the racing team. This heritage is not replicable — no amount of capital expenditure can manufacture decades of racing victories, cultural mythology, and the emotional resonance of the Prancing Horse. Hermes is often cited as the closest analogue in luxury goods: both companies use artificial scarcity, long waiting lists, and a deep heritage narrative to maintain pricing power that actually strengthens over time rather than eroding.

The moat type is overwhelmingly brand/trust, reinforced by a deliberate scarcity mechanism that functions similarly to supply-side network effects. Ferrari produces roughly 14,000 cars per year against a demand pool that could absorb multiples of that volume. This constraint is not a production limitation — it is a strategic choice. By keeping supply below demand, Ferrari ensures that waiting lists remain long (often 2+ years), resale values remain at or above MSRP for many models, and the ownership experience retains its exclusivity. This flywheel is self-reinforcing: strong resale values attract collector-investors, which deepens demand, which allows Ferrari to push average selling prices higher through personalization and limited editions. In FY2025, revenue grew 7% on essentially flat shipments — pure pricing power.

The numerical evidence for moat durability is compelling. Gross margins have been above 49% for multiple years, reaching 51.7% in FY2025. EBITDA margins at 38.7% are in luxury-goods territory, not automotive territory. Operating margins of 29.3% compare to mid-single-digits for volume automakers and low-teens for premium brands like BMW or Mercedes. ROIC at 24.3% and ROE at 42.9% confirm that this pricing power translates into genuine economic returns, not just accounting margins. Interest coverage at 51x and net debt near zero demonstrate that the moat generates abundant free cash flow with minimal capital structure risk.

Looking forward 10 years, the core question is whether Ferrari's moat survives the electric transition. Management has approached this with notable discipline. At the October 2025 Capital Markets Day, Ferrari halved its 2030 EV target to just 20% of the lineup, signaling that electrification will be additive rather than a wholesale pivot. The Ferrari Luce, the company's first BEV, was announced with in-house motor, battery, and inverter production — a deliberate choice to maintain performance DNA rather than outsource to commodity suppliers. The interior was designed in collaboration with Jony Ive's LoveFrom studio, positioning the Luce as a design statement rather than a compliance vehicle. Early indications suggest Ferrari is treating the EV transition the way it treats everything else: with restraint, exclusivity, and a focus on emotional experience over specifications.

The moat's weakest point is precisely this transition period. If the Luce fails to deliver the visceral Ferrari driving experience — the sound, the drama, the sense of occasion — it could undermine the argument that Ferrari transcends powertrain technology. Additionally, as more luxury and performance brands launch high-end EVs (Porsche Taycan, Rimac, Lotus Emeya), Ferrari faces a window where its traditional ICE advantage is clear but its EV credentials are unproven. However, Ferrari's moat has never been primarily about horsepower numbers — it is about aspiration, heritage, and the social signal of ownership. These advantages do not reset with a powertrain change. The moat also does not reset every product cycle; Ferrari's brand equity accrues across decades and model generations. A single disappointing model has never materially dented the franchise.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari has one of the widest and most durable moats in any public company — a 76-year heritage brand with proven pricing power, 50%+ gross margins, and a scarcity flywheel that strengthens with time.
- **Bear case:** The EV transition introduces a non-trivial risk that the emotional core of the Ferrari experience (engine sound, mechanical drama) cannot be fully replicated electrically, potentially narrowing the moat for the first time in decades.
- **Confidence:** High — The brand moat has survived multiple technological transitions (naturally aspirated to turbo, manual to dual-clutch) and competitive threats over decades; the EV risk is real but manageable given management's disciplined approach.

## Red Flags

- Ferrari Luce is unproven — if the first BEV underwhelms, it could create a rare crack in brand perception
- Chinese luxury demand has shown volatility; Ferrari's Greater China exposure creates cyclical risk at the margin
- Regulatory risk: EU emissions regulations could eventually force faster electrification than Ferrari's preferred pace
- Personalization revenue growth, while margin-accretive, could eventually face a ceiling as average transaction prices approach diminishing returns territory
- Formula 1 competitive results matter to brand heat — a sustained period of poor racing performance could modestly cool demand

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari possesses one of the widest moats in public markets — a heritage brand with pricing power that actually strengthens through artificial scarcity, evidenced by decade-plus sustained margins at luxury-goods levels and ROIC well above cost of capital. The only deduction reflects genuine uncertainty around whether the moat fully translates to an electric powertrain world, though early indications and management discipline suggest it will.

# Management & Capital Allocation — RACE

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Ferrari FY 2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Ferrari 2025 Remuneration Report, Ferrari Capital Markets Day 2025, Bloomberg (shareholder pact renewal Jan 2026), yfinance financial data, Ferrari corporate governance filings, ainvest buyback analysis, Global Finance Magazine (Vigna interview)

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Controlling shareholders Exor (24.7% equity / 36.5% voting) and Piero Ferrari (10.5% equity / 15.5% voting) provide long-term stewardship alignment, reinforced by a renewed shareholder pact through 2029 | 5 |
| 2 | Capital allocation is disciplined and owner-oriented: EUR 1.3B returned to shareholders in FY2025 (buybacks + dividends), with a new EUR 3.5B multi-year buyback program launched January 2026 | 5 |
| 3 | Ferrari achieved net industrial debt of just EUR 32M at year-end 2025 while funding EUR 950M in capex and returning EUR 1.3B to shareholders — exceptional capital efficiency | 4 |
| 4 | Share count is declining: buybacks are more than offsetting equity compensation dilution, creating genuine per-share value compounding | 4 |
| 5 | CEO Vigna's compensation is heavily equity-weighted (84% bonuses/stock vs 16% salary), but total pay of EUR 10.9M is high relative to peers of Ferrari's revenue size | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Ferrari's ownership structure is one of its most underappreciated strengths. Exor N.V., the Agnelli family's holding company chaired by John Elkann, controls 24.7% of equity and 36.5% of voting rights. Piero Ferrari, son of founder Enzo Ferrari, holds 10.5% of equity and 15.5% of voting rights. Together, these two parties control over 48% of voting power through a loyalty voting mechanism that rewards long-term holders with enhanced votes. In January 2026, Exor and the Ferrari family renewed their shareholder pact through 2029 with automatic renewal provisions, signaling continued alignment. John Elkann serves as chairman and Piero Ferrari as vice chairman, providing direct family oversight at the board level. This is a meaningfully owner-operated governance structure — not the dispersed, agency-problem-prone setup common among large-cap companies.

Capital allocation has been outstanding by any measure. In FY2025, Ferrari returned more than EUR 1.3 billion to shareholders through a combination of share repurchases (approximately EUR 785M) and dividends. The company simultaneously funded EUR 950M in capital expenditures — including construction of the E-Building for EV production — and still ended the year with net industrial debt of just EUR 32 million. This is remarkable capital efficiency: Ferrari generates enough cash to invest aggressively in the business, return substantial capital to shareholders, and maintain a fortress balance sheet all at the same time. The company exceeded the profitability targets from its 2026 business plan one year ahead of schedule and is ahead on its buyback program. Looking forward, the Capital Markets Day outlined EUR 7.0 billion in total shareholder remuneration through 2031, split equally between buybacks and dividends, with the payout ratio increased to 40% of adjusted net profit.

The share count trend confirms that buybacks are creating real per-share value. Diluted EPS reached EUR 8.96 in FY2025, up from prior years, driven both by earnings growth and a shrinking share base. While stock-based compensation expense has increased (EUR 3.0M in 2025 vs EUR 1.0M in 2022), the buyback program is orders of magnitude larger, ensuring net share reduction. This is a management team that understands per-share value creation, not just headline growth. The multi-year buyback structure (EUR 3.5B through 2030) also suggests disciplined, programmatic execution rather than opportunistic timing — though at Ferrari's typical valuation multiples, one could argue that organic reinvestment or special dividends might occasionally offer better per-share returns than repurchasing shares at 25-30x earnings.

CEO Benedetto Vigna, who joined from STMicroelectronics in 2021, has proven an effective steward. His technology background (MEMS sensors, semiconductor) is well-suited to navigating the EV transition, and his public communications reflect engineering discipline rather than promotional excess. Vigna speaks in specifics — margins, capex returns, production timelines — rather than aspirational generalities. His compensation structure is heavily weighted toward equity (84% in bonuses and stock-based incentives versus 16% base salary), which aligns his interests with shareholders. However, total compensation of EUR 10.9M is elevated for a company of Ferrari's revenue size (EUR 7.1B), though not unreasonable given the market cap (USD 56.5B) and profitability. The remuneration report shows performance share units (PSUs) with fair values that have increased materially, from EUR 162/share in 2022 to EUR 365/share in 2025, suggesting the equity awards are tied to rising performance targets.

On governance, the dual-class loyalty voting structure is a double-edged sword. It ensures long-term orientation and insulates management from short-term activist pressure — both positives for a luxury brand where patience is essential. However, it also concentrates power with Exor/Elkann, which means minority shareholders have limited recourse if the controlling family ever pursues value-destructive strategies. To date, there is no evidence of this — Elkann's track record across the Exor portfolio (including the Stellantis restructuring) shows pragmatic, returns-focused thinking. Board independence is adequate, with a mix of independent directors alongside family representatives, though the board is not as independent as pure-governance purists might prefer.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari is controlled by patient, long-term owners (Agnelli family) who have demonstrated outstanding capital allocation — returning EUR 1.3B to shareholders annually while maintaining near-zero net debt and funding the EV transition from cash flow.
- **Bear case:** The loyalty voting structure concentrates power with Exor, and CEO compensation is high relative to revenue size; buybacks at premium valuations may not always be the optimal use of capital.
- **Confidence:** High — The ownership structure, capital allocation track record, and balance sheet strength are all verifiable and have been consistent for years; the main judgment call is whether buybacks at current multiples are truly optimal.

## Red Flags

- Loyalty voting gives Exor/Agnelli family 36.5% voting power from 24.7% economic ownership — standard for European luxury but limits minority shareholder influence
- CEO total compensation of EUR 10.9M is high for the revenue base, though aligned through equity weighting
- Stock-based compensation expense has tripled from 2022 to 2025 (EUR 1.0M to EUR 3.0M), though immaterial relative to buyback spending
- Buybacks at 25-30x earnings may not be the most accretive use of capital versus organic reinvestment or holding cash for opportunistic deployment
- John Elkann's concurrent role as Exor CEO and Ferrari chairman creates potential attention-splitting, though no operational issues have surfaced

## Score: 8 / 10

Ferrari's management and capital allocation earn a strong score: controlling family ownership provides genuine long-term alignment, capital returns are substantial and growing, the balance sheet is pristine, and share count is declining. The deduction from a 9 reflects the concentrated voting structure, elevated CEO pay, and the open question of whether programmatic buybacks at premium multiples represent truly optimal capital deployment for a company that could compound reinvested capital at 24% ROIC.

# Business Economics — RACE

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Ferrari FY2025 annual results (Feb 10, 2026), Ferrari corporate filings, yfinance data, MacroTrends, GuruFocus, StockAnalysis.com, Motley Fool, Investing.com

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC of 24.3% and ROE of 42.9% in FY2025, both trending upward over 5 years — world-class capital efficiency | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margin expanded to 51.7% (from 48.0% in FY2022), driven by personalization mix and pricing power on flat unit volumes | 5 |
| 3 | FCF reached EUR 1.4B (19.7% margin) with FCF conversion improving to 88% from 62% prior year; industrial FCF surpassed EUR 1.5B | 4 |
| 4 | Revenue grew 7% to EUR 7.1B on essentially flat shipments (~13,640 units), demonstrating pure pricing/mix-driven growth | 5 |
| 5 | CapEx of EUR 943M (13.2% of revenue) is elevated for a "luxury" business, reflecting EV transition and new model development | 3 |
| 6 | Personalization accounts for ~20% of car revenue at extremely high incremental margins, acting as a structural margin tailwind | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Ferrari's returns on capital are exceptional and improving. ROIC has risen from roughly 18-20% in 2020-2022 to 24.3% in FY2025. ROE stands at 42.9%, partly amplified by leverage but still reflecting genuinely high operating returns. ROA of 16.6% on a EUR 9.6B asset base confirms this is not just a leverage story. The 5-year trajectory is upward and stable — there are no wild swings. These are Hermes-tier returns for what is technically classified as an automaker, placing Ferrari firmly in the luxury goods category economically.

**Margin Structure.** The margin profile is outstanding and expanding. Gross margin of 51.7% is up from 48.0% just three years ago — a 370bps improvement driven almost entirely by richer product mix and personalization attach rates, not volume growth. Operating margin reached 29.3% (up from 24.0% in FY2022), and EBITDA margin hit 38.8%. Net margin of 22.3% is clean. The key insight is that Ferrari shipped roughly the same number of cars in FY2025 as FY2024 (~13,640 units) yet grew revenue 7% and operating profit 12%. This is pricing power in its purest form. Management has stated explicitly that "our pricing power is not at an end." Personalization revenue (~20% of car revenues) carries near-100% incremental margins, functioning as a structural margin escalator.

**Cash Generation.** Operating cash flow reached EUR 2.3B in FY2025, up from EUR 1.9B prior year. Free cash flow was EUR 1.4B on a reported basis, with industrial FCF exceeding EUR 1.5B. FCF conversion (FCF/net income) improved dramatically to 88% from 62%. Owner earnings of approximately EUR 1.3B (after maintenance capex, estimated at 70% of total capex) are real and growing. The company returned over EUR 1.3B to shareholders in FY2025 through buybacks (EUR 785M) and dividends, yet net industrial debt fell to just EUR 32M. This is a genuine cash machine.

**Capital Intensity and Revenue Quality.** CapEx of EUR 943M (13.2% of revenue) is the one area that warrants scrutiny. This is elevated relative to a typical luxury goods business and reflects Ferrari's investment in its EV platform (the electric Ferrari is expected around 2026-2027), new model development, and the e-building factory in Maranello. Management guides EUR 4.7B in cumulative capex through 2030, offset by EUR 8.0B in projected industrial FCF — so growth investment is self-funded with substantial excess. The capex cycle should moderate as EV infrastructure buildout completes. Revenue quality is high: the order backlog extends well over a year, personalization is recurring per vehicle, and the brand licensing/lifestyle segment (sponsorship, merchandise, Ferrari World) is growing. There is minimal one-time revenue.

**Operating Leverage.** Ferrari exhibits strong operating leverage. Revenue grew 7% in FY2025 while EBIT grew 12% — a 1.7x profit multiplier on incremental revenue. This has been the pattern for several years: flat-to-low-single-digit unit growth translates into high-single-digit revenue growth via mix/price, which in turn drives double-digit profit growth. The fixed cost base (the Maranello factory, engineering staff, F1 operations) is largely stable, so each incremental euro of personalization or model-mix upgrade flows through at high margins. The 2026 guidance (revenue ~EUR 7.5B, EBITDA margin ~39%) implies continued operating leverage.

**R&D Spending.** R&D of EUR 919M (12.9% of revenue) is substantial and rising, driven by electrification. This is capitalized as an investment in future competitive positioning — Ferrari cannot afford to be late to the hybrid/EV luxury segment. The spending is manageable relative to cash generation and is better thought of as growth capex protecting the moat rather than maintenance spend.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari is a luxury goods business disguised as an automaker, with 50%+ gross margins, 24% ROIC, pricing power on flat volumes, and a self-funding growth investment cycle — the economics are getting better, not worse.
- **Bear case:** Elevated capex for EV transition could compress returns if the electric Ferrari fails to command traditional pricing premiums, and the 13% capex-to-revenue ratio is high for a company valued as a luxury compounder.
- **Confidence:** High — The financial data is unambiguous: margins are expanding, returns are rising, cash generation is strong, and the growth is price/mix-driven rather than volume-dependent. The only uncertainty is whether EV-era economics will match ICE-era economics.

## Red Flags

- CapEx intensity at 13.2% of revenue is elevated for a luxury business; needs to moderate post-EV buildout
- R&D at 12.9% of revenue is high and rising — justified by electrification but creates near-term earnings drag
- US tariff exposure (~25% of units sold to US) creates headline risk, though Ferrari has demonstrated ability to pass through price increases
- Flat unit shipments in FY2025 were deliberate (model changeover), but any sustained volume decline would pressure fixed cost absorption

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari delivers world-class returns on capital (ROIC 24%, ROE 43%), expanding margins (gross 52%, operating 29%), and strong FCF generation — all on flat unit volumes — making it one of the highest-quality business models in public markets, docked only for elevated near-term capex intensity during the EV transition.

# Balance Sheet Safety — RACE

**Analyst Role:** Balance Sheet Safety Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** Ferrari FY2025 annual results (Feb 10, 2026), Ferrari corporate filings, yfinance data, cbonds.com, CRIF Ratings, MacroTrends, TradingEconomics

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Net industrial debt reduced to just EUR 32M at end of FY2025, effectively a net-cash position after returning EUR 1.3B to shareholders | 5 |
| 2 | Interest coverage of 51.1x (EBIT/interest) is fortress-grade — interest expense of EUR 41M is trivial relative to EUR 2.1B operating income | 5 |
| 3 | Current ratio of 3.4x with EUR 3.4B working capital provides exceptional short-term liquidity | 4 |
| 4 | Debt/EBITDA improved to 1.0x (from 1.6x), with net debt/EBITDA at just 0.5x — among the lowest in any industrial company | 5 |
| 5 | Adjusted net leverage expected to turn negative by 2027 (net cash position) assuming continued buybacks and regular dividends | 4 |
| 6 | EUR 734M in bond repayments made in 2025 (including EUR 451M maturity), demonstrating active deleveraging with no refinancing stress | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels.** Ferrari's total debt stands at EUR 2.9B with long-term debt of EUR 2.7B and short-term obligations well-covered. However, the headline debt figure overstates the risk significantly. Net industrial debt — which management and rating agencies treat as the core metric — fell to just EUR 32M at year-end 2025. This means Ferrari's operating business is essentially debt-free. The gross debt carries low coupons from bonds issued during the low-rate era. Debt-to-equity of 73.8% has improved from 108% and continues trending down. Debt/EBITDA at 1.0x and net debt/EBITDA at 0.5x are conservative by any standard. Credit agency CRIF rates Ferrari BBB+ stable, with commentary suggesting the company is "close to AA-" and could be upgraded as metrics continue to improve through 2027.

**Interest Coverage.** This is where Ferrari's safety becomes unambiguous. With EBIT of EUR 2.1B against interest expense of just EUR 41.2M, interest coverage stands at 51.1x. This is not a typo — Ferrari earns 51 times its annual interest bill. Even in a severe stress scenario where EBIT halved, coverage would remain above 25x. The interest expense is economically irrelevant to this business. Ferrari could service its entire debt load from a single quarter's operating income.

**Liquidity.** Cash on hand of EUR 1.5B against current liabilities of EUR 1.4B gives a current ratio of 3.4x with working capital of EUR 3.4B. Total current assets of EUR 4.8B provide a massive liquidity buffer. At zero revenue, Ferrari's cash alone would cover approximately 12-13 months of operating expenses (estimated at EUR 120-130M/month in fixed costs). With credit facilities in addition to cash, survival capacity extends well beyond 24 months. The order backlog extending over a year means a zero-revenue scenario is essentially theoretical — customers have already committed.

**Refinancing Risk.** Ferrari repaid EUR 734M in debt during 2025, including a EUR 451M bond that matured in the first half. These repayments were funded entirely from operating cash flow with no new issuance required. The remaining debt has a well-laddered maturity profile. Given that industrial FCF exceeds EUR 1.5B annually and net industrial debt is just EUR 32M, refinancing risk is negligible. The company could theoretically retire all remaining gross debt within two years from cash flow alone if it chose to stop buybacks. Analysts project the adjusted net leverage ratio will turn negative by 2027, meaning Ferrari will be in a net cash position even after continued buybacks of ~EUR 700M/year and regular dividends.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Ferrari's off-balance-sheet exposure is modest. The company operates from owned facilities in Maranello, limiting lease obligations. Pension liabilities are typical for a European manufacturer of this size and are not a material risk. The financial services arm (Ferrari Financial Services) carries securitization debt that is ring-fenced from the industrial business — this is why the "industrial net debt" metric is the appropriate measure. There are no unusual contingent liabilities, guarantees, or structured financing arrangements that would alter the safety assessment.

**Stress Test: Revenue Drops 30% for 2 Years.** In this scenario, revenue would fall from EUR 7.1B to approximately EUR 5.0B. Assuming operating margins compress to ~20% (from 29.3%) due to fixed cost deleveraging, EBIT would be roughly EUR 1.0B. Interest expense remains ~EUR 41M, so coverage stays above 24x. FCF would decline but remain positive at perhaps EUR 400-500M after reduced capex. Net industrial debt of EUR 32M means there is no debt spiral risk. Ferrari would likely pause buybacks (saving EUR 700-800M/year) and trim capex, easily preserving cash. The company would emerge from a two-year downturn with its balance sheet intact and no need for equity issuance. Historically, Ferrari's volumes proved resilient even during 2008-2009 and COVID, with demand recovering quickly due to the extended order backlog.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari operates with essentially zero net industrial debt, 51x interest coverage, and EUR 1.5B+ annual FCF — this is a fortress balance sheet that could survive virtually any economic scenario without external financing.
- **Bear case:** Gross debt of EUR 2.9B still exists and carries refinancing risk if capital markets freeze, though the company's cash generation makes this largely theoretical.
- **Confidence:** High — The numbers are unambiguous: net industrial debt of EUR 32M, 51x coverage, 3.4x current ratio, and improving trajectory toward net cash by 2027. There is no reasonable stress scenario where Ferrari faces financial distress.

## Red Flags

- Gross debt of EUR 2.9B is non-trivial in absolute terms, even if coverage ratios make it safe
- Financial services arm carries separate securitization debt that is excluded from industrial metrics — warrants monitoring if auto loan delinquencies rise
- Share buyback program (EUR 3.5B through 2030) could slow deleveraging if management prioritizes returns over balance sheet strength, though current cash generation easily supports both
- EV transition capex (EUR 4.7B through 2030) is a significant commitment, though projected EUR 8.0B in industrial FCF over the same period provides ample funding

## Score: 9 / 10

Ferrari's balance sheet is fortress-grade: net industrial debt of just EUR 32M, interest coverage of 51x, current ratio of 3.4x, and a clear trajectory to net cash by 2027 — the company could survive a multi-year revenue collapse without external financing, docked one point only because gross debt of EUR 2.9B still exists on the books.
