## Detailed Analysis

**Holding period: 10+ years, comfortably.** Ferrari is one of the few businesses where a multi-decade holding period is genuinely supportable. The brand is 80+ years old and strengthening. The scarcity model — deliberate production limits well below demand — is self-reinforcing: fewer cars produced increases exclusivity, which supports pricing power, which grows margins without volume risk. The order backlog extending to end-2027 provides near-term visibility, while the structural demand from ultra-high-net-worth individuals (UHNWI population growing 5-7% annually) supports long-term volume. The EV transition is a medium-term thesis test (2026-2028), but even if the electric strategy underperforms, Ferrari's ICE and hybrid lineup remains dominant. This is not a business whose thesis depends on a single quarter or product cycle. The investment case is about owning a perpetuity on one of the world's strongest luxury franchises.

**Volatility tolerance and drawdown history.** The reported beta of 0.55 suggests Ferrari is a low-volatility name, and over long periods that is broadly true — it moves less than the market on average. However, the 39% peak-to-trough drawdown from $519 (July 2025) to $312 (March 2026) is a sharp reminder that low beta does not mean low drawdown risk. The selloff was driven by tariff fears, a broader luxury derating, and the stock simply being extremely overvalued at $519. Previous drawdowns include a ~30% decline during COVID (2020) and a ~25% pullback in late 2022. An investor in Ferrari should expect 25-40% drawdowns every 3-5 years and be psychologically prepared to hold through them. The current drawdown, while painful, is not unusual for a stock that previously traded at 50x+ earnings.

**Catalysts and timeline.** Several identifiable catalysts could rerate the stock in either direction over the next 6-18 months. Positive: (1) US-EU tariff resolution or reduction below 15% — would remove the largest overhang immediately. (2) Successful launch and strong order intake for the Ferrari Luce EV — expected in late 2026, this would validate the electrification strategy and potentially expand the addressable market. (3) FY2026 results exceeding €7.5B revenue guidance — Ferrari has a pattern of conservative guidance followed by beats. (4) Continued buyback execution reducing share count. Negative: (1) Tariff escalation beyond 15%. (2) Disappointing Luce reception or EV-related margin pressure. (3) Global luxury demand softening. Timeline for clarity: Q3/Q4 2026, when Luce orders and H1 results provide data on both catalysts.

**Position sizing implications.** The Monte Carlo P(IV > Price) of 20.2% means the quantitative case for owning Ferrari at $317 is weak. The bear-to-bull IV spread of $174-$365 is wide ($191 range on a $317 stock), reflecting genuine uncertainty. Buffett-style position sizing would dictate: (a) a full position (3-5% of portfolio) requires high conviction that the stock is undervalued — not the case here; (b) a starter position (1-2%) could be justified if the investor has strong qualitative conviction in the franchise and views the drawdown as an opportunity to average in; (c) zero position is the disciplined choice until price reaches the $240-270 zone where the risk-reward flips. Given the 80% overvaluation probability from Monte Carlo, the quantitatively honest answer is to wait. The business deserves a spot on the watchlist, not in the portfolio — yet.

**Correlation and portfolio overlap.** Ferrari's factor exposures are unusual and valuable for diversification. It has low correlation to the broader auto sector (Ford, GM, Toyota trade on volume cycles; Ferrari trades on luxury sentiment). It has moderate correlation to European luxury goods (Hermes, LVMH) but with a distinct revenue model (goods vs. experiences, units in thousands vs. millions). EUR-denominated costs with USD/GBP revenue exposure provides a natural hedge for USD-based portfolios during EUR weakness, but creates a headwind during EUR strength. Sector classification as "Consumer Discretionary/Autos" understates its defensive qualities — Ferrari's demand is far less cyclical than the sector label implies. For a portfolio already heavy in European luxury (Hermes, LVMH) or European industrials, Ferrari adds marginal diversification. For a US-tech-heavy portfolio, it adds significant diversification value.

**Entry plan, sell rules, and review cadence.** The disciplined approach for Ferrari at current prices is a tiered entry strategy. Do not buy at $317. Set limit orders or alerts at: (1) $270 — starter position (1.5% of portfolio), where price approaches quant base IV and forward P/E drops to ~22x; (2) $240 — add to position (total 3%), where MOS becomes meaningfully positive against base IV; (3) $200 — full conviction position (4-5%), near the bear IV and implying genuine dislocation. Sell rules: (a) full exit if the brand moat is demonstrably impaired (e.g., volume expansion beyond 15,000 units diluting exclusivity, or a catastrophic brand scandal); (b) trim at 40x+ forward P/E or if position exceeds 6% of portfolio; (c) do not sell on tariff noise or quarterly misses — these are temporary. Review cadence: quarterly after earnings, with a deep review after Luce launch data arrives (Q4 2026).

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Ferrari is a generational franchise worth owning for decades, and a 39% drawdown from its overvalued peak has brought the stock closer to a level where patient investors can begin building a position — several identifiable catalysts over the next 12 months could drive a re-rating.
- **Bear case:** Even after the correction, the stock trades above quantitative fair value in 80% of scenarios, and buying here would be paying a premium on hope that catalysts materialize — discipline demands waiting for a better entry in the $240-270 range.
- **Confidence:** High — Ferrari is clearly a long-duration compounder worth monitoring closely, and the entry discipline framework is well-supported by the quant model and valuation analysis.

## Red Flags

- Buying at $317 means initiating with negative MOS — a temperament challenge for any value investor
- 39% drawdown may create anchoring bias ("it's cheap because it fell") when the stock is still above fair value
- EV launch in 2026 introduces a period of elevated uncertainty that makes timing an entry harder
- Low beta masks real drawdown severity — investors may underestimate position volatility
- Tariff situation is fluid and could worsen, extending the drawdown period and testing patience

## Score: 5 / 10

Ferrari is an exceptional long-duration franchise that belongs on every quality investor's watchlist, but at $317 the entry timing is poor — the quant model, valuation work, and margin of safety analysis all point to waiting for a more attractive price, and the disciplined move is to watch rather than act.
