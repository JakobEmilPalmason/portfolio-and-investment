## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Costco carries $8.2B in total debt against $15.3B in cash, producing a net cash position of approximately $7.1B. Long-term debt stands at $5.7B with only $75M in current maturities as of FY2025 — there is no near-term refinancing cliff. Finance lease liabilities account for $1.4B of the total debt figure, reflecting Costco's mix of owned and leased properties. The debt-to-equity ratio has declined steadily from 0.45 in 2020 to 0.28 in 2025, and debt-to-capital from 0.31 to 0.20 over the same period. Costco's debt is overwhelmingly fixed-rate, long-dated senior unsecured notes, eliminating floating-rate risk. The company has historically issued debt opportunistically at attractive rates and has not needed to access debt markets for operational funding. This is a balance sheet that generates optionality — the cash pile is growing toward another potential special dividend ($12-15/share, as analysts anticipate), rather than being consumed by debt service.

**Interest Coverage.** At 71.2x EBIT/interest, Costco's interest coverage is not just comfortable — it is effectively irrelevant. Interest expense of $154M in FY2025 (down from $169M in FY2024) represents less than 2% of operating income. Even in a severe downturn scenario, interest coverage would remain well above 20x. This ratio alone signals fortress-level balance sheet safety. The declining interest expense reflects Costco paying down maturing debt without full replacement, further de-risking the balance sheet.

**Liquidity Position.** Cash of $15.3B provides enormous liquidity. The current ratio of 1.0x deserves context: Costco collects membership fees upfront, turns inventory rapidly (11-12x per year), and receives payment from customers at point of sale while stretching payables to suppliers. This negative working capital cycle means the current ratio structurally understates liquidity. Working capital of $1.3B is positive despite the tight current ratio. Costco also maintains undrawn revolving credit facilities (typically $1-2B for companies of this size and rating). Daily cash generation from 600+ warehouses serving 145.9 million cardholders provides a continuous liquidity stream that balance sheet snapshots cannot fully capture.

**Refinancing Risk.** Refinancing risk is effectively zero. The combination of net cash position, AA/Aa3 credit ratings, and minimal near-term maturities ($75M) means Costco could retire all outstanding debt from cash on hand and still have $7B+ remaining. The S&P upgrade to AA in January 2025 — placing Costco alongside names like Microsoft, Johnson & Johnson, and Apple in the credit hierarchy — ensures that any future debt issuance would be at the lowest possible spreads. Costco's last significant debt issuances were done at highly favorable rates, and the declining total debt balance suggests the company is in a deleveraging posture rather than seeking to grow borrowings.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Items.** Finance lease liabilities of $1.4B are already captured in total debt. Operating lease obligations (now on-balance-sheet under ASC 842) add further committed payments but are backed by productive warehouse assets generating strong cash flows. Costco's defined benefit pension exposure is minimal — the company primarily operates a 401(k) plan for US employees. There are no significant contingent liabilities, guarantees, or off-balance-sheet vehicles that would materially alter the risk profile. The clean structure is consistent with Costco's straightforward business model.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** A 30% revenue decline would reduce net sales from ~$270B to ~$189B. Even assuming operating margins compress to 2.0% (from 3.8%) due to fixed cost deleverage, operating income would be ~$3.8B. Interest expense remains ~$154M, preserving coverage above 24x. Membership fee income would prove resilient (members maintained renewals through COVID at 91%+ rates), likely declining only 5-10% to ~$4.8B. FCF would turn negative temporarily as the company maintains warehouse investment, but the $15.3B cash cushion absorbs 3+ years of negative FCF without touching debt markets. Costco would not need to issue equity, draw down credit facilities for survival, or restructure debt. The membership model — where customers have pre-committed — provides a floor under revenue that most retailers lack. This business survives a depression-level scenario with its balance sheet intact.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A net cash fortress with AA/Aa3 ratings, 71x interest coverage, and $15B+ in cash — Costco's balance sheet is among the safest in the entire S&P 500 and provides optionality for special dividends, acquisitions, or accelerated expansion.
- **Bear case:** The $6.5B FY2026 capex commitment and potential special dividend could draw down the cash buffer; a 1.0x current ratio leaves limited margin if supplier terms tighten during a crisis.
- **Confidence:** High — The balance sheet data is unambiguous; AA-rated, net cash, minimal maturities, and no off-balance-sheet complexity; there are no hidden risks to discover here.

## Red Flags

- Current ratio of 1.0x is optically low, though structurally appropriate for the business model
- Finance lease liabilities of $1.4B are growing as the warehouse footprint expands
- A large special dividend ($6-7B) would temporarily reduce the cash cushion, though it would be replenished within a year from FCF
- Rising capex ($6.5B in FY2026) could pressure free cash flow if revenue growth slows

## Score: 9 / 10

Costco operates with a net cash balance sheet, AA/Aa3 credit ratings, 71x interest coverage, and sufficient liquidity to survive a multi-year depression without external capital — the only deduction reflects the optically tight current ratio and rising capex commitments that will temporarily consume cash.
