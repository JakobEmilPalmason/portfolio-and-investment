## Detailed Analysis

Costco sells a curated selection of roughly 3,800 SKUs (versus 100,000+ at a typical supermarket) of groceries, household goods, electronics, apparel, and sundries to its members through warehouse-format stores. Its customers are primarily middle- to upper-income households and small businesses who pay an annual membership fee ($65 basic, $130 executive) for the privilege of shopping. The executive tier now represents 74.3% of total sales and 39.7 million members, indicating strong trade-up behavior that directly increases membership fee income.

The revenue model is deceptively simple but often misunderstood. Costco is not really a retailer — it is a subscription business that happens to sell goods at near cost. Merchandise gross margins run around 12.8%, and the company deliberately prices products at the lowest possible markup (capped historically around 14-15%). The real profit engine is membership fees, which generated $5.3B in FY2025 revenue at near-100% margins. This fee income essentially IS the profit. In FY2024, membership fees accounted for approximately 65-72% of net operating income depending on the measurement period. This makes Costco's profit stream far more predictable than a traditional retailer's.

The key business drivers are remarkably few: (1) membership count and renewal rates — which have been above 90% globally and above 92% in the U.S./Canada for years; (2) comparable sales growth, which ran at 6.4% in Q1 FY2026; and (3) net new warehouse openings, with 28 planned for FY2026 on a base of 924. A secondary driver is the mix shift toward executive memberships, which doubles the fee per household and includes 2% cash-back rewards that deepen engagement. Digital sales surged 20.5% in Q1 FY2026 but remain a relatively small share of total revenue.

Revenue predictability is among the highest in all of retail. The membership fee stream is contractual annual revenue with 90%+ renewal. Merchandise revenue is driven by high-frequency consumables (food and sundries are ~60% of sales) that generate repeat weekly visits. There is zero meaningful customer concentration — no single member, business, or geography dominates. The model is explainable in under two minutes: "Costco charges people to shop, sells goods at near cost, and keeps the membership fee as profit. Members renew at 92% because the savings far exceed the fee."

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** One of the most transparent and predictable business models in global retail — subscription economics with 92% renewal rates and steady warehouse expansion runway
- **Bear case:** The simplicity of the model means growth is inherently capped by warehouse rollout pace and population growth; digital disruption remains a modest but real long-term question
- **Confidence:** High — This business is as understandable as any large-cap company in existence

## Red Flags

- Membership fee increases (most recent: September 2024) could theoretically dent renewal rates, though no evidence of this so far
- E-commerce is growing fast but still represents a small fraction of revenue; the physical warehouse model has geographic limits
- International expansion introduces currency and regulatory complexity, though execution has been strong historically

## Score: 9 / 10

Costco's business model is among the most transparent in the S&P 500 — a membership subscription layered on top of a high-volume, low-margin warehouse retailer with only 2-3 key drivers and zero customer concentration.
