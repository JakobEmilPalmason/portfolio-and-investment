## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Costco's ROIC trajectory tells a compelling story: 21.6% in FY2022, 20.5% in FY2023, 23.1% in FY2024, and 24.2% in FY2025. These are exceptional figures for a business operating on razor-thin margins in physical retail. ROE runs even higher at 27-31%, partially reflecting the capital-light balance sheet relative to earnings power. ROA at 9-10.5% is strong for a retailer carrying significant inventory and property. The consistency matters as much as the level — there is no year where returns dip below 20%, indicating the economic model is structurally advantaged rather than cyclically lucky.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** Costco's gross margin of 12.8% looks thin in isolation but is deliberately so — the company prices to maximize member value, not margin. The real signal is the direction: gross margins have expanded 70 basis points over four years (12.1% to 12.8%), and operating margins have expanded 40 basis points (3.4% to 3.8%). Net margins held steady at 2.6-2.9%. This gradual improvement, combined with 7-8% revenue growth, translates into net income growth of 8-13% annually. The operating leverage is real but gentle — profits grow modestly faster than revenue, reflecting scale efficiencies in distribution, procurement, and membership fee leverage over a fixed cost base. In Q1 FY2026, the company "widened its margin and delivered leverage gains on the bottom line," confirming the trend continues.

**Cash Generation and Owner Earnings.** FCF of $7.8B on net income of $8.1B produces an exceptional 96.8% conversion ratio. Owner earnings calculated simply (NI + D&A - CapEx) come to $5.0B, while the quant model's adjusted owner earnings (using maintenance capex only, ~44% of total) yield $8.1B. The gap between these two figures represents Costco's growth investment — roughly $3.1B annually in new warehouse construction that is not required to maintain current earning power. This is a business that could stop growing tomorrow and generate $8B+ in distributable cash flow. The $15.3B cash balance (growing toward a potential special dividend) is further evidence that cash generation exceeds both investment needs and regular capital returns.

**Capital Intensity.** Total capex of $5.5B (FY2025) is rising to an estimated $6.5B in FY2026, but this is almost entirely discretionary growth spending — new warehouses, relocations, depot expansion, and digital infrastructure. Maintenance capex at ~44% of total ($2.4B) is modest relative to the asset base and revenue. Each new warehouse costs roughly $100-150M all-in and generates $150-250M in annual revenue within a few years, implying strong incremental returns on growth capex. The business is capital-intensive in absolute terms but capital-efficient in return terms. Costco funds all growth from operations with no need for debt or equity issuance.

**Revenue Quality.** Costco's revenue has two components with very different characteristics. Merchandise sales ($269.9B in FY2025) are volume-driven, with same-store sales growing 7.1% on a combination of traffic increases and modest ticket growth. This is among the most resilient revenue streams in retail — members pre-pay for access, creating behavioral lock-in. Membership fee income ($5.3B) is a pure recurring revenue stream with 93%+ renewal rates in the US/Canada, growing 10% organically before the fee increase. After the September 2024 fee hike (the first in seven years), membership income jumped 14% in Q1 FY2026. Total paid households reached 81.4 million (+5.2%), with executive memberships growing 9.1% to 39.7 million. This is as close to subscription revenue as physical retail gets.

**Operating Leverage Dynamics.** Costco's operating model delivers moderate but consistent operating leverage. The membership fee base acts as a high-margin fixed revenue layer — as it grows, it contributes disproportionately to operating income. SG&A leverage is gradual; the company reinvests efficiency gains into lower prices rather than capturing them as margin. Net income grew from $5.8B to $8.1B over FY2022-2025 (40% cumulative, ~12% CAGR) on revenue growth of 21% cumulative (~6.5% CAGR), confirming that profits do grow faster than revenue, though not dramatically so. This is by design — Costco's model prioritizes member value over margin expansion, which paradoxically strengthens the long-term moat.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** ROIC consistently above 20% with expanding margins, $8B+ in adjusted owner earnings, and a membership model that is both recurring and accelerating after the fee increase — this is one of the highest-quality economic engines in retail.
- **Bear case:** Absolute margins remain thin (3.8% operating), leaving limited buffer if competitive intensity rises or cost inflation outpaces pricing discipline; $6.5B capex ramp increases execution risk on new warehouse returns.
- **Confidence:** High — Four years of audited financials show a clear, stable, and improving pattern across every key metric; the business model is well understood and has been stress-tested through multiple economic cycles.

## Red Flags

- Thin absolute margins mean even small cost disruptions (tariffs, wage inflation, shrinkage) can compress profitability disproportionately
- CapEx ramp from $5.5B to $6.5B is significant; if new warehouse productivity disappoints, ROIC could decline
- SBC of $860M is non-trivial relative to $5.0B simple owner earnings (17% dilution factor)
- E-commerce margins are lower than warehouse margins, and digital investment is accelerating

## Score: 9 / 10

Costco delivers elite, consistent returns on capital (ROIC 20-24%), exceptional cash conversion (97%), and a membership-driven recurring revenue model that is unique in physical retail — the only deductions are for thin absolute margins and an accelerating capex program that needs continued execution.
