## Detailed Analysis

**Owner Earnings and Earning Power.** The quant model anchored on FY2024 financials: revenue of £26.3B, operating margin 9.3%, and owner earnings of ~£2.0B. BAE has since reported FY2025 results showing revenue of £30.7B, underlying EBIT of £3.32B, and return on sales of 10.8%. Free cash flow came in at £2.16B despite higher capex and R&D. This means the DCF starting point understates current earning power by roughly 15–20%. If re-run with FY2025 inputs, all three scenario IVs would shift meaningfully higher, further reinforcing the undervaluation signal.

**What Must Be True for Today's Price.** At 2,156 GBp with ~2.9B shares outstanding, the market values BAE's equity at roughly £62.5B. To justify this via a simple owner-earnings capitalization, the market is implicitly discounting ~£2.2B in sustainable owner earnings at roughly 3.5% (earnings yield). That is extremely undemanding — it essentially assumes zero real growth, which contradicts the £83.6B backlog, NATO spending ramp, and management's 7–9% guided revenue growth. The market appears to be applying a "defense premium discount" — pricing the stock as if the spending cycle is temporary rather than structural.

**Multiples in Context.** The trailing P/E of 31.7x looks elevated but is misleading: it captures a year of heavy reinvestment and below-trend FCF conversion. The forward P/E of ~23x on FY2026 consensus is more instructive. Against guided EPS growth of 9–11%, this yields a PEG of roughly 2.2x — not cheap in absolute terms but reasonable for a business with decade-long backlog visibility, government-backed revenue streams, and improving margins. European defense peers trade at wide-ranging multiples: Rheinmetall at ~39x forward (pricing 40%+ growth), while Leonardo trades at ~18x. BAE sits mid-range, reflecting its diversified but lower-growth-rate profile.

**Scenario Analysis.** The corrected quant model produces: Bear IV 1,446 GBp (assumes margin compression and WACC of 5.5%), Base IV 2,688 GBp, Bull IV 3,941 GBp (margin expansion, sustained high growth). The bear case requires operating margins reverting below 9% and revenue growth decelerating sharply — unlikely given the backlog and spending trajectory. The base case assumes current margins hold with moderate growth fade — this is conservative. The bull case assumes BAE captures outsized share of the NATO ramp with margin improvement toward 12% — plausible given the THAAD expansion deal and munitions demand. On corrected sensitivity analysis, even the most pessimistic grid cell (10.3% growth, 6.5% WACC) produces 2,369 GBp — still 10% above today's price.

**Risks to Valuation.** The quant model's WACC of 4.5% is derived from a near-zero beta (0.01), which is an artifact of BAE's defensive characteristics but arguably understates the true cost of capital. A more realistic WACC of 7–8% would compress base IV toward 1,800–2,200 GBp, making the stock closer to fairly valued. Additionally, the high terminal value dependency (81–87% of enterprise value) means the valuation is sensitive to long-run assumptions. If defense spending normalizes post-2030, terminal multiples may contract.

**Net Assessment.** Even with conservative adjustments — a higher WACC, margin mean-reversion, and lower terminal multiple — the stock appears to trade at or modestly below fair value. Using the quant model as-is (with the GBp correction), the base case suggests nearly 25% upside. The key question is whether the spending cycle is cyclical (3–5 years) or structural (decade+). The evidence — NATO 2% floors becoming 3% targets, backlog extending past 2035, and geopolitical tensions showing no signs of abating — favors the structural interpretation.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Structural NATO spending ramp to 3% GDP, £83.6B backlog providing decade-long visibility, margin expansion toward 12%+, and a re-run DCF on FY2025 actuals would push base IV above 3,000 GBp — 40%+ upside.
- **Bear case:** Near-zero beta understates true WACC; a realistic 7–8% discount rate compresses IV to ~2,000 GBp, making the stock fairly valued. Defense spending cycle proves shorter than expected, terminal multiple contracts, and FCF conversion disappoints as capex ramps.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The backlog and spending trajectory are hard to argue with, but the valuation is sensitive to discount rate assumptions given high terminal value dependency.

## Red Flags
- Quant model beta of 0.01 produces an artificially low WACC (4.5%); true cost of equity is likely 7–9%
- Terminal value represents 81–87% of enterprise value — extreme sensitivity to long-run assumptions
- Trailing P/E of 31.7x is near decade highs; any earnings miss would hit the multiple hard
- FY2026 FCF guidance of >£1.3B is below FY2025's £2.16B due to prepayment outflows — headline FCF drop could spook the market
- P/B of 548x reflects minimal tangible book value — equity cushion is almost entirely goodwill and intangibles

## Score: 7 / 10
BAE trades modestly below corrected base intrinsic value with a strong backlog tailwind, but the artificially low WACC flatters the DCF and the stock is not obviously cheap on traditional multiples — a fair-to-attractive valuation rather than a screaming bargain.
