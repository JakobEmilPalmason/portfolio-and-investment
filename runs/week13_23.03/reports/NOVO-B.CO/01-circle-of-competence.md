## Detailed Analysis

Novo Nordisk sells injectable and oral medications for diabetes and obesity, primarily under the brands Ozempic (diabetes), Wegovy (obesity), and Rybelsus (oral diabetes). The company also has a smaller but meaningful rare disease segment (haemophilia, growth disorders). Revenue is earned through pharmaceutical distribution channels — wholesalers, pharmacy benefit managers, and increasingly direct government programs. The business model is straightforward: develop patented biologic drugs, manufacture at scale, and sell at premium prices into chronic disease markets where patients need ongoing treatment.

The key revenue drivers are (1) GLP-1 prescription volume, which continues to grow as obesity treatment becomes more mainstream and payer coverage expands; (2) pricing, which is under severe downward pressure in the U.S. from government negotiation (the TrumpRx program has set cash-pay prices at $349-350/month, down from roughly $1,000+); (3) geographic expansion, where Novo holds 68% GLP-1 volume share in international markets versus roughly 42% in the U.S.; and (4) pipeline conversion, particularly CagriSema (regulatory submission planned for 2026) and amycretin (entering Phase 3 in 2026).

Revenue predictability is high on the diabetes side — patients require chronic medication indefinitely, and switching costs are meaningful (doctor supervision, titration schedules, side-effect familiarity). The obesity side is less predictable: treatment duration is uncertain, payer coverage varies, and the market is still maturing. That said, the obesity opportunity is enormous — an estimated 800 million people globally are obese, and penetration remains in low single digits.

Customer concentration is low at the patient level (millions of individual patients globally), but there is meaningful payer concentration: the top three U.S. PBMs (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) control formulary access and wield significant pricing leverage. Government payers (Medicare, Medicaid, European national health systems) are an increasingly large share of the customer base, adding political risk but also volume stability.

This is a business anyone can explain in two minutes: Novo makes GLP-1 drugs that help people manage diabetes and lose weight. They sell globally, with the U.S. as the largest profit pool. The company is essentially a one-molecule franchise (semaglutide) scaling into the largest chronic disease markets in the world.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The GLP-1 market is the largest new drug category in a generation, Novo is the co-leader with decades of diabetes expertise, and the business is fundamentally simple to understand.
- **Bear case:** Extreme product concentration in semaglutide means a single clinical, competitive, or regulatory setback could impair the majority of revenue.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model, revenue drivers, and competitive dynamics are transparent and well-documented.

## Red Flags
- Over 90% revenue concentration in a single molecule family (semaglutide/liraglutide)
- U.S. pricing environment has shifted dramatically — 70% price cuts are structural, not temporary
- Generic semaglutide has launched in India, with patent expirations approaching in other major markets (Canada, Brazil, China)
- Compounding pharmacies are selling copycat versions at lower prices, partially eroding branded demand

## Score: 9 / 10
Novo Nordisk is one of the most understandable businesses in global pharma — a pure-play GLP-1 franchise with transparent economics, clear revenue drivers, and a product anyone can explain. The only complexity is navigating the rapidly shifting pricing and competitive landscape, but the business itself is crystal clear.
