## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital: elite but normalising.** Novo Nordisk's ROIC trajectory tells a story of two phases. Through FY2023, returns were extraordinary -- 70.5% ROIC, 88% ROE -- reflecting a capital-light biological drug business scaling into massive GLP-1 demand. The invested capital base was modest (DKK 128B in FY2023) relative to earnings power. Starting in FY2024 and accelerating into FY2025, the company embarked on a once-in-a-generation manufacturing expansion. Invested capital ballooned to DKK 316B, including the ~$11B (DKK ~75B) Catalent fill-finish site acquisition. The resulting FY2025 ROIC of 36% is still exceptional by any industrial standard -- it would rank in the top decile of global large-caps -- but the downward trajectory reflects the denominator expanding faster than earnings. If this capex cycle peaks as guided (DKK 55B in 2026, declining thereafter), ROIC should stabilise and eventually recover as the new capacity generates revenue.

**Margin structure: temporarily pressured but structurally strong.** Gross margins of 81% remain outstanding for a pharmaceutical company and reflect the pricing power of patent-protected biological therapies. The 370 bps YoY decline is largely explainable by one-off items: ~DKK 3B in restructuring charges and production asset impairments, plus Catalent-related depreciation hitting COGS for the first time. Operating margin compressed from 44.2% to 41.3%, with the incremental drag from R&D spend (up DKK 3.9B YoY) and capacity ramp costs. Looking ahead, the 2026 outlook is concerning: the MFN agreement in the US and semaglutide LOE in certain international markets will pressure pricing, while competition from Eli Lilly (tirzepatide) and emerging oral GLP-1 entrants intensifies. Management is guiding for operating profit decline of 5-13% at CER -- a meaningful reversal from years of 20%+ growth. The structural margin question is whether Novo can defend 40%+ operating margins through a period of lower pricing and higher depreciation from its expanded asset base.

**Cash generation: capex trough obscures underlying strength.** The headline FCF figure of DKK 29B (9.4% FCF margin) is misleading in isolation. Operating cash flow remained robust at DKK 119B, essentially flat YoY despite the margin compression. The FCF collapse is entirely a function of capex: DKK 90.1B in FY2025 vs DKK 14.8B just three years prior. The quant model's separation of maintenance vs growth capex is instructive -- maintenance capex is only DKK 22B (the D&A proxy), meaning adjusted owner earnings are DKK 102.4B, fully in line with net income. This confirms that the core business remains a cash machine; the temporary FCF weakness is a strategic choice to build manufacturing capacity for the next decade. Capex is guided to moderate to DKK 55B in 2026 and decline further, which should restore FCF to DKK 60B+ within 2-3 years assuming stable operating cash flow.

**Revenue quality and operating leverage.** Novo Nordisk's revenue is highly recurring in nature. Diabetes therapies (insulin, GLP-1 for T2D) serve chronic conditions requiring lifelong treatment -- effectively a subscription model. The obesity franchise (Wegovy) is newer but exhibits strong refill persistence once patients are on therapy. Revenue grew at a 20%+ CAGR from FY2022-FY2025, driven by GLP-1 volume and pricing. However, the 2026 guidance (-5% to -13% CER) marks a stark inflection. The MFN agreement directly compresses US pricing, while semaglutide loss of exclusivity in certain international markets opens the door to biosimilar competition. Operating leverage, which amplified margins on the way up (operating margin expanded from 42% to 44% over FY2022-FY2024), will work in reverse if revenue contracts while the fixed-cost base is larger due to the manufacturing expansion. The company is partially offsetting this through a "company-wide transformation" (cost optimisation programme), but the near-term earnings trajectory is clearly negative.

**Capital intensity: transitional surge, not structural shift.** The capex-to-revenue ratio spiked from 8% (FY2022) to 29% (FY2025), but this is a discrete investment cycle rather than a permanent change in the business model. Novo is building API manufacturing, fill-finish capacity, and packaging lines to meet GLP-1 demand that it simply could not serve from existing facilities. The Catalent acquisition added three large-scale sites. Once this cycle completes (management signals declining capex from 2026), the business should return to a more normalised 10-15% capex/revenue ratio, with the new capacity supporting materially higher volumes. The risk is that if demand disappoints (due to competition or pricing), the company will be stuck with underutilised capacity and elevated depreciation.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Core earnings power (DKK 102B+ owner earnings) is intact and temporarily obscured by a capex supercycle that will peak and decline, restoring FCF and eventually ROIC as new capacity fills with volume.
- **Bear case:** The 2026 revenue decline (-5% to -13% CER) may not be temporary if MFN pricing, biosimilar competition, and Medicaid coverage cuts structurally compress the GLP-1 franchise economics; negative operating leverage on a larger fixed-cost base could erode margins further.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The underlying business economics are clearly elite, but the FY2025-2026 transition creates genuine uncertainty about the pace of normalisation and whether peak margins are behind us.

## Red Flags
- FCF conversion collapsed to 28% in FY2025; even with capex moderating, it will take 2+ years to recover to historical levels
- 2026 guidance of -5% to -13% revenue and operating profit decline is the first negative growth outlook in over a decade
- Gross margin erosion (81% vs 85% prior) may not fully reverse if Catalent depreciation is structural and MFN pricing compresses realisations
- Buybacks slashed from DKK 30B (FY2023) to DKK 1.4B (FY2025) as cash is directed to capex -- shareholder returns temporarily impaired
- Risk of capacity overshoot if GLP-1 demand moderates due to competition or payer pushback

## Score: 7 / 10
Novo Nordisk's core business economics remain elite -- 36% ROIC even in a heavy investment year, 81% gross margins, and DKK 102B in owner earnings. The score is pulled down from 8-9 by the sharp near-term deterioration: collapsing FCF conversion, first-ever negative revenue guidance, and uncertainty over whether the capex supercycle will deliver adequate returns on the incremental capital deployed.
