## Detailed Analysis

Novo Nordisk is undergoing its most significant leadership and capital allocation transition in decades. CEO Lars Fruergaard Jorgensen, who led the company from 2017 through the semaglutide boom, stepped down in August 2025 under pressure from the Novo Nordisk Foundation. His replacement, Maziar Mike Doustdar, previously served as EVP of International Operations — the division where Novo holds 68% GLP-1 market share. The transition was not a smooth planned succession; it was driven by the Foundation's dissatisfaction with the stock's 55% decline from its peak and a disagreement with the board over the pace of leadership renewal. The board chairman also departed in an October 2025 shakeup, and an Extraordinary General Meeting in November 2025 elected new board members aligned with the Foundation's vision. This degree of governance turmoil is unusual for a company of Novo's stature and signals genuine strategic disagreement at the top.

The Foundation structure is a double-edged sword. The Novo Nordisk Foundation — one of the world's wealthiest charitable foundations — holds all A shares, giving it majority voting control. This insulates the company from short-term activist pressure and ensures a long-term orientation. However, it also means minority B-share holders (the publicly traded shares) have limited governance influence. The Foundation's willingness to force out the CEO and reshape the board demonstrates it is an active, interventionist owner — arguably a positive for accountability, but it creates uncertainty about strategic direction during transitions.

Insider ownership is effectively zero. Tracking sources show 0.00% insider holdings, and multiple executives (including departing CEO Jorgensen) sold shares in 2025 — Jorgensen disposed of ~34,000 shares at $86-87 (equivalent to roughly DKK 600) in February 2025, well before the stock's worst decline. No executive was a notable buyer as the stock fell from DKK 530+ to DKK 225. The absence of insider buying at a 55% drawdown is a negative signal — management is not putting personal capital at risk alongside shareholders. Compensation is structured with performance shares and short-term bonuses tied to financial metrics, but without meaningful personal ownership, the alignment is contractual rather than intrinsic.

Capital allocation has undergone a dramatic shift. From FY2022 to FY2025, the company pivoted from a capital-light, shareholder-return-focused model to an aggressive capacity-building mode. Capex exploded from DKK 14.8B to DKK 90.1B — a 6x increase in three years. This funded the $11B acquisition of three Catalent fill-finish sites, a $4.1B new facility in Clayton, North Carolina, a $2.2B upgrade in Chartres, France, and a $6B expansion of the Kalundborg, Denmark campus. Simultaneously, buybacks collapsed: DKK 29.9B (FY2023) to DKK 20.2B (FY2024) to just DKK 1.4B (FY2025). The dividend yield has risen to 6.7% largely because the stock price has fallen, not because of increased payouts. Total debt has increased 5x to DKK 131B, though at 0.8x Debt/EBITDA and 32x interest coverage, the balance sheet remains conservatively leveraged.

The capital allocation question is whether this massive manufacturing bet is visionary or poorly timed. The bull case: GLP-1 demand will be supply-constrained for years, and owning 14 fill-finish sites globally creates a structural advantage that Lilly and smaller competitors cannot easily match. The bear case: Novo is investing at peak multiples into capacity for a drug (semaglutide) facing generic competition within 5-6 years, pricing compression now, and clinical inferiority to the leading competitor's molecule. The FCF margin collapse from 36% (FY2022) to 9.4% (FY2025) reflects this investment cycle — it is intentional, not a sign of business deterioration, but the return on this capital is far from guaranteed.

Management communication has been mixed. The company has been forthright about competitive challenges and pricing headwinds, providing specific 2026 guidance (5-13% sales decline). However, the CagriSema spin — positioning a drug that failed to match tirzepatide as still "clinically meaningful" — felt more like politician-speak than owner-candor. The new CEO has had limited public track record in the role, making it difficult to assess his strategic vision and communication style.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Foundation oversight ensures long-term thinking, the manufacturing buildout could prove visionary if GLP-1 demand scales as expected, and new leadership may bring fresh strategic clarity at a time when the stock is deeply discounted.
- **Bear case:** Zero insider ownership, a forced CEO transition, board-level governance turmoil, and a massive capex bet funded by 5x debt growth during a period of eroding competitive position suggest management is spending aggressively into an uncertain future without personal skin in the game.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the leadership transition is too recent to evaluate the new CEO's caliber, and the capital allocation shift is a high-conviction bet whose outcome will not be clear for 2-3 years.

## Red Flags

- Board chairman also departed in October 2025; new board elected at EGM in November 2025
- Zero meaningful insider ownership; executives were net sellers throughout the decline
