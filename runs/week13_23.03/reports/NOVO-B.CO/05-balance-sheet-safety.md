## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and structure: rapid increase from a near-zero base.** Novo Nordisk's balance sheet underwent a dramatic transformation in FY2024-FY2025. As recently as FY2023, total debt was DKK 27B with net debt of just DKK 6.9B -- effectively a net-cash position. By FY2025, total debt reached DKK 131B (long-term: DKK 111.7B, short-term: DKK 19.3B) with net debt of DKK 95.9B. The primary driver was the Catalent fill-finish site acquisition (~$11B / ~DKK 75B upfront, financed mainly with debt) plus incremental borrowing to fund the broader capex programme. The company utilises a Euro Medium Term Note programme for bond issuances, with maturities stretching to 2034, providing a well-laddered maturity profile. Debt/Equity stands at 67.5%, up from 25% in FY2023 but moderate by global pharma standards. The absolute debt level, while materially higher than historical, remains proportionate to the company's earnings power.

**Interest coverage and debt serviceability.** Interest expense jumped from DKK 542M (FY2023) to DKK 4.2B (FY2025), reflecting the new debt load and higher rate environment. Interest coverage at 32x (EBIT/interest) remains extremely robust -- this is a company generating DKK 128B in operating income against DKK 4.2B in interest costs. Even using the more conservative FCF-based coverage (FY2025 FCF of DKK 29B / interest of DKK 4.2B = 6.9x), debt service is comfortable, and this ratio will improve as capex moderates. The 2026 guidance for net financial items of DKK 2.3B gain indicates that currency hedging gains (primarily USD/DKK) more than offset the incremental interest burden, providing a near-term buffer. Novo Nordisk maintains strong investment-grade credit ratings, and the bond programme's terms reflect this creditworthiness.

**Liquidity position: adequate but not abundant.** Cash and short-term investments of DKK 27B against current liabilities of DKK 215.7B yield a current ratio of 0.8x. Working capital is negative at -DKK 43.2B. In isolation, these figures might appear concerning, but they are typical for large pharmaceutical companies that benefit from extended supplier payment terms and collect receivables faster than they pay vendors. The negative working capital is a source of cash, not a drain. That said, with DKK 19.3B in short-term debt and ongoing capex of DKK 55B+ in 2026, the company will need continued access to capital markets or operating cash flow to meet near-term obligations. Given DKK 119B in annual operating cash flow, this is not a meaningful risk.

**Refinancing risk.** The Euro Medium Term Note programme with maturities through 2034 suggests a well-distributed maturity schedule without a dangerous concentration in any single year. Long-term debt of DKK 111.7B vs short-term of DKK 19.3B indicates the company has termed out the majority of its borrowings. Refinancing risk is low: even if credit markets tightened materially, Novo Nordisk's earnings stability, investment-grade rating, and essential-product franchise would ensure access to capital. The bigger question is whether the company adds further debt -- the capex programme still requires DKK 55B in 2026, and if FCF remains constrained (say DKK 40-50B), incremental borrowing may be needed. However, management has signalled declining capex from 2026, which should shift the company to deleveraging mode.

**Off-balance-sheet obligations and contingencies.** Novo Nordisk faces the standard pharmaceutical contingencies: patent litigation (particularly around semaglutide, its core molecule), regulatory commitments, and long-term supply agreements. The MFN agreement in the US effectively commits the company to lower pricing, which is an off-balance-sheet economic obligation that will compress future cash flows. Operating leases and pension obligations exist but are not material relative to the company's scale. There are no disclosed off-balance-sheet vehicles or material guarantees that would alter the leverage picture.

**Stress test: 30% revenue decline for 2 years.** If revenue dropped 30% from FY2025's DKK 309B to ~DKK 216B and stayed there for two years: Gross profit at 81% margin would be ~DKK 175B. After operating expenses (assume DKK 125B, reduced from ~DKK 180B through cuts to discretionary R&D and commercial spend), operating income would be ~DKK 50B. After tax (~DKK 12B) and interest (~DKK 5B), net income would be ~DKK 33B. Operating cash flow would likely be DKK 50-60B (adding back depreciation, reducing working capital). Against total debt of DKK 131B (with DKK 19B short-term), the company could cover all debt service and maintenance capex (DKK 22B) from operating cash flow alone. It would need to slash growth capex and buybacks, but survival is not in question. Novo Nordisk would not need an equity raise even under severe stress. The business model -- high gross margins, recurring revenue from chronic disease treatment, essential medicines -- provides strong downside protection.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Leverage is modest (0.6x net debt/EBITDA) relative to earnings power, the debt was deployed into productive manufacturing assets, and declining capex from 2026 should enable rapid deleveraging while maintaining the dividend.
- **Bear case:** Debt increased 5x in two years during a period where the revenue outlook is now negative; if the GLP-1 franchise economics deteriorate structurally, the company will be carrying elevated debt and a larger fixed-cost base into a downturn.
- **Confidence:** High -- Even under adversarial assumptions, Novo Nordisk's balance sheet provides ample safety margin. The debt increase is material but proportionate, and the company's earnings power dwarfs its obligations.

## Red Flags
- Total debt increased 5x in 2 years (DKK 27B to DKK 131B); while manageable at current earnings, this represents a permanent increase in financial risk vs the near-zero-debt historical baseline
- Interest expense up 8x from FY2023 to FY2025 (DKK 0.5B to DKK 4.2B); further increases possible if additional borrowing is needed
- Current ratio below 1.0x (0.8x) with negative working capital; normal for pharma but leaves limited buffer against short-term liquidity shocks
- Buyback programme essentially suspended (DKK 1.4B in FY2025 vs DKK 30B in FY2023), signalling management prioritisation of capex over shareholder returns
- If 2026 revenue declines materialise at the worse end of guidance (-13% CER), leverage ratios will deteriorate before they improve

## Score: 7 / 10
Novo Nordisk's balance sheet remains conservatively leveraged by any absolute standard -- 0.6x net debt/EBITDA, 32x interest coverage, and ample cash generation even under stress. The score reflects the rapid pace of debt accumulation (5x in 2 years) coinciding with the first negative revenue outlook in a decade, which introduces timing risk around the deleveraging path. A fortress balance sheet (9-10) this is no longer, but it remains solidly investment-grade and far from distress.
