## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Novo Nordisk sells GLP-1 injectable and oral drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy) for diabetes and obesity — chronic conditions requiring lifelong treatment that drive 90%+ of revenue from a single molecule family (semaglutide).
2. **Moat**: A century of diabetes expertise, 81% gross margins, and massive biologic manufacturing scale (DKK 90B capex in FY2025 across 14 fill-finish sites) create barriers competitors need 3-5 years to replicate, though Eli Lilly has taken the U.S. prescription lead at 58% share with clinically superior tirzepatide.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of 36% and owner earnings of DKK 102B confirm elite economics even in a heavy investment year, but FCF conversion collapsed to 28% due to a capex supercycle and 2026 guidance projects the first revenue decline (-5% to -13%) in over a decade.
4. **Management**: The Novo Nordisk Foundation forced out the CEO in August 2025 after a 55% stock decline, the board chairman also departed, insider ownership is zero, and no executive bought shares during the drawdown — this is a leadership team in transition without skin in the game.
5. **Debt risk**: Debt quintupled to DKK 131B but sits at just 0.8x EBITDA with 32x interest coverage, and even a 30% revenue decline for two years would leave DKK 50-60B in operating cash flow — survival is not in question.
6. **Price**: At DKK 239 (10.4x trailing earnings), the market must believe revenue will decline permanently and margins will compress below 30% — a scenario inconsistent with an 81% gross margin business in a $100B+ market growing to $150B+ by 2030.
7. **Margin of safety**: The stress-tested bear IV of DKK 350 provides 31.7% price MOS, the downside/upside asymmetry is roughly 1:4 (27% downside to DKK 175 vs 109% upside to DKK 500 base case), and no Monte Carlo simulation out of 10,000 produced an IV below the current price.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Novo's GLP-1 market share drops below 25% (currently 39%), signaling permanent competitive defeat to Lilly, or if an FDA black box warning emerges on the GLP-1 drug class.

## Key Risks

1. **Eli Lilly's sustained efficacy advantage.** Tirzepatide delivers ~20% weight loss vs semaglutide's ~14%. If this gap persists and payers shift formularies, Novo could lose another 10-15 percentage points of market share, compressing margins 5-8 points. This is the single most consequential risk.

2. **CagriSema pipeline failure.** The REDEFINE 4 trial showed 23% vs 25.5% weight loss versus tirzepatide. If higher-dose trials (H2 2026) also fail to differentiate, Novo has no competitive answer for 3-4 years, collapsing the pipeline narrative and likely the multiple.

3. **GLP-1 pricing collapse.** Medicare/Medicaid negotiations, IRA price controls, compounding pharmacy proliferation, and emerging market generics could compress net pricing 30-40% over 3-5 years. Volume growth may partially offset, but margin impact would be material.

4. **Manufacturing overinvestment.** DKK 90B/year in capex builds capacity for demand that may not materialize at expected volumes or prices. If demand disappoints, these are sunk costs depressing ROIC for years.

5. **Long-term GLP-1 safety signal.** The entire class has been widely used at obesity doses for less than 5 years. A cardiovascular, cancer, or thyroid signal in long-term data would be catastrophic and un-hedgeable.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) CagriSema higher-dose trials deliver 25%+ weight loss, fundamentally changing the competitive narrative; (2) 2026 actual revenue comes in at the better end of guidance (-5% vs -13%), signaling the trough is shallower than feared; (3) stock falls below DKK 200, widening the margin of safety further on an intact thesis.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) GLP-1 market share drops below 25% (currently 39%), indicating permanent competitive defeat; (2) FDA black box warning or class-wide safety concern for GLP-1 drugs; (3) Stock exceeds DKK 500 (base case IV), begin trimming — sell half above DKK 650.

## Detailed Analysis

Novo Nordisk sells injectable and oral medications for diabetes and obesity, primarily under the brands Ozempic (diabetes), Wegovy (obesity), and Rybelsus (oral diabetes). The company also has a smaller but meaningful rare disease segment (haemophilia, growth disorders). Revenue is earned through pharmaceutical distribution channels — wholesalers, pharmacy benefit managers, and increasingly direct government programs. The business model is straightforward: develop patented biologic drugs, manufacture at scale, and sell at premium prices into chronic disease markets where patients need ongoing treatment.

The key revenue drivers are (1) GLP-1 prescription volume, which continues to grow as obesity treatment becomes more mainstream and payer coverage expands; (2) pricing, which is under severe downward pressure in the U.S. from government negotiation (the TrumpRx program has set cash-pay prices at $349-350/month, down from roughly $1,000+); (3) geographic expansion, where Novo holds 68% GLP-1 volume share in international markets versus roughly 42% in the U.S.; and (4) pipeline conversion, particularly CagriSema (regulatory submission planned for 2026) and amycretin (entering Phase 3 in 2026).

Revenue predictability is high on the diabetes side — patients require chronic medication indefinitely, and switching costs are meaningful (doctor supervision, titration schedules, side-effect familiarity). The obesity side is less predictable: treatment duration is uncertain, payer coverage varies, and the market is still maturing. That said, the obesity opportunity is enormous — an estimated 800 million people globally are obese, and penetration remains in low single digits.

Customer concentration is low at the patient level (millions of individual patients globally), but there is meaningful payer concentration: the top three U.S. PBMs (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) control formulary access and wield significant pricing leverage. Government payers (Medicare, Medicaid, European national health systems) are an increasingly large share of the customer base, adding political risk but also volume stability.

This is a business anyone can explain in two minutes: Novo makes GLP-1 drugs that help people manage diabetes and lose weight. They sell globally, with the U.S. as the largest profit pool. The company is essentially a one-molecule franchise (semaglutide) scaling into the largest chronic disease markets in the world.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The GLP-1 market is the largest new drug category in a generation, Novo is the co-leader with decades of diabetes expertise, and the business is fundamentally simple to understand.
- **Bear case:** Extreme product concentration in semaglutide means a single clinical, competitive, or regulatory setback could impair the majority of revenue.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model, revenue drivers, and competitive dynamics are transparent and well-documented.

## Red Flags
- Over 90% revenue concentration in a single molecule family (semaglutide/liraglutide)
- U.S. pricing environment has shifted dramatically — 70% price cuts are structural, not temporary
- Generic semaglutide has launched in India, with patent expirations approaching in other major markets (Canada, Brazil, China)
- Compounding pharmacies are selling copycat versions at lower prices, partially eroding branded demand

## Score: 9 / 10
Novo Nordisk is one of the most understandable businesses in global pharma — a pure-play GLP-1 franchise with transparent economics, clear revenue drivers, and a product anyone can explain. The only complexity is navigating the rapidly shifting pricing and competitive landscape, but the business itself is crystal clear.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — NOVO-B.CO

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), quant-valuation model, Novo Nordisk investor relations, CNBC, BioPharma Dive, Fierce Pharma, pharmaphorum, Grand View Research, web search for GLP-1 competitive landscape

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Gross margins of 81% (FY2025) and ROIC of 36% confirm strong pricing power, though both are declining from peak levels (84.7% gross margin, 70.5% ROIC in FY2023) | 5 |
| 2 | Eli Lilly's tirzepatide has overtaken semaglutide in U.S. prescriptions (58% vs 42%), demonstrating the moat is narrower than perceived two years ago | 5 |
| 3 | CagriSema failed to demonstrate non-inferiority to tirzepatide on weight loss (23% vs 25.5%), undermining the next-generation competitive narrative | 5 |
| 4 | Novo holds 68% GLP-1 volume share internationally and retains a 100-year legacy in diabetes care, providing deep physician relationships and regulatory expertise | 4 |
| 5 | Massive manufacturing investment ($11B Catalent sites + $9B in 2025 capex) creates a supply-side advantage that is difficult to replicate quickly | 4 |
| 6 | Patent expirations are approaching: generics already launched in India, with Canada, Brazil, and China following — the exclusivity window is narrowing | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

Novo Nordisk's moat rests on several interlocking advantages: (1) regulatory and clinical barriers to entry in biologics, (2) century-long brand trust in diabetes care, (3) massive manufacturing scale for peptide drugs, and (4) switching costs embedded in physician prescribing habits and patient titration protocols. For decades, Novo was the undisputed leader in insulin and GLP-1 therapy. The question now is whether this moat is durable or eroding.

The evidence is mixed. On the positive side, gross margins remain extraordinary at 81%, and even after significant price cuts, the company generated DKK 127.7B in operating income on DKK 309B revenue. ROIC at 36% is well above cost of capital, confirming economic value creation. Novo's international dominance (68% GLP-1 volume share) reflects deep institutional relationships with healthcare systems worldwide. The manufacturing moat is real — biologics are inherently harder to manufacture than small molecules, and Novo's DKK 90.1B capex in FY2025 (acquiring three Catalent fill-finish sites and expanding facilities in Denmark, France, the U.S., Brazil, and China) creates production capacity that would take competitors 3-5 years to replicate.

However, the moat has visibly narrowed. Eli Lilly's tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) has proven clinically superior on weight loss endpoints and has captured the majority of U.S. GLP-1 prescriptions. The CagriSema Phase 3 disappointment in February 2026 — failing to match tirzepatide's 25.5% weight loss — was a significant blow to the narrative that Novo's next-generation pipeline would recapture superiority. Meanwhile, the regulatory moat is weakening: Indian generics have launched at 80% discounts, compounding pharmacies are selling semaglutide copies in the U.S., and patents in multiple major markets expire within the next 5-6 years.

The brand advantage in diabetes remains formidable — Novo has been the trusted name in diabetes care for a century, and physicians are slow to change prescribing habits for chronic conditions. But in the newer, faster-moving obesity market, brand loyalty is weaker: patients and payers are more price-sensitive, and Lilly's superior efficacy data gives it a clear clinical advantage. The switching cost moat is also being tested by government price-setting (TrumpRx program) and PBM formulary decisions that can redirect millions of prescriptions overnight.

Looking 10 years out, the moat's durability hinges on two factors: (1) whether amycretin (entering Phase 3, targeting both GLP-1 and amylin pathways) can deliver differentiated efficacy, and (2) whether Novo's manufacturing scale and international relationships can sustain volume growth even as pricing compresses. The obesity market is so large (projected to reach $100B+ by 2030) that even a diminished share in a growing market can support strong economics. But Novo is no longer the clear category leader — it is now one of two dominant players, and arguably the weaker one on current clinical data.

The weakest point in the moat is clinical differentiation. In pharma, the ultimate moat is having the best drug. Lilly currently has the better weight loss data, and Novo's attempt to leapfrog with CagriSema fell short. The pipeline (amycretin, oral formulations) offers hope but not certainty.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A century of diabetes expertise, 81% gross margins, massive manufacturing scale, 68% international market share, and a deep pipeline (amycretin Phase 3) give Novo enduring competitive advantages in the world's largest chronic disease markets.
- **Bear case:** Eli Lilly has taken the clinical and market share lead in obesity, CagriSema disappointed, generics are arriving, and government pricing is compressing the value of the franchise — the moat may be narrower and less durable than the historical financials suggest.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the moat is real but measurably eroding; the competitive dynamics are shifting faster than in any prior period in Novo's history.

## Red Flags
- U.S. market share has dropped from dominant to minority position (42%) versus Eli Lilly (58%)
- CagriSema failed to match tirzepatide on the primary weight loss endpoint
- ROIC has halved from 70.5% (FY2023) to 36.0% (FY2025), partly due to massive capex but also reflecting margin compression
- Generic semaglutide is already on the market in India at 80% discounts; more markets to follow
- Compounding pharmacies represent an unregulated competitive threat in the U.S.
- Gross margin has declined from 84.7% to 81.0% in two years — early sign of pricing pressure flowing through

## Score: 7 / 10
Novo Nordisk has a clear moat — 81% gross margins, century-long brand trust, and manufacturing scale are not easily replicated. But the moat has narrowed meaningfully: Eli Lilly has taken the clinical and prescription share lead in the largest growth market (obesity), generics are arriving, and pricing power is compressing under government pressure. This is a strong moat under real competitive stress, not a wide and unchallenged one.

# Management & Capital Allocation — NOVO-B.CO

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), Novo Nordisk investor relations, CNBC, BioPharma Dive, Nasdaq, PharmExec, Simply Wall St, Novo Nordisk Remuneration Report 2025, web search for governance and insider activity

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | CEO Lars Fruergaard Jorgensen was replaced in August 2025 by Maziar Mike Doustdar amid stock decline and pressure from the Novo Nordisk Foundation — a significant governance event | 5 |

| 5 | Debt increased 5x from DKK 25.8B (FY2022) to DKK 131.0B (FY2025), funding the $11B Catalent site acquisition and massive capacity buildout | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

Novo Nordisk is undergoing its most significant leadership and capital allocation transition in decades. CEO Lars Fruergaard Jorgensen, who led the company from 2017 through the semaglutide boom, stepped down in August 2025 under pressure from the Novo Nordisk Foundation. His replacement, Maziar Mike Doustdar, previously served as EVP of International Operations — the division where Novo holds 68% GLP-1 market share. The transition was not a smooth planned succession; it was driven by the Foundation's dissatisfaction with the stock's 55% decline from its peak and a disagreement with the board over the pace of leadership renewal. The board chairman also departed in an October 2025 shakeup, and an Extraordinary General Meeting in November 2025 elected new board members aligned with the Foundation's vision. This degree of governance turmoil is unusual for a company of Novo's stature and signals genuine strategic disagreement at the top.

The Foundation structure is a double-edged sword. The Novo Nordisk Foundation — one of the world's wealthiest charitable foundations — holds all A shares, giving it majority voting control. This insulates the company from short-term activist pressure and ensures a long-term orientation. However, it also means minority B-share holders (the publicly traded shares) have limited governance influence. The Foundation's willingness to force out the CEO and reshape the board demonstrates it is an active, interventionist owner — arguably a positive for accountability, but it creates uncertainty about strategic direction during transitions.

Insider ownership is effectively zero. Tracking sources show 0.00% insider holdings, and multiple executives (including departing CEO Jorgensen) sold shares in 2025 — Jorgensen disposed of ~34,000 shares at $86-87 (equivalent to roughly DKK 600) in February 2025, well before the stock's worst decline. No executive was a notable buyer as the stock fell from DKK 530+ to DKK 225. The absence of insider buying at a 55% drawdown is a negative signal — management is not putting personal capital at risk alongside shareholders. Compensation is structured with performance shares and short-term bonuses tied to financial metrics, but without meaningful personal ownership, the alignment is contractual rather than intrinsic.

Capital allocation has undergone a dramatic shift. From FY2022 to FY2025, the company pivoted from a capital-light, shareholder-return-focused model to an aggressive capacity-building mode. Capex exploded from DKK 14.8B to DKK 90.1B — a 6x increase in three years. This funded the $11B acquisition of three Catalent fill-finish sites, a $4.1B new facility in Clayton, North Carolina, a $2.2B upgrade in Chartres, France, and a $6B expansion of the Kalundborg, Denmark campus. Simultaneously, buybacks collapsed: DKK 29.9B (FY2023) to DKK 20.2B (FY2024) to just DKK 1.4B (FY2025). The dividend yield has risen to 6.7% largely because the stock price has fallen, not because of increased payouts. Total debt has increased 5x to DKK 131B, though at 0.8x Debt/EBITDA and 32x interest coverage, the balance sheet remains conservatively leveraged.

The capital allocation question is whether this massive manufacturing bet is visionary or poorly timed. The bull case: GLP-1 demand will be supply-constrained for years, and owning 14 fill-finish sites globally creates a structural advantage that Lilly and smaller competitors cannot easily match. The bear case: Novo is investing at peak multiples into capacity for a drug (semaglutide) facing generic competition within 5-6 years, pricing compression now, and clinical inferiority to the leading competitor's molecule. The FCF margin collapse from 36% (FY2022) to 9.4% (FY2025) reflects this investment cycle — it is intentional, not a sign of business deterioration, but the return on this capital is far from guaranteed.

Management communication has been mixed. The company has been forthright about competitive challenges and pricing headwinds, providing specific 2026 guidance (5-13% sales decline). However, the CagriSema spin — positioning a drug that failed to match tirzepatide as still "clinically meaningful" — felt more like politician-speak than owner-candor. The new CEO has had limited public track record in the role, making it difficult to assess his strategic vision and communication style.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Foundation oversight ensures long-term thinking, the manufacturing buildout could prove visionary if GLP-1 demand scales as expected, and new leadership may bring fresh strategic clarity at a time when the stock is deeply discounted.
- **Bear case:** Zero insider ownership, a forced CEO transition, board-level governance turmoil, and a massive capex bet funded by 5x debt growth during a period of eroding competitive position suggest management is spending aggressively into an uncertain future without personal skin in the game.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the leadership transition is too recent to evaluate the new CEO's caliber, and the capital allocation shift is a high-conviction bet whose outcome will not be clear for 2-3 years.

## Red Flags
- CEO forced out by Foundation after 55% stock decline — not a planned succession
- Board chairman also departed in October 2025; new board elected at EGM in November 2025
- Zero meaningful insider ownership; executives were net sellers throughout the decline
- Capex surged 6x in three years (DKK 14.8B to DKK 90.1B) while buybacks collapsed 95% (DKK 29.9B to DKK 1.4B)
- Total debt increased 5x from DKK 25.8B to DKK 131.0B to fund capacity buildout
- New CEO Maziar Mike Doustdar has limited public track record in the top role
- FCF margin collapsed from 36% to 9.4% — intentional but high-stakes

## Score: 5 / 10
Management and governance are in transition. The Foundation provides long-term alignment but forced out the CEO and reshaped the board amid genuine strategic disagreement. Zero insider ownership is a persistent negative. The massive capex pivot is either a bold and correct bet on GLP-1 demand or a poorly timed investment at peak cycle. Until the new leadership team establishes a track record and the returns on the manufacturing buildout become visible, this is an average stewardship score reflecting real uncertainty.

# Business Economics -- NOVO-B.CO

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Novo Nordisk Annual Report 2025, Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), quant-valuation model output, Novo Nordisk 2026 outlook press release (Feb 2026), GlobeNewsWire filings, MacroTrends historical data

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC declined sharply from 70.5% (FY2023) to 36.0% (FY2025) as invested capital more than doubled due to massive manufacturing buildout and the Catalent acquisition | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margin compressed 370 bps YoY to 81.0% in FY2025 due to Catalent depreciation/amortisation, restructuring charges (~DKK 3B one-off), and capacity ramp costs | 4 |
| 3 | FCF conversion collapsed from 84% (FY2023) to 28% (FY2025) as capex surged to DKK 90.1B; however, ~76% of capex is growth-related, not maintenance | 5 |
| 4 | Adjusted owner earnings (subtracting only maintenance capex) remain robust at DKK 102.4B in FY2025, essentially equal to net income, confirming the asset-light nature of the core business | 4 |
| 5 | Revenue compounded at 20%+ CAGR over FY2022-FY2025 (DKK 177B to 309B) driven by GLP-1 franchise, but 2026 guidance projects -5% to -13% adjusted sales decline at CER due to MFN pricing, semaglutide LOE in some markets, and competitive pressure | 5 |
| 6 | R&D intensity at 16.8% of revenue (DKK 52B) reflects heavy investment in next-generation pipeline; operating leverage historically strong but now partially offset by capacity build costs | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital: elite but normalising.** Novo Nordisk's ROIC trajectory tells a story of two phases. Through FY2023, returns were extraordinary -- 70.5% ROIC, 88% ROE -- reflecting a capital-light biological drug business scaling into massive GLP-1 demand. The invested capital base was modest (DKK 128B in FY2023) relative to earnings power. Starting in FY2024 and accelerating into FY2025, the company embarked on a once-in-a-generation manufacturing expansion. Invested capital ballooned to DKK 316B, including the ~$11B (DKK ~75B) Catalent fill-finish site acquisition. The resulting FY2025 ROIC of 36% is still exceptional by any industrial standard -- it would rank in the top decile of global large-caps -- but the downward trajectory reflects the denominator expanding faster than earnings. If this capex cycle peaks as guided (DKK 55B in 2026, declining thereafter), ROIC should stabilise and eventually recover as the new capacity generates revenue.

**Margin structure: temporarily pressured but structurally strong.** Gross margins of 81% remain outstanding for a pharmaceutical company and reflect the pricing power of patent-protected biological therapies. The 370 bps YoY decline is largely explainable by one-off items: ~DKK 3B in restructuring charges and production asset impairments, plus Catalent-related depreciation hitting COGS for the first time. Operating margin compressed from 44.2% to 41.3%, with the incremental drag from R&D spend (up DKK 3.9B YoY) and capacity ramp costs. Looking ahead, the 2026 outlook is concerning: the MFN agreement in the US and semaglutide LOE in certain international markets will pressure pricing, while competition from Eli Lilly (tirzepatide) and emerging oral GLP-1 entrants intensifies. Management is guiding for operating profit decline of 5-13% at CER -- a meaningful reversal from years of 20%+ growth. The structural margin question is whether Novo can defend 40%+ operating margins through a period of lower pricing and higher depreciation from its expanded asset base.

**Cash generation: capex trough obscures underlying strength.** The headline FCF figure of DKK 29B (9.4% FCF margin) is misleading in isolation. Operating cash flow remained robust at DKK 119B, essentially flat YoY despite the margin compression. The FCF collapse is entirely a function of capex: DKK 90.1B in FY2025 vs DKK 14.8B just three years prior. The quant model's separation of maintenance vs growth capex is instructive -- maintenance capex is only DKK 22B (the D&A proxy), meaning adjusted owner earnings are DKK 102.4B, fully in line with net income. This confirms that the core business remains a cash machine; the temporary FCF weakness is a strategic choice to build manufacturing capacity for the next decade. Capex is guided to moderate to DKK 55B in 2026 and decline further, which should restore FCF to DKK 60B+ within 2-3 years assuming stable operating cash flow.

**Revenue quality and operating leverage.** Novo Nordisk's revenue is highly recurring in nature. Diabetes therapies (insulin, GLP-1 for T2D) serve chronic conditions requiring lifelong treatment -- effectively a subscription model. The obesity franchise (Wegovy) is newer but exhibits strong refill persistence once patients are on therapy. Revenue grew at a 20%+ CAGR from FY2022-FY2025, driven by GLP-1 volume and pricing. However, the 2026 guidance (-5% to -13% CER) marks a stark inflection. The MFN agreement directly compresses US pricing, while semaglutide loss of exclusivity in certain international markets opens the door to biosimilar competition. Operating leverage, which amplified margins on the way up (operating margin expanded from 42% to 44% over FY2022-FY2024), will work in reverse if revenue contracts while the fixed-cost base is larger due to the manufacturing expansion. The company is partially offsetting this through a "company-wide transformation" (cost optimisation programme), but the near-term earnings trajectory is clearly negative.

**Capital intensity: transitional surge, not structural shift.** The capex-to-revenue ratio spiked from 8% (FY2022) to 29% (FY2025), but this is a discrete investment cycle rather than a permanent change in the business model. Novo is building API manufacturing, fill-finish capacity, and packaging lines to meet GLP-1 demand that it simply could not serve from existing facilities. The Catalent acquisition added three large-scale sites. Once this cycle completes (management signals declining capex from 2026), the business should return to a more normalised 10-15% capex/revenue ratio, with the new capacity supporting materially higher volumes. The risk is that if demand disappoints (due to competition or pricing), the company will be stuck with underutilised capacity and elevated depreciation.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Core earnings power (DKK 102B+ owner earnings) is intact and temporarily obscured by a capex supercycle that will peak and decline, restoring FCF and eventually ROIC as new capacity fills with volume.
- **Bear case:** The 2026 revenue decline (-5% to -13% CER) may not be temporary if MFN pricing, biosimilar competition, and Medicaid coverage cuts structurally compress the GLP-1 franchise economics; negative operating leverage on a larger fixed-cost base could erode margins further.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The underlying business economics are clearly elite, but the FY2025-2026 transition creates genuine uncertainty about the pace of normalisation and whether peak margins are behind us.

## Red Flags
- FCF conversion collapsed to 28% in FY2025; even with capex moderating, it will take 2+ years to recover to historical levels
- 2026 guidance of -5% to -13% revenue and operating profit decline is the first negative growth outlook in over a decade
- Gross margin erosion (81% vs 85% prior) may not fully reverse if Catalent depreciation is structural and MFN pricing compresses realisations
- Buybacks slashed from DKK 30B (FY2023) to DKK 1.4B (FY2025) as cash is directed to capex -- shareholder returns temporarily impaired
