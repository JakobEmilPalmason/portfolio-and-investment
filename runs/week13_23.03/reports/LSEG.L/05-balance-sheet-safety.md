## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and structure.** At year-end 2025, LSEG carried total debt of approximately £11.7B (per Yahoo Finance data) with operating net debt of £8.2B. The company's own reporting cites operating net debt at £8.175B. The difference between gross and net debt reflects approximately £2.9B in cash and short-term investments. The debt is a mix of sterling and US dollar-denominated bonds at investment-grade rates. Notably, £1.359B in bonds came due in 2026, which LSEG proactively refinanced through a $3B (approximately £2.4B) US dollar bond sale in March 2026 across 3-year, 5-year, and 10-year tranches. This refinancing was well-received by the market and executed at competitive spreads, reflecting the company's strong credit profile. Long-term debt stood at £7.9B, with the remainder in shorter-dated facilities and commercial paper. The debt/equity ratio of 59.2% (Yahoo Finance) or 37.4% (Simply Wall St, depending on methodology) reflects different treatments of equity and clearing-related balances. On any measure, leverage is moderate for an infrastructure business with this earnings stability.

**Interest coverage.** Reported EBIT/interest coverage improved materially to 7.1x in FY2025 from 4.7x in FY2024, driven by the recovery in operating income from £1.4B to £2.2B. On an adjusted operating profit basis (£3.5B), coverage is approximately 9.6x -- comfortably above any stress threshold. The improvement in FY2025 partly reflects the lapping of Refinitiv integration charges that depressed FY2024 operating income. Average cost of debt appears to be in the 2.5-3.0% range, which is attractive for an A-rated borrower and well below LSEG's return on its operating assets. Interest expense has been relatively stable at £324M-339M annually despite rising gross debt, suggesting good treasury management and staggered maturities that limited refinancing at peak rates.

**Liquidity.** LSEG held £2.9B in cash and short-term investments at year-end 2025. The current ratio of 1.0x looks tight on paper but is meaningless as a measure of LSEG's liquidity because the balance sheet is dominated by clearing house pass-through assets and liabilities (approximately £763B on each side). Stripping out CCP-related balances, LSEG's operating liquidity is ample: £2.4B of annual equity free cash flow, £2.9B of cash on hand, and access to revolving credit facilities and commercial paper programs backed by an A3 credit rating. The March 2026 bond issuance demonstrated that capital markets remain wide open for LSEG, even in a period of elevated market uncertainty.

**The clearing house balance sheet distortion.** This is the single most important thing to understand about LSEG's balance sheet. Total reported assets are approximately £797B, but the vast majority -- over £760B -- consists of clearing member margin deposits, default fund contributions, and associated receivables/payables held by LCH (LSEG's clearing house subsidiary). These assets are matched by corresponding liabilities; they are client money that passes through the CCP. LSEG bears no economic risk on these balances (beyond the remote tail risk of a clearing member default that exhausts the waterfall). The result is that standard balance sheet ratios (ROA, current ratio, debt/total assets) are all meaningless unless adjusted for CCP pass-throughs. LSEG's "own" balance sheet -- excluding clearing member assets -- is approximately £30-35B, consisting primarily of goodwill/intangibles from Refinitiv, operating assets, and cash. This is the relevant balance sheet for credit analysis.

**Refinancing risk and maturity schedule.** With £1.359B maturing in 2026 already addressed through the March bond sale, near-term refinancing risk is minimal. The debt portfolio appears well-laddered across multiple years and currencies (GBP and USD), reducing concentration risk around any single maturity date. The A3 rating and stable outlook from Moody's, combined with the strong March 2026 execution, suggest LSEG faces no meaningful refinancing risk in the medium term. The primary risk would be a multi-notch downgrade scenario, which appears remote given improving earnings trajectory and declining leverage. Management has committed to maintaining leverage within the 1.5-2.5x target range, which provides a clear framework for balancing shareholder returns with balance sheet discipline.

**Stress test: 30% revenue drop sustained for 2 years.** This is an extreme but instructive scenario. LSEG's FY2025 total income was approximately £9.0B. A 30% decline would reduce revenue to approximately £6.3B. With approximately 60% subscription revenue (highly sticky, with 90%+ retention rates even in downturns), a 30% revenue drop would require near-complete collapse of transactional and market-linked revenues. In the 2008-2009 financial crisis, exchange and data businesses typically saw 10-20% revenue declines, not 30%. Nevertheless, in such a scenario: (a) EBITDA at a compressed 35% margin on £6.3B revenue would yield approximately £2.2B -- still sufficient to cover £340M of interest expense 6.5x over; (b) capex could be cut to maintenance levels of perhaps £400-500M; (c) the dividend could be suspended (saves ~£750M); (d) buybacks would obviously cease. Under this severe stress, LSEG would remain solvent and continue to generate positive free cash flow, though leverage would temporarily spike above the target range. The subscription revenue floor is the key safety valve -- it prevents the kind of revenue collapse that would threaten debt covenants.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Investment-grade rated with 1.8x leverage, strong capital market access, and a subscription revenue base that provides a durable cash flow floor even in severe downturns.
- **Bear case:** Absolute debt levels are high at £11.7B gross, and the large clearing house balance sheet creates analytical complexity that could mask emerging risks; any CCP loss event would be highly disruptive even if remote.
- **Confidence:** High -- leverage ratios, credit rating, and refinancing activity are all well-documented in public filings; the clearing house balance sheet distortion is well-understood by institutional investors but may confuse retail analysis.

## Red Flags
- Debt/equity ratio rose from 33.9% (FY2022) to 59.2% (FY2025), driven partly by aggressive buybacks reducing equity; this trajectory warrants monitoring
- Working capital collapsed from £1.5B in FY2024 to £55M in FY2025 (per Yahoo Finance) -- likely a CCP timing effect on clearing balances, but deserves investigation
- The £760B+ of CCP assets/liabilities, while pass-through in normal times, represent systemic risk exposure in a tail event (clearing member default cascade); LCH is systemically important and subject to rigorous regulatory oversight, but the risk is non-zero
- Net debt increased from £6.2B (FY2022) to £8.3B (FY2025), partly funding £4.9B of cumulative buybacks; the company is choosing shareholder returns over deleveraging
- S&P and Fitch ratings were not confirmed in this research; only Moody's A3 verified

## Score: 7 / 10
LSEG's balance sheet is solid for an investment-grade financial infrastructure company: 1.8x net leverage, 7-10x interest coverage, ample liquidity, and strong capital market access. The score is held back from 8 by rising absolute debt levels, the complexity of the CCP-inflated balance sheet, and the choice to lever up via buybacks rather than deleverage. The business could comfortably survive a severe downturn, but the rising debt/equity trajectory deserves attention.
