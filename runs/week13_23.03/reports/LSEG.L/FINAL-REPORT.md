## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: LSEG sells financial data subscriptions, index licensing, and clearing/trading infrastructure to thousands of global financial institutions, with ~70% recurring revenue and 90%+ retention rates that make cancellation operationally painful.
2. **Moat**: LCH clears >90% of global interest rate swaps (a regulatory-mandated near-monopoly), FTSE Russell indices are embedded in trillions of passive AUM, and 33+ petabytes of proprietary data create switching costs that no competitor can replicate quickly.
3. **Economics**: 88% gross margins, 50.3% EBITDA margins expanding 150bps/year, and 2x operating leverage (7.1% revenue growth converting to 15.7% EPS growth) confirm this is a capital-light compounder generating GBP 2.4B in equity free cash flow.
4. **Management**: Schwimmer delivered Refinitiv synergies ahead of target and compounded EPS at 15%+, but owns only 0.019% of shares, took a 46% pay raise that triggered a shareholder revolt, and accelerated buybacks to GBP 3B under Elliott activist pressure.
5. **Debt risk**: At 1.8x net debt/EBITDA with A3 credit rating and 9.6x adjusted interest coverage, LSEG would remain solvent and FCF-positive even in a 30% revenue decline scenario, though rising absolute debt (GBP 8.3B net) funding buybacks deserves monitoring.
6. **Price**: At 8,246 GBp (15.5x forward P/E vs peers at ~28x), the market must believe organic growth permanently stalls below 5% and margins stop expanding -- assumptions contradicted by every segment growing and EBITDA margins hitting record highs.
7. **Margin of safety**: The 13% price discount to bear-case IV of 7,200 GBp is thin by Buffett standards, but the business-level protection is exceptional -- 70% recurring revenue, deep switching costs, and 88% gross margins mean earnings hold up even in severe downturns.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if organic revenue growth turns negative for two consecutive quarters, signaling that AI disintermediation or structural data commoditization is destroying the franchise rather than the temporary Q4 2026 revenue warning.

## Key Risks

1. **Q4 FY2026 revenue warning (issued 24 March 2026):** Magnitude undisclosed. If this signals structural deceleration rather than a one-off, growth assumptions and the valuation case weaken materially.
2. **AI disintermediation:** LLMs and alternative data providers could erode the value of LSEG's traditional terminal/data feed business if clients can extract comparable insights from cheaper AI-powered tools. The Microsoft partnership is the right strategic response, but the risk is real.
3. **Rising leverage funding buybacks:** Net debt increased from GBP 6.2B to GBP 8.3B (FY2022-2025) while total shareholder returns exceeded FCF in FY2025 -- the company is choosing returns over deleveraging under activist pressure.
4. **Regulatory risk:** FCA investigation into low-latency connectivity pricing could set precedent for broader data pricing scrutiny. EU data sovereignty mandates could fragment the global data business.
5. **Bloomberg competitive threat:** Workspace terminal still trails Bloomberg in front-office mindshare; the core Data & Analytics division grew only 5% vs high-single-digit growth elsewhere.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) Q4 revenue shortfall explained as transitory with Q1 FY2027 confirming 6%+ organic growth; (2) Stock pulls back toward 7,500 GBp or below, increasing MOS to 20%+; (3) EBITDA margin expansion continues on track toward 52-53% in FY2027.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) Organic revenue growth turns negative for two consecutive quarters; (2) EBITDA margin contracts below 45%, signaling moat erosion; (3) EV/EBITDA exceeds 22x or stock surpasses 12,000 GBp (approaching bull-case IV).

# Circle of Competence -- LSEG.L

**Analyst Role:** Business Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** LSEG 2025 Preliminary Results (26 Feb 2026), LSEG Q3 2025 Trading Update, LSEG/Microsoft partnership announcements, Morningstar analyst notes, Yahoo Finance financials, LSEG investor relations materials, The TRADE news coverage

## Detailed Analysis

LSEG is a global financial markets infrastructure and data company. Following the transformative 2021 acquisition of Refinitiv (from Thomson Reuters/Blackstone), LSEG shifted from being primarily a stock exchange and clearing house operator to a business where the majority of revenue comes from selling financial data, analytics, indices, and risk intelligence to banks, asset managers, hedge funds, corporates, and other financial institutions. The business is best understood as four interlocking divisions.

**Data & Analytics** (~45% of revenue, ~GBP 4.0bn in FY2025) is the largest division and the former Refinitiv core. It sells real-time and reference data feeds, Workspace terminals (competing with Bloomberg Terminal), and analytics APIs. Growth was 5.0% organically in FY2025, with analytics accelerating to high single digits driven by API sales. Revenue here is overwhelmingly subscription-based with multi-year contracts and high retention rates. The key revenue drivers are terminal seats, data feed licenses, and API consumption.

**Markets** (~35% of revenue) encompasses capital markets (equities, fixed income trading venues), FX (FXall, Matching), OTC derivatives, and the Tradeweb stake. This division grew 8.9% in FY2025 with double-digit growth in FX, Tradeweb, and OTC derivatives. Revenue is a mix of transaction fees (volume-dependent) and connectivity/access fees (recurring). The LCH clearing house, the world's largest interest rate swap clearer, sits here and provides critical infrastructure with regulatory-mandated clearing requirements supporting volumes.

**FTSE Russell** (~10% of revenue, ~GBP 960m) is the index business. Revenue comes from asset-based fees tied to AUM tracking FTSE indices, plus licensing and subscription analytics. It grew 7.3% organically in FY2025. This is a high-margin, capital-light business that benefits from rising global AUM.

**Risk Intelligence** (~6% of revenue, ~GBP 570m) provides KYC, compliance screening, and financial crime data. It was the fastest-growing division at 11.7% organic growth in FY2025, benefiting from increasing regulatory compliance requirements globally.

The business model can be explained simply: LSEG collects, curates, and distributes financial data and operates the plumbing (exchanges, clearing houses, trading venues) through which financial markets function. Customers pay recurring subscriptions for data access and transaction fees for using the infrastructure. The combination means that roughly 70% of revenue is recurring and contractual, with the remainder driven by market activity levels (trading volumes, capital markets issuance).

Key revenue drivers are: (1) net new data subscriptions and ASV growth, (2) trading volumes across FX, derivatives, and equities, (3) global AUM levels (for FTSE Russell), and (4) regulatory tightening (for Risk Intelligence). Revenue predictability is high for the subscription base and moderate for the transaction-driven Markets segment. Customer concentration is low -- LSEG serves thousands of financial institutions globally, with the largest customers being major global banks that use multiple LSEG products simultaneously.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** LSEG is a subscription-heavy data utility for global finance with 70% recurring revenue, strong retention, and multiple secular growth tailwinds (AI data demand, regulatory compliance, index investing).
- **Bear case:** The Data & Analytics division (the largest) grows at only mid-single digits, and AI could commoditize some data distribution if LLMs reduce terminal demand.
- **Confidence:** High -- the business model is well-documented, the revenue mix is transparent, and the competitive dynamics are observable.

## Red Flags
- The Markets division (~35% of revenue) is partly transaction-dependent and can decline in low-volatility or risk-off periods
- Data & Analytics organic growth (5.0%) lags the other three divisions, raising questions about whether the core Refinitiv business is ex-growth
- The Workspace terminal product still trails Bloomberg Terminal in mindshare and feature depth among front-office users
- Currency complexity: reporting in GBP but significant USD and EUR revenue creates FX translation noise

## Score: 8 / 10
LSEG is a highly understandable financial infrastructure and data business with transparent revenue streams, predictable subscription economics, and clear competitive dynamics -- well within the circle of competence for anyone who follows financial services.

# Durable Competitive Advantage -- LSEG.L

**Analyst Role:** Business Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** LSEG 2025 Preliminary Results (26 Feb 2026), Morningstar wide moat analysis, Morningstar fair value notes (Feb-Mar 2026), Third Bridge LSEG growth strategy analysis, LSEG/Microsoft partnership materials, GuruFocus moat score, Yahoo Finance financials, The TRADE news coverage

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Morningstar assigns a Wide Moat rating; GuruFocus moat score of 8/10 -- driven by switching costs and intangible data assets | 5 |
| 2 | LCH clears >90% of global interest rate swaps -- a near-monopoly with regulatory-mandated clearing requirements | 5 |
| 3 | LSEG owns 33+ petabytes of proprietary financial data stretching back decades, much of it hard to replicate | 5 |
| 4 | Gross margins of 88% in FY2025 (up from 86% in FY2022) demonstrate pricing power and operating leverage | 4 |
| 5 | FTSE Russell indices are embedded in trillions of dollars of passive AUM -- switching would require index rebalancing across thousands of portfolios | 4 |
| 6 | AI disruption is the key moat risk: Morningstar sees LSEG data as an input to AI (positive), but the market sold off 10% in Feb 2026 on AI disintermediation fears | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

LSEG possesses multiple, overlapping moat sources. The strongest and most durable is **switching costs**. Financial institutions integrate LSEG data feeds, Workspace terminals, and trading connectivity deep into their technology stacks, risk models, compliance workflows, and reporting systems. Ripping out a Bloomberg or Refinitiv data feed is not a weekend project -- it requires months of IT work, revalidation of downstream models, and retraining of users. This is why customer retention rates in Data & Analytics are high and ASV growth compounds steadily. The same dynamic applies to LCH clearing: once a bank has its collateral, margin systems, and risk management built around LCH, switching to a rival clearinghouse is enormously costly and operationally risky.

The second moat source is **intangible assets** -- specifically, proprietary data. LSEG's datasets include decades of tick-by-tick pricing, corporate actions, ownership records, ESG scores, and alternative data. These are not freely available on the internet. As Morningstar argued in their February 2026 analysis, "concerns around AI fail to differentiate between easily accessible public financial data and hard-to-replicate proprietary datasets." The key distinction is that AI agents and LLMs need high-quality, structured, curated financial data as inputs -- which makes LSEG a supplier to the AI ecosystem, not a victim of it. The Microsoft partnership (MCP server for Copilot Studio) directly monetizes this dynamic.

The third moat source is **network effects and regulatory entrenchment** in the Markets division. LCH's dominance in interest rate swap clearing creates a liquidity network effect: the more participants clear through LCH, the more efficient netting becomes, reducing margin costs for everyone. Competitors would need to replicate this liquidity pool, which is nearly impossible when regulatory mandates channel volumes to established CCPs. Similarly, FTSE Russell indices benefit from an **ecosystem lock-in**: once an index becomes the benchmark for billions in passive assets, ETFs, and derivatives, switching to a rival index family creates enormous tracking error and operational disruption.

**Where is the moat weakest?** The Workspace terminal product (formerly Refinitiv Eikon) competes directly with Bloomberg Terminal in a market where Bloomberg's brand, data quality, and integrated messaging (IB chat) remain dominant. LSEG has been gaining share, but Bloomberg's moat in terminal distribution is formidable. Additionally, the Data & Analytics division's 5% organic growth -- while solid -- trails the faster-growing divisions, suggesting that the core data subscription business faces competitive pressure from both Bloomberg and newer fintech data providers. The AI disruption question is legitimate: if LLMs become capable of aggregating and structuring public financial data cheaply, the value of some of LSEG's less-proprietary datasets could erode.

Over a 10-year horizon, the moat appears durable but faces meaningful uncertainty on the AI front. The clearing and index businesses are essentially unassailable on that timeframe. The data business has structural advantages (proprietary datasets, switching costs, regulatory requirements for auditable data provenance) but will need to continue innovating its delivery mechanisms -- the Microsoft partnership is the right strategic response. EBITDA margins have expanded from 43% in FY2022 to 48.5% in FY2025, demonstrating that the moat translates into pricing power and operating leverage. Advantages do not reset with product cycles; this is infrastructure, not a consumer tech business.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** A wide-moat financial infrastructure company with regulatory entrenchment, 88% gross margins, expanding EBITDA margins, and proprietary data assets that become more valuable as AI adoption increases.
- **Bear case:** AI commoditizes data distribution faster than expected, the Workspace terminal never closes the gap with Bloomberg, and the 5% growth in the core Data & Analytics division proves to be the ceiling, not the floor.
- **Confidence:** High -- the moat sources are observable and quantifiable in margins, market share data, and retention metrics; the AI risk is real but appears manageable.

## Red Flags
- The Feb 2026 AI selloff (9.7% in one day) shows the market is nervous about AI disintermediation risk, even if the fundamental case for data ownership is sound
- Bloomberg remains the dominant terminal franchise; LSEG's Workspace has not yet proven it can take meaningful share from Bloomberg in front-office workflows
- Elliott Management's activist campaign implicitly challenges whether management is maximizing the value of LSEG's moat assets or whether the portfolio should be restructured
- Debt/EBITDA of 2.6x in FY2025 (up from 2.4x) while executing a massive GBP 3bn buyback -- leverage is creeping higher

## Score: 8 / 10
LSEG has a wide, multi-layered moat driven by switching costs, proprietary data, regulatory entrenchment, and network effects in clearing -- the AI disruption risk is the primary reason this is not a 9.

# Management & Capital Allocation -- LSEG.L

**Analyst Role:** Business Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** LSEG 2025 Preliminary Results (26 Feb 2026), LSEG H2 2025 Earnings Call transcript highlights, Semafor interview with David Schwimmer (27 Mar 2026), Investing.com FY25 coverage, Simply Wall St management analysis, CityAM shareholder revolt coverage, DevDiscourse/British Brief activist pressure coverage, Yahoo Finance financials

| 6 | Elliott Management took a stake and is pushing for portfolio review, margin improvement, and better AI narrative -- management has partially responded | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider Ownership and Skin in the Game.** CEO David Schwimmer holds approximately GBP 14m in LSEG stock, representing about 0.019% of shares outstanding. For a GBP 41bn company, this is a modest personal stake -- it is meaningful in absolute terms but does not represent generational wealth tied to the stock. Schwimmer joined from Goldman Sachs in 2018 and has built his position primarily through long-term incentive plan vesting. The broader board and executive team hold positions through LTIP awards, but there is no founder or controlling shareholder with a large block (Thomson Reuters sold down its post-Refinitiv stake). This is a professionally managed company, not an owner-operator.

**Capital Allocation Track Record.** LSEG's capital allocation over the past four years has been increasingly shareholder-friendly. Buybacks have scaled dramatically: GBP 383m in 2022, GBP 1.2bn in 2023, GBP 1.1bn in 2024, and GBP 2.1bn in 2025 -- with GBP 3.0bn announced for the next 12 months. The share count has declined from approximately 555m post-Refinitiv to ~499m currently, a ~10% reduction. Dividends have grown steadily (15% increase in FY2025 to 103p final). Total shareholder returns (buyback + dividend) in FY2025 were approximately GBP 2.8bn against equity free cash flow of GBP 2.4bn, meaning the company is returning more than its FCF and funding the excess partly with leverage. Bolt-on M&A has been running at ~GBP 700m annually, focused on filling gaps in the data and analytics portfolio. The Refinitiv acquisition itself (GBP 27bn in 2021) was transformative and by most measures successful -- synergies are ahead of target, the data-heavy revenue mix is more valuable, and margins have expanded.

**Compensation Design.** Schwimmer's FY2024 total compensation of GBP 7.8m comprised GBP 1.3m salary, GBP 3.0m annual bonus, and GBP 2.4m from LTIP vesting, plus GBP 734k in share price growth. The 46% year-over-year increase triggered a shareholder revolt -- Glass Lewis raised concerns about the potential GBP 13.2m pay package in 2024. The LTIP is tied to TSR performance and financial metrics (EPS growth, revenue growth), which broadly aligns incentives with shareholders. However, the magnitude of pay increases during a period when the stock underperformed peers raises legitimate questions about pay-for-performance alignment.

**Candor and Consistency.** Schwimmer has been consistent in messaging about the Refinitiv integration, the Microsoft partnership, and medium-term growth targets. The 2026 guidance of 6.5-7.5% organic income growth and 80-100bps margin expansion is clear and measurable. In the recent Semafor interview (March 27, 2026), Schwimmer directly addressed the Elliott campaign, AI concerns, and global market dynamics -- he spoke like a pragmatic operator rather than a defensive politician. He acknowledged the activist's presence without being confrontational and framed the GBP 3bn buyback as organic capital allocation rather than a capitulation. That said, the timing of the buyback acceleration (coinciding with Elliott's arrival) makes it hard to separate genuine capital allocation conviction from reactive shareholder management.

**Governance Structure.** LSEG has a standard UK FTSE 100 governance setup with a separate chairman (currently Don Robert) and CEO. The board includes independent non-executives. There is no dual-class share structure or poison pill. Elliott's ability to take a stake and push for changes demonstrates that the governance structure is functioning -- shareholders can influence management. The risk is that management becomes too focused on short-term total shareholder return (buybacks, margin targets) at the expense of long-term investment in product and technology, which is exactly what Elliott tends to optimize for.

**Net Debt Trajectory.** Management has guided to net debt/EBITDA of approximately 2.0x, which they describe as providing "healthy strategic flexibility." FY2025 net debt was GBP 8.3bn (1.8x EBITDA). However, with GBP 3bn in buybacks over the next 12 months plus ~GBP 700m in bolt-on M&A, leverage will likely rise toward 2.5x unless FCF growth offsets the outflows. This is manageable for an infrastructure business with 70% recurring revenue, but it means the balance sheet is being optimized for returns rather than safety.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Management has delivered on Refinitiv synergies, compounded EPS at 15%+, grown dividends, and is returning massive capital to shareholders while maintaining a clear growth strategy around AI-ready data and the Microsoft partnership.
- **Bear case:** The buyback acceleration looks partly reactive to activist pressure, CEO compensation is generous relative to stock performance, insider ownership is modest, and leverage is rising as the company returns more than its FCF.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High -- capital allocation decisions are transparent and trackable, but the Elliott dynamic introduces uncertainty about whether current strategy reflects genuine conviction or activist-driven positioning.

## Red Flags
- Shareholder revolt on CEO compensation (46% increase, Glass Lewis objection) suggests pay-for-performance misalignment
- GBP 3bn buyback announced under activist pressure from Elliott -- difficult to assess whether this is the optimal use of capital or a defensive response
- Total shareholder returns (GBP 2.8bn) exceeded equity FCF (GBP 2.4bn) in FY2025, meaning returns are partly debt-funded
- CEO ownership of 0.019% is low for a company of this size -- incentives are primarily compensation-driven rather than ownership-driven
- Elliott is pushing for a portfolio review, which could mean forced divestitures that may not be in long-term shareholders' best interest

## Score: 7 / 10
Management has executed well on Refinitiv integration and delivered strong financial results, but modest insider ownership, a pay controversy, and activist-driven capital allocation decisions prevent a higher score -- this is competent professional management, not owner-operator excellence.

# Business Economics -- LSEG.L

**Analyst Role:** Financial Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Data Sources:** LSEG 2025 Preliminary Results RNS (26 Feb 2026), LSEG H1 2025 Interim Report, LSEG Q3 2025 Trading Update, Yahoo Finance (yfinance auto-fetch), Seeking Alpha, Investing.com, LSEG Investor Relations

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Adjusted EBITDA margin reached 50.3% in FY2025, up 150bps YoY, with constant-currency expansion of 210bps | 5 |
| 2 | Approximately 60% of revenue is subscription-based with 90%+ retention in Data & Analytics; ASV growth of 5.6% | 5 |
| 3 | Reported ROIC of 5.3% and ROE of 5.8% are depressed by ~GBP 27B of goodwill/intangibles from the Refinitiv acquisition, not by poor operating performance | 4 |
| 4 | Equity free cash flow of GBP 2.4B in FY2025, up 12% YoY, with capex intensity declining to 10.2% of revenue (down 110bps) | 4 |
| 5 | Organic revenue growth of 7.1% across the group, with every segment contributing: D&A +5.0%, FTSE Russell +7.3%, Risk Intelligence +11.7%, Markets +8.9% | 4 |
| 6 | Earnings growth substantially exceeds revenue growth: adjusted EPS +15.7% on 7.1% organic revenue growth, demonstrating strong operating leverage | 5 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital: the goodwill distortion.** LSEG's reported ROIC (5.3%) and ROE (5.8%) look mediocre at first glance but are almost entirely a function of the massive Refinitiv acquisition in January 2021, which loaded approximately GBP 27B of goodwill and intangible assets onto the balance sheet. Invested capital stands at GBP 30.9B, of which the vast majority is acquisition-related. On a tangible capital basis, LSEG's returns would be dramatically higher -- the business generates GBP 3.5B+ of adjusted operating profit on relatively modest tangible fixed assets. The correct lens here is cash returns on the operating business, not headline ROIC. The improving trend (ROIC from 3.1% in FY2024 to 5.3% in FY2025) reflects both earnings recovery and buyback-driven equity reduction. Over time, as goodwill amortisation and buybacks shrink the capital base while earnings grow, reported returns should steadily improve.

**Margin structure and operating leverage.** LSEG's margin profile is outstanding for a financial infrastructure business. Gross margins run at 88%, reflecting the low marginal cost of serving additional users on existing platforms. Adjusted EBITDA margins hit 50.3% in FY2025, expanding 150bps on a reported basis and 210bps in constant currency. Adjusted operating margins reached approximately 39% (GBP 3,506M adjusted operating profit on GBP 8,986M total income). The gap between reported operating margin (23.2%) and adjusted operating margin (~39%) reflects amortisation of acquired intangibles and integration costs -- real cash costs that are declining each year. Management guides for a further 150bps of EBITDA margin expansion per year through 2029, which is credible given platform consolidation, AI-driven productivity gains, and declining integration spend. The SwapClear revenue share renegotiation added approximately 100bps of structural margin improvement.

**Cash generation and owner earnings.** Equity free cash flow of GBP 2.4B represents strong conversion from GBP 4.5B of adjusted EBITDA (approximately 53% conversion after capex, interest, and tax). Capital expenditure of GBP 919M (10.2% of revenue) is declining in intensity and management targets 8% by 2029. The distinction between maintenance and growth capex is blurred at LSEG -- much of the spend is on platform migration (moving Refinitiv's legacy infrastructure to Microsoft Azure cloud), which is both necessary to maintain competitiveness and growth-enabling. Reported owner earnings (net income + D&A - capex) of GBP 1.6B in FY2025 understate true cash generation because net income includes large non-cash amortisation charges on acquired intangibles. A better proxy is adjusted operating cash flow minus capex, which yields approximately GBP 2.4B. Buybacks totalled GBP 2.2B in FY2025, indicating management confidence in cash generation sustainability and a commitment to returning excess capital.

**Revenue quality and durability.** This is one of LSEG's strongest attributes. Approximately 60% of total revenue is subscription-based, with the Data & Analytics division (nearly half of group revenue) carrying 90%+ IP-protected data and feeds revenue. FTSE Russell's index licensing fees are largely recurring (linked to AUM in index-tracking products). Post-Trade clearing fees are transaction-linked but benefit from structural growth in cleared derivatives volumes. The subscription base provides a pricing yield of approximately 3.5% annually, essentially built-in organic growth before any volume or new product uplift. Annual Subscription Value (ASV) growth of 5.6% provides good forward visibility. Customer retention rates are high because switching costs for embedded data feeds and analytics terminals are substantial. The combination of subscription dominance, high switching costs, and pricing power creates a revenue base that is unusually predictable and defensible for a business of this scale.

**Capital intensity trajectory.** LSEG is past the peak of its Refinitiv integration capex cycle. Total capex fell from 11.3% of revenue in FY2024 to 10.2% in FY2025, with management guiding toward 8% by 2029. This declining capex intensity, combined with growing revenue, means free cash flow should expand at a rate meaningfully faster than revenue growth. The cloud migration to Azure, while costly upfront, should reduce long-term infrastructure maintenance costs and improve scalability. This is a classic case of a business transitioning from a heavy investment phase to a harvest phase, with significant FCF margin expansion ahead.

**Operating leverage.** The 2025 results demonstrated textbook operating leverage: 7.1% organic revenue growth converted to 11.8% adjusted EBITDA growth and 15.7% adjusted EPS growth. This 2x+ earnings multiplier on revenue growth is structural, not a one-off, because the incremental cost of serving additional users on data platforms and processing additional clearing volume is minimal. Management expects this dynamic to persist through at least 2029 as platform consolidation continues and AI tools reduce operational costs.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Subscription-heavy revenue mix with 50%+ EBITDA margins, declining capex intensity, and structural operating leverage should drive mid-teens EPS growth and accelerating FCF for years.
- **Bear case:** Reported returns on capital remain optically low due to Refinitiv goodwill, and any slowdown in financial markets activity or data spend could compress the transactional revenue that drives margin expansion.
- **Confidence:** High -- FY2025 reported results, segment disclosures, and management guidance are all publicly available and internally consistent; margin and cash flow trends are well-documented across multiple reporting periods.

## Red Flags
- Reported ROIC/ROE remain below cost of capital on a GAAP basis due to goodwill; if the Refinitiv acquisition ultimately proves overpriced, these returns may never normalise fully
- Large gap between reported and adjusted operating profit (GBP 2.2B vs GBP 3.5B) means significant ongoing non-cash charges; investors must trust that adjustments are legitimate
- FY2025 capex spiked to GBP 1.9B on Yahoo Finance data (versus GBP 919M in LSEG's own reporting) -- this discrepancy likely reflects different treatment of capitalised costs vs CCP-related flows; clarity on true capex matters
- Stock-based compensation (GBP 176M) is modest but growing and should be treated as a real cost

## Score: 8 / 10
LSEG's business economics are strong: 50%+ EBITDA margins, 60% subscription revenue, declining capex intensity, and demonstrated 2x operating leverage on revenue growth. The only reason this is not a 9 is the optical drag from Refinitiv goodwill on reported capital returns and the capex data discrepancy that warrants further investigation.

# Balance Sheet Safety -- LSEG.L

**Analyst Role:** Financial Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-29

## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and structure.** At year-end 2025, LSEG carried total debt of approximately GBP 11.7B (per Yahoo Finance data) with operating net debt of GBP 8.2B. The company's own reporting cites operating net debt at GBP 8.175B. The difference between gross and net debt reflects approximately GBP 2.9B in cash and short-term investments. Notably, GBP 1.359B in bonds came due in 2026, which LSEG proactively refinanced through a $3B (approximately GBP 2.4B) US dollar bond sale in March 2026 across 3-year, 5-year, and 10-year tranches. This refinancing was well-received by the market and executed at competitive spreads, reflecting the company's strong credit profile. Long-term debt stood at GBP 7.9B, with the remainder in shorter-dated facilities and commercial paper. The debt/equity ratio of 59.2% (Yahoo Finance) or 37.4% (Simply Wall St, depending on methodology) reflects different treatments of equity and clearing-related balances. On any measure, leverage is moderate for an infrastructure business with this earnings stability.

**Interest coverage.** Reported EBIT/interest coverage improved materially to 7.1x in FY2025 from 4.7x in FY2024, driven by the recovery in operating income from GBP 1.4B to GBP 2.2B. On an adjusted operating profit basis (GBP 3.5B), coverage is approximately 9.6x -- comfortably above any stress threshold. The improvement in FY2025 partly reflects the lapping of Refinitiv integration charges that depressed FY2024 operating income. Average cost of debt appears to be in the 2.5-3.0% range, which is attractive for an A-rated borrower and well below LSEG's return on its operating assets. Interest expense has been relatively stable at GBP 324M-339M annually despite rising gross debt, suggesting good treasury management and staggered maturities that limited refinancing at peak rates.

**Liquidity.** LSEG held GBP 2.9B in cash and short-term investments at year-end 2025. The current ratio of 1.0x looks tight on paper but is meaningless as a measure of LSEG's liquidity because the balance sheet is dominated by clearing house pass-through assets and liabilities (approximately GBP 763B on each side). Stripping out CCP-related balances, LSEG's operating liquidity is ample: GBP 2.4B of annual equity free cash flow, GBP 2.9B of cash on hand, and access to revolving credit facilities and commercial paper programs backed by an A3 credit rating. The March 2026 bond issuance demonstrated that capital markets remain wide open for LSEG, even in a period of elevated market uncertainty.

**The clearing house balance sheet distortion.** This is the single most important thing to understand about LSEG's balance sheet. Total reported assets are approximately GBP 797B, but the vast majority -- over GBP 760B -- consists of clearing member margin deposits, default fund contributions, and associated receivables/payables held by LCH (LSEG's clearing house subsidiary). These assets are matched by corresponding liabilities; they are client money that passes through the CCP. LSEG bears no economic risk on these balances (beyond the remote tail risk of a clearing member default that exhausts the waterfall). The result is that standard balance sheet ratios (ROA, current ratio, debt/total assets) are all meaningless unless adjusted for CCP pass-throughs. LSEG's "own" balance sheet -- excluding clearing member assets -- is approximately GBP 30-35B, consisting primarily of goodwill/intangibles from Refinitiv, operating assets, and cash. This is the relevant balance sheet for credit analysis.

**Refinancing risk and maturity schedule.** With GBP 1.359B maturing in 2026 already addressed through the March bond sale, near-term refinancing risk is minimal. The debt portfolio appears well-laddered across multiple years and currencies (GBP and USD), reducing concentration risk around any single maturity date. The A3 rating and stable outlook from Moody's, combined with the strong March 2026 execution, suggest LSEG faces no meaningful refinancing risk in the medium term. The primary risk would be a multi-notch downgrade scenario, which appears remote given improving earnings trajectory and declining leverage. Management has committed to maintaining leverage within the 1.5-2.5x target range, which provides a clear framework for balancing shareholder returns with balance sheet discipline.

**Stress test: 30% revenue drop sustained for 2 years.** This is an extreme but instructive scenario. LSEG's FY2025 total income was approximately GBP 9.0B. In the 2008-2009 financial crisis, exchange and data businesses typically saw 10-20% revenue declines, not 30%. Nevertheless, in such a scenario: (a) EBITDA at a compressed 35% margin on GBP 6.3B revenue would yield approximately GBP 2.2B -- still sufficient to cover GBP 340M of interest expense 6.5x over; (b) capex could be cut to maintenance levels of perhaps GBP 400-500M; (c) the dividend could be suspended (saves ~GBP 750M); (d) buybacks would obviously cease. Under this severe stress, LSEG would remain solvent and continue to generate positive free cash flow, though leverage would temporarily spike above the target range. The subscription revenue floor is the key safety valve -- it prevents the kind of revenue collapse that would threaten debt covenants.

- **Bear case:** Absolute debt levels are high at GBP 11.7B gross, and the large clearing house balance sheet creates analytical complexity that could mask emerging risks; any CCP loss event would be highly disruptive even if remote.

- Working capital collapsed from GBP 1.5B in FY2024 to GBP 55M in FY2025 (per Yahoo Finance) -- likely a CCP timing effect on clearing balances, but deserves investigation

## Score: 7 / 10
LSEG's balance sheet is solid for an investment-grade financial infrastructure company: 1.8x net leverage, 7-10x interest coverage, ample liquidity, and strong capital market access. The score is held back from 8 by rising absolute debt levels, the complexity of the CCP-inflated balance sheet, and the choice to lever up via buybacks rather than deleverage.
