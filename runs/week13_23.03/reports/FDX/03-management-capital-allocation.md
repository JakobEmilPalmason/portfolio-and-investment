## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Raj Subramaniam holds approximately 98,519 shares of FDX, valued at roughly $35M at mid-2024 prices. That is meaningful in absolute terms but modest relative to the $85B market cap — representing less than 0.1% of shares outstanding. The company's executive stock ownership policy requires the CEO to hold 6x base salary in stock (base salary is $1.575M as of October 2025, implying a target of ~$9.4M), which he well exceeds. The ownership structure is manager-as-employee rather than owner-operator. Fred Smith, the founder who stepped back from CEO in 2022, was the last person at FedEx who behaved like a true owner. Subramaniam is a career insider (joined as associate marketing analyst in 1991), which confers deep institutional knowledge but is not the same as capital-at-risk ownership.

**Capital allocation track record.** The FY2023–FY2025 capital allocation story is best summarized as: cost cuts first, then cash returns. The DRIVE program delivered its full $4B target on schedule — $2.2B in FY2025 alone — and CapEx fell from $5.2B in FY2024 to $4.1B in FY2025 (a 22% reduction), reaching the lowest CapEx-as-percent-of-revenue ratio in company history at 4.6%. This is the right move for a business that over-invested during the pandemic-era volume surge.

The cash return program has been aggressive. FedEx bought back $3.0B of stock in FY2025, $2.5B in FY2024, and $1.5B in FY2023 — total ~$7B over three years. Combined with dividends, the company returned $4.3B in FY2025 alone. The share count has declined meaningfully. On a $85B market cap, $7B in buybacks over three years represents ~8% of market cap retired — a genuine tailwind to per-share value. The $5B buyback authorization announced in March 2024 signals continued commitment.

On M&A, there are no major acquisition announcements in this period — Subramaniam has been an internal restructurer, not an empire builder. The most significant strategic move is actually a divestiture (the Freight spin-off), which is the correct direction for a company that needed to simplify. The decision to separate FedEx Freight — which will become the largest LTL carrier in North America as a standalone with 355 service centers and 39,000 employees — is a bold unlocking move. FedEx Freight's standalone multiple as an LTL-focused business should be higher than the conglomerate discount it previously carried.

**Compensation design.** CEO total compensation for FY2025 was $12.87M, of which 88.5% is variable (stock and cash bonus). This alignment is structurally correct — management earns more if the stock appreciates. The annual report does not disclose which specific metrics drive the bonus (revenue, adjusted EPS, ROIC, or operating income), which is a transparency gap. SBC is not explicitly broken out in the financials provided, but management's heavy reliance on equity grants means share count reduction from buybacks partly offsets new option/RSU grants — a common corporate finance treadmill. The company does appear to use per-share metrics (adjusted EPS) as a key benchmark, which is better than pure revenue or EBITDA targets.

**Candor and consistency.** Subramaniam has been candid about the scale of FedEx's problems when he took over in 2022 — the company was famously inefficient, operating parallel Express and Ground networks that drove to the same address with different trucks. His shareholder communications have been focused on the DRIVE targets, which he has met. FY2025 adjusted EPS of $16.81 vs. FY2026 guidance of $17.20–$19.00 suggests confidence in continued improvement. One candor note: the revenue guidance for FY2025 was repeatedly adjusted downward throughout the year as volume softness persisted — a reminder that guidance accuracy in a volume-sensitive business is structurally difficult.

**Governance.** The board composition is independent-majority, with typical Fortune 100 governance. No obvious red flags. FedEx's classified board structure provides some insulation from activist pressure, which could be a feature (long-term focus) or a bug (management entrenchment), depending on your view.

**The simple tell: owner or politician?** Raj Subramaniam talks like a manager who is deeply competent but not yet an owner. His communications center on execution metrics (DRIVE savings, CapEx as % of revenue, Network 2.0 facility count), which is precisely the right language for a turnaround phase. He is less poetic about long-term competitive positioning than Fred Smith was, but far more rigorous about cost discipline. The pilot labor dispute is a yellow flag: five-plus years of stalled contract negotiations, with pilots working under an expired contract, suggests either poor labor relations management or a deliberate cost-containment strategy that comes at the cost of employee morale. The September 2025 no-confidence vote was public and embarrassing for a CEO who openly champions FedEx culture.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Subramaniam is executing a genuinely difficult operational transformation — DRIVE delivered on time, CapEx is declining, the Freight spin-off unlocks value, and aggressive buybacks create per-share value even on flat revenue.
- **Bear case:** The labor dispute overhang (5,000+ pilots with no pay raise in 5 years, no-confidence vote) is a latent operational risk; management may be optimizing for near-term EPS at the expense of long-term cultural and operational health.
- **Confidence:** Medium — capital allocation mechanics are clearly improving, but the labor relations situation introduces a governance risk that is unresolved and operationally material.

## Red Flags

- Pilots' no-confidence vote in CEO (September 2025) — represents 5,000+ pilots, contract expired/stalled since 2021
- Insider ownership is real but not "founder-level" — management is steward-class, not owner-class
- Revenue has been flat-to-declining ($93.5B in FY2022 to $87.9B in FY2025) during the same period buybacks accelerated — raises the question of whether capital is being returned because no reinvestment opportunities exist
- FedEx Freight separation requires significant management attention during a period when the core network transformation (Network 2.0) is also mid-execution — execution bandwidth risk
- Compensation metric details (what specifically drives the 88.5% variable pay) are not fully disclosed

## Score: 6 / 10

Subramaniam has earned credit for executing DRIVE on target and making bold structural decisions (Freight spin-off, Network 2.0), and the capital return program is shareholder-friendly — but the unresolved pilot labor dispute, modest insider ownership, and uncertain revenue trajectory keep this from being a high-conviction management story.
