## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels: Better Than They Look at First Glance**

The headline total debt figure of $37.4B is attention-grabbing, but the composition matters significantly. Long-term debt (traditional bonds and notes) stands at $19.2B, down from $20.5B in FY2023 — a modest but consistent paydown trajectory. The "total debt" figure of $37.4B includes approximately $16.8B in operating lease liabilities (current: $2.6B; long-term: $14.2B) that are now on-balance-sheet under ASC 842 accounting standards. These lease obligations are real economic commitments, but they are not financial debt in the traditional sense — they represent contracted facility and aircraft lease payments, many of which are operationally necessary and would exist in any iteration of the business. Stripping out operating leases, financial debt is approximately $20.6B, yielding a net financial debt (after $5.5B cash) of roughly $15.1B — the figure cited in the financial context. Net Debt/EBITDA of 1.4x on that basis is conservative for an investment-grade logistics company.

The broader Debt/EBITDA of 3.6x (total debt including leases) is the number to watch as a stress indicator. For a business generating $10.5B in EBITDA, $37.4B in total obligations is a 3.6x multiple that is manageable in the base case but tighter in a downturn. The long-term lease obligations represent a semi-fixed cost structure that does not flex easily in a revenue contraction.

**Interest Coverage: Comfortable**

EBIT/Interest of 7.9x means FedEx's operating earnings are nearly 8 times its annual interest bill. At $789M in interest expense against $6.0B in operating income, there is substantial room for earnings deterioration before debt service becomes problematic. Even in a scenario where operating income fell by 50% (to ~$3B), interest coverage would remain above 4x — a level that investment-grade issuers typically maintain. This is a genuine strength and limits near-term financial distress risk.

**Liquidity: Adequate, Not Generous**

Cash of $5.5B against current liabilities of $15.4B yields a current ratio of 1.2x — functional but thin. The current liabilities include the $2.6B current portion of operating leases, not just short-term debt. For a company generating $7B in annual operating cash flow, the absolute liquidity position is more comfortable than the current ratio alone implies; FedEx can service near-term obligations from operations. The company also has access to revolving credit facilities (not specifically quantified in the data provided), which is typical for investment-grade issuers at this scale. The thin current ratio is worth monitoring but is not alarming given the cash generation profile.

**Refinancing Risk: Contained but Not Zero**

FedEx's long-term debt declined from $20.5B (FY2023) to $19.2B (FY2025), and the current portion of long-term debt spiked to approximately $1.4B in FY2025, indicating near-term maturities. Credit ratings of BBB (S&P) and Baa2 (Moody's) — both solid investment grade — maintain access to capital markets at reasonable spreads. A notable structural event is the planned June 2026 spinoff of FedEx Freight, for which FedEx Freight Holding Co. pre-financed $3.7B in senior notes in February 2026. S&P rated FedEx Freight at BBB-. This debt is expected to transfer with the spun-out entity, which would reduce the parent's consolidated debt obligations. The spinoff is structurally credit-positive for the remaining FedEx, assuming the Freight business carries its proportionate share of the balance sheet.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations: Leases Are Now On-Balance-Sheet**

Since ASC 842 adoption, FedEx's operating leases are on the balance sheet. Total operating lease liabilities as of FY2025 are approximately $16.8B ($2.6B current + $14.2B long-term). These primarily represent aircraft leases (a large portion of Express fleet), ground facility leases, and vehicle leases. These commitments are long-dated and largely non-cancellable, representing a source of fixed-cost rigidity. Network 2.0 station closures — targeting 475+ facility closures by end of 2027 — will gradually reduce the lease liability over time as contracts expire or are terminated. One genuine off-balance-sheet item is pension obligations beyond the recorded net liability — specifically from legacy international/TNT operations. FedEx made $800M in voluntary U.S. pension contributions in the nine months ending February 2025, indicating management is actively managing this exposure. The U.S. qualified pension plan is reportedly fully funded under ERISA, but international pension obligations (UK, Netherlands, Belgium from legacy TNT) carry underfunding risk in adverse interest rate environments.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years**

A 30% revenue decline from $87.9B would leave revenues at approximately $61.5B. If operating margins compressed by half (to ~3.5%) under that scenario, EBIT would fall to approximately $2.2B. Against interest expense of ~$800M, interest coverage would remain around 2.8x — pressured but technically solvent. The greater risk is the lease obligation structure: operating lease payments (~$3.5B annually based on EBITDA and D&A composition) are contractually fixed regardless of revenue. A severe multi-year volume decline could push FedEx toward covenant renegotiation and potential credit downgrade, but outright insolvency risk appears remote given the asset base, brand, and essential-service nature of the business. FedEx survived COVID with minimal disruption and navigated the 2023 post-pandemic volume cliff without a credit event. A 30% revenue shock would be more severe than either of those, but the business is not fragile. Survival: yes. Stress-free: no.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Net financial debt of 1.4x EBITDA, 7.9x interest coverage, and the Freight spinoff reducing the consolidated debt load make this a financially conservative company with room to fund transformation.
- **Bear case:** Total obligations including $16.8B in operating leases create semi-fixed cost rigidity; a sustained revenue decline combined with lease exits, pension contributions, and integration spending could materially tighten liquidity and risk a ratings downgrade to BBB-.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the debt picture is well-documented and investment-grade ratings are well-supported; the main unknowns are pension funding under stress and lease obligations during Network 2.0 restructuring.

---

## Red Flags

- Total obligations including operating leases ($37.4B) represent 3.6x EBITDA — higher leverage than the headline net debt figure implies.
- Operating lease liabilities of $16.8B are largely non-cancellable, creating fixed-cost rigidity that does not flex with revenue.
- $800M in voluntary pension contributions in nine months ending February 2025 — suggests pension liability is larger or more volatile than the fully-funded headline implies.
- Current ratio of 1.2x is thin, though operating cash flow generation mitigates near-term liquidity concern.
- FedEx Freight spinoff pre-financing $3.7B in new senior notes — while expected to transfer with the spinoff, the transition period carries execution risk.

---

## Score: 6 / 10

FedEx's core financial debt position is genuinely conservative (net debt/EBITDA 1.4x, interest coverage 7.9x, investment-grade ratings), but the $16.8B in operating lease liabilities and uncertain pension exposure create a meaningfully higher all-in obligation picture that warrants a middle-of-road score rather than a strong one.
