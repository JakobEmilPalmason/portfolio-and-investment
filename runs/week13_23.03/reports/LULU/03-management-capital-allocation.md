## Detailed Analysis

Lululemon's capital allocation track record under Calvin McDonald (CEO from 2018 to January 2026) was genuinely strong by the numbers. Revenue grew from approximately $3.3 billion in FY2018 to $10.6 billion in FY2025 — more than tripling in seven years. Margins expanded simultaneously: gross margins improved from roughly 55% to 59.2%, and operating margins climbed from roughly 20% to 23.7%. ROIC remained above 36% throughout, peaking at 42.6%. This is the profile of a management team that grew the business while improving its economics — the hallmark of good capital allocation.

The buyback program has been particularly impressive. Lululemon has repurchased over $5.5 billion in stock since FY2015, reducing the diluted share count from approximately 130 million to 110 million shares. In FY2025, the company deployed $1.6 billion in repurchases — roughly equal to its entire free cash flow — and the board authorized an additional $1 billion, bringing the remaining authorization to approximately $1.7 billion. Critically, these buybacks are now being executed at much lower valuations (12x trailing P/E) than the historical average, which represents genuinely accretive capital allocation. The buyback at current prices is arguably the single best use of capital available to management.

Stock-based compensation is exceptionally well-controlled at $90 million, representing just 0.85% of revenue and approximately 5% of free cash flow. This is far below typical consumer retail or tech companies and means the buyback program is genuinely returning value to shareholders rather than merely offsetting SBC dilution. Net shares outstanding are declining in real terms.

The governance situation, however, is the dominant concern. McDonald's departure was abrupt and is widely interpreted as the board losing patience with slowing US growth. The interim co-CEO arrangement (CFO Frank and CCO Maestrini) is a holding pattern, not a strategy. With no permanent CEO named and a major proxy fight underway, the company is effectively being run by committee during a period that demands decisive strategic action — particularly regarding the US turnaround, international expansion pace, and competitive positioning against Alo Yoga and Vuori.

Chip Wilson's proxy fight introduces both opportunity and risk. Wilson, who holds approximately 8-10% of shares, has nominated three directors (including former On Running co-CEO Marc Maurer) and publicly called for broader board overhaul. His nominees have relevant operational experience, and his criticism — that the board has become complacent and needs retail-experienced directors — has merit. However, proxy fights create distraction, and the outcome (which directors, which strategic direction) remains uncertain heading into the 2026 annual meeting. Elliott Management's $1 billion stake and reported involvement in the CEO search adds another powerful voice but also another layer of complexity.

One area of concern is the "Power of Three x2" growth strategy, which originally targeted $12.5 billion in revenue by FY2026. With FY2026 guidance of $11.35-11.50 billion, the company will miss this target meaningfully. Whether this reflects overambitious goal-setting or genuine execution shortfalls under McDonald's leadership is debatable, but the miss undermines management credibility on forward guidance. The cautious FY2026 outlook (2-4% revenue growth, 8% EPS decline) may reflect conservatism from an interim team or genuine headwinds from tariffs, promotional activity, and competitive pressure.

The balance sheet remains a fortress: $2.0 billion in cash, debt/EBITDA of just 0.5x, and a current ratio of 2.2x. This financial strength gives any incoming CEO significant strategic flexibility — whether for accelerated buybacks, international investment, or even M&A. The balance sheet is unambiguously a positive stewardship marker.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A proven capital allocation track record ($5.5B+ in buybacks, expanding margins, 41% ROIC), fortress balance sheet, minimal SBC, and activist pressure from Wilson and Elliott could catalyze a positive leadership transition and strategic reset.
- **Bear case:** The company is in a leadership vacuum at a critical moment — no permanent CEO, an active proxy fight, a missed growth target, and decelerating US performance create execution risk that strong financials alone cannot resolve.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The financial track record is verifiably strong, but the current governance situation is genuinely uncertain and the quality of the next CEO will be determinative for the next 3-5 years.

## Red Flags

- No permanent CEO since January 31, 2026 — interim co-CEO arrangement is a stopgap, not a strategy
- Active proxy fight from founder Chip Wilson with demands for broad board overhaul
- "Power of Three x2" revenue target ($12.5B by FY2026) will be missed by ~$1B
- FY2026 guidance implies 8% EPS decline — the first meaningful earnings contraction in years
- Insider ownership among current management and board appears modest (exact figures not disclosed in recent filings, which itself is a mild transparency concern)

## Score: 6 / 10

The historical capital allocation record is strong (7-8 range), but the current governance situation — CEO vacancy, proxy fight, missed targets, activist pressure — pulls the score down significantly. A score of 6 reflects genuine stewardship risk from the leadership transition that could either resolve positively (new CEO + refreshed board) or create a prolonged period of strategic drift. The financial track record earns benefit of the doubt, but the score cannot be higher until leadership is settled.
