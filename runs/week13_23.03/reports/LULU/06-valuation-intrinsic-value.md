## Detailed Analysis

The quant DCF model anchors bear/base/bull intrinsic values at $288/$438/$611 per share. The base case assumes 9.8% revenue growth in Year 1 fading to 3% by Year 5, a 23.7% operating margin, WACC of 9.6%, and a 15x exit EV/EBITDA multiple. These assumptions are reasonable but arguably conservative given lululemon's track record: the company grew revenue from $6.3B to $10.6B between FY2022 and FY2025, maintained 59% gross margins, and generated $1.6B in free cash flow in FY2025. The WACC of 9.6% derived from CAPM (beta 1.01, risk-free 4.5%, equity premium 5.5%) is standard and defensible. I find the base-case assumptions fair and the bear case genuinely conservative, particularly the implied terminal growth and exit multiple.

Where I depart slightly from the quant model is on near-term execution risk. FY2026 guidance of 2-4% revenue growth and $12.10-$12.30 EPS represents a real earnings trough — an 8% decline from FY2025. This is not captured in the quant model's smooth fade from 9.8% growth. However, this near-term weakness is precisely what creates the valuation opportunity. If we stress-test the model by cutting Year 1 growth to 3% (matching the low end of guidance) and pushing the margin down 100bps to 22.7%, the base-case IV still lands around $380-$400, more than double the current price. The business would need to experience a permanent structural decline — sustained revenue contraction, margin compression to the low teens, and loss of international growth — to justify a price below $200.

The multiples context is extraordinary. LULU's trailing PE of 12x represents a 70%+ discount to its 10-year median of ~42x. The EV/EBITDA of 6.9x is approaching its all-time low of 6.63x. For context, Nike — which has lower margins, lower ROIC, and a more troubled turnaround story — trades at 42x earnings. Even applying a permanently derated multiple of 15-18x forward earnings to the guided $12.20 midpoint yields a price of $183-$220, 15-38% above current levels. The market is pricing LULU as though its competitive advantages have been permanently eroded, its international growth is worthless, and the brand is in terminal decline.

The owner earnings picture remains strong. Adjusted owner earnings of $1.8B against a market cap of $18.6B implies an owner earnings yield of roughly 9.7% — exceptional for a business with 41% ROIC and a net cash position. The company returned $1.6B via buybacks in FY2025 alone, nearly 9% of its current market cap. If management continues buying back shares at these depressed levels, per-share value accretion could be substantial. The balance sheet is a fortress: $2.0B cash, $1.6B total debt, Debt/EBITDA of 0.5x, current ratio of 2.2x. There is zero balance sheet risk here.

What must be true for the current price to be justified? The market is implicitly discounting a scenario where: (1) North American same-store sales decline persistently, (2) international growth fails to offset domestic weakness, (3) margins compress permanently from increased competition and promotional activity, and (4) the leadership vacuum leads to strategic missteps. While these risks are real, the implied expectation is far more pessimistic than the likely range of outcomes for a brand generating $10.6B in revenue, 59% gross margins, and 41% ROIC. Even the bear-case DCF scenario, which assumes significant deterioration, produces a $288 IV — 81% above the current price.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** LULU is a high-quality, high-ROIC compounder temporarily mispriced at trough multiples due to cyclical headwinds and a CEO transition; normalization to even modest historical multiples yields 80-150%+ upside.
- **Bear case:** North American brand erosion is structural, not cyclical; competition from Alo Yoga, Vuori, and reinvigorated Nike permanently compresses margins and growth, and the leadership vacuum delays any turnaround.
- **Confidence:** High — The valuation case is compelling across every framework (DCF, multiples, owner earnings yield, Monte Carlo); the disagreement is on timing and business trajectory, not on whether the stock is cheap.

## Red Flags

- FY2026 EPS guidance down 8% YoY — earnings are declining, not just decelerating
- No permanent CEO appointed; co-CEO interim arrangement introduces execution uncertainty
- North American comparable sales declining; core market showing saturation signals
- Brand heat may have peaked — Alo Yoga, Vuori, and others eroding share among younger demographics
