## Detailed Analysis

Lululemon's return-on-capital profile is exceptional by any standard. The company's trailing ROIC of 41.3% and ROE of 42.4% place it in the top tier of global consumer companies. Over the past five years, ROIC has averaged above 30%, consistently exceeding specialty retail peers like Nike (~28%), Gildan (~15%), and Puma (~10%). These returns are not an artifact of financial engineering or excessive leverage — debt-to-EBITDA sits at just 0.5x and the balance sheet carries $2.0B in cash against $1.6B in total debt (almost entirely operating lease liabilities under ASC 842). The high returns are genuinely earned through brand pricing power, vertical control of design, and a direct-to-consumer distribution model that captures full retail margin.

The margin structure has historically been outstanding: FY2025 full-year gross margin of 59.2%, operating margin of 23.7%, and net margin of 17.1%. These are luxury-tier margins on what is technically athletic apparel. However, Q4 FY2025 revealed a significant crack: gross margin fell 550 bps year-over-year to 54.9%, with 520 bps attributable to product margin decline (primarily tariffs at 560 bps headwind, partially offset by mix). Management expects the tariff gross impact to rise from $275M in FY2025 to $380M in FY2026, with only $160M offset through efficiency initiatives. This leaves roughly $220M of unmitigated tariff cost flowing through margins. The FY2026 EPS guide of $12.10-$12.30 versus FY2025's $14.64 implies meaningful margin compression ahead.

Cash generation remains a strength. Owner earnings of ~$1.8B (net income adjusted for D&A and maintenance capex) represent robust conversion. FCF of $1.6B at an 87% conversion rate funds the entire $1.6B buyback program without touching the cash balance or adding debt. The company has reduced its share count meaningfully over time, amplifying per-share economics. Capital intensity is moderate: maintenance capex of ~$447M supports the existing store fleet and systems, while growth capex of ~$243M funds new store openings and international expansion. For a retailer with 700+ locations, this is an efficient capital deployment model.

Revenue quality is mixed. The DTC e-commerce channel provides some recurring purchase behavior among loyal customers, but lululemon sells discretionary apparel — there is no contractual recurring revenue. North America, which represents the majority of sales, was flat in FY2025 with comparable sales declining 2%. The growth engine has shifted to international markets, particularly China Mainland (+28% revenue, +26% comps), but this introduces execution risk and geopolitical exposure. The full-year FY2025 revenue of $10.6B (up from $9.6B in FY2024) reflects solid top-line growth, but the Americas deceleration is concerning for a brand that built its moat in North American athleisure.

Operating leverage, which historically amplified margins as revenue scaled, is now working in reverse. The combination of tariff headwinds, increased promotional activity (markdowns), and a CEO departure in January 2026 creates a period of elevated uncertainty. The business economics remain structurally excellent — 40%+ ROIC, strong FCF, low capital intensity — but the near-term trajectory of margins and earnings is clearly deteriorating. The question is whether this is cyclical (tariffs, leadership transition) or structural (brand fatigue, competition from Alo Yoga, Vuori, On Running).

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A 40%+ ROIC business with $1.6B FCF, net cash, and a functioning international growth engine is temporarily depressed by tariffs and a leadership vacuum — the economic engine is intact.
- **Bear case:** North American brand momentum has stalled, tariff costs are structural not transient, and margin compression from 60% gross to mid-50s% signals a permanent re-rating of the business model.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The historical economics are undeniably strong, but the Q4 margin compression and FY2026 earnings decline guidance introduce real uncertainty about the forward path.

## Red Flags

- Q4 FY2025 gross margin down 550 bps YoY — largest single-quarter compression in years
- FY2026 guided EPS of $12.10-$12.30 represents ~8% decline despite revenue growth
- CEO Calvin McDonald departed January 2026 with no permanent successor named
- North America comparable sales declined 2% in Q4 — the core market is stagnating
- Tariff headwind expected to grow from $275M to $380M in FY2026

## Score: 8 / 10

The underlying business economics — 41% ROIC, 59% gross margins, $1.6B FCF on modest capex — are genuinely excellent and rank among the best in consumer retail, but the clear near-term margin deterioration from tariffs and North American softness prevents a top-tier score.
