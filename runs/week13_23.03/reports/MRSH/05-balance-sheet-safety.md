## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Marsh McLennan carries $21.4B in total debt, with ~$19.3B in senior unsecured notes and ~$250M in subsidiary debt as of December 31, 2025. This represents a significant increase from ~$11.8B at year-end 2023, driven almost entirely by the $7.25B senior notes offering in October 2024 to fund the $7.75B McGriff acquisition. Debt/EBITDA at 2.9x is above the company's historical range of 1.5-2.0x but remains within investment-grade comfort zones. Marsh maintains investment-grade ratings (A-/A3), which provide access to favorable refinancing terms. The debt is entirely fixed-rate senior unsecured notes (with the exception of $300M in floating-rate notes due 2027), eliminating interest rate risk on the vast majority of the debt stack. The maturity schedule is well-laddered: $600M matures in Q1 2026, with subsequent tranches spread across 2027, 2030, 2031, 2035, 2036, 2044, and 2055. No single year presents a refinancing cliff.

**Interest Coverage Deterioration.** The decline in interest coverage from 9.7x in FY2022 to 6.8x in FY2025 is the most notable balance sheet concern. This is almost entirely explained by the McGriff financing: interest expense jumped from $700M to $960M year-over-year. However, 6.8x coverage on $960M of interest still provides a substantial cushion. Operating income of $6.2B could absorb a further 40% decline before interest coverage would breach 3.0x (a typical credit-distress threshold). Management has signaled intent to deleverage back toward 2.0-2.5x Debt/EBITDA over the next 2-3 years through organic EBITDA growth and modest debt paydown, which would restore coverage toward 8-9x.

**Liquidity Position.** Cash of $2.7B plus the $600M near-term maturity being easily covered by FCF ($5.0B annual run-rate) makes the liquidity position adequate. The current ratio of 1.1x appears thin in isolation, but this is structurally normal for insurance brokers. A significant portion of current liabilities consists of fiduciary funds owed to insurers — these are matched by fiduciary assets held in trust, creating an optical mismatch that does not represent actual liquidity risk. Marsh also maintains a $3.0B revolving credit facility (undrawn as of year-end 2025) as a backstop.

**Refinancing Risk.** With investment-grade ratings, a well-laddered maturity profile, and no single-year maturity exceeding $2B, refinancing risk is low. The October 2024 offering demonstrated strong market access — Marsh placed $7.25B across seven tranches at coupons ranging from 4.55% to 5.40%, even in a higher-rate environment. The weighted average cost of debt is estimated at ~4.5%, which is manageable relative to the company's 14% ROIC. A credit crunch scenario (e.g., spreads widen 200bps) would increase financing costs but not threaten solvency — the earliest large maturity requiring refinancing is likely 2027 ($950M + $300M floating rate).

**Off-Balance-Sheet Considerations.** Marsh carries operating lease obligations (offices globally), pension liabilities through Mercer (though Mercer's own pension obligations have been significantly de-risked over the past decade), and contingent liabilities from errors & omissions (E&O) insurance claims — a standard industry risk. None of these represent material threats to solvency. The company divested Mercer's UK pension administration and US health benefits businesses as part of its post-McGriff portfolio rationalization, reducing off-balance-sheet complexity.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** Revenue drops from $27B to $18.9B. Assuming operating margins compress from 23% to 15% (substantial deleverage), operating income would fall to ~$2.8B. Interest expense at $960M would still be covered 2.9x. FCF would decline sharply — perhaps to $1.5-2.0B — but would remain positive. The company could cover all debt maturities through 2028 from operating cash flow alone, without accessing capital markets. Equity issuance would not be required. In practice, insurance brokerage revenue is less cyclical than most industries — even during the 2008-2009 crisis, Marsh's revenue declined only ~5%, not 30%. A 30% decline would represent an unprecedented catastrophe for the insurance industry, and even in that scenario, Marsh would survive.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Deliberate leverage-up for McGriff acquisition with a clear deleveraging glide path, $5B FCF provides enormous debt service capacity, and investment-grade access ensures refinancing flexibility even in stressed markets.
- **Bear case:** Debt/EBITDA of 2.9x is the highest in at least a decade, interest coverage has nearly halved, and any integration stumble or economic downturn would slow the expected deleveraging timeline.
- **Confidence:** High — The debt structure is transparent, maturities are well-laddered, and the business model's cash generation resilience is proven across multiple cycles including 2008-2009.

## Red Flags
- Debt/EBITDA of 2.9x is meaningfully above historical 1.5-2.0x range; deleveraging dependent on continued organic growth
- Interest expense nearly doubled in two years ($530M FY2023 to $960M FY2025); consumes 15.5% of operating income
- $7.75B McGriff acquisition creates execution risk — if revenue synergies disappoint, the leverage ratio stays elevated longer
- Current ratio of 1.1x leaves minimal buffer for unexpected cash needs, though fiduciary structure mitigates this optically
- Floating-rate exposure limited ($300M) but rising-rate environment increases refinancing cost for future maturities

## Score: 7 / 10
Marsh McLennan's balance sheet is conservatively leveraged for a business of its quality and cash generation, but the McGriff-driven step-up to 2.9x Debt/EBITDA and halved interest coverage represent real, if manageable, risk — preventing a higher score until deleveraging progresses.
