## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Marsh McLennan earns fees and commissions by placing insurance and advising on risk for 95% of the Fortune 1000, without bearing any underwriting risk itself.
2. **Moat**: Switching brokers means migrating decades of claims data, coverage structures, and insurer relationships — 90%+ retention rates and 18 consecutive years of margin expansion prove the switching costs are real.
3. **Economics**: $5.0B FCF on $291M capex (120% conversion), 29% ROE, and 42% gross margins confirm this is an asset-light toll collector that turns almost every dollar of profit into cash.
4. **Management**: Capital allocation is disciplined — 15 years of dividend increases, consistent buybacks, and reliable mid-single-digit organic growth — but insider ownership is modest (~$17M for the CEO) and the $7.75B McGriff deal was funded entirely with debt.
5. **Debt risk**: At 2.9x Debt/EBITDA with $5B annual FCF covering $960M interest expense 5.2x, Marsh can service its debt even if revenue drops 30% for two years — the company survived 2008 with only a 5% revenue decline.
6. **Price**: At 15.3x forward earnings versus a historical 20-25x range, the market must believe organic growth has permanently downshifted to 3% and McGriff integration will destroy value — neither is likely for the world's dominant insurance broker.
7. **Margin of safety**: Conservative IV of $231 puts MOS at 25%, the stock trades below the worst-case cell in the sensitivity grid ($216), and Monte Carlo gives 99.9% probability of undervaluation — the downside to bear IV is only 10%.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Organic growth below 2% for two consecutive years or Debt/EBITDA exceeding 3.5x without a clear paydown path would signal the compounding engine has stalled and McGriff was a strategic error.

## Key Risks

1. **McGriff integration risk**: The $7.75B acquisition (largest in company history) pushed debt to 2.9x EBITDA and requires successful integration of 3,500 employees and $1.3B in revenue — if synergies disappoint, leverage stays elevated longer and goodwill impairment risk rises.
2. **Organic growth deceleration**: FY2025 organic growth of 4% is the lowest in recent history, down from 7% in FY2024 — if this is structural rather than cyclical, the compounding thesis weakens and the valuation discount may persist.
3. **Interest coverage compression**: Interest expense nearly doubled to $960M, dropping coverage from 9.7x to 6.8x — while still comfortable, further deterioration would pressure the credit profile and limit capital allocation flexibility.
4. **Goodwill concentration**: ~$24B in goodwill (~41% of total assets) creates impairment risk if acquisition returns disappoint over time.
5. **Consulting segment cyclicality**: Oliver Wyman's project-based revenue is more volatile than the brokerage core, and Mercer faces competitive dynamics in HR consulting.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) Price drops to $160 or below (near bear IV), warranting a larger 4-5% position. (2) McGriff accretion becomes visible in FY2026 earnings with organic growth re-accelerating to 5%+. (3) Debt/EBITDA declines toward 2.5x, confirming the deleveraging glide path.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) Price exceeds base IV of $256 — begin trimming. (2) Organic growth falls below 2% for two consecutive years, signaling structural deterioration. (3) Debt/EBITDA exceeds 3.5x without a clear paydown path, or management pursues another large debt-funded acquisition before delevering from McGriff.

# Circle of Competence — MRSH

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Marsh McLennan 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release, BusinessWire Q1 2025 results, company investor relations, Insurance Business Mag, Morningstar, MacroTrends, project financials context

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |

| 1 | Marsh McLennan is the world's largest insurance broker and risk advisor, operating in 130 countries with 95,000+ employees and $27B in 2025 revenue | 5 |
| 2 | The business model is fee-based and commission-based brokerage — asset-light, capital-light, and highly recurring | 5 |
| 3 | Four distinct but complementary segments (Marsh 54%, Mercer 23%, Oliver Wyman 13%, Guy Carpenter 10%) provide diversification across risk, reinsurance, HR consulting, and management consulting | 4 |
| 4 | Revenue is overwhelmingly tied to global commercial insurance premium volumes and corporate demand for advisory services — both structurally growing | 4 |
| 5 | Marsh advises ~95% of Fortune 1000 companies, indicating deep penetration of the world's largest enterprises | 5 |

## Detailed Analysis

Marsh McLennan Companies operates as a professional services firm organized around risk, insurance, and consulting. The business is straightforward: it acts as an intermediary between companies seeking insurance coverage and the insurers who underwrite that risk. Marsh, the flagship segment generating 54% of revenue, places commercial insurance and arranges risk transfer programs for corporate clients. Guy Carpenter (10% of revenue) does the same at the reinsurance layer — helping insurers themselves manage their risk portfolios. Together, Risk and Insurance Services accounts for roughly 64% of total revenue.

The remaining 36% comes from Consulting. Mercer (23% of revenue) provides HR consulting, retirement plan administration, investment advisory, and health benefits design. Oliver Wyman (13%) is a management consulting firm focused on financial services, aviation, and other specialized industries. While less obviously connected to the insurance brokerage core, both consulting arms cross-sell into the same corporate client base and reinforce the "trusted advisor" relationship.

The revenue model is a blend of commissions (percentage of insurance premiums placed) and fees (flat or retainer-based for consulting and advisory work). This is critical: Marsh McLennan does not bear underwriting risk. It earns regardless of whether claims occur. Revenue rises naturally with insurance premium inflation, which has been positive in most years. The company also benefits from the secular trend of increasing global risk complexity — cyber threats, climate events, regulatory change — which drives demand for sophisticated risk advisory.

Key revenue drivers are: (1) global commercial insurance premium volumes and pricing trends, (2) corporate demand for risk transfer and advisory, (3) Mercer's AUM growth and retirement/benefits consulting demand, and (4) Oliver Wyman's project-based consulting pipeline. Revenue predictability is high. Insurance brokerage renewals are annual and sticky; Mercer manages long-duration retirement and benefits relationships; only Oliver Wyman's project-based work introduces meaningful cyclicality.

Customer concentration risk is minimal. With 95% of the Fortune 1000 as Marsh clients and operations spanning 130 countries, no single client or region dominates. Revenue is globally diversified with the US representing the largest market but not an overwhelming majority.

This is a business you can explain in two minutes: Marsh McLennan helps companies buy insurance and manage risk, and separately advises them on their people (HR, retirement, benefits) and strategy. It earns fees and commissions for this advisory work without taking on insurance risk itself. The model is asset-light, capital-light, and generates substantial free cash flow.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The world's largest risk intermediary with a fee-based model that grows with insurance premiums, risk complexity, and corporate advisory demand — all secular tailwinds.
- **Bear case:** Organic growth is mid-single-digit; the business is ultimately tied to global economic activity and corporate insurance spending, which can slow in recessions.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is transparent, well-documented, and has produced consistent results for decades.

## Red Flags
- Oliver Wyman's project-based consulting revenue is more cyclical and less predictable than the brokerage and Mercer segments
- The McGriff acquisition ($7.75B in 2024) adds integration risk and temporarily elevates debt
- Revenue growth is reported vs. underlying (organic) — acquisitions inflate headline numbers; underlying growth was 4% in 2025

## Score: 9 / 10
Marsh McLennan's business is crystal clear: fee-based insurance brokerage and consulting with no underwriting risk, highly recurring revenue, minimal customer concentration, and a model that can be explained in a single sentence.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — MRSH

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Marsh McLennan 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release, Business Insurance rankings, Morningstar analysis, InsuranceBusiness Mag, Investing.com Q2 2025 slides, MatrixBCG competitive landscape, project financials context

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Marsh McLennan has been the #1 global insurance broker by revenue for 15 consecutive years, with a commanding scale advantage over competitors | 5 |
| 2 | Client switching costs are exceptionally high — 95% of Fortune 1000 companies use Marsh, and brokerage relationships are deeply embedded in corporate risk management processes | 5 |
| 3 | 18 consecutive years of adjusted operating margin expansion demonstrates durable pricing power and operational leverage | 5 |
| 4 | Proprietary data on global risk, claims, and pricing across 130 countries creates an information asymmetry that is nearly impossible to replicate | 4 |
| 5 | The "tollbooth" position in the insurance value chain means Marsh earns on premium flow without bearing underwriting risk | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

Marsh McLennan's moat is multi-layered and among the widest in financial services. The primary source is switching costs. Corporate insurance programs are complex, multi-year constructs. A large multinational's risk transfer program involves dozens of coverage lines, hundreds of insurer relationships, proprietary data on claims history, and deep integration with the broker's analytics and placement platforms. Switching brokers is disruptive, risky (coverage gaps during transition), and expensive. This is why client retention rates in commercial brokerage are estimated at 90%+ industry-wide, and likely higher at Marsh given its dominance with the largest accounts.

Scale economics are the second moat pillar. With $27B in revenue and 95,000 employees, Marsh McLennan dwarfs competitors. The next-largest brokers — Aon ($15B revenue) and WTW ($10B revenue) — are meaningfully smaller. This scale advantage compounds: more premium placement volume gives Marsh greater leverage with insurers to negotiate favorable terms, which attracts more clients, which generates more data. The company's vast proprietary datasets on global risk, claims patterns, and pricing trends are a genuine information advantage that smaller competitors cannot match.

The "tollbooth" analogy is apt. Insurance premiums must flow through brokers. As global commercial insurance premiums grow — driven by inflation, new risk categories (cyber, climate, pandemic), and emerging market insurance penetration — Marsh's commission revenue grows without requiring proportional capital investment. This is a volume-driven business with inherent operating leverage, evidenced by 18 consecutive years of margin expansion (30bps in 2025, 80bps in 2024).

Brand and trust matter enormously in this business. When a CFO is choosing who to trust with their company's risk management, the consequences of a poor choice are severe. This naturally favors the established, credentialed incumbent. Marsh's relationships with the world's largest insurers and reinsurers give it placement capabilities that smaller brokers cannot replicate. Guy Carpenter, the #2 global reinsurance broker, reinforces this by providing end-to-end risk transfer from primary insurance through reinsurance layers.

The moat's weakest point is in the consulting segments. Oliver Wyman and Mercer, while strong brands, operate in more competitive consulting markets where switching costs are lower and project-based work is inherently less sticky. However, these businesses benefit from cross-selling into Marsh's insurance client base, giving them a distribution advantage that standalone consultancies lack.

Looking 10 years out, the moat is likely to widen. Increasing risk complexity (cyber, ESG, geopolitical) favors sophisticated advisors with global scale. Data advantages compound over time. Regulatory requirements for corporate risk management grow more stringent. There is no realistic scenario where insurance brokerage becomes commoditized or disintermediated — the advisory and placement function is too complex and high-stakes.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The widest moat in professional services: 15 years as #1 broker, 18 years of margin expansion, 90%+ retention, and a tollbooth position that grows with global insurance premiums.
- **Bear case:** Consolidation among competitors (Aon-WTW merger attempt) could narrow the scale gap; consulting segments face more competitive dynamics than brokerage.
- **Confidence:** High — The evidence for moat durability is overwhelming across retention, margins, market share, and structural competitive positioning.

## Red Flags
- Aon and WTW remain credible competitors at scale, particularly for the largest multinational accounts
- Legacy technology systems and data silos between brands (Marsh, Mercer, Oliver Wyman) may limit the data moat's full potential
- The McGriff acquisition adds mid-market exposure that faces more competitive dynamics than the large-account market
- Regulatory risk: insurance brokerage compensation transparency has been an issue historically (Spitzer investigation in 2004)

## Score: 9 / 10
Marsh McLennan possesses a wide, multi-layered moat built on switching costs, scale, data, and a tollbooth position in the insurance value chain, supported by 18 years of consecutive margin expansion and 15 years as the #1 global broker — this is among the most durable competitive positions in financial services.

# Management & Capital Allocation — MRSH

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Marsh McLennan 2025 proxy statement, SEC Form 4 filings, Q4 2025 earnings release, Simply Wall St, GuruFocus, TipRanks buyback data, Yahoo Finance, Investing.com earnings transcript, project financials context

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Capital allocation is disciplined and balanced: $4.5B deployed in 2025 across dividends, buybacks, and acquisitions, with $5B planned for 2026 | 5 |
| 2 | 15 consecutive years of dividend increases with a 10% raise in 2025 (to $3.43/share annualized) demonstrates shareholder commitment | 4 |
| 3 | CEO John Doyle's direct ownership is modest (~$17M, 0.015% of shares) — meaningful but not founder-level skin in the game | 3 |
| 4 | The McGriff acquisition ($7.75B, largest in company history) was strategically sound but pushed debt to 2.9x EBITDA | 4 |
| 5 | Share count has declined from ~500M+ to ~494M shares through consistent buybacks ($300M/quarter cadence in recent periods) | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

Marsh McLennan's management team, led by CEO John Doyle since 2023, operates with the discipline of a well-run institution rather than a founder-led company. Doyle came up through the Marsh brokerage business and speaks the language of a practitioner — earnings calls focus on organic growth rates, margin progression, and client outcomes rather than vague strategic visions. The management bench is deep, with long-tenured segment leaders across Marsh, Guy Carpenter, Mercer, and Oliver Wyman.

Capital allocation follows a clear hierarchy: (1) organic investment and hiring, (2) dividends, (3) acquisitions, and (4) share repurchases. The 2025 deployment of $4.5B and planned 2026 deployment of $5B represent roughly 90-100% of free cash flow being returned or deployed, which is appropriate for a mature, asset-light business. The dividend has grown for 15 consecutive years with the most recent increase of 10%, and the current 2.07% yield is well-covered by earnings (41% payout ratio on adjusted EPS of $9.75). Share repurchases have been consistent at roughly $300M/quarter, reducing the share count modestly over time. The buyback approach is steady rather than opportunistic — they buy in all market conditions, which is fine for a compounder but means they are not backing up the truck when shares are cheap.

The elephant in the room is the McGriff acquisition. At $7.75B, it was the largest deal in company history — adding $1.3B in annual revenue and 3,500 employees to the mid-market insurance brokerage platform. The strategic logic is sound: mid-market insurance brokerage is fragmented, fast-growing, and generates sticky recurring revenue. McGriff is expected to be accretive from 2026. However, the deal pushed debt to $21.4B (2.9x EBITDA), and the company is spending $450-500M on retention incentives over three years. This is not reckless leverage for a business with this cash flow profile, but it represents a meaningful increase from the company's historically conservative balance sheet.

On insider ownership, John Doyle directly holds ~87,000 shares worth approximately $15-17M. This is meaningful compensation-level ownership but not the kind of concentrated stake that aligns management with shareholders at a visceral level. The September 2025 Form 4 filing showed Doyle exercising 21,079 options at $63.09 and immediately selling the shares at $205.65 — routine option monetization but not a confidence signal. Executive compensation is structured with 50% stock options and 50% PSUs for long-term incentives, which ties payouts to share price appreciation and per-share metrics. The 2025 long-term incentive grant of $16.75M (up 22% from $13.75M) is generous but typical for a company of this size.

Governance is solid. The board is entirely independent except for the CEO, with annual director elections and no dual-class share structure. The recent addition of Peter Harrison (former Schroders CEO) to the board adds capital allocation expertise at a time when the company is managing elevated M&A activity and a larger capital deployment budget. The company's 18 consecutive years of margin expansion speaks to institutional discipline that transcends any individual CEO.

Management communicates clearly and consistently. Guidance has been reliable — they guided to mid-single-digit organic growth and margin expansion, and they delivered. They do not overpromise. The Thrive cost-savings program ($400M over 3 years) is a specific, measurable commitment rather than an aspirational target.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Institutional discipline that has produced 18 years of margin expansion, 15 years of dividend growth, and consistent capital returns — management is a reliable steward of a compounding machine.
- **Bear case:** Insider ownership is institutional-grade rather than owner-operator level; the McGriff acquisition represents a step-change in M&A ambition that could lead to further large deals at elevated prices.
- **Confidence:** High — The track record of execution, capital discipline, and transparent communication is extensive and verifiable.

## Red Flags
- CEO's direct ownership (~$17M) is modest relative to total compensation (~$23M in 2024); incentive alignment depends primarily on equity-linked compensation rather than outright ownership
- McGriff deal elevated debt to 2.9x EBITDA, temporarily above the company's historical comfort zone
- CEO sold shares via option exercise in September 2025 — routine but directionally negative signal at these prices
- Executive compensation has been rising meaningfully (22% increase in LTI grants), which deserves monitoring
- Buyback program is mechanical rather than opportunistic, meaning shares are repurchased regardless of valuation

## Score: 7 / 10
Marsh McLennan's management is a competent institutional steward with an exceptional long-term track record of margin expansion and capital returns, but modest insider ownership and a mechanical (rather than opportunistic) approach to capital allocation prevent a higher score — this is professional management, not owner-operators.

# Business Economics — MRSH

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Marsh McLennan FY2025 earnings release (Jan 2026), Q3 2025 investor presentation, 10-K filings (2022-2024), MacroTrends historical financials, yfinance data, insurance industry research

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC of 14.0% on a large invested capital base of $34.7B, with ROE at 29.3% — solid but not exceptional for an asset-light broker | 4 |
| 2 | 18 consecutive years of adjusted operating margin expansion, reaching ~27% adjusted operating margin in FY2025 | 5 |
| 3 | FCF of $5.0B with 120% FCF conversion ratio — cash generation significantly exceeds reported net income | 5 |
| 4 | Revenue grew ~10% in FY2025 to $27.0B (4% organic + McGriff acquisition), with underlying growth consistently 4-5% for years | 4 |
| 5 | CapEx of just $291M on $27B revenue (1.1% of sales) confirms extremely asset-light model | 4 |
| 6 | Insurance brokerage revenue is highly recurring with client retention rates estimated at 90%+ industry-wide for large brokers | 5 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Marsh McLennan's ROIC of 14.0% is respectable but diluted by the substantial goodwill and intangibles from acquisitions — most notably the $7.75B McGriff deal closed in November 2024. Stripping out goodwill, returns on tangible capital are dramatically higher, reflecting the true asset-light economics of insurance broking and consulting. ROE of 29.3% tells the more accurate story: this is a business that generates exceptional returns on the equity actually required to operate. Over FY2022-FY2025, ROE has remained consistently above 25%, and ROIC has stayed in the 13-15% range even as the denominator swelled with acquisition-driven intangibles. The gap between ROIC and ROE is a structural feature of serial acquirers in professional services — it does not indicate poor capital allocation.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** The 18-year streak of adjusted margin expansion is remarkable and arguably the single most impressive metric in Marsh's profile. Gross margins of 42.3% and operating margins of 23.1% (GAAP) reflect the compounding benefit of scale in a brokerage model: incremental revenue carries minimal variable cost. Adjusted operating income grew 11% on 10% revenue growth in FY2025, demonstrating continued operating leverage. The consulting segment (Mercer + Oliver Wyman) carries somewhat lower margins than the insurance broking segment (Marsh + Guy Carpenter), but the blended margin trajectory is clearly upward. SBC of $394M is moderate at 1.5% of revenue and does not materially distort the margin picture.

**Cash Generation.** FCF of $5.0B represents a 25% increase year-over-year and converts at 120% of net income — a hallmark of high-quality businesses where depreciation exceeds maintenance capex and working capital is favorable. Owner earnings of $4.8B (NI $4.2B + D&A $910M - CapEx $291M) are essentially equivalent to FCF, confirming minimal working capital drag. The company deployed $2.0B in share buybacks (its largest ever annual amount) and funded a $7.75B acquisition, yet still maintained $2.7B in cash. This level of cash generation provides enormous strategic flexibility.

**Capital Intensity and Asset-Lightness.** CapEx at $291M on $27B revenue is strikingly low — just 1.1% of sales. This is a people-and-relationships business: Marsh places insurance, Guy Carpenter places reinsurance, Mercer advises on HR/benefits, and Oliver Wyman consults on strategy. None require factories, heavy equipment, or physical infrastructure beyond offices and IT systems. The entire CapEx line is essentially maintenance. This asset-light structure is why FCF conversion consistently exceeds 100%.

**Revenue Quality.** Insurance brokerage is among the most recurring revenue models in financial services. Policies renew annually, and the broker of record typically retains the client unless actively displaced — large commercial brokers like Marsh report retention rates above 90%. Revenue is further supported by rising insurance premiums (a secular tailwind in the current hard-market environment) and organic cross-selling into the existing client base. The consulting businesses (Mercer, Oliver Wyman) are somewhat more cyclical and project-based, but Mercer's defined-benefit and health administration businesses carry multi-year contracts. Underlying revenue growth of 4% in FY2025 sits on top of an already large base and has been remarkably consistent (4-5% organic growth for over a decade).

**Operating Leverage Assessment.** Adjusted operating income grew 11% versus 10% revenue growth in FY2025 — positive operating leverage, though modest. The company has demonstrated it can expand margins while investing in growth (headcount, technology, acquisitions). The consulting segment occasionally compresses margins during investment cycles, but the insurance broking segment reliably delivers positive operating leverage due to its scale advantages and network effects.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The world's largest insurance broker with 18 consecutive years of margin expansion, 90%+ client retention, and $5B in annual FCF on minimal capex — a compounding machine with durable structural advantages.
- **Bear case:** ROIC of 14% is good-not-great due to acquisition-heavy capital deployment, and the McGriff deal added significant debt, which could weigh on returns if integration stumbles.
- **Confidence:** High — Insurance broking economics are well-understood, Marsh's financial track record is long and consistent, and the business model is proven through multiple cycles.

## Red Flags
- ROIC diluted by $7.75B McGriff acquisition goodwill; true economic returns harder to assess until integration matures
- Consulting segment (Mercer, Oliver Wyman) carries lower margins and more cyclical revenue than broking
- Interest expense jumped from $700M to $960M in FY2025, absorbing a growing share of operating income
- SBC of $394M is modest but represents real dilution (~0.8% of market cap annually)

## Score: 8 / 10
Marsh McLennan's business economics are strong — asset-light model, 120% FCF conversion, 18 years of margin expansion, and highly recurring revenue — but ROIC of 14% (depressed by acquisition-laden goodwill) and the step-up in debt-funded M&A prevent a top-tier score.

# Balance Sheet Safety — MRSH

**Analyst Role:** Balance Sheet Safety Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-24
**Data Sources:** Marsh McLennan FY2025 earnings release (Jan 2026), 10-K filings (2023-2024), SEC 8-K filings for senior notes offerings, MacroTrends balance sheet data, investor presentations, yfinance data

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Total debt of $21.4B with Debt/EBITDA of 2.9x — elevated but manageable for a business with $5B annual FCF | 5 |
| 2 | Interest coverage declined from 9.7x (FY2022) to 6.8x (FY2025) due to $7.25B senior notes issuance to fund McGriff | 5 |
| 3 | Debt maturity profile is well-laddered with tranches extending to 2055; no single maturity exceeds $2B | 4 |
| 4 | $5.0B in annual FCF can service $960M in interest expense 5.2x and retire $600M near-term maturity without stress | 4 |
| 5 | Current ratio of 1.1x is thin but typical for insurance brokers who hold fiduciary cash (matched liabilities) | 3 |
| 6 | $7.75B McGriff acquisition funded primarily with debt represents a deliberate leverage-up with a clear deleveraging path | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Marsh McLennan carries $21.4B in total debt, with ~$19.3B in senior unsecured notes and ~$250M in subsidiary debt as of December 31, 2025. This represents a significant increase from ~$11.8B at year-end 2023, driven almost entirely by the $7.25B senior notes offering in October 2024 to fund the $7.75B McGriff acquisition. Debt/EBITDA at 2.9x is above the company's historical range of 1.5-2.0x but remains within investment-grade comfort zones. Marsh maintains investment-grade ratings (A-/A3), which provide access to favorable refinancing terms. The debt is entirely fixed-rate senior unsecured notes (with the exception of $300M in floating-rate notes due 2027), eliminating interest rate risk on the vast majority of the debt stack. The maturity schedule is well-laddered: $600M matures in Q1 2026, with subsequent tranches spread across 2027, 2030, 2031, 2035, 2036, 2044, and 2055. No single year presents a refinancing cliff.

**Interest Coverage Deterioration.** The decline in interest coverage from 9.7x in FY2022 to 6.8x in FY2025 is the most notable balance sheet concern. This is almost entirely explained by the McGriff financing: interest expense jumped from $700M to $960M year-over-year. However, 6.8x coverage on $960M of interest still provides a substantial cushion. Operating income of $6.2B could absorb a further 40% decline before interest coverage would breach 3.0x (a typical credit-distress threshold). Management has signaled intent to deleverage back toward 2.0-2.5x Debt/EBITDA over the next 2-3 years through organic EBITDA growth and modest debt paydown, which would restore coverage toward 8-9x.

**Liquidity Position.** Cash of $2.7B plus the $600M near-term maturity being easily covered by FCF ($5.0B annual run-rate) makes the liquidity position adequate. The current ratio of 1.1x appears thin in isolation, but this is structurally normal for insurance brokers. A significant portion of current liabilities consists of fiduciary funds owed to insurers — these are matched by fiduciary assets held in trust, creating an optical mismatch that does not represent actual liquidity risk. Marsh also maintains a $3.0B revolving credit facility (undrawn as of year-end 2025) as a backstop.

**Refinancing Risk.** With investment-grade ratings, a well-laddered maturity profile, and no single-year maturity exceeding $2B, refinancing risk is low. The October 2024 offering demonstrated strong market access — Marsh placed $7.25B across seven tranches at coupons ranging from 4.55% to 5.40%, even in a higher-rate environment. The weighted average cost of debt is estimated at ~4.5%, which is manageable relative to the company's 14% ROIC. A credit crunch scenario (e.g., spreads widen 200bps) would increase financing costs but not threaten solvency — the earliest large maturity requiring refinancing is likely 2027 ($950M + $300M floating rate).

**Off-Balance-Sheet Considerations.** Marsh carries operating lease obligations (offices globally), pension liabilities through Mercer (though Mercer's own pension obligations have been significantly de-risked over the past decade), and contingent liabilities from errors & omissions (E&O) insurance claims — a standard industry risk. None of these represent material threats to solvency. The company divested Mercer's UK pension administration and US health benefits businesses as part of its post-McGriff portfolio rationalization, reducing off-balance-sheet complexity.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** Revenue drops from $27B to $18.9B. Assuming operating margins compress from 23% to 15% (substantial deleverage), operating income would fall to ~$2.8B. Interest expense at $960M would still be covered 2.9x. FCF would decline sharply — perhaps to $1.5-2.0B — but would remain positive. The company could cover all debt maturities through 2028 from operating cash flow alone, without accessing capital markets. Equity issuance would not be required.
