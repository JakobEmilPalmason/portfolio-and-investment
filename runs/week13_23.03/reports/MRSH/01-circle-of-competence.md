## Detailed Analysis

Marsh McLennan Companies operates as a professional services firm organized around risk, insurance, and consulting. The business is straightforward: it acts as an intermediary between companies seeking insurance coverage and the insurers who underwrite that risk. Marsh, the flagship segment generating 54% of revenue, places commercial insurance and arranges risk transfer programs for corporate clients. Guy Carpenter (10% of revenue) does the same at the reinsurance layer — helping insurers themselves manage their risk portfolios. Together, Risk and Insurance Services accounts for roughly 64% of total revenue.

The remaining 36% comes from Consulting. Mercer (23% of revenue) provides HR consulting, retirement plan administration, investment advisory, and health benefits design. Oliver Wyman (13%) is a management consulting firm focused on financial services, aviation, and other specialized industries. While less obviously connected to the insurance brokerage core, both consulting arms cross-sell into the same corporate client base and reinforce the "trusted advisor" relationship.

The revenue model is a blend of commissions (percentage of insurance premiums placed) and fees (flat or retainer-based for consulting and advisory work). This is critical: Marsh McLennan does not bear underwriting risk. It earns regardless of whether claims occur. Revenue rises naturally with insurance premium inflation, which has been positive in most years. The company also benefits from the secular trend of increasing global risk complexity — cyber threats, climate events, regulatory change — which drives demand for sophisticated risk advisory.

Key revenue drivers are: (1) global commercial insurance premium volumes and pricing trends, (2) corporate demand for risk transfer and advisory, (3) Mercer's AUM growth and retirement/benefits consulting demand, and (4) Oliver Wyman's project-based consulting pipeline. Revenue predictability is high. Insurance brokerage renewals are annual and sticky; Mercer manages long-duration retirement and benefits relationships; only Oliver Wyman's project-based work introduces meaningful cyclicality.

Customer concentration risk is minimal. With 95% of the Fortune 1000 as Marsh clients and operations spanning 130 countries, no single client or region dominates. Revenue is globally diversified with the US representing the largest market but not an overwhelming majority.

This is a business you can explain in two minutes: Marsh McLennan helps companies buy insurance and manage risk, and separately advises them on their people (HR, retirement, benefits) and strategy. It earns fees and commissions for this advisory work without taking on insurance risk itself. The model is asset-light, capital-light, and generates substantial free cash flow.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The world's largest risk intermediary with a fee-based model that grows with insurance premiums, risk complexity, and corporate advisory demand — all secular tailwinds.
- **Bear case:** Organic growth is mid-single-digit; the business is ultimately tied to global economic activity and corporate insurance spending, which can slow in recessions.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is transparent, well-documented, and has produced consistent results for decades.

## Red Flags
- Oliver Wyman's project-based consulting revenue is more cyclical and less predictable than the brokerage and Mercer segments
- The McGriff acquisition ($7.75B in 2024) adds integration risk and temporarily elevates debt
- Revenue growth is reported vs. underlying (organic) — acquisitions inflate headline numbers; underlying growth was 4% in 2025

## Score: 9 / 10
Marsh McLennan's business is crystal clear: fee-based insurance brokerage and consulting with no underwriting risk, highly recurring revenue, minimal customer concentration, and a model that can be explained in a single sentence.
