## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Marsh McLennan's ROIC of 14.0% is respectable but diluted by the substantial goodwill and intangibles from acquisitions — most notably the $7.75B McGriff deal closed in November 2024. Stripping out goodwill, returns on tangible capital are dramatically higher, reflecting the true asset-light economics of insurance broking and consulting. ROE of 29.3% tells the more accurate story: this is a business that generates exceptional returns on the equity actually required to operate. Over FY2022-FY2025, ROE has remained consistently above 25%, and ROIC has stayed in the 13-15% range even as the denominator swelled with acquisition-driven intangibles. The gap between ROIC and ROE is a structural feature of serial acquirers in professional services — it does not indicate poor capital allocation.

**Margin Structure and Operating Leverage.** The 18-year streak of adjusted margin expansion is remarkable and arguably the single most impressive metric in Marsh's profile. Gross margins of 42.3% and operating margins of 23.1% (GAAP) reflect the compounding benefit of scale in a brokerage model: incremental revenue carries minimal variable cost. Adjusted operating income grew 11% on 10% revenue growth in FY2025, demonstrating continued operating leverage. The consulting segment (Mercer + Oliver Wyman) carries somewhat lower margins than the insurance broking segment (Marsh + Guy Carpenter), but the blended margin trajectory is clearly upward. SBC of $394M is moderate at 1.5% of revenue and does not materially distort the margin picture.

**Cash Generation.** FCF of $5.0B represents a 25% increase year-over-year and converts at 120% of net income — a hallmark of high-quality businesses where depreciation exceeds maintenance capex and working capital is favorable. Owner earnings of $4.8B (NI $4.2B + D&A $910M - CapEx $291M) are essentially equivalent to FCF, confirming minimal working capital drag. The company deployed $2.0B in share buybacks (its largest ever annual amount) and funded a $7.75B acquisition, yet still maintained $2.7B in cash. This level of cash generation provides enormous strategic flexibility.

**Capital Intensity and Asset-Lightness.** CapEx at $291M on $27B revenue is strikingly low — just 1.1% of sales. This is a people-and-relationships business: Marsh places insurance, Guy Carpenter places reinsurance, Mercer advises on HR/benefits, and Oliver Wyman consults on strategy. None require factories, heavy equipment, or physical infrastructure beyond offices and IT systems. The entire CapEx line is essentially maintenance. This asset-light structure is why FCF conversion consistently exceeds 100%.

**Revenue Quality.** Insurance brokerage is among the most recurring revenue models in financial services. Policies renew annually, and the broker of record typically retains the client unless actively displaced — large commercial brokers like Marsh report retention rates above 90%. Revenue is further supported by rising insurance premiums (a secular tailwind in the current hard-market environment) and organic cross-selling into the existing client base. The consulting businesses (Mercer, Oliver Wyman) are somewhat more cyclical and project-based, but Mercer's defined-benefit and health administration businesses carry multi-year contracts. Underlying revenue growth of 4% in FY2025 sits on top of an already large base and has been remarkably consistent (4-5% organic growth for over a decade).

**Operating Leverage Assessment.** Adjusted operating income grew 11% versus 10% revenue growth in FY2025 — positive operating leverage, though modest. The company has demonstrated it can expand margins while investing in growth (headcount, technology, acquisitions). The consulting segment occasionally compresses margins during investment cycles, but the insurance broking segment reliably delivers positive operating leverage due to its scale advantages and network effects.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The world's largest insurance broker with 18 consecutive years of margin expansion, 90%+ client retention, and $5B in annual FCF on minimal capex — a compounding machine with durable structural advantages.
- **Bear case:** ROIC of 14% is good-not-great due to acquisition-heavy capital deployment, and the McGriff deal added significant debt, which could weigh on returns if integration stumbles.
- **Confidence:** High — Insurance broking economics are well-understood, Marsh's financial track record is long and consistent, and the business model is proven through multiple cycles.

## Red Flags
- ROIC diluted by $7.75B McGriff acquisition goodwill; true economic returns harder to assess until integration matures
- Consulting segment (Mercer, Oliver Wyman) carries lower margins and more cyclical revenue than broking
- Interest expense jumped from $700M to $960M in FY2025, absorbing a growing share of operating income
- SBC of $394M is modest but represents real dilution (~0.8% of market cap annually)

## Score: 8 / 10
Marsh McLennan's business economics are strong — asset-light model, 120% FCF conversion, 18 years of margin expansion, and highly recurring revenue — but ROIC of 14% (depressed by acquisition-laden goodwill) and the step-up in debt-funded M&A prevent a top-tier score.
