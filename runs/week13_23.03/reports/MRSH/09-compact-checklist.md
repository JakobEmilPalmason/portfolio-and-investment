# Compact Checklist — MRSH

**Date:** 2026-03-24

1. **Business**: Marsh McLennan earns fees and commissions by placing insurance and advising on risk for 95% of the Fortune 1000, without bearing any underwriting risk itself.
2. **Moat**: Switching brokers means migrating decades of claims data, coverage structures, and insurer relationships — 90%+ retention rates and 18 consecutive years of margin expansion prove the switching costs are real.
3. **Economics**: $5.0B FCF on $291M capex (120% conversion), 29% ROE, and 42% gross margins confirm this is an asset-light toll collector that turns almost every dollar of profit into cash.
4. **Management**: Capital allocation is disciplined — 15 years of dividend increases, consistent buybacks, and reliable mid-single-digit organic growth — but insider ownership is modest (~$17M for the CEO) and the $7.75B McGriff deal was funded entirely with debt.
5. **Debt risk**: At 2.9x Debt/EBITDA with $5B annual FCF covering $960M interest expense 5.2x, Marsh can service its debt even if revenue drops 30% for two years — the company survived 2008 with only a 5% revenue decline.
6. **Price**: At 15.3x forward earnings versus a historical 20-25x range, the market must believe organic growth has permanently downshifted to 3% and McGriff integration will destroy value — neither is likely for the world's dominant insurance broker.
7. **Margin of safety**: Conservative IV of $231 puts MOS at 25%, the stock trades below the worst-case cell in the sensitivity grid ($216), and Monte Carlo gives 99.9% probability of undervaluation — the downside to bear IV is only 10%.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Organic growth below 2% for two consecutive years or Debt/EBITDA exceeding 3.5x without a clear paydown path would signal the compounding engine has stalled and McGriff was a strategic error.
