## Detailed Analysis

Marsh McLennan's moat is multi-layered and among the widest in financial services. The primary source is switching costs. Corporate insurance programs are complex, multi-year constructs. A large multinational's risk transfer program involves dozens of coverage lines, hundreds of insurer relationships, proprietary data on claims history, and deep integration with the broker's analytics and placement platforms. Switching brokers is disruptive, risky (coverage gaps during transition), and expensive. This is why client retention rates in commercial brokerage are estimated at 90%+ industry-wide, and likely higher at Marsh given its dominance with the largest accounts.

Scale economics are the second moat pillar. With $27B in revenue and 95,000 employees, Marsh McLennan dwarfs competitors. The next-largest brokers — Aon ($15B revenue) and WTW ($10B revenue) — are meaningfully smaller. This scale advantage compounds: more premium placement volume gives Marsh greater leverage with insurers to negotiate favorable terms, which attracts more clients, which generates more data. The company's vast proprietary datasets on global risk, claims patterns, and pricing trends are a genuine information advantage that smaller competitors cannot match.

The "tollbooth" analogy is apt. Insurance premiums must flow through brokers. As global commercial insurance premiums grow — driven by inflation, new risk categories (cyber, climate, pandemic), and emerging market insurance penetration — Marsh's commission revenue grows without requiring proportional capital investment. This is a volume-driven business with inherent operating leverage, evidenced by 18 consecutive years of margin expansion (30bps in 2025, 80bps in 2024).

Brand and trust matter enormously in this business. When a CFO is choosing who to trust with their company's risk management, the consequences of a poor choice are severe. This naturally favors the established, credentialed incumbent. Marsh's relationships with the world's largest insurers and reinsurers give it placement capabilities that smaller brokers cannot replicate. Guy Carpenter, the #2 global reinsurance broker, reinforces this by providing end-to-end risk transfer from primary insurance through reinsurance layers.

The moat's weakest point is in the consulting segments. Oliver Wyman and Mercer, while strong brands, operate in more competitive consulting markets where switching costs are lower and project-based work is inherently less sticky. However, these businesses benefit from cross-selling into Marsh's insurance client base, giving them a distribution advantage that standalone consultancies lack.

Looking 10 years out, the moat is likely to widen. Increasing risk complexity (cyber, ESG, geopolitical) favors sophisticated advisors with global scale. Data advantages compound over time. Regulatory requirements for corporate risk management grow more stringent. There is no realistic scenario where insurance brokerage becomes commoditized or disintermediated — the advisory and placement function is too complex and high-stakes.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The widest moat in professional services: 15 years as #1 broker, 18 years of margin expansion, 90%+ retention, and a tollbooth position that grows with global insurance premiums.
- **Bear case:** Consolidation among competitors (Aon-WTW merger attempt) could narrow the scale gap; consulting segments face more competitive dynamics than brokerage.
- **Confidence:** High — The evidence for moat durability is overwhelming across retention, margins, market share, and structural competitive positioning.

## Red Flags
- Aon and WTW remain credible competitors at scale, particularly for the largest multinational accounts
- Legacy technology systems and data silos between brands (Marsh, Mercer, Oliver Wyman) may limit the data moat's full potential
- The McGriff acquisition adds mid-market exposure that faces more competitive dynamics than the large-account market
- Regulatory risk: insurance brokerage compensation transparency has been an issue historically (Spitzer investigation in 2004)

## Score: 9 / 10
Marsh McLennan possesses a wide, multi-layered moat built on switching costs, scale, data, and a tollbooth position in the insurance value chain, supported by 18 years of consecutive margin expansion and 15 years as the #1 global broker — this is among the most durable competitive positions in financial services.
