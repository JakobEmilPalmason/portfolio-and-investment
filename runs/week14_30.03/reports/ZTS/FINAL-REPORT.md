## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Zoetis sells medicines, vaccines, and diagnostics for companion animals (~65% of $9.5B revenue) and livestock (~35%), with repeat purchases driven by pet populations and herd health rather than discretionary spending.
2. **Moat**: Regulatory barriers requiring 5-10 year species-specific FDA/EMA approvals, deep vet switching costs, and 17% global market share built over 60+ years deliver 72% gross margins — 10+ points above industry average.
3. **Economics**: ROIC expanded from 18.9% to 24.1% over four years, owner earnings reached $2.5B, and revenue grew 17% while EPS grew 34% on an asset-light model requiring only 5% of revenue in maintenance capex.
4. **Management**: Compensation is 92% incentive-based with PSUs that vested at 0% when TSR lagged, M&A has been disciplined bolt-ons, but $3.2B in buybacks at $140-170 funded partly by $1.75B in convertible debt looks expensive with shares now at $118.
5. **Debt risk**: Interest coverage of 16x, Debt/EBITDA of 2.3x, and $2.3B in cash against well-laddered maturities (nothing due in 2026) mean Zoetis survives even a 30% revenue decline — though the 37% one-year debt increase for buybacks deserves monitoring.
6. **Price**: At 15x EV/EBITDA versus a 13-year median of 24x, the market is pricing in permanent growth deceleration to 2-3% and an elevated risk premium from Librela safety concerns — both of which must persist for the current price to be fair.
7. **Margin of safety**: The 72% gross margins, 24% ROIC, and diversified $9B+ non-Librela revenue base protect earnings power, but the stock sits 35.6% above the $87 bear-case IV, so there is business protection but no price cushion against the worst scenario.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if the FDA recalls or materially restricts Librela prescribing, organic revenue growth falls below 2% for two consecutive years, or net debt exceeds 4x EBITDA without a credible deleveraging plan.

## Key Risks

1. **Librela franchise impairment** (Likelihood: Medium, Impact: High) — FDA adverse event reports include neurological effects and deaths in dogs; Q4 2025 revenue declined 32%; tail risk of recall or litigation is not priced in.
2. **Growth deceleration to commodity pharma rates** (Likelihood: Medium, Impact: Medium) — 2026 guidance of 3-5% organic growth is below the historic 7-9% range; if this becomes permanent, the stock deserves 14-16x EV/EBITDA, not 20-25x.
3. **Debt-funded buybacks at elevated prices** (Likelihood: Already occurred, Impact: Medium) — $3.2B in FY2025 buybacks at $140-170, partly funded by $1.75B convertible notes, was value-destructive given today's $118 price and raises leverage to 2.3x Debt/EBITDA.
4. **Competitive disruption in companion animal** (Likelihood: Low-Medium, Impact: Medium) — Elanco's Zenrelia and Credelio Quattro are gaining traction; biosimilar risk to CYTOPOINT could pressure margins.
5. **FX headwinds on international revenue** (Likelihood: Medium, Impact: Low) — ~50% of revenue is international, exposing results to USD strength vs EUR, BRL, JPY.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) Librela quarterly revenue stabilizes or shows sequential improvement for 2+ consecutive quarters; (2) stock declines to $105-110 approaching Monte Carlo P5, providing genuine price margin of safety; (3) CYTOPOINT long-acting approval creates a new growth lever in late 2026.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) FDA recalls or materially restricts Librela prescribing; (2) organic revenue growth falls below 2% for two consecutive years; (3) net debt exceeds 4x EBITDA without a credible deleveraging plan.

# Circle of Competence — ZTS

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Data Sources:** Zoetis FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Zoetis 10-K filing (2025), Yahoo Finance, StockAnalysis.com, Zoetis investor relations, Morningstar

| 3 | Top 10 product lines account for ~57% of revenue; Simparica franchise ($1.5B) and dermatology portfolio ($1.7B) are the two largest growth engines | 5 |
| 4 | Revenue is highly recurring: veterinary medicines and vaccines are repeat purchases driven by pet populations and herd health, not discretionary spending | 5 |
| 5 | Geographic split is roughly 54% U.S. ($5.1B) and 46% International ($4.3B), providing genuine global diversification | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Zoetis sell, and to whom?** Zoetis is the world's largest pure-play animal health company. It sells pharmaceutical products (parasiticides, anti-infectives, pain/sedation drugs), vaccines, dermatology treatments, and diagnostics for companion animals (dogs, cats, horses) and livestock (cattle, swine, poultry, fish, sheep). Its customers are veterinarians, livestock producers, and pet owners — with veterinarians serving as the primary gatekeepers and prescribers. The company was spun out of Pfizer in 2013, inheriting over 60 years of animal health expertise.

**How does it make money?** The revenue model is product sales — Zoetis manufactures and sells branded pharmaceuticals and biologics through direct sales forces and distributors. There is no subscription or platform component. Revenue is recognized on product shipment/delivery to distributors and veterinary clinics. The model is simple: develop or acquire a drug, get regulatory approval, sell it through vet channels at high gross margins (71.8% in FY2025). The company operates in seven product categories, with parasiticides and dermatology being the largest and fastest-growing.

**Key drivers.** The business is driven by 2-4 variables: (1) global pet population growth and the humanization-of-pets trend, which increases spending per animal; (2) new product launches and lifecycle management of existing blockbusters like Simparica Trio and Apoquel; (3) livestock productivity demands as global protein consumption rises; and (4) pricing power within the veterinary channel, where brand trust and regulatory barriers support premium pricing. The companion animal segment has been the primary growth engine, though livestock delivered a strong 8% organic growth in FY2025.

**Revenue predictability.** Animal health spending is among the most recurring categories in healthcare. Pets need parasite prevention monthly, vaccines annually, and dermatology treatments on an ongoing basis. Livestock producers treat herds as a cost of production. This is not cyclical discretionary spending — it sits closer to consumer staples in predictability. The 2022-2025 revenue trajectory ($8.1B to $9.5B) shows steady mid-single-digit growth with no revenue declines, even through economic uncertainty. The 2026 guide of $9.8-10.0B implies continued 3-5% organic growth.

**Customer concentration.** Customer concentration is low. Zoetis sells to tens of thousands of veterinary clinics and livestock operations globally. No single customer represents a material share of revenue. Distribution is through major veterinary distributors (Patterson, Henry Schein) but these are channels, not end customers. The risk of losing any single customer is negligible.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** Easily. Zoetis makes medicines and vaccines for animals. Vets prescribe them, pet owners and farmers pay for them. More pets, more spending per pet, more livestock needing treatment — that is the entire thesis. The products are branded, regulated, and repurchased regularly. It is one of the most understandable businesses in healthcare.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The pet humanization megatrend, strong product pipeline, and recurring nature of animal health spending make Zoetis a predictable compounder with a business model anyone can understand.
- **Bear case:** Revenue concentration in two product franchises (Simparica and dermatology) creates event risk if competition erodes share in these categories.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is transparent, well-documented, and has been stable for over a decade since the Pfizer spinoff.

## Red Flags
- None identified for business clarity. This is among the most understandable businesses in the healthcare universe.

## Score: 9 / 10
Zoetis is a textbook Circle of Competence business: simple product-for-payment model, recurring demand driven by pet and livestock populations, low customer concentration, and explainable in under two minutes. The only reason it is not a 10 is the modest complexity of tracking seven product categories across eight species and two geographies.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ZTS

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Data Sources:** Zoetis FY2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Zoetis 10-K, Morningstar moat analysis, Investing.com, MarketsAndMarkets veterinary biologics report, Grand View Research animal health competitive landscape, company investor presentations

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Zoetis holds ~17% global animal health market share and ~25% of companion animal health — dominant #1 position with no close pure-play peer | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margins of 71.8% (10+ points above industry average) sustained over a decade demonstrate pricing power and cost advantages at scale | 5 |
| 3 | Regulatory moat is significant: animal drug approvals require species-specific clinical trials, and the FDA/EMA approval process takes 5-10 years, creating high barriers to entry | 5 |
| 4 | Librela/Solensia mAb franchise declined 3% operationally in 2025 (Q4 down 11%) due to social media backlash over side effects, showing moat vulnerability in novel product categories | 4 |
| 5 | Elanco's Zenrelia and Credelio Quattro are posting strong early demand, representing the first serious competitive threat to Zoetis's companion animal dominance in years | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Moat type identification.** Zoetis benefits from multiple reinforcing moat sources. The primary moats are: (1) **Regulatory barriers** — every animal health product requires species-specific regulatory approval, which is costly and time-consuming. Zoetis has the largest portfolio of approved products globally, built over 60+ years. A new entrant cannot replicate this overnight. (2) **Switching costs** — veterinarians develop treatment protocols around specific products and are reluctant to switch due to familiarity, patient history, and the risk of adverse outcomes with unfamiliar drugs. (3) **Scale economics** — with $9.5B in revenue, Zoetis can spread R&D costs ($625M+/year) across a much larger base than any competitor. This allows investment in niche species and indications that smaller players cannot justify. (4) **Brand and trust** — the Zoetis sales force has deep relationships with veterinarians built over decades; trust in animal pharma is critical because vets stake their reputation on treatment outcomes.

**Durability — will it exist in 10 years?** The moat sources are structural and slow-moving. Regulatory barriers are not diminishing. Pet humanization is a generational trend that increases willingness to pay for branded animal health products. The vet-Zoetis relationship is reinforced by diagnostics integration and data services. The biggest durability question is whether monoclonal antibody competition (Elanco, others) can erode Zoetis's share in high-growth companion animal categories. However, Zoetis's breadth across parasiticides, vaccines, dermatology, pain, and diagnostics means no single product loss would break the moat. The company will almost certainly still be the #1 animal health company in 10 years.

**Evidence in numbers.** Gross margins have been 70-72% for years, well above the ~60% industry average. ROIC has expanded from 18.9% (2022) to 24.1% (2025), indicating the moat is widening, not narrowing. Operating margins at 38% are best-in-class. The Simparica franchise crossed $1B in U.S. sales with 12% operational growth in 2025. Revenue has compounded at ~5-6% annually without acquisitive growth, suggesting organic pricing power and volume gains. Market share has been stable to growing.

**Where is the moat weakest?** The moat is most vulnerable in newer therapeutic categories where Zoetis does not yet have multi-decade entrenchment. The Librela/Solensia monoclonal antibody franchise — once expected to be a blockbuster growth driver — declined 3% in 2025 and saw an 11% Q4 drop, partly due to social media concerns about side effects. This is a warning: in categories where Zoetis is the first mover rather than the incumbent, it lacks the trust moat it enjoys in established categories. Competition from Elanco (Zenrelia, Credelio Quattro) is real and could pressure companion animal growth rates.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** Partially, but less than in human pharma. Animal health patents matter, but the barriers are more about regulatory approvals, vet relationships, and portfolio breadth than any single patent. Generic competition exists but is less aggressive than in human pharma — vets are conservative prescribers and the cost of animal drugs is low relative to the emotional value of pet health. Zoetis's advantages do not fully reset with each product cycle; the installed base of vet relationships and the breadth of the approved portfolio carry forward.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Zoetis's moat is multi-layered (regulatory, switching costs, scale, trust) and self-reinforcing — the dominant position in a growing, non-cyclical industry with 70%+ gross margins and expanding ROIC is as close to a wide moat as healthcare offers.
- **Bear case:** The Librela/Solensia setback shows that in novel therapeutic categories, Zoetis's moat is narrower, and competition from Elanco and generics could compress growth and margins in key franchises over the next 3-5 years.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat is well-established and evidenced by a decade of financial data, though the competitive dynamics in companion animal mAbs warrant monitoring.

## Red Flags
- Librela/Solensia franchise declined 3% in 2025 (Q4 down 11%) — social media side-effect concerns are a reputational risk in a trust-based business
- Elanco's new product launches (Zenrelia, Credelio Quattro) are gaining traction and could pressure Zoetis's companion animal share

## Score: 8 / 10
Zoetis possesses a wide, durable moat built on regulatory barriers, vet switching costs, scale, and brand trust, evidenced by 72% gross margins and 24% ROIC. The score is 8 rather than 9 because the Librela/Solensia stumble reveals that the moat is narrower in newer product categories, and Elanco's competitive emergence introduces genuine uncertainty in the companion animal growth story.

# Management & Capital Allocation — ZTS

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Data Sources:** Zoetis FY2025 earnings release, Zoetis 2025 proxy statement, Simply Wall St insider data, Salary.com executive compensation, Fintel insider trading data, MacroTrends share count data, Yahoo Finance, company investor presentations

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Diluted share count has declined consistently from ~470M (2020) to ~440M (2025), a ~6-7% cumulative reduction demonstrating disciplined buyback execution | 5 |
| 2 | $3.2B returned via buybacks in FY2025 plus ~$800M in dividends — total shareholder return exceeds free cash flow, funded partly by $1.75B convertible note issuance | 4 |
| 3 | CEO compensation is 92% incentive-based with 50% of LTI in PSUs tied to relative TSR; the 2022-2024 PSU cycle vested at 0% (17th percentile TSR), proving pay-for-performance works | 5 |
| 4 | Insider ownership is modest and incentive-driven — no large founder-type stakes; management acts more as professional stewards than owner-operators | 3 |
| 5 | M&A has been disciplined: 17 acquisitions since IPO, mostly small bolt-ons in diagnostics, genetics, and digital — no empire-building mega-deals | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership — skin in the game?** Insider ownership at Zoetis is limited. CEO Kristin Peck and the executive team hold shares primarily through equity incentive grants rather than through large personal purchases. Institutional investors (Vanguard, BlackRock, Capital Group) dominate the shareholder base. There have been zero insider transactions in the most recent 90-day window. This is typical for a professional-management company spun out of Pfizer rather than a founder-led enterprise. Management has skin in the game through equity comp, but it is not the deep personal ownership that Buffett ideally looks for. The equity incentives are well-structured, but the absence of significant open-market insider buying at current depressed prices ($118, near 52-week lows) is a mild negative signal.

**Capital allocation.** Zoetis's capital allocation follows a clear hierarchy: (1) reinvest in the business via R&D and capex (~$625M R&D + $621M capex in FY2025), (2) return excess cash to shareholders via buybacks and dividends, (3) opportunistic bolt-on M&A. In FY2025, the company returned over $4B to shareholders ($3.2B buybacks + ~$800M dividends) against $2.3B in FCF. The gap was funded partly through the $1.75B convertible note offering (0.25% coupon, due 2029) explicitly earmarked for buybacks. This is aggressive but rational: the notes were issued at a historically low coupon, and the stock was trading well below intrinsic value estimates. The dividend was raised 6% in Q1 2026, marking a decade of consecutive increases. The 1.8% yield is modest but growing.

Were the buybacks done at attractive prices? The weighted-average repurchase price in FY2025 was likely in the $140-170 range given the stock's trajectory. With the stock now at $118 and analyst targets at $145-151 median, the most recent buybacks look somewhat expensive in hindsight — a common trap for companies with mandated buyback programs. However, the long-term track record of reducing share count by 1.5-2% annually while growing EPS from ~$4 to ~$6 over 2020-2025 is genuinely accretive.

**M&A track record.** Zoetis has made approximately 17 acquisitions since its 2013 IPO, averaging 1-2 per year. These have been overwhelmingly small, strategic bolt-ons: Basepaws (pet genetics), Petmedix (species-specific antibodies), Performance Livestock Analytics (digital), Fish Vet Group (aquaculture diagnostics), Jurox (pain/sedation). The $350M divestiture of medicated feed additives to Phibro in 2024 shows willingness to prune non-core assets. There have been no transformative mega-deals or overpaid acquisitions. This is disciplined capital allocation.

**Compensation design.** CEO Kristin Peck's total compensation was $17.2M in FY2024, with 92% incentive-based. The structure is: base salary (~8%), annual cash bonus tied to revenue/adjusted EPS/FCF, and long-term equity split 50% PSUs (relative TSR vs. S&P 500 over 3 years) and 50% stock options. The 2022-2024 PSU cycle vested at 0% because Zoetis's TSR ranked at the 17th percentile of the S&P 500 — this is strong evidence that the pay-for-performance mechanism actually works. There is no single-trigger change of control, and the company has a robust clawback policy. The comp design is above average for a large-cap pharma company.

**Candor and governance.** Zoetis has an independent board chair (separate from CEO), majority-independent board with directors from pharma, consumer, finance, and veterinary backgrounds. The company's earnings communications are straightforward — the Q4 2025 call acknowledged the Librela/Solensia headwinds directly rather than burying them. The 2026 guidance range of 3-5% organic growth is conservative relative to the historical 6% run rate, suggesting management sets achievable targets. Governance structure is standard large-cap quality with no dual-class shares or poison pills.

**Do they talk like owners or politicians?** More like professional stewards than either. The communications are clear, data-driven, and honest about challenges (Librela social media concerns, competitive pressures). There is no visionary founder rhetoric, but also no evasive corporate-speak. Kristin Peck has been CEO since January 2020 and has navigated the company through COVID, supply chain disruptions, and competitive shifts without strategic missteps. The tone is competent and measured.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Management has a decade-long track record of disciplined capital allocation — consistent share count reduction, growing dividends, bolt-on M&A without overpaying, and a compensation structure that genuinely penalizes underperformance (0% PSU vesting in 2022-2024).
- **Bear case:** Insider ownership is low and incentive-driven; the aggressive $3.2B buyback in FY2025 (partially debt-funded) was done at prices above today's level, suggesting buyback timing discipline could be improved.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the capital allocation framework is sound and the comp structure is well-designed, but the lack of deep insider ownership and the debt-funded buyback program at elevated prices prevent full conviction.

## Red Flags
- FY2025 total shareholder returns ($4B) significantly exceeded FCF ($2.3B), with the gap funded by $1.75B in convertible debt — this is not sustainable if repeated annually
- No meaningful insider buying at current prices near 52-week lows, despite management's stated belief the stock is undervalued

## Score: 7 / 10
Zoetis management is a competent, professional stewardship team with a well-designed comp structure (92% incentive, 0% PSU vesting when TSR lagged) and disciplined M&A. The score is 7 rather than 8 because insider ownership is thin, the debt-funded buyback acceleration raises leverage questions, and the buyback program has not demonstrated strong price discipline — paying $140-170 for shares now at $118 is value-destructive in the near term.

# Business Economics — ZTS

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-04
**Data Sources:** Zoetis FY2025/FY2024/FY2023/FY2022 financials (yfinance), Zoetis Q4 2025 earnings release (Feb 2026), Zoetis investor presentations, quant-valuation model output, web search for segment data and capital allocation details

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC has expanded from 18.9% (FY2022) to 24.1% (FY2025), consistently above 20% in recent years — elite capital efficiency | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margins expanded steadily from 69.6% to 71.8% over four years, demonstrating durable pricing power in a non-discretionary market | 5 |
| 3 | Owner earnings grew from $2.0B to $2.5B (FY2022-FY2025), a 25% increase, with maintenance capex only ~5% of revenue — asset-light model | 4 |
| 4 | Revenue is ~65% companion animal (high recurring, growing diagnostics at 13% operational) and ~35% livestock (more cyclical but diversified across species) | 4 |
| 5 | Operating leverage present: FY2025 revenue grew ~2% reported (6% organic), while operating income grew 6% and EPS grew 10% — amplified by buybacks | 4 |
| 6 | FCF conversion improved from 62.7% (FY2022) to 85.4% (FY2025), though FY2025 OCF of $2.9B was slightly below FY2024's $3.0B despite higher earnings | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Zoetis delivers elite returns. ROIC climbed from 18.9% in FY2022 to 24.1% in FY2025 — a trajectory that reflects both margin expansion and capital discipline rather than financial engineering. ROE is exceptionally high at 66% in FY2025, though this is amplified by aggressive buybacks reducing equity (stockholders' equity fell from $4.8B to $3.3B in one year). ROA at 17.3% confirms returns are real and not merely a leverage artifact. The trend is clearly upward and stable — no year below 18.9% ROIC in the four-year window. This is a business that compounds intrinsic value at a high rate.

**Margin Structure.** Gross margins of 71.8% are remarkable for a company manufacturing physical products (biologics, pharmaceuticals, diagnostics equipment). The steady four-year expansion from 69.6% signals genuine pricing power — Zoetis operates in a market where veterinarians and pet owners are relatively price-insensitive, and switching costs for established treatments are meaningful. Operating margins at 38.0% (up from 36.2%) reflect disciplined SG&A management alongside modest R&D increases ($698M in FY2025, 7.3% of revenue). Net margins at 28.2% are best-in-class for a healthcare manufacturer. EBITDA margins at 43.0% leave substantial room to absorb cyclical downturns.

**Cash Generation.** FCF of $2.3B in FY2025 on $9.5B revenue is strong, though FCF was flat year-over-year despite earnings growth — likely reflecting working capital timing or elevated capex. Owner earnings (NI + D&A - CapEx) of $2.5B represent a cleaner picture and grew steadily. FCF conversion (FCF/NI) improved materially from 62.7% to 85.4% over four years, indicating the business is becoming more efficient at converting accounting profits to cash. Stock-based compensation at $83M (3% of net income) is modest and does not meaningfully dilute the FCF picture.

**Capital Intensity.** Zoetis is relatively asset-light for a pharmaceutical manufacturer. Maintenance capex (approximated by D&A) runs ~$487M or 5.1% of revenue. Total capex of $621M in FY2025 implies only ~$134M in growth capex. The quant model confirms maintenance capex at 78% of total capex — the business does not require heavy reinvestment to sustain its earnings power. Total assets of $15.5B support $9.5B in revenue and $2.7B in net income, a capital-efficient setup. The $3.2B in buybacks in FY2025 (funded partly by the $1.75B convertible notes) represents management choosing to return capital rather than chase acquisitions, which is consistent with a mature compounder.

**Revenue Quality.** Approximately 65% of revenue comes from companion animal products, which have strong recurring characteristics: parasiticides (Simparica Trio franchise at ~$1.5B, growing 12% operationally), dermatology treatments (~$1.7B, growing 6%), and diagnostics (growing 13% operationally). Pet healthcare spending is highly non-discretionary — once a pet owner starts a treatment protocol, they rarely switch or stop. The livestock segment (~35%) is more cyclical and commodity-exposed but diversified across cattle, swine, poultry, and aquaculture. Revenue growth is a blend of volume (new product uptake, geographic expansion) and price (mid-single-digit annual increases). The diagnostics business adds a razor-and-blade recurring revenue stream. Zoetis guided FY2026 revenue of $9.825-$10.025B, implying 3-5% growth.

**Operating Leverage.** The business exhibits meaningful operating leverage. FY2025 organic operational revenue grew 6%, while organic adjusted net income grew 7%. EPS grew from $5.47 to $6.02 (10%), amplified by the share count reduction from buybacks. Over the four-year window, revenue grew 17% cumulatively ($8.1B to $9.5B) while net income grew 29% ($2.1B to $2.7B) and EPS grew 34% ($4.49 to $6.02). The fixed-cost manufacturing base and high-margin product mix create a natural profit amplifier as revenue scales.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Elite ROIC expanding above 24%, 72% gross margins with pricing power in a non-discretionary market, and growing recurring revenue from diagnostics and parasiticide franchises create a durable compounding engine.
- **Bear case:** FCF was flat YoY despite earnings growth, FY2026 guided growth of only 3-5% is modest for the valuation, and the livestock segment remains exposed to cyclical swings and currency headwinds.
- **Confidence:** High — four years of consistent financial data, well-understood business model, transparent segment reporting, and a long public track record since the 2013 Pfizer spin-off.

## Red Flags
- FY2025 OCF ($2.9B) was slightly below FY2024 ($3.0B) despite higher net income — worth monitoring for working capital trends
- $3.2B in buybacks in FY2025 exceeded FCF of $2.3B, funded partly by the convertible notes issuance — management is leveraging the balance sheet for returns
- Guided FY2026 growth of 3-5% (reported) is below recent organic growth rates, reflecting FX headwinds

## Score: 9 / 10
Zoetis exhibits elite business economics: consistently expanding ROIC above 20%, best-in-class margins with pricing power, strong and improving FCF conversion, an asset-light model, and meaningful operating leverage. The only deduction is the flat year-over-year FCF and modest near-term growth guidance; otherwise this is a textbook quality compounder.
