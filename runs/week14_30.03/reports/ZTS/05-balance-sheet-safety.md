## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Recent Increases.** The headline story is the $2.5B increase in total debt during FY2025. This was driven by two distinct issuances: (1) In August 2025, Zoetis issued $1.85B in senior notes — $850M of 4.150% notes due 2028 and $1.0B of 5.000% notes due 2035. Proceeds were used to repay $750M of 4.500% notes due 2025 and $600M of 5.400% notes due 2025, with the remainder for general purposes. This was pure refinancing, extending maturities and lowering coupon rates by 35-40 basis points. (2) In December 2025, Zoetis issued $1.75B of 0.25% convertible senior notes due 2029. Proceeds were used primarily to repurchase ~$1.6B of common stock (with ~$163M for capped call transactions and ~$250M for concurrent share purchases). This was a deliberate decision to swap equity for very cheap debt to fund buybacks. The net effect: Debt/EBITDA rose from 1.7x to 2.3x, and Net Debt/EBITDA from 1.2x to 1.7x. At 2.3x Debt/EBITDA, this is comfortably within the 2-3x range typical for investment-grade quality businesses. The leverage is intentional, not distressed.

**Interest Coverage.** Despite higher absolute debt, interest coverage actually improved to 16.1x in FY2025, up from 14.9x in FY2024. This reflects two factors: operating income grew faster than interest expense, and the convertible notes carry a near-zero 0.25% coupon that adds negligible interest cost. At $222M annual interest expense on $3.6B operating income, Zoetis could absorb a dramatic earnings decline before debt service becomes stressed. Even in a severe downturn scenario (operating income cut in half to $1.8B), interest coverage would still be ~8x — well above danger levels.

**Liquidity Position.** Cash and short-term investments of $2.3B cover more than 10x annual interest expense and could fund the company for roughly 12 months of total operating expenses without any revenue. The current ratio of 3.0x (up from 1.8x in FY2024) reflects $6.8B in current assets against $2.2B in current liabilities — the improvement is partly due to the convertible note proceeds sitting in cash at year-end and the absence of near-term debt maturities in current liabilities. Zoetis also maintains a revolving credit facility (typically $1B+ for companies of this size) providing additional backstop liquidity. Combined with $2.3B in annual FCF generation, the liquidity picture is robust.

**Debt Maturity Schedule.** Based on the Q3 2025 10-Q (pre-convertible notes) and subsequent issuances, the approximate maturity profile is: nothing due in 2026, $750M in 2027, $1.35B in 2028 (the new senior notes), $1.75B in 2029 (convertible notes, though may convert to equity if stock rises above $148.20), and approximately $5.05B due after 2029. This is well-laddered with no near-term refinancing cliff. The largest near-term maturity ($750M in 2027) represents less than one-third of annual FCF. Critically, Zoetis is investment-grade rated (Moody's Baa1, S&P BBB+), ensuring continued capital markets access at favorable rates.

**Equity Compression and Leverage Ratios.** The most aggressive aspect of the balance sheet is the equity compression. Stockholders' equity fell from $4.8B (FY2024) to $3.3B (FY2025), driven by $3.2B in buybacks that exceeded net income of $2.7B. D/E of 277% looks alarming in isolation but is misleading for a company deliberately shrinking its equity base through buybacks. The more relevant metric is Debt/EBITDA (2.3x) and the ability to service and repay debt from cash flows. Many quality compounders (Visa, Moody's, S&P Global) operate with negative equity due to buybacks — the key question is whether cash generation can sustain the debt load, and at $2.3B annual FCF against $222M interest expense, it clearly can.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** If revenue dropped 30% (from $9.5B to $6.65B), assuming operating margins compress to ~30% (from 38%, reflecting some fixed cost deleverage), operating income would be approximately $2.0B. Interest expense at ~$250M (assuming slightly higher from new debt) would still be covered 8x. FCF would likely fall to ~$1.2-1.4B, still comfortably positive. Over two years, the company would generate $2.4-2.8B in cumulative FCF, enough to cover any maturing debt without accessing capital markets. A 30% revenue decline is also extremely unlikely for animal health — even during 2008-2009, animal pharma revenue declined only low single digits. The companion animal segment (65% of revenue) has proven essentially recession-proof, as pet owners continue treating their animals through downturns. Zoetis would survive this stress scenario comfortably without equity issuance.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Well-laddered maturities with no 2026 obligations, 16x interest coverage, $2.3B cash on hand, and recession-resistant revenue make this balance sheet highly survivable even in severe downturns — the convertible notes at 0.25% represent exceptionally cheap financing.
- **Bear case:** Debt increased 37% in one year primarily to fund buybacks, equity has been compressed to $3.3B, and the convertible notes introduce potential dilution of ~11.8M shares (2.8% of outstanding) if the stock recovers above $148.20.
- **Confidence:** High — debt structure is publicly documented through SEC filings, maturities are well-defined, and the business model's recession-resistance is empirically demonstrated across multiple cycles.

## Red Flags
- Total debt increased $2.5B in a single year, with $1.75B explicitly to fund buybacks — this is financial engineering, not operational necessity
- D/E ratio of 277% reflects deliberate equity destruction through buybacks exceeding earnings
- Convertible notes could add ~11.8M shares (2.8% dilution) if stock rises above $148.20 conversion price, partially offsetting the buyback benefit
- FY2025 buybacks of $3.2B exceeded FCF of $2.3B — the company borrowed to buy back stock at prices ($120-170 range) that may prove above intrinsic value given the current price of $117.94

## Score: 7 / 10
Zoetis maintains strong interest coverage (16x), manageable leverage (2.3x Debt/EBITDA), well-laddered maturities, and ample liquidity backed by recession-resistant cash flows. The deduction from a higher score reflects the aggressive 37% debt increase in FY2025 driven primarily by buyback-financing, the compressed equity base, and the reality that management chose to lever up at stock prices that have since declined meaningfully — a capital allocation concern that touches balance sheet prudence. The underlying business can easily service this debt, but the trajectory of leverage bears monitoring.
