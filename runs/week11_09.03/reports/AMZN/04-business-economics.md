## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital:** Amazon's ROIC has undergone a dramatic reversal since the 2022 trough, when COVID-era overexpansion collided with cost inflation to produce a net loss of -$2.7B. From that nadir, operating income expanded to $36.9B (2023), $68.6B (2024), and $80.0B (2025). Using NOPAT / average invested capital, ROIC is estimated at approximately 16% in 2024 and 14% in 2025 (slight compression from the accelerating capex cycle). ROE, computed as net income / average shareholders' equity ($77.7B / [(286.0 + 411.1) / 2] = ~22%), has recovered from near-zero. Both metrics would be substantially higher if growth capex were excluded from the denominator; on a maintenance-only basis, ROIC likely exceeds 25%. By comparison, the Retail-Cyclical industry median ROIC is approximately 4–5% — Amazon is running at 3–4× the sector median even including its growth capex drag.

**Margin structure:** The blended 11.2% operating margin masks extraordinary segment divergence. AWS ($128.7B revenue, up from ~$91B in 2023) operates at approximately 35% margin, implying ~$45B in segment operating income — generated on just 18% of total revenue. North America retail ($426B+ revenue) runs at approximately 6–7% operating margins, with Q4 2025 showing $11.5B operating income on ~$127B revenue (9.1% margin, highest ever). International ($173B revenue) reached $1.0B operating income in Q4 2025, a meaningful inflection from years of losses — full-year International operating income is estimated at $3–5B. Advertising services ($68.5B revenue, 21.8% growth) is not separately disclosed as a segment but estimated to carry 40–50%+ operating margins based on the structural economics: zero incremental distribution infrastructure, search-and-display inventory running on existing marketplace real estate. The practical implication is that AWS + advertising (~27% of revenue) generate approximately 75–80% of total operating income.

**Cash generation and owner earnings:** Operating cash flow was $139.5B in 2025 (+20% YoY from $115.9B in 2024), continuing a powerful multi-year expansion: $46.8B (2022) → $84.9B (2023) → $115.9B (2024) → $139.5B (2025). The GAAP FCF of $11.2B ($139.5B operating cash flow minus $131.8B capex) is deeply misleading. Of the $131.8B capex, management has confirmed the preponderance is growth investment in AI inference capacity and new AWS data centers. Estimated maintenance/sustaining capex for the existing asset base is $30–40B, yielding normalized owner earnings of approximately $100–110B — roughly 9–10× reported GAAP FCF. Amazon also incurred $22B+ in stock-based compensation, which is a real dilutive cost and should be deducted from owner earnings; adjusted owner earnings are therefore approximately $78–88B. FCF conversion from EBITDA ($145.7B) at the operating cash flow level is 96%, excellent; at the GAAP FCF level it is 5%, which is the distortion from the capex cycle.

**Capital intensity and the AI capex cycle:** Amazon is the most capital-intensive large-cap technology company in the world in the current cycle. Capex: $52.7B (2023) → $83.0B (2024) → $131.8B (2025), with 2026 guided at approximately $200B. This capex is almost entirely growth investment: Amazon is building Trainium/Inferentia AI chip infrastructure, sovereign-cloud data centers for regulated industries, and expanding fulfillment automation. The historical analogue — AWS's 2013–2017 buildout, which also destroyed FCF — eventually produced the most profitable infrastructure business in history. The case that the current cycle follows the same path rests on AWS Q4 2025 growth accelerating to 24% YoY (from 17% for full-year 2025), backlog growth, and cloud market share data. The risk is magnitude: $200B in annual capex has no modern peer; if AI demand is lumpy rather than linear, the capex cycle could extend well beyond 2027.

**Revenue quality:** AWS generates consumption-based revenue under multi-year enterprise agreements — high quality, low churn, with meaningful committed contract backlogs. Advertising is effectively a structural toll on the marketplace: 3P sellers must bid for visibility, making this spending recurring and tied to the platform's existence rather than discretionary marketing budgets. Prime subscriptions ($49.6B, 2025) are annually renewable recurring revenue with empirically low churn. Third-party seller services ($172.2B) are sticky due to FBA logistics integration. First-party online store revenue ($269.3B) is more discretionary but anchored by Prime convenience lock-in. The composition is favorable: approximately 55–60% of revenue is highly recurring or structurally recurring; 40–45% is discretionary retail. Blended revenue quality is well above the retail sector average.

**Operating leverage:** The margin trajectory is a textbook demonstration of operating leverage. Revenue 2022→2025: $514B → $717B (+39%). Operating income 2022→2025: $12.2B → $80.0B (+555%). The incremental operating margin over that three-year period was approximately 33% — far above the blended 11.2% margin, confirming genuine fixed-cost leverage. AWS is the most extreme example: data center depreciation, engineer headcount, and network costs are largely fixed once built; incremental cloud consumption flows through at near-35% margins. The North America retail segment shows similar dynamics as fulfillment density improves: Q4 2025 North America operating margin was 9.1%, the highest in company history. Advertising operating leverage is structurally unlimited — the inventory exists independent of revenue, so every additional dollar of ad spend is almost entirely margin expansion.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The $200B capex cycle enables AWS to dominate AI cloud infrastructure, generating owner earnings of $150B+ by 2028-2030, with advertising growing to $100B+ — the business economics become extraordinary.
- **Bear case:** Capex returns are lower than expected, margins plateau at current levels, and ROIC stays in the 12-15% range rather than expanding to 20%+.
- **Confidence:** High on the structural economics; Medium on whether the current capex cycle generates the returns management projects.

## Red Flags

- Reported FCF of $11.2B on a company with $80B operating income looks alarming in isolation; investors must understand this is growth capex, not business deterioration.
- SBC of $22B+ is a real economic cost that is added back in FCF calculations — owner earnings should be reduced by this amount.
- International retail continues to suppress consolidated margins and has done so for 15+ years without a clear timeline to consistent profitability.
- The $200B capex 2026 guidance raises the question: what happens to ROIC if cloud demand turns out to be lumpy rather than linear?

## Score: 8 / 10

Amazon's underlying economics are strong — AWS and advertising are exceptional businesses, ROIC is recovering and well above industry norms, and operating leverage is clearly evident in the margin expansion — but the extraordinary capital intensity of the current investment cycle, the large SBC costs, and the ongoing dilution prevent a higher score; the business engine itself is excellent, the investment cycle is a timing risk.
