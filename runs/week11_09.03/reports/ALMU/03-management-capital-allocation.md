## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership (shares, not just options):** CEO Jonathan Klamkin owns approximately 1,429,398 shares (post-sale), representing roughly 10.3% of the company. At the current market cap of ~$331M, this stake is worth approximately $23.6M — a meaningful personal financial exposure that provides incentive alignment. Mark N. Tompkins, the largest individual shareholder, owns approximately 1.79M shares or 9.92% of the company. Total insider ownership is approximately 25.8%, which is relatively high for a public company of this size and represents meaningful skin-in-the-game. However, the direction of insider activity is negative: all insider transactions in the past three months have been sales, with no open-market purchases recorded. The CEO's 150,000-share sale in March 2026 — even if conducted under a pre-established 10b5-1 plan — comes at a time when the company remains pre-profit and dependent on equity issuances to fund operations, creating a mixed signal.

**Capital allocation track record:** At this stage of Aeluma's development, "capital allocation" is essentially synonymous with "cash management and equity issuance strategy," as the company has no retained earnings, no buybacks, no dividends, and no history of M&A. The company's capital allocation decisions are: (1) raise equity to fund operations; (2) pursue government R&D contracts to generate non-dilutive revenue; (3) invest in manufacturing capacity and headcount to scale. On the equity front, the 10-K filing disclosed $12.6M raised in a recent offering, and the total share count has grown 45.9% over the past year — a rate of dilution that is severe for existing shareholders. Cash stands at $38.6M as of December 31, 2025, which is substantial relative to the $5M–$6M annual revenue base and likely sufficient to fund operations for several years at current burn rates. The decision to build a large cash buffer is prudent for a pre-commercial technology company, but it was achieved by diluting shareholders significantly.

**Compensation design:** CEO compensation of ~$255K in base salary is modest relative to peers in the semiconductor industry, which might suggest founder-operator mentality. However, equity compensation (stock options and RSUs) is meaningful: the 10-K discloses that higher SBC drove part of the GAAP net loss increase in recent quarters. The compensation structure appears to rely heavily on equity grants, which contributes to the dilution problem. Without access to the full proxy statement details, the specific SBC as a percentage of revenue is not precisely calculable, but given ~$5M in revenue and several million in annual operating losses, SBC likely represents a material percentage of total compensation costs. The CEO received a bonus grant of 6,253 stock options and 2,403 RSUs that vested immediately (disclosed alongside the March 2026 share sale), suggesting the equity grant and sale occurred in the same period — a governance practice that, while legal under 10b5-1, raises eyebrows.

**Candor and consistency:** Dr. Klamkin's public communications (earnings calls, conference presentations, press releases) reflect a technically credible founder-scientist who speaks with clarity about the technology roadmap. The FY2026 revenue guidance range of $4.0M–$6.0M is reasonably conservative given H1 actuals of $2.7M. The company is transparent about its government contract dependency and has not oversold near-term commercial prospects to the degree one might expect from a typical pre-revenue startup. However, the company has been public since at least 2022 and remains pre-commercial six years after founding — suggesting that the commercialization timeline has been longer than originally articulated.

**Governance structure:** Aeluma appears to have a standard single-class share structure (no dual-class). The board composition information was not fully available from web searches but the company maintains standard corporate governance disclosures on its IR website. No related-party transactions or self-dealing red flags were identified in web research. The 10b5-1 plan for the CEO's share sale is standard governance practice for corporate insiders. Montrose Capital Partners is identified as an institutional backer. The company's audit and compensation committees appear to function normally for a small-cap NASDAQ company.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Founder-CEO with significant personal equity stake (~10%), modest cash compensation, and government-backed credibility suggests owner-operator alignment, and the strong cash position ($38.6M) with no debt gives management runway to execute.
- **Bear case:** Aggressive share dilution (45.9% share count growth in one year), consistent insider selling with no open-market buys, and a 10b5-1 plan sale coinciding with equity grants suggests management is managing personal wealth rather than building shareholder value at this stage.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Insider ownership percentages and transaction history are well-documented via SEC filings; the assessment of capital allocation quality is constrained by the early-stage nature of the company, where the record is too short and thin to draw firm conclusions.

## Red Flags

- Share count grew 45.9% in one year — severe ongoing dilution via equity issuances
- All insider transactions in the past 3 months are sales; no open-market buying
- CEO sold $902,500 in stock in the same period an equity bonus grant vested — optics are poor even if legally structured
- Company has been public for several years and remains entirely pre-commercial product revenue — commercialization timelines have been repeatedly extended
- SBC likely represents a material percentage of revenue given small revenue base and GAAP net losses driven partly by compensation costs
- No demonstrated capital allocation track record beyond equity fundraising and cash management

## Score: 4 / 10

Klamkin's meaningful founder equity stake and modest cash compensation provide some alignment, but the aggressive share dilution, consistent insider selling with no buying, and lack of any commercial revenue after 6 years of operation reflect a management record that is below average — the company is in survival/fundraising mode rather than compounding shareholder value.
