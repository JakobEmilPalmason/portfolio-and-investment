## Detailed Analysis

**What does Aeluma sell and to whom?** Aeluma, Inc. (NASDAQ: ALMU) is a Goleta, California-based semiconductor company founded in 2019 by Dr. Jonathan Klamkin, a UC Santa Barbara professor of electrical and computer engineering. The company develops optoelectronic and electronic devices — primarily InGaAs photodetectors and compound semiconductor wafers — using a proprietary heterogeneous integration platform that grows high-performance III-V compound semiconductor materials on large-diameter (up to 12-inch) silicon wafers. Today, Aeluma's customers are almost exclusively U.S. government agencies: DARPA, NASA, the U.S. Navy, and the Department of Energy. The company also has early-stage relationships with commercial partners like Thorlabs, but commercial product revenue is currently negligible.

**How does it make money?** Aeluma generates revenue in two ways: (1) R&D contract services — performing funded research under government grants and contracts, which is currently the overwhelming majority of revenue; and (2) product and service sales — selling semiconductor wafers, chips, or custom fab services to commercial customers. In Q1 FY2026 (ended September 30, 2025), government contract revenue was $1.34M vs. $41K commercial, making the ratio approximately 97:3. This is not yet a product company in the traditional sense — it is a government-funded R&D operation with aspirations to commercialize its technology at scale. The DARPA contract alone ($11.7M over 36 months) is the single largest revenue driver in the near term.

**Key revenue drivers (2-4 variables):** The business currently turns on a small number of variables: (1) government contract wins and milestone completions — lose one major contract and revenue collapses; (2) the ability to prove out large-diameter wafer manufacturing at commercial cost points; (3) progress toward design wins with commercial customers in SWIR sensing, LiDAR, AR/VR, or quantum applications; and (4) the pace of the SWIR sensor market's adoption and cost sensitivity. The pathway from contract-funded R&D to product revenue depends entirely on successfully executing on technical milestones and then finding commercial customers willing to qualify and integrate Aeluma's devices.

**Revenue predictability:** Revenue visibility is low. Government contracts provide some short-term visibility — the DARPA contract schedules approximately $6M over the first 18 months and $5.7M over the next 18 months contingent on milestones — but contract R&D is inherently lumpy, milestone-dependent, and subject to government budget cycles. Commercial revenue is effectively zero today. FY2025 revenue was $4.67M (+408% YoY), but that growth rate was driven by the DARPA contract win, not organic business expansion. FY2026 guidance of $4.0M–$6.0M implies potential revenue softness relative to FY2025 actuals.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** The core idea is understandable in concept: Aeluma has figured out how to grow high-performance compound semiconductors (InGaAs) on cheap, large silicon wafers (12-inch), which in theory enables the cost-effective mass production of sensors that currently require expensive 2–4 inch indium phosphide wafers. If the manufacturing process works at scale, Aeluma could supply photodetectors for LiDAR, SWIR imaging, AR/VR, and quantum sensing at far lower cost than incumbents. The government is paying them to prove this out. The complexity arises in evaluating whether the manufacturing platform will actually work at commercial scale, whether commercial customers will adopt it, and whether the company can survive long enough to find out. The business model is not complex, but the technology risk and the gap between today's R&D revenue and any commercial product business creates significant opacity.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Aeluma's heterogeneous integration platform is a genuine manufacturing breakthrough that, if proven at commercial scale, could disrupt a $4B+ SWIR sensor market with government-validated technology and a first-mover cost advantage.
- **Bear case:** Aeluma is a university spinout with $5M in government-funded revenue, no commercial product traction, extreme customer concentration, ongoing dilutive losses, and a long uncertain road to any scalable product business.
- **Confidence:** Low — The business concept is intelligible, but evaluating whether the manufacturing platform will reach commercial viability requires deep semiconductor process expertise that is outside a generalist analyst's circle of competence.

## Red Flags

- 97% government contract revenue with essentially zero commercial product revenue after 6+ years of operation
- 71% of FY2025 revenue from a single undisclosed customer
- Revenue guidance for FY2026 ($4.0M–$6.0M) implies potential flat-to-down vs. FY2025 actuals ($4.67M)
- Share count grew 45.9% in the past year due to equity issuances — severe ongoing dilution
- The company has yet to demonstrate commercial product-market fit in any end market
- CEO sold 150,000 shares in March 2026 ($902,500) via a 10b5-1 plan while company is pre-profit

## Score: 3 / 10

The business model is conceptually understandable but Aeluma is effectively a deep-tech R&D startup with no commercial product revenue, extreme customer concentration, and a highly uncertain path to commercialization — falling well outside a generalist investor's circle of competence and requiring specialized semiconductor manufacturing expertise to evaluate.
