## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels**

Boston Scientific's gross debt at year-end 2025 was approximately $12.0B (short-term debt ~$480M, long-term debt ~$11.5B). This compares to an annualized EBITDA of ~$5.0B, implying a Debt/EBITDA ratio of approximately 2.4x on a gross basis and roughly 1.9x net (given $1.96B cash). Management's stated gross leverage target is 2.25–2.50x, and the company finished 2025 slightly below the midpoint of that range. At end-2024, long-term debt was ~$9.0B, increasing from ~$8.6B at end-2023 — the Axonics close in November 2024 was funded in part by a €2.0B Senior Notes offering completed in February 2024 and a new $3.0B revolving credit facility. The trend is upward in absolute debt, but EBITDA is growing faster, so leverage ratios have been improving on a normalized basis.

By medtech peer standards, BSX's leverage is on the moderate-to-elevated side. It is not a fortress balance sheet (Danaher-style net cash), but neither is it over-leveraged in a concerning way. The company has demonstrated consistent ability to deleverage after acquisitions using its strong FCF generation.

**Interest Coverage**

With EBIT of approximately $3.8B and interest expense of approximately $305M in 2024 (with EBIT growing further in 2025), the interest coverage ratio is approximately 16.5x. This is a very comfortable coverage ratio — the company earns more than 16 times its annual interest cost. Even if interest rates were to rise materially or EBIT were to contract significantly, the interest burden would remain manageable for years. There is no near-term debt service stress. Interest expense as a percentage of revenue is under 2%, which is low for a company with ~$12B in gross debt, reflecting the benefit of investment-grade credit ratings and long-dated, relatively low-coupon debt.

**Liquidity**

At December 31, 2025, BSX had $1.96B in cash and cash equivalents. The company also maintains a $3.0B revolving credit facility maturing 2031, providing immediate access to incremental liquidity if needed. Total available liquidity therefore approximates $5B. In a scenario where capital markets were to close (e.g., credit crunch), BSX has sufficient internal resources and committed bank facilities to operate comfortably for multiple years. The FCF run-rate of $3.66B per year (2025) provides strong organic cash generation to meet obligations. Short-term debt maturities of ~$480M are fully covered by cash on hand. The current ratio and working capital details are not precisely confirmed from available sources, but given the cash position and revolving facility, liquidity risk is low.

**Refinancing Risk**

BSX's debt is investment-grade (S&P: A-), meaning it has access to public debt markets at attractive rates and is not dependent on junk-market financing. The A- upgrade from S&P in 2025 is a meaningful signal of improving balance sheet health. With a $3.0B revolver maturing 2031 and long-dated senior notes (the company issued €2B in 2024 senior notes with presumably 7–10 year tenors), there is no wall of near-term maturities. The company is a serial issuer of investment-grade notes and has demonstrated the ability to access debt markets across varying rate environments. Refinancing risk is low.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

BSX, like all large device manufacturers, carries operating lease obligations, pension-related liabilities (modest by industrial standards), and product liability contingencies. As a company that has faced historical litigation (mesh products, legacy device issues), legal contingencies are a known feature of the BSX risk profile, though these have been manageable and are disclosed. The company likely has operating leases for sales offices, distribution centers, and facilities globally — these are captured in IFRS/GAAP lease accounting (ASC 842) and included in the reported debt figures. No extraordinary off-balance-sheet structures were identified in available sources. The goodwill balance is large (~$15–17B estimated) given M&A history, and a significant impairment would affect book equity but not cash flows.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years**

Scenario: Revenue falls from $20.1B to ~$14.1B in Year 1 and holds at ~$14.1B in Year 2 (roughly back to 2023 levels). Assumptions:
- Operating margins compress to ~15% (COVID-level), generating EBIT of ~$2.1B
- Interest expense stays at ~$400M (slightly higher to reflect rate risk)
- Capex reduced to ~$600M
- Operating cash flow approximately $1.5–1.8B under this scenario

Under this stress test, BSX generates approximately $1.5B in FCF per year, which comfortably covers its ~$400M annual interest burden. Even in this severe scenario, the company does not approach a default risk — it has $2B in cash + $3B revolver + strong FCF to fund operations. Equity issuance would not be necessary. This is a resilient balance sheet under plausible stress.

The primary risk scenario would be a simultaneous: (a) severe revenue contraction, (b) sharp increase in interest rates applied to floating-rate debt, AND (c) inability to access capital markets — a truly catastrophic macro-scenario. Even then, the interest coverage from operating cash flow would remain positive. Conclusion: BSX would survive a 30% two-year revenue shock without equity issuance.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Investment-grade (A-) rated balance sheet with 1.9x net leverage, 16.5x interest coverage, $5B liquidity, and $3.7B annual FCF — the company services its debt easily and deleverages naturally with each year of growth.
- **Bear case:** Absolute gross debt of $12B is large and growing with each acquisition cycle; if BSX pursues another large deal (the company targets $4–5B per year in acquisitions), leverage could spike back above 2.5x before FCF reduces it.
- **Confidence:** High — The key metrics (debt/EBITDA, interest coverage, cash, credit rating) are sourced from official company releases and rating agency data; the stress test conclusion is directionally sound.

## Red Flags

- Serial acquisition model means debt will persistently increase; the balance sheet never reaches a true "net cash" or fortress state as long as M&A remains the core growth strategy.
- Goodwill and intangible assets likely represent a very large share of the asset base (~$30B+ total assets with substantial goodwill); a material goodwill impairment — while non-cash — would highlight overpayment risk on past deals.
- Operating lease and legal contingency obligations add non-trivial off-balance-sheet exposure that is difficult to fully quantify from public sources alone.
- Interest expense of $305M in 2024 will increase meaningfully in 2025–2026 as higher-rate debt issued in 2024 (Eurobonds, new credit facility) flows through fully — though coverage remains comfortable.

## Score: 7 / 10

A solid but not exceptional balance sheet: investment-grade rating, strong interest coverage (~16.5x), and ample FCF provide genuine resilience, but $12B in gross debt accumulated through M&A and a serial-acquisition model that perpetually reloads leverage prevent a higher rating.
