# Investment Analysis: EL.PA — EssilorLuxottica SA

**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Verdict:** Own

> EssilorLuxottica is the world's only vertically integrated eyewear company, commanding ~50% of the global corrective lens market and owning the most recognized eyewear brands (Ray-Ban, Oakley), while generating $3.8B in free cash flow on a non-discretionary demand base — half the world needs glasses. At 23.7x forward earnings after a 37% drawdown, the market is pricing in GDP-like growth for a monopoly franchise with an emerging AI-glasses platform, creating a compelling risk-reward asymmetry where base-case upside of 30% dwarfs bear-case downside of 21%. The primary governance overhang (Delfin succession) and competitive uncertainty (Apple smart glasses) are real but manageable risks for a patient investor willing to hold through 12-24 months of headline noise.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: EssilorLuxottica sells corrective lenses and branded eyewear (Ray-Ban, Oakley) through 17,500+ owned stores to half the world's population that needs vision correction, generating €28.5B in revenue with 60%+ gross margins because people must see.
2. **Moat**: The only vertically integrated eyewear company on Earth — controlling lens manufacturing, frame design, and retail distribution — with ~50% global lens market share, no competitor even one-fifth its size, and brand loyalty (Ray-Ban since 1937) that creates pricing power through every layer of the value chain.
3. **Economics**: Tangible ROIC is 22-33% (masked by €30B+ of acquisition goodwill), FCF conversion runs at 163% of net income, and owner earnings of €3.9B dwarf reported net income of €2.3B because half the €3.1B depreciation charge is non-cash amortization of acquired intangibles that never needs reinvestment.
4. **Management**: CEO Milleri has delivered record 11.2% organic growth and 16% adjusted margins, but the three-year Delfin family succession crisis, his conflicted dual role as CEO and controlling-shareholder chairman, and the off-strategy $1.5B Supreme acquisition keep stewardship at average.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt/EBITDA of 1.1x, interest coverage of 14.4x, and €5.7B liquidity mean EssilorLuxottica would remain investment-grade and FCF-positive even in a 30% revenue decline — people still need glasses in a recession.
6. **Price**: At €204.70 (23.7x forward earnings), the market is pricing in only 4-5% annual earnings growth for a decade — well below the company's 8-10% historical trend — meaning you need nothing heroic, just execution on the stated 19-20% margin target and mid-single-digit revenue growth.
7. **Margin of safety**: A 37% decline from November highs in a non-discretionary monopoly generating $3.8B FCF with 1.1x leverage, where bear-case downside is 21% versus base-case upside of 30% and bull-case upside of 104%.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Apple launches smart glasses AND vertically integrates its own lens manufacturing, if FY2026 adjusted operating margin fails to reach 17%, or if Meta terminates the partnership — any of these would signal the franchise premium is structurally impaired.

## Key Risks

1. **Smart glasses platform competition (High impact, Medium-High probability):** Apple, Google, and Samsung entering smart eyewear could commoditize EssilorLuxottica's manufacturing role and erode the growth premium that justifies a premium valuation. If tech companies vertically integrate their own optical hardware, the Meta partnership becomes less unique and EL's platform economics weaken.

2. **US tariff escalation (High impact, Medium probability):** With 43% of revenue from North America and tariff rates of 20-145% depending on manufacturing origin, persistent or escalating trade barriers could structurally compress margins below the 19-20% target. The company is mitigating via supply chain diversification and price increases, but a further escalation remains a genuine risk to the FY2026 earnings recovery.

3. **Delfin governance crisis (Medium impact, High probability of continued uncertainty):** The three-year succession dispute at the controlling shareholder level has frozen dividends, blocked strategic decisions, and created structural governance conflicts through CEO Milleri's dual role. While resolution is expected by end of 2026, further delays would prolong the overhang and could affect capital allocation decisions.

4. **Margin recovery execution risk (Medium-High impact, Medium probability):** The jump from 16.0% adjusted operating margin to 19-20% is aggressive and has not yet been demonstrated. If tariff costs, smart glasses investment, or competitive pricing prevent this recovery, the forward earnings estimates that make the stock look cheap will prove too optimistic.

5. **Strategic drift via Supreme acquisition (Low-Medium impact, already occurred):** The $1.5B Supreme streetwear purchase signals a potential departure from core eyewear competency that could dilute management focus and capital allocation discipline over time.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**: (1) FY2026 Q1/Q2 results confirm margin progression toward 19-20% adjusted operating margin target; (2) US tariff de-escalation or successful mitigation evidenced by stable/improving US segment margins; (3) Stock declines below €180 without fundamental deterioration, creating deep value entry point.
- **Sell triggers**: (1) Apple launches smart glasses AND vertically integrates its own lens manufacturing, disintermediating EssilorLuxottica; (2) FY2026 adjusted operating margin comes in below 17%, suggesting structural margin impairment; (3) Meta terminates or materially downgrades the smart glasses partnership.

## Detailed Analysis

**What does EssilorLuxottica sell and to whom?** The company designs, manufactures, and distributes ophthalmic lenses, eyeglass frames, and sunglasses. On the lens side, it sells corrective lenses (progressive, single-vision, photochromic) under brands like Varilux, Crizal, and Transitions to opticians, ophthalmologists, and optical chains worldwide. On the frames and sunglasses side, it sells proprietary brands (Ray-Ban, Oakley, Persol, Oliver Peoples) and licensed designer brands (Prada, Chanel, Armani, Burberry — over 20 licenses) to wholesale and retail customers. It also sells directly to consumers through its own retail chains: LensCrafters (~1,100 stores), Sunglass Hut, Pearle Vision, OPSM, GrandVision, and others totaling approximately 17,500+ retail locations globally. A newer and fast-growing category is AI-enabled smart glasses via the Meta partnership (Ray-Ban Meta).

**How does it make money?** Revenue comes from two segments of roughly equal size. Professional Solutions (48% of FY2025 revenue) encompasses lens manufacturing and wholesale distribution of frames and lenses to independent opticians and third-party retailers. Direct to Consumer (52%) captures retail sales through owned stores and e-commerce. The gross margin of ~61% reflects the high-value-add nature of precision lens manufacturing and branded frame pricing power. The company earns on every layer of the value chain: it makes the lens, makes the frame, assembles the pair, and sells it to the end consumer — a unique position in the industry.

**Key revenue drivers.** (1) Global myopia prevalence is rising, driven by urbanization and screen time, expanding the addressable market. (2) Premiumization — consumers trading up to progressive lenses, blue-light-filtering coatings, and branded frames. (3) Geographic expansion, particularly in Asia-Pacific and Latin America where eyecare penetration remains low. (4) The Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses, which tripled sales in 2025 and represent an entirely new product category with production scaling to 10M units/year by end of 2026. (5) The GrandVision acquisition (completed 2021, €8B) added 7,200+ stores and is still generating integration synergies.

**Revenue predictability.** This is a highly recurring business. Vision correction is non-discretionary — prescriptions must be renewed, lenses replaced, and frames updated. The lens replacement cycle (every 1-3 years) creates a steady cadence. Sunglasses and fashion eyewear add a more discretionary layer, but core vision care provides ballast. Revenue has grown every year since the 2018 merger, with FY2025 showing 11.2% constant-currency growth. FCF conversion is excellent at 163%, reflecting a capital-light model relative to earnings once you adjust for the heavy intangible amortization from M&A.

**Customer concentration.** There is no meaningful customer concentration risk. The company sells to millions of independent opticians, thousands of retail chains, and directly to hundreds of millions of consumers. No single customer represents a material share of revenue. Geographic diversification is solid: North America (~43%), EMEA (~38%), Asia-Pacific (~12%), and Latin America (~7%) based on H1 2025 data.

**Can you explain it in two minutes?** Absolutely. EssilorLuxottica makes the lenses that go in your glasses and the frames they sit in, owns the biggest brands (Ray-Ban, Oakley), and runs the stores where you buy them (LensCrafters, Sunglass Hut). Half the world needs glasses, and that number is growing. The company controls the entire supply chain from lab to counter, which is unique in the industry. It is now also the manufacturer of Meta's AI smart glasses, opening a new growth vector. This is an easy-to-understand, structurally growing business with multiple revenue layers.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Structurally growing, non-discretionary demand for vision correction combined with unique vertical integration and a new smart-glasses growth vector makes this one of the most understandable and durable consumer healthcare businesses globally.
- **Bear case:** The ~37% price decline from November 2025 highs may reflect concerns about the Meta partnership economics (pricing disputes reported in Feb 2026), tariff risks on imported eyewear, and near-term margin pressure from the Supreme acquisition.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is transparent, well-documented, and structurally simple to understand despite its global scale.

## Red Flags
- The Supreme acquisition ($1.5B, July 2024) is a departure from core eyewear competency and raises questions about strategic discipline
- Reported tension with Meta over smart glasses pricing (Feb 2026) could affect the highest-growth product line
- The stock's ~37% decline in 4 months suggests the market is pricing in risks not yet fully visible

## Score: 9 / 10
EssilorLuxottica is among the most understandable businesses in the global consumer/healthcare space — non-discretionary demand, clear value chain, transparent economics, and a business model explainable in under two minutes. The only deduction is the foray into Supreme (streetwear), which muddies the narrative slightly.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — EL.PA

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Data Sources:** EssilorLuxottica FY2025 results press release, Moody's credit opinion (Sep 2025), Yahoo Finance financials, Rijnberk Invest Insights analysis, Fortune (Jun 2025), CNBC reporting, company investor relations, Freakonomics podcast on eyewear pricing

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | EssilorLuxottica holds ~50%+ global market share in corrective lenses and ~27% of the total eyewear market — a dominant position no competitor comes close to matching | 5 |
| 2 | The company is the only vertically integrated player in the industry, operating 600+ factories, 128 distribution centers, and 17,500+ retail stores | 5 |
| 3 | Brand portfolio includes Ray-Ban (the world's best-selling eyewear brand), Oakley, Varilux, Transitions, and 20+ luxury licenses, creating multi-layered pricing power | 5 |
| 4 | Gross margins have held at 60-64% for four consecutive years despite inflationary pressures, demonstrating pricing power | 4 |
| 5 | Competitors are structurally disadvantaged: the next largest pure-play rivals (Safilo, Marchon) are a fraction of EssilorLuxottica's size and lack vertical integration | 4 |
| 6 | Low ROIC (~5.2%) is misleading — it reflects massive goodwill and intangibles from the Essilor-Luxottica merger and GrandVision acquisition, not poor capital efficiency | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Moat type: Vertical integration + Brand + Scale + Switching costs.** EssilorLuxottica possesses the rarest moat structure in consumer goods: it controls every step of an industry's value chain while simultaneously owning the most recognized brands in the category. No other eyewear company manufactures both lenses and frames at scale, operates its own retail chains, and owns the dominant consumer brands. This vertical integration creates a reinforcing loop — the company captures margin at each layer, funds R&D from combined cash flows, and uses its retail footprint to push its own products and brands.

**Brand moat.** Ray-Ban is the single most recognized eyewear brand in the world, with a heritage dating to 1937. It has transcended functional eyewear to become a cultural icon, providing pricing power that persists across economic cycles. Oakley dominates performance/sports eyewear. On the lens side, Varilux is the global standard in progressive lenses, and Transitions is synonymous with photochromic technology. These brands are not easily replicated — they represent decades of marketing investment, professional trust (opticians recommend Varilux/Crizal by name), and consumer habituation. The licensing model adds further reach: Prada, Chanel, Armani, and Burberry eyewear are all manufactured by EssilorLuxottica, creating dependency from luxury houses that lack eyewear manufacturing capability.

**Scale advantage.** With 600+ factories and €28.5B in revenue, EssilorLuxottica operates at a scale that provides significant unit cost advantages in lens grinding, coating, and frame production. Its 128 distribution centers enable same-day or next-day delivery to opticians in most markets — a service level independent labs cannot match. The company invests ~€664M annually in R&D (2.3% of revenue), more than most competitors' entire revenue base. This R&D spending produced the Stellest myopia management lens, advanced digital surfacing technology, and the Ray-Ban Meta platform.

**Switching costs.** For opticians and eye care professionals, switching away from EssilorLuxottica's lens portfolio means retraining staff, changing lab equipment calibrations, and losing access to Varilux/Transitions/Crizal — brands their patients specifically request. The professional solutions segment benefits from deep integration with independent opticians' workflows. For retail consumers, brand loyalty to Ray-Ban and LensCrafters creates habitual purchasing patterns. For luxury houses, terminating an EssilorLuxottica license means finding an alternative manufacturer capable of delivering quality at scale — options are extremely limited.

**Durability evidence in the numbers.** (1) Gross margins of 60-64% over FY2022-2025 demonstrate sustained pricing power. (2) Revenue has grown every year since the 2018 merger, from ~€16B to €28.5B. (3) FCF has grown from €3.2B (FY2022) to €3.8B (FY2025) with conversion consistently above 140%. (4) The company has maintained or expanded market share in every major geography. (5) Adjusted operating margin reached 16.0% in FY2025, a record.

**Where is the moat weakest?** (1) Online disruptors like Warby Parker, Zenni, and EyeBuyDirect have captured share in basic frames and single-vision lenses by bypassing traditional distribution. While these players remain small (Warby Parker ~1-2% US market share), they pressure the low end. (2) The licensing model is a two-edged sword: luxury houses can theoretically switch manufacturers, though in practice few alternatives exist. Kering pulled its Gucci license in 2017 to build its own eyewear division — this remains the most significant competitive action against EssilorLuxottica in the past decade, and it had minimal financial impact. (3) The Meta partnership, while lucrative, creates dependency on a single tech partner for the smart glasses category. Reports of pricing disagreements in February 2026 highlight this risk. (4) Regulatory antitrust scrutiny is always a background risk for a company with this level of market dominance.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** The moat is arguably the widest in all of consumer healthcare — no competitor can replicate the combination of lens leadership, brand dominance, and retail scale, and the smart glasses partnership with Meta adds an entirely new dimension of competitive advantage.
- **Bear case:** Online direct-to-consumer disruptors are slowly eroding the low end; the Meta partnership introduces dependency on a tech giant whose interests may diverge; and antitrust risk is non-trivial for a company often described as a near-monopoly.
- **Confidence:** High — The competitive advantages are structural, quantifiable, and have been widening for over two decades through organic growth and disciplined M&A.

## Red Flags
- Warby Parker and Zenni continue to grow in the US, pressuring entry-level price points
- Meta/EssilorLuxottica pricing dispute (Feb 2026) could escalate if smart glasses economics are not mutually attractive
- EU and US antitrust regulators imposed conditions on the GrandVision acquisition; further M&A may face increasing scrutiny
- Supreme acquisition ($1.5B) could dilute management focus away from core moat reinforcement

## Score: 9 / 10
EssilorLuxottica possesses one of the widest and most durable moats in global consumer goods — a vertically integrated near-monopoly with dominant brands, structural scale advantages, and high switching costs, tempered only by the emerging online DTC threat and the complexity introduced by the Meta partnership dependency.

# Management & Capital Allocation — EL.PA

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16

| 5 | Over 80% of executive compensation is performance-linked, and the board includes a majority of independent directors, consistent with AFEP-MEDEF governance code | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and alignment.** The dominant shareholder is Delfin S.a r.l., which holds approximately 32% of EssilorLuxottica shares. Delfin was the personal holding vehicle of the late Leonardo Del Vecchio, founder of Luxottica. Upon his death in June 2022, ownership was split equally (12.5% each) among his wife and seven children/stepchildren. This fragmented ownership has created a three-year governance crisis: the heirs have been unable to agree on strategic direction, dividend policy, or board composition. Dividends from Delfin to the family have been capped at 10% of net profit, and meaningful Delfin-level governance changes have been frozen. As of March 2026, Leonardo Maria Del Vecchio is reportedly close to a deal to buy out two siblings' stakes, which would give him 37.5% of Delfin and effective control. This leveraged buyout is expected to complete by end of 2026. If successful, it would resolve the governance overhang and restore strategic clarity at the shareholder level.

**CEO and management quality.** Francesco Milleri has served as CEO since June 2022 and also holds the role of Chairman. He was a close confidant of Leonardo Del Vecchio and has deep institutional knowledge. Under his leadership, the company has achieved record revenue growth (11.2% constant currency in FY2025), successfully integrated GrandVision, scaled Ray-Ban Meta production, and expanded adjusted operating margins to 16.0%. Milleri is also Chairman of Delfin, which creates an unusual and potentially conflicting dual role — he simultaneously runs the operating company and chairs the entity that controls the largest shareholder. This dual-hatting raises governance questions, though operationally Milleri has delivered. The proposed board renewal (February 2026) proposes renewal of eight directors, suggesting continuity rather than disruption.

**Capital allocation track record.** (1) **M&A:** The GrandVision acquisition (€8B, 2021) was strategically sound — it added 7,200 retail stores and consolidated the European optical retail market. Integration has proceeded well and is contributing to margin expansion. The Supreme acquisition ($1.5B, 2024) is more controversial — it ventures outside core eyewear into streetwear, and the strategic logic (lifestyle brand platform) is unproven. This is the one capital allocation decision that raises a yellow flag. (2) **Buybacks:** The company repurchased ~€376M of shares in FY2025 and launched a new 5M-share buyback program in February 2026. However, buybacks to date are small relative to the company's €3.8B FCF, and shares are primarily repurchased for employee stock plans rather than for cancellation. Net share count has been roughly stable. (3) **Dividends:** A €4.00/share dividend has been proposed for FY2025 (payable June 2026), implying ~€1.85B in total payouts. However, the Delfin dispute has suppressed Delfin-level dividends, which indirectly affects the company's shareholder return signaling. Moody's projects annual EssilorLuxottica dividend payments of €600-650M in 2025-2026 (lower than the €1.2B paid in 2024) due to an increase in dividends paid in shares rather than cash. (4) **Reinvestment:** R&D spending of €664M (2.3% of revenue) and capex of ~€1.5B/year support organic growth. The investment in Ray-Ban Meta production capacity (targeting 10M units/year by end 2026) appears well-timed given demand.

**Compensation and governance.** Over 80% of executive compensation is conditional on company performance, with criteria spanning financial metrics and ESG targets. The board includes a majority of independent directors and operates under the French AFEP-MEDEF corporate governance code. Committees include Nomination & Compensation, Audit & Risk, Sustainability, and a Governance Committee. The governance framework is structurally sound at the EssilorLuxottica board level — the dysfunction sits above at the Delfin shareholder level.

**Candor assessment.** Management communication has been generally transparent. The FY2025 results presentation clearly broke out segment performance, smart glasses contribution, and margin drivers. However, the company has been notably reticent about the Delfin succession dispute and the Meta pricing disagreement — both are material issues where greater transparency would be appreciated. The company does not provide formal long-term EPS guidance, which is common among European large-caps but limits forward visibility.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** If the Delfin succession resolves in 2026 as expected, it removes the single largest governance risk, unlocks more aggressive capital return, and allows management to pursue long-term strategy without shareholder-level paralysis.
- **Bear case:** The Delfin dispute could drag on, Milleri's dual role creates governance conflicts, the Supreme acquisition suggests strategic drift, and the Meta pricing tension introduces a new management challenge at a critical moment for the smart glasses category.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Operational execution is strong, but the unresolved Delfin succession and dual-hatting of the CEO create meaningful governance uncertainty that could take another 12+ months to fully resolve.

## Red Flags
- Three-year Delfin governance crisis remains unresolved as of March 2026, with frozen dividends and blocked decisions at the controlling shareholder level
- CEO Milleri's dual role as EssilorLuxottica Chairman/CEO and Delfin Chairman creates structural governance conflicts
- Supreme acquisition ($1.5B) represents strategic drift from core competency
- Share buybacks are primarily for employee plans, not shareholder-accretive cancellations
- Reported Meta pricing disagreement (Feb 2026) requires management navigation at a sensitive growth inflection point

## Score: 6 / 10
Operational execution under Milleri has been excellent, but the unresolved Delfin succession crisis, CEO dual-hatting, the puzzling Supreme acquisition, and modest shareholder returns relative to cash generation bring the stewardship score to average — governance clarity is the key variable, and it remains unresolved.

# Business Economics — EL.PA

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Data Sources:** EssilorLuxottica FY2025 results press release (Feb 2026), yfinance auto-fetched financials, GuruFocus ROIC data, MacroTrends margin history, Alpha Spread profitability ratios, EssilorLuxottica H1 2025 interim report, Moody's credit opinion (Sep 2025)

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Reported ROIC of ~5% is artificially depressed by ~€30B+ of goodwill/intangibles from the Essilor-Luxottica merger and GrandVision acquisition; ROIC on tangible capital employed is likely 20-30%+ | 5 |
| 2 | Adjusted operating margin reached 16.0% in FY2025 (vs. GAAP 11.9%), with ~€910M/year of merger-related PPA amortization depressing GAAP figures | 5 |
| 3 | Free cash flow of €2.8B (record) with FCF conversion at 163% of net income, demonstrating the asset-light economics once past acquisition accounting | 4 |
| 4 | Revenue grew 11.2% at constant currency in FY2025 to €28.5B, the first year of double-digit organic growth, driven by AI smart glasses (7M+ units sold) | 4 |
| 5 | Gross margin dipped from ~63.4% to 60.6% in FY2025, partly from US tariff headwinds and lower-margin wearables mix — a trend to monitor | 3 |
| 6 | Capital intensity is moderate: capex ~€1.5B/year on ~€28.5B revenue (5.3% of sales), with D&A of €3.1B inflated by acquisition-related amortization | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

### Returns on Capital: The Goodwill Distortion

EssilorLuxottica's reported ROIC of approximately 5% looks deeply unimpressive for a supposed quality compounder, but this is almost entirely a mirage created by acquisition accounting. The 2018 merger between Essilor and Luxottica, followed by the 2021 GrandVision acquisition (€7.2B), loaded the balance sheet with an estimated €30B+ in goodwill and identifiable intangible assets. Invested capital stands at approximately €49.7B, of which the vast majority is non-cash acquisition-related assets that do not require replacement or reinvestment.

If we strip out goodwill and acquired intangibles to isolate the tangible capital base, the picture transforms dramatically. The underlying business generates approximately €2.6B in NOPAT (adjusted operating profit of ~€4.5B taxed at an effective rate of ~25%) on tangible invested capital that is likely in the €8-12B range, implying a tangible ROIC in the range of 22-33%. This is consistent with what you would expect from a vertically integrated monopolist controlling both manufacturing (lenses, frames) and distribution (retail chains, insurance networks). ROE at 5.8% suffers from the same inflated equity base. The underlying business earns exceptional returns; the balance sheet simply carries the purchase price premium.

### Margin Structure: Adjusted vs. GAAP

The margin story requires understanding two layers. GAAP operating margin was 11.9% in FY2025 — seemingly flat over four years (12.9% in 2022, 12.5% in 2023, 13.0% in 2024). However, the company's adjusted operating margin, which strips out ~€910M/year in PPA amortization from the Essilor-Luxottica and GrandVision deals, reached 16.0% in FY2025 — a record. This adjusted figure better reflects the operational economics of the business.

Gross margin declined from 63.4% in FY2024 to 60.6% in FY2025. Management attributed this to three factors: (1) US tariffs on imports from Thailand (up to 36%) and China (up to 145%), (2) a higher mix of lower-margin wearables/smart glasses products, and (3) currency headwinds. The company responded with mid-single-digit price increases in the US. While the tariff pressure is a near-term negative, the gross margin remains very healthy at over 60%, well above most consumer/healthcare companies, reflecting the extraordinary pricing power of brands like Ray-Ban, Oakley, and the dominance of Essilor in corrective lenses.

### Cash Generation: The Crown Jewel

Free cash flow is where EssilorLuxottica's quality truly shines. The company generated a record €2.8B in free cash flow in FY2025, up from €3.2B to €3.4B in prior years (note: Yahoo data shows $3.8B which likely includes different currency/adjustments). FCF conversion of 163% of net income is extraordinary — driven by the fact that D&A (€3.1B) substantially exceeds maintenance capex requirements. Much of the D&A is non-cash amortization of acquired intangibles that will eventually roll off, meaning true owner earnings are significantly higher than reported net income.

Operating cash flow of €5.3B provides ample coverage for capex (€1.5B), dividends (~€1.5B), and bolt-on acquisitions, with substantial surplus remaining. Owner earnings (NI + D&A - capex) of €3.9B represent a more accurate view of economic earnings than reported net income of €2.3B.

### Revenue Quality and Operating Leverage

EssilorLuxottica's revenue is exceptionally high-quality. Corrective lenses are a recurring, non-discretionary purchase — approximately 60% of the world's population needs vision correction, and prescriptions must be updated regularly. The company's vertically integrated model (manufacturing lenses, designing frames, operating retail stores like Sunglass Hut and LensCrafters, and managing insurance via EyeMed) creates multiple revenue touches per customer.

FY2025 revenue of €28.5B grew 11.2% at constant currency — the first year of double-digit organic growth in the company's history.
