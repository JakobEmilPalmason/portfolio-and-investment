## Detailed Analysis

### Debt Levels and Structure

EssilorLuxottica carries total debt of €14.4B against stockholders' equity of €38.9B, resulting in a debt/equity ratio of 37% — moderate and well within comfort zones for a company of this scale. Net debt of €7.3B (after €3.5B cash) has been stable over the past two years despite the $1.5B Supreme acquisition, reflecting strong cash generation that absorbs deal-related borrowing.

The debt structure is well-diversified. The company funds through a combination of Eurobonds, commercial paper (~€2.4B outstanding), and bilateral bank facilities. In August 2024, EssilorLuxottica issued €2B in new Eurobonds (including a tranche maturing January 2030) to fund Supreme and refinance a €1.5B bond maturing in 2025. Long-term debt stood at €6.8B in FY2025, down from €8.6B in FY2024, largely due to the reclassification of a €1.25B Eurobond maturing in January 2026 to current liabilities. This reclassification explains the current ratio compression to 1.0x — not a liquidity concern but a timing artifact.

Gross debt has grown from €11.7B in 2023 to €14.4B in 2025, a 23% increase. While this is partly acquisition-driven, the pace of debt accumulation warrants attention. Management has guided to net debt/EBITDA remaining at or below 2.0x, which provides a clear guardrail.

### Interest Coverage and Debt Service

Interest coverage of 14.4x (EBIT/interest) in FY2025 is robust, though it has compressed from 20.9x in FY2024 due to rising interest expense (€239M vs. €165M). The increase reflects both higher gross debt levels and the repricing of some obligations at higher rates. At €6.5B EBITDA, the company generates more than 27x its interest expense — an exceptionally comfortable position.

Operating cash flow of €5.3B comfortably covers all fixed obligations: interest (€239M), capex (€1.5B), and dividends (~€1.5B), leaving ~€2.1B of surplus cash flow for acquisitions and debt repayment. This self-funding capacity is a key safety feature.

### Liquidity Position

As of mid-2025, EssilorLuxottica reported total available liquidity of approximately €5.7B: €2.8B in cash and cash equivalents plus €2.9B in undrawn credit facilities (including a €1.75B syndicated revolving credit facility maturing in 2028 and additional bilateral facilities). This liquidity pool comfortably covers the €3.7B in debt maturities expected through year-end 2026 (including the €2.4B commercial paper program and the €1.25B Eurobond).

The company's cash position increased from €2.3B to €3.5B during FY2025, suggesting proactive pre-funding of near-term maturities. Management expects cash flow from operations of €5.6-6.0B per year through 2026, providing ongoing refinancing capacity.

### Credit Ratings

Moody's has issued regular credit opinions on EssilorLuxottica, with the most recent update in September 2025. While the specific rating grade was not directly disclosed in public search results, the Moody's assessment projects leverage (adjusted debt/EBITDA) remaining at or below 2.0x over the next one to two years — consistent with a solid investment-grade profile. The company's access to diverse capital markets (Eurobond, commercial paper, bank facilities) at competitive rates confirms strong creditworthiness. Investment-grade issuers of this caliber typically carry ratings in the A/A2 range.

### Stress Test: 30% Revenue Drop

In a severe scenario where revenue drops 30% (from €28.5B to ~€20B), modeled as follows:

- **Revenue:** €20B (30% decline)
- **Gross profit:** ~€12B (assuming 60% gross margin holds, which is conservative)
- **Operating income (adjusted):** ~€2.0-2.5B (assuming ~50% decremental margins on lost revenue, with fixed cost absorption)
- **EBITDA:** ~€4.0-4.5B (D&A largely fixed)
- **Interest expense:** €239M (unchanged)
- **Interest coverage:** ~8-10x EBIT basis, ~17-19x EBITDA basis — still very comfortable
- **FCF:** ~€1.0-1.5B (after capex reduction to ~€1.0B and dividend cut)
- **Net debt/EBITDA:** ~1.6-1.8x — still within investment-grade parameters

The business would survive a severe downturn comfortably. Corrective lenses are non-discretionary (people need to see), and even sunglasses/luxury eyewear proved resilient during COVID-19. The 2020 experience is instructive: revenue dropped ~20% but the company remained profitable and cash-generative throughout. The combination of high gross margins, low capital intensity, and essential product demand creates a natural floor under earnings.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations

Total debt of €14.4B includes lease liabilities (IFRS 16), which are already on-balance-sheet. The company operates over 18,000 retail stores globally (including GrandVision's 7,200+ stores), so lease obligations are material but already captured in reported figures. No significant off-balance-sheet SPVs, guarantees, or contingent liabilities have been flagged in recent filings. Pension obligations are modest relative to the company's European operations.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Conservative balance sheet with 1.1x net debt/EBITDA, 14x+ interest coverage, €5.7B liquidity, and €5.3B operating cash flow — this business could take a 30% revenue hit and remain investment-grade.
- **Bear case:** Total debt grew 23% in two years to €14.4B, interest coverage compressed from 21x to 14x, and management appetite for acquisitions (Supreme, potential others) could push leverage higher in a downturn.
- **Confidence:** High — The balance sheet is clearly safe, well-structured, and supported by predictable, non-discretionary cash flows; the only question is whether the acquisition pace remains disciplined.

## Red Flags
- Total debt grew from €11.7B to €14.4B in two years (+23%) — acquisition-driven but bears monitoring
- Interest coverage compressed from 20.9x to 14.4x in one year due to rising interest expense
- Current ratio at 1.0x (from 1.1x) — technically tight, though explained by bond maturity reclassification
- Working capital turned negative (-€144M) for the first time in the data series

EssilorLuxottica's balance sheet is conservatively structured with low leverage, ample liquidity, strong interest coverage, and predictable non-discretionary cash flows that would sustain the business through a severe downturn.
