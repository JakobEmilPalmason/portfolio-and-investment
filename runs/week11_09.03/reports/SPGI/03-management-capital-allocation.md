## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Specific insider ownership percentages were not surfaced in web search results and are classified as "not found" for this analysis. What is known is that Doug Peterson's total compensation package over 12 years as CEO was tied to outcomes that took the market cap from $16B to over $135B — suggesting alignment of incentives even if direct share ownership figures are unavailable. Martina Cheung's compensation profile and share ownership details were not found in the search results; this should be verified through proxy filings before forming a strong view.

**Capital allocation track record — buybacks and dividends.** The track record here is excellent. Under Peterson, SPGI returned more than $30 billion to shareholders over his tenure via dividends and repurchases while the company's value grew by $119B+. In 2025 alone — the first full year under new CEO Cheung — $6.2B was returned ($5.0B buybacks, $1.2B dividends). The company has committed to returning at least 85% of adjusted FCF in 2026 as well, with $6B+ of FCF projected. Dividends have grown for 53 consecutive years, which is not a streak a management team breaks without extreme provocation. This is shareholder-friendly behavior by any standard.

The buyback quality question — are they buying cheap or expensive? — is harder to assess without a current valuation anchor (which is covered in Umbrella 7). What can be said is that SPGI has consistently reduced share count over many years: diluted shares fell ~2% in 2025, and the trend has been downward for years post-IHS Markit. The company did issue shares for the IHS Markit deal (an all-stock transaction), which temporarily expanded the share count significantly in 2022. Since then, it has been working to offset that dilution through buybacks — a reasonable approach, though the all-stock deal price was inherently high given 2021–2022 market valuations.

**M&A: IHS Markit — the defining deal.** The $44B all-stock merger with IHS Markit in 2022 was enormous — a bet-the-farm acquisition for a company that had been leaner and more focused pre-deal. The original synergy targets were $480M in annual run-rate cost savings and $350M in revenue synergies. Based on 2024–2025 margin expansion (adjusted operating margin reached 49.0% in 2024, up 310 bps year-over-year) and on-track segment revenue growth in Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights, the integration appears to have met or exceeded its cost synergy targets. Revenue synergy realization is harder to attribute but Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights have both grown in the mid-to-high single digits post-merger, consistent with the thesis. The Mobility spinoff (announced April 2025) also reflects disciplined post-merger portfolio pruning — management recognized that CARFAX and the automotive data business fits better as a standalone company than inside a financial data conglomerate.

**Compensation design.** The search results indicate that SBC was approximately $429M on a trailing twelve-month basis in mid-2024, declining 17% year-over-year. At roughly 3% of $14B revenue, this is not alarming. Whether compensation is tied to per-share metrics vs. revenue/EBITDA could not be confirmed from available sources — proxy statement review would be required for a definitive view.

**Candor and consistency.** The Q4 2025 earnings call transcript and investor day materials reflect a management team that guides conservatively and delivers above the midpoint. The 2025 revenue guidance entering the year was for ~5–7% growth; actual was 8%. Adjusted EPS grew 14%, and 2026 guidance calls for $19.40–$19.65 diluted EPS — an 8–10% increase. This pattern of conservative guidance and consistent delivery is a hallmark of management teams that understand their business and don't overpromise to please the Street. The decision to announce the Mobility spinoff proactively — rather than waiting for activist pressure — is another signal of management acting like owners.

**New CEO Martina Cheung.** Cheung was previously president of S&P Global Ratings and S&P Global Market Intelligence — essentially running the two highest-priority segments before becoming CEO. She is an internal operator who knows the business deeply. The Paragon Intel analysis noted her strength in M&A execution and Market Intelligence growth but flagged potential inexperience with the Indices segment. The first six months of her tenure (November 2024 through March 2026) produced a strong Q4 2025 result, a $6.2B capital return, the Mobility spinoff announcement, and 2026 guidance above consensus — a solid early record.

**Governance.** No dual-class share structure was identified. No related-party transactions were flagged in search results. The board structure appears standard for a large-cap financial services company. The 53-year consecutive dividend increase streak suggests consistent governance that prioritizes shareholders.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Cheung proves as shareholder-friendly as Peterson, continues disciplined buybacks at below-intrinsic-value prices, Mobility spin unlocks value, and SPGI compounds per-share earnings at 10–12% annually for the next decade.
- **Bear case:** The IHS Markit revenue synergies take longer to materialize than expected, Cheung faces a challenging first economic cycle with elevated rates suppressing Ratings transaction revenue, and buybacks at peak market prices prove value-dilutive rather than accretive.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Peterson's 12-year track record is outstanding and well-documented. Cheung's first year is promising but too early to call definitively. The IHS Markit integration appears on track but the revenue synergy picture requires more time to fully evaluate.

## Red Flags

- IHS Markit was an all-stock acquisition executed at 2021 valuations — inherently expensive. Cost synergies appear on track, but the full value of the deal will take years to fully prove out.
- Martina Cheung has limited public track record as CEO; her management style on capital allocation, M&A, and candor with shareholders is not yet fully established.
- Stock-based compensation (~$429M TTM mid-2024) is a meaningful offset to reported earnings; while the trend is declining, it should be monitored as AI hiring could push SBC upward.
- Insider ownership figures were not surfaced through available sources — proxy filing review required to confirm skin in the game.
- The Mobility spinoff, while strategically sound, introduces near-term execution risk and reporting complexity for investors tracking the remaining business.

## Score: 8 / 10

S&P Global's capital allocation track record under Peterson was exemplary — $30B+ returned to shareholders, a 53-year dividend streak, and the successful (if expensive) IHS Markit integration. The new CEO inherits a well-run enterprise and has delivered a strong first year. The deduction reflects the transition risk of a new CEO, incomplete visibility into Cheung's long-term capital allocation philosophy, and the unresolved question of how much the all-stock IHS Markit deal ultimately cost shareholders in dilution terms.
