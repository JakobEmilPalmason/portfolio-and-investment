# Investment Analysis: SPGI — S&P Global Inc.

**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Verdict:** Watch

> S&P Global is one of the most defensible large-cap businesses on earth — a regulatory-enforced near-monopoly in bond ratings, an irreplaceable benchmark franchise in the S&P 500, and a subscription data empire with high switching costs — generating $5.5B in annual free cash flow with margins only monopolies achieve. ## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: S&P Global charges mandatory tolls on global capital markets — issuers pay for credit ratings they cannot legally skip, fund managers pay asset-linked fees to license the S&P 500 benchmark, and financial institutions pay recurring subscriptions for proprietary data they have embedded into their workflows.

2. **Moat**: A government-enforced regulatory barrier (NRSRO recognition) locks out new rating agencies, $19 trillion in AUM benchmarked to S&P indices creates a self-reinforcing network standard no competitor can displace, and deep workflow integration in Market Intelligence makes switching costly even when a better product exists.

3. **Economics**: The Ratings and Indices segments earn 64–69% GAAP operating margins, the overall business generates $5.5B in free cash flow on $15.3B in revenue (~36% FCF margin), and adjusted EPS grew 14% in 2025 on 8% revenue growth — clear evidence of both pricing power and operating leverage.

4. **Management**: Under Doug Peterson and continued by new CEO Martina Cheung, S&P Global returned $6.2B to shareholders in 2025 alone ($5B buybacks + $1.2B dividends), has compounded dividends for 53 consecutive years, and commits to returning at least 85% of adjusted FCF annually — a record that speaks louder than any stated philosophy.

5. **Debt risk**: With 21x interest coverage, net debt of $10.7B against $5.5B in annual FCF (roughly 2x payback), and fixed-rate debt laddered to 2031–2035, SPGI could survive a 30% revenue decline for two consecutive years and still service all obligations without strain.

6. **Price**: At $429 (~22x 2026 adjusted EPS), the market is pricing in roughly 7–8% FCF growth tapering to 3–4% terminal growth — not perfection, but these assumptions must hold; if organic growth averages 4–5% instead of 7%, the base-case intrinsic value falls to ~$320–$350 and the current price offers no cushion.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is the margin of safety — a regulatory near-monopoly in bond ratings, an irreplaceable benchmark in the S&P 500, and $5.5B in annual FCF that could retire all long-term debt in two years — but the price margin of safety is thin, with conservative DCF models placing fair value at $369–$435, essentially at or below the current price.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Ratings segment revenue declines for three or more consecutive quarters due to structural (not cyclical) causes, if Market Intelligence organic growth falls below 3% for two consecutive years signaling AI-driven subscription erosion, or if management executes a large dilutive acquisition at peak-cycle valuations that destroys the per-share compounding.

## Key Risks

1. **Ratings revenue cyclicality (Likelihood: Medium / Impact: High)** — The Ratings segment (~31% of revenue) is the highest-margin division but the most volatile, with revenues tied directly to debt issuance volumes. A prolonged period of elevated rates or credit spread widening could suppress transaction revenue for multiple years, as occurred in 2022–2023. This is the single most likely near-term earnings headwind.

2. **AI disruption of Market Intelligence (Likelihood: Low-Medium / Impact: Medium)** — Market Intelligence (~32% of revenue) faces credible competitive pressure from AI-native data platforms and Bloomberg's continued dominance on the trading desk. Large language models could commoditize financial data delivery at lower cost, potentially eroding retention and pricing power over a 3–7 year horizon. Switching costs remain high but are not permanent.

3. **Price multiple compression without fundamental cause (Likelihood: Medium / Impact: Moderate)** — SPGI currently trades at 22x forward earnings, well below its historical 30–37x range. If macro de-rating continues (rising bond yields, financial services sector multiple compression), the stock could re-test $340–$370 even with intact fundamentals. This is market risk, not business risk, but it represents real near-term capital impairment.

4. **Mobility spin-off execution risk (Likelihood: Low-Medium / Impact: Medium)** — The planned mid-2026 separation of the automotive data business introduces regulatory and tax uncertainty. A delayed Form 10, adverse tax ruling, or weak reception for the separated entity would delay the expected re-rating of Core SPGI and add headline noise through year-end 2026.

5. **Regulatory attack on "issuer pays" ratings model (Likelihood: Low / Impact: High)** — The structural conflict of interest in the ratings business (issuers pay for ratings they want to be favorable) has attracted congressional and EU regulatory attention since 2008. No structural reform has materialized in 17 years, but if it did — forced separation, fee caps, or alternative model mandates — it would strike at the highest-margin division. The probability is low; the potential impact is severe.

## Detailed Analysis

**What does S&P Global sell and to whom?** S&P Global is a data and intelligence company that serves financial markets. It sells five main categories of product: (1) credit ratings on bonds, which banks, pension funds, and governments worldwide rely on to assess default risk; (2) financial data and analytics through Market Intelligence, used by buy-side and sell-side institutions; (3) index products — the S&P 500 and thousands of other benchmarks — licensed to ETF managers, fund families, and derivatives exchanges; (4) energy and commodity pricing data through Commodity Insights, sold to energy companies, traders, and policymakers; and (5) automotive data (CARFAX, dealer tools) through Mobility, sold to dealers, insurers, and OEMs. Customers are overwhelmingly institutional — banks, asset managers, insurance companies, corporates, and governments.

**How does it make money?** The revenue model has two distinct engines. The Ratings business earns fees from issuers — companies and governments — each time they issue a bond or want to maintain a credit rating. This revenue is partly recurring (surveillance fees) and partly transaction-linked (new issuances), making it sensitive to debt market volumes. The other four segments are predominantly subscription-based: customers pay annual or multi-year fees for ongoing access to data platforms, benchmarks, and analytics tools. S&P Dow Jones Indices also earns asset-linked fees (a share of AUM in products benchmarked to S&P indices), which grow as index-linked ETF assets swell — a compelling passive royalty stream that requires no incremental cost to service.

**What are the key drivers?** There are four variables that matter most. First, debt issuance volumes: when interest rates are high and corporates postpone new bond deals, Ratings revenue suffers. When rates fall and refinancing activity surges, Ratings revenue spikes. Second, equity market levels and AUM growth: the Indices segment's asset-linked fees scale directly with the dollar value of assets benchmarked to S&P products. Third, subscription renewal rates and pricing power in Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights — these drive the majority of stable, recurring revenue. Fourth, the broader macro environment for financial market activity: more transactions, more data demand, more regulatory complexity all lift the business. The 2024–2025 period benefited from a strong rebound in Ratings following a tepid 2022–2023 debt issuance environment.

**How predictable is the revenue?** The majority — roughly 75–80% — of total revenue is recurring. Market Intelligence (~$4.9B) and Commodity Insights (~$2.2B) are nearly entirely subscription-based, with multi-year contracts and high renewal rates. The Indices segment has a hybrid model: subscription/exchange fees are stable, while asset-linked fees fluctuate with AUM levels. Ratings is the most volatile segment because a significant portion ties directly to whether borrowers are tapping capital markets. In a year like 2023, when bond issuance slumped, Ratings revenue declined. In 2024–2025, a recovery in investment-grade and high-yield issuance drove Ratings revenues back up ~8%. Overall, the business is more predictable than the Ratings label might suggest, because subscriptions across the other four segments provide a durable revenue floor.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?** Customers span the global financial system — there is no single dominant customer. On the Ratings side, every major corporation, bank, and sovereign government that issues debt is a customer. On the data side, virtually every large asset manager, investment bank, and financial institution subscribes to at least one S&P product. Customer concentration is low; no single customer likely accounts for more than 2–3% of revenue. This broad dispersion makes the revenue stream unusually stable. Switching behavior varies: Market Intelligence customers face meaningful switching costs (data embedded in workflows), while Ratings customers theoretically could use Moody's or Fitch but rarely do so for investment-grade mandates where dual ratings are standard.

**Can you explain this business in two minutes?** Yes, easily. S&P Global is paid to tell the world what things are worth and how risky they are. Bond investors need credit ratings before they buy. ETF investors use S&P 500 benchmarks every day without thinking about it — and fund managers pay S&P for that. Energy traders need Platts price assessments. Banks need financial data that Bloomberg doesn't fully cover. S&P sits at critical information chokepoints in global markets and charges tolls to pass through. The business is not trivial to understand in full, but the core logic is simple: financial markets cannot function without trusted data and benchmarks, and S&P is one of only two or three institutions globally that provide them at scale.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Rising debt issuance volumes drive a multi-year Ratings rebound while index AUM growth compounds passively, pushing adjusted operating margins toward 52%+ by 2030.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged credit market freeze (elevated rates, recession) hammers Ratings transaction revenue while AI-driven data commoditization erodes Market Intelligence subscription pricing.
- **Confidence:** High — this business model is well-documented, multi-decade, and straightforward to understand at the segment level. The main uncertainty is the cyclicality of Ratings revenue and the pace of Mobility separation.

## Red Flags

- The Ratings segment's transaction revenue (roughly 20–25% of total company revenue) is cyclical and tied to debt market conditions — not a structural concern but a source of earnings volatility.
- The IHS Markit acquisition ($44B, 2022) significantly expanded the business; while integration is largely complete, Market Intelligence is still absorbing legacy IHS products, adding some operational complexity.
- Mobility spinoff (mid-2026) will remove ~$1.75B of revenue, temporarily changing the revenue mix and requiring investors to re-underwrite the remaining business profile.
- Post-spinoff, the segment reporting structure will change, which introduces some analytical noise in near-term comparisons.

## Score: 9 / 10

S&P Global's business model is one of the clearest and most coherent in the large-cap universe — financial infrastructure embedded so deeply into global capital markets that it is genuinely difficult to imagine the system operating without it. The slight deduction reflects the Ratings segment's inherent cyclicality and the transitional complexity introduced by the Mobility spinoff.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — SPGI

**Date:** 2026-03-13

| 1 | S&P Global controls approximately 40% of the global credit ratings market; combined with Moody's (~34%), the two firms hold roughly 80% of global market share — a duopoly with limited competitive erosion in 50+ years | 5 |

| 3 | Adjusted operating margins reached 49.0% in 2024 and are guided toward 48.5%–49.5% in 2025 (ex-Mobility), with a 2030 target of ~52.4% — a multi-decade record of high-margin business that is the clearest empirical evidence of a wide moat | 5 |
| 4 | Market Intelligence customers face deep workflow integration switching costs — financial data is embedded in trading desks, risk systems, and compliance workflows, making migration to a competitor costly and disruptive | 4 |
| 5 | Entry into credit ratings is blocked by SEC/ESMA regulatory recognition requirements (NRSRO status in the US), brand trust built over a century, and the "issuer pays" model that structurally rewards incumbents | 5 |
| 6 | Free cash flow conversion has been at or above 100% of adjusted net income every year, and 2025 FCF returned $6.2 billion to shareholders — this consistency is only possible with a genuine cost moat | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What type of moat exists?** S&P Global has not one but four overlapping moat sources, which is rare and makes it exceptionally durable.

The first and most important is a **regulatory moat with brand trust**. Becoming a Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization (NRSRO) in the US requires SEC approval — a process that takes years and has been granted to only a handful of firms. Globally, ESMA recognition for EU use carries similar barriers. The "Big Three" (S&P, Moody's, Fitch) are hardwired into bond indenture language, capital regulations, pension fund mandates, and central bank collateral frameworks. Even if a new agency produced better ratings, institutional buyers cannot use them until they are recognized by the regulatory infrastructure. This is a structural barrier of extraordinary durability — it has not meaningfully eroded in 50 years.

The second moat is **switching costs** in the data businesses. Market Intelligence customers (banks, asset managers, insurers) embed S&P data into their internal systems, risk models, and regulatory reporting workflows. Ripping out a data provider is not like canceling a magazine subscription — it requires months of IT work, workflow redesign, and staff retraining. Commodity Insights (formerly Platts) is similarly entrenched: Platts price benchmarks are written directly into oil and gas contracts, LNG deals, and commodity derivatives. The benchmark itself becomes the switching cost.

The third moat is a **network effect in indices**, specifically for the S&P Dow Jones Indices business. The S&P 500 is the dominant benchmark not because it is technically superior to competing indices, but because every portfolio manager, every ETF issuer, and every derivative exchange uses it. This creates a self-reinforcing standard: as more assets track the S&P 500, it becomes the de facto definition of "the market," which draws in even more AUM and licensing revenue. The $19 trillion+ in assets benchmarked to S&P indices as of recent estimates is a network effect that would take decades to dislodge.

The fourth is a **data depth and history moat**. S&P Global has accumulated decades of proprietary historical financial data, credit opinion archives, commodity price histories, and automotive data. This archive — particularly in Ratings and Commodity Insights — cannot be replicated quickly by a new entrant. Historical data is itself a product that customers pay for, and the longer S&P operates, the deeper and more valuable this archive becomes.

**How durable is the moat?** The regulatory and benchmark moats have shown no meaningful erosion across multiple business cycles and regulatory reforms (including post-2008 scrutiny of rating agencies). The data/workflow moat is slightly more vulnerable: a well-capitalized competitor (Bloomberg, FactSet, or an AI-native startup) could theoretically win market share in Market Intelligence by offering superior AI-powered analytics at lower cost. However, the switching cost structure — data embedded in workflows — limits how quickly that disruption could occur even if a superior product emerged. The Morningstar commentary specifically noted that "existing clients would face switching costs in the market intelligence segment even in the face of artificial intelligence disruption." The Commodity Insights moat is strongest of all the data segments: benchmark pricing written into long-term contracts is essentially impossible to displace mid-contract.

**Evidence of moat in the numbers.** Adjusted operating margins were 49.0% in 2024 — among the highest of any large-cap company on earth. The Indices segment runs at ~70% operating margin. Free cash flow conversion has been at or above 100% of adjusted net income every year — a remarkable consistency that reflects a business where customers pay upfront subscriptions and capital requirements are minimal. Revenue has grown from roughly $7.4B in 2019 to $15.3B in 2025 — more than doubling — while margins expanded, not contracted. This is the signature of a pricing-power business.

**Where is the moat weakest?** The Market Intelligence segment faces the most credible competitive threat. Bloomberg Terminal dominates on the trading desk. FactSet, Refinitiv/LSEG, and Morningstar all compete in adjacent spaces. AI-native data providers could potentially package equivalent financial data with better UX at lower price. This is the segment where S&P is a strong #2 or #3 in several sub-categories rather than a dominant #1. The risk is real but manageable — switching costs remain high, and S&P has been investing heavily in AI-integrated data products to defend the position.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** No. Unlike hardware or software companies where product advantages can be erased by the next generation, S&P's advantages are structural and institutional. A better ratings methodology does not displace S&P's regulatory recognition. A better index algorithm does not displace the S&P 500's status as the universal benchmark. The advantages compound over time rather than decay.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The regulatory moat in Ratings, the benchmark network effect in Indices, and subscription stickiness in Market Intelligence combine to generate 10%+ annual EPS growth for the next decade with minimal capital investment — a near-perfect compounder.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged regulatory review of NRSRO conflicts-of-interest (the "issuer pays" model has attracted scrutiny since 2008) leads to structural reforms that reduce Ratings pricing power, while AI-native competitors erode Market Intelligence retention rates over 5–7 years.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat evidence is unusually strong and multi-dimensional. The main uncertainty is the pace of AI disruption in data products and how S&P's Market Intelligence products compete with next-generation analytics platforms.

## Red Flags

- The "issuer pays" model in credit ratings is a structural conflict of interest that has attracted regulatory scrutiny since the 2008 financial crisis. While no structural reform has materialized, this remains a latent political risk.
- Bloomberg Terminal retains significant mindshare on the trading desk — Market Intelligence has not fully closed the gap, particularly among top-tier sell-side institutions.
- The S&P 500 index methodology is straightforward and could theoretically be replicated by a competitor index; the moat here is institutional inertia and regulatory recognition, not technical complexity. If that inertia shifted, the asset-linked fee stream would be at risk — though the probability of the S&P 500 being replaced as the dominant benchmark is extremely low.
- AI disruption risk to the data businesses is real and not fully quantifiable. A well-funded AI-native competitor offering commoditized financial data with better interfaces could erode Market Intelligence retention at the margin.

## Score: 9 / 10

S&P Global has one of the widest and most durable economic moats of any company in the large-cap universe — regulatory recognition barriers in Ratings, benchmark network effects in Indices, and deep workflow switching costs in Market Intelligence create a layered defense that has compounded value for decades. The single-point deduction reflects the genuine, if slow-moving, risk of AI disruption in the data segment and the unresolved structural tension in the "issuer pays" ratings model.

Based on 2024–2025 margin expansion (adjusted operating margin reached 49.0% in 2024, up 310 bps year-over-year) and on-track segment revenue growth in Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights, the integration appears to have met or exceeded its cost synergy targets. This pattern of conservative guidance and consistent delivery is a hallmark of management teams that understand their business and don't overpromise to please the Street. The decision to announce the Mobility spinoff proactively — rather than waiting for activist pressure — is another signal of management acting like owners.

**New CEO Martina Cheung.** Cheung was previously president of S&P Global Ratings and S&P Global Market Intelligence — essentially running the two highest-priority segments before becoming CEO. She is an internal operator who knows the business deeply. The Paragon Intel analysis noted her strength in M&A execution and Market Intelligence growth but flagged potential inexperience with the Indices segment. The first six months of her tenure (November 2024 through March 2026) produced a strong Q4 2025 result, a $6.2B capital return, the Mobility spinoff announcement, and 2026 guidance above consensus — a solid early record.

## Detailed Analysis

**Holding Period Suitability**

SPGI is one of the cleanest long-duration businesses available to public market investors. The investment thesis does not depend on near-term events, earnings beats, or macro catalysts. It depends on these durable realities: (1) the global bond market will continue to issue debt and require credit ratings, (2) the passive investing revolution will continue to license the S&P 500 index, and (3) financial professionals will continue to pay for proprietary data and analytics.

None of these trends require a near-term catalyst to play out — they compound over years and decades. The thesis is completely valid without any specific event occurring. Holding for 5–10 years requires no heroic assumptions beyond "the global financial system continues to function."

The Mobility spin-off is a near-term event (mid-2026) that might improve the risk/reward, but it is a bonus, not the thesis. If it gets delayed a year, the core business keeps compounding.

**Volatility Tolerance**

The stock's current 52-week range ($381.61 to $579.05) implies a peak-to-trough drawdown of ~34%. The stock is sitting ~26% below its peak as of this writing. An investor who bought near the August 2025 high is sitting on a significant paper loss.

The key question is: did the business deteriorate to justify this decline? The answer is no. Full-year 2025 adjusted EPS grew 14% to $17.83. Revenue grew 8%. FCF was $5.46B. The sell-off was driven by two things: (1) the 2026 guidance missed elevated Street expectations, and (2) broader market risk-off sentiment pulling down financial services stocks. The fundamentals are intact.

A patient investor who bought SPGI at $429 and watched it fall to $381 (the recent low) would need to hold through a 11% paper loss without panicking. Given that the business is clearly not impaired, this is exactly the kind of drawdown to hold or add through — not sell through.

Historically, SPGI has experienced deep drawdowns during credit crises (2008) and COVID (2020) but recovered strongly each time because the underlying business was never structurally impaired. This is a stock that behaves volatile because markets are emotional, not because the business is fragile.

**Catalysts**

Near-term catalyst: Mobility spin-off completion (mid-2026, ~3–6 months away). This is within management's control to a significant degree (subject to regulatory approvals and Form 10 effectiveness). The market appears to be discounting some execution uncertainty on this spin-off — completion should serve as a re-rating event for Core SPGI, as the remaining business (Ratings, Market Intelligence, Indices, Energy) commands a higher pure-play data multiple.

Medium-term catalyst: Ratings revenue recovery. If debt issuance volumes return to the 2021–2022 pace as borrowers refinance at lower rates, Ratings operating leverage is significant. This is dependent on the macro rate environment — partially outside management's control.

Ongoing compounder (not a catalyst): $5B+ annual buyback program at current prices is reducing share count ~3% annually, mechanically growing EPS even without revenue growth. At $429/share, $5B buys ~11.6M shares (~3.7% of float), which is a meaningful EPS tailwind with no effort.

**Position Sizing Logic**

SPGI is a business you can understand deeply and hold with conviction. The main risks are cyclical (Ratings revenue) and macro (rate environment) — not existential. These risks are manageable and bounded.

Appropriate sizing logic:
- Core position (high conviction, 3–8% of portfolio): appropriate if you have thoroughly analyzed the business and accept that fair-to-slightly-rich valuation means near-term returns may be muted.
- Starter position (2–3%): appropriate if waiting for a better entry point (sub-$400 would restore a more meaningful margin of safety) or still building conviction on the Mobility spin-off dynamics.
- Avoid oversizing (>10%): while the business is excellent, there is no screaming margin of safety at $429 — concentration above 10% of portfolio requires a stronger valuation cushion.

A practical approach: initiate a starter position at current prices and add meaningfully if the stock tests the $380–400 range again (which was the recent 52-week low, and represents a ~7–15% decline from here).

**Correlation and Portfolio Overlap**

SPGI is a financial services company but it is not a bank or insurer — it does not carry credit risk on its own balance sheet. It is a data/information infrastructure business that happens to serve financial markets. This makes it less correlated with a typical financial services portfolio than its sector classification implies.

It does overlap with: other financial data businesses (Bloomberg, MSCI, FactSet, Verisk), passive investment beneficiaries (Vanguard, BlackRock positions), and companies with significant Ratings exposure. Investors already holding MSCI or Moody's should be careful about over-concentration in the financial data/ratings theme — owning both SPGI and MSCI is roughly two bets on the same "financial information as a tollbooth" theme.

**Process Discipline**

Entry plan: Current price ($429) is reasonable for a starter position; add on dips toward $380–$400 (recently tested, represents a genuine margin of safety entry). Do not chase above $480–$500 without reassessing the thesis.

Sell rules:
- Thesis break: Ratings revenue declines for 3+ consecutive quarters due to structural (not cyclical) causes; AI demonstrably erodes Market Intelligence subscriptions; management destroys capital through dilutive M&A.
- Valuation extreme: Stock re-rates to 35x+ forward earnings without commensurate earnings growth acceleration — sell at least a partial position.
- Better alternative: A higher-conviction opportunity with a larger margin of safety becomes available.

Review cadence: Quarterly review of the thesis tied to each earnings release. Key metrics to track: organic revenue growth by segment, Market Intelligence net revenue retention, Ratings issuance volumes, and FCF conversion rate.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Patient holder earns 7–10% annually in FCF-per-share growth plus modest multiple expansion from the Mobility spin-off, compounding to a 50–70% total return over 5 years without requiring any heroic assumptions.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged credit market freeze (elevated rates, recession) suppresses Ratings revenue for 2–3 years, FCF growth stalls, and the stock languishes at $380–420 — the patient investor earns minimal returns but does not suffer permanent capital loss.
- **Confidence:** High — SPGI's business model, compounding mechanics, and capital allocation are highly predictable; the main uncertainty is macro timing, not business quality.

## Red Flags

- The stock's 26% drawdown from peak occurred during what appears to be intact business fundamentals — this tests investor discipline; a weak-handed owner might sell at exactly the wrong time.
- The 2026 guidance miss (below Street expectations) on the Q4 2025 earnings call caused a significant one-day sell-off — this could repeat if future quarters disappoint consensus, creating unnecessary volatility for holders.
- Mobility spin-off introduces ~6 months of headline uncertainty; the stock may remain range-bound until the spin-off completes and investors can value the two parts separately.
- Investors already holding Moody's (MCO) or MSCI have significant overlap with SPGI — double-counting the financial data/ratings theme is a real portfolio risk.

## Score: 8 / 10

SPGI is an excellent long-duration fit for a patient, Buffett-style investor — the business compoundsreliably, capital allocation is shareholder-friendly, volatility is driven by market emotion not business deterioration, and the thesis is simple enough to hold through drawdowns with conviction; the only reason it doesn't score higher is that a slightly below-fair-value entry price would improve the risk/reward.
