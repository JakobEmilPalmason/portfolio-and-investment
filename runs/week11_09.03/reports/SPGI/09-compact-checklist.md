# Compact Checklist — SPGI

**Date:** 2026-03-13

1. **Business**: S&P Global charges mandatory tolls on global capital markets — issuers pay for credit ratings they cannot legally skip, fund managers pay asset-linked fees to license the S&P 500 benchmark, and financial institutions pay recurring subscriptions for proprietary data they have embedded into their workflows.

2. **Moat**: A government-enforced regulatory barrier (NRSRO recognition) locks out new rating agencies, $19 trillion in AUM benchmarked to S&P indices creates a self-reinforcing network standard no competitor can displace, and deep workflow integration in Market Intelligence makes switching costly even when a better product exists.

3. **Economics**: The Ratings and Indices segments earn 64–69% GAAP operating margins, the overall business generates $5.5B in free cash flow on $15.3B in revenue (~36% FCF margin), and adjusted EPS grew 14% in 2025 on 8% revenue growth — clear evidence of both pricing power and operating leverage.

4. **Management**: Under Doug Peterson and continued by new CEO Martina Cheung, S&P Global returned $6.2B to shareholders in 2025 alone ($5B buybacks + $1.2B dividends), has compounded dividends for 53 consecutive years, and commits to returning at least 85% of adjusted FCF annually — a record that speaks louder than any stated philosophy.

5. **Debt risk**: With 21x interest coverage, net debt of $10.7B against $5.5B in annual FCF (roughly 2x payback), and fixed-rate debt laddered to 2031–2035, SPGI could survive a 30% revenue decline for two consecutive years and still service all obligations without strain.

6. **Price**: At $429 (~22x 2026 adjusted EPS), the market is pricing in roughly 7–8% FCF growth tapering to 3–4% terminal growth — not perfection, but these assumptions must hold; if organic growth averages 4–5% instead of 7%, the base-case intrinsic value falls to ~$320–$350 and the current price offers no cushion.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself is the margin of safety — a regulatory near-monopoly in bond ratings, an irreplaceable benchmark in the S&P 500, and $5.5B in annual FCF that could retire all long-term debt in two years — but the price margin of safety is thin, with conservative DCF models placing fair value at $369–$435, essentially at or below the current price.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Ratings segment revenue declines for three or more consecutive quarters due to structural (not cyclical) causes, if Market Intelligence organic growth falls below 3% for two consecutive years signaling AI-driven subscription erosion, or if management executes a large dilutive acquisition at peak-cycle valuations that destroys the per-share compounding.
