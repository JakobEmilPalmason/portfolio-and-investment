## Detailed Analysis

**What does S&P Global sell and to whom?** S&P Global is a data and intelligence company that serves financial markets. It sells five main categories of product: (1) credit ratings on bonds, which banks, pension funds, and governments worldwide rely on to assess default risk; (2) financial data and analytics through Market Intelligence, used by buy-side and sell-side institutions; (3) index products — the S&P 500 and thousands of other benchmarks — licensed to ETF managers, fund families, and derivatives exchanges; (4) energy and commodity pricing data through Commodity Insights, sold to energy companies, traders, and policymakers; and (5) automotive data (CARFAX, dealer tools) through Mobility, sold to dealers, insurers, and OEMs. Customers are overwhelmingly institutional — banks, asset managers, insurance companies, corporates, and governments.

**How does it make money?** The revenue model has two distinct engines. The Ratings business earns fees from issuers — companies and governments — each time they issue a bond or want to maintain a credit rating. This revenue is partly recurring (surveillance fees) and partly transaction-linked (new issuances), making it sensitive to debt market volumes. The other four segments are predominantly subscription-based: customers pay annual or multi-year fees for ongoing access to data platforms, benchmarks, and analytics tools. S&P Dow Jones Indices also earns asset-linked fees (a share of AUM in products benchmarked to S&P indices), which grow as index-linked ETF assets swell — a compelling passive royalty stream that requires no incremental cost to service.

**What are the key drivers?** There are four variables that matter most. First, debt issuance volumes: when interest rates are high and corporates postpone new bond deals, Ratings revenue suffers. When rates fall and refinancing activity surges, Ratings revenue spikes. Second, equity market levels and AUM growth: the Indices segment's asset-linked fees scale directly with the dollar value of assets benchmarked to S&P products. Third, subscription renewal rates and pricing power in Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights — these drive the majority of stable, recurring revenue. Fourth, the broader macro environment for financial market activity: more transactions, more data demand, more regulatory complexity all lift the business. The 2024–2025 period benefited from a strong rebound in Ratings following a tepid 2022–2023 debt issuance environment.

**How predictable is the revenue?** The majority — roughly 75–80% — of total revenue is recurring. Market Intelligence (~$4.9B) and Commodity Insights (~$2.2B) are nearly entirely subscription-based, with multi-year contracts and high renewal rates. The Indices segment has a hybrid model: subscription/exchange fees are stable, while asset-linked fees fluctuate with AUM levels. Ratings is the most volatile segment because a significant portion ties directly to whether borrowers are tapping capital markets. In a year like 2023, when bond issuance slumped, Ratings revenue declined. In 2024–2025, a recovery in investment-grade and high-yield issuance drove Ratings revenues back up ~8%. Overall, the business is more predictable than the Ratings label might suggest, because subscriptions across the other four segments provide a durable revenue floor.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?** Customers span the global financial system — there is no single dominant customer. On the Ratings side, every major corporation, bank, and sovereign government that issues debt is a customer. On the data side, virtually every large asset manager, investment bank, and financial institution subscribes to at least one S&P product. Customer concentration is low; no single customer likely accounts for more than 2–3% of revenue. This broad dispersion makes the revenue stream unusually stable. Switching behavior varies: Market Intelligence customers face meaningful switching costs (data embedded in workflows), while Ratings customers theoretically could use Moody's or Fitch but rarely do so for investment-grade mandates where dual ratings are standard.

**Can you explain this business in two minutes?** Yes, easily. S&P Global is paid to tell the world what things are worth and how risky they are. Bond investors need credit ratings before they buy. ETF investors use S&P 500 benchmarks every day without thinking about it — and fund managers pay S&P for that. Energy traders need Platts price assessments. Banks need financial data that Bloomberg doesn't fully cover. S&P sits at critical information chokepoints in global markets and charges tolls to pass through. The business is not trivial to understand in full, but the core logic is simple: financial markets cannot function without trusted data and benchmarks, and S&P is one of only two or three institutions globally that provide them at scale.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Rising debt issuance volumes drive a multi-year Ratings rebound while index AUM growth compounds passively, pushing adjusted operating margins toward 52%+ by 2030.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged credit market freeze (elevated rates, recession) hammers Ratings transaction revenue while AI-driven data commoditization erodes Market Intelligence subscription pricing.
- **Confidence:** High — this business model is well-documented, multi-decade, and straightforward to understand at the segment level. The main uncertainty is the cyclicality of Ratings revenue and the pace of Mobility separation.

## Red Flags

- The Ratings segment's transaction revenue (roughly 20–25% of total company revenue) is cyclical and tied to debt market conditions — not a structural concern but a source of earnings volatility.
- The IHS Markit acquisition ($44B, 2022) significantly expanded the business; while integration is largely complete, Market Intelligence is still absorbing legacy IHS products, adding some operational complexity.
- Mobility spinoff (mid-2026) will remove ~$1.75B of revenue, temporarily changing the revenue mix and requiring investors to re-underwrite the remaining business profile.
- Post-spinoff, the segment reporting structure will change, which introduces some analytical noise in near-term comparisons.

## Score: 9 / 10

S&P Global's business model is one of the clearest and most coherent in the large-cap universe — financial infrastructure embedded so deeply into global capital markets that it is genuinely difficult to imagine the system operating without it. The slight deduction reflects the Ratings segment's inherent cyclicality and the transitional complexity introduced by the Mobility spinoff.
