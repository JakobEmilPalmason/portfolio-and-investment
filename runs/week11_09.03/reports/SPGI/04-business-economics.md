## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital.** Reported ROIC for SPGI sits in the 7–10% range on a trailing basis (GuruFocus shows a 3-year average of 6.6% and a TTM of ~7.9% as of late 2025). This number is inflated downward by the $44B IHS Markit acquisition (closed 2022), which added an enormous slug of goodwill and intangibles to the asset base. The underlying business — absent the acquisition premium — generates returns on tangible capital that are orders of magnitude higher. ROE stands at approximately 13%, also artificially depressed by the massive equity base created by the merger. For context: the Ratings and Indices segments require virtually no physical capital. Their competitive position comes from brand, regulatory recognition, and network effects, none of which appear on the balance sheet at replacement cost. Investors should treat GAAP ROIC here as a floor, not a ceiling.

**Margin structure.** The margin profile is exceptional and improving. Company-wide GAAP operating margin was 39% in 2024 and expanded to 42% in 2025. But the segment breakdown reveals even sharper economics: Ratings posted a 64% GAAP operating margin in 2025 (adjusted: 65%), and Indices posted 69% GAAP (adjusted: 71%). These are not software multiples applied to a commodity business — they reflect genuine pricing power and structural competitive advantage. Market Intelligence (the data/analytics platform) runs thinner at 20% GAAP / 34% adjusted in 2025, reflecting the higher cost structure of a data-intensive subscription business. Mobility (automotive data) sits at 22% GAAP / 40% adjusted. Net income for FY2025 was approximately $4.5B on $15.3B in revenue, implying a net margin near 29%.

**Cash generation.** FCF in FY2025 was $5.5B (a slight 2% decline from $5.57B in 2024, which itself was up 56% from 2023). Cash from operations in FY2025 was $5.7B. With capex of approximately $195M, FCF conversion from operating cash flow exceeds 96%. FCF conversion relative to net income is also robust: $5.5B FCF against ~$4.5B net income implies FCF-to-net-income conversion of roughly 122%, well above 100% — a powerful signal that reported earnings understate actual cash generation, likely due to large non-cash amortization charges from the IHS Markit deal. Owner earnings (net income + D&A - maintenance capex) are estimated near $6B+ annually, given total D&A of approximately $2.5B in recent years against very modest maintenance capex. The company itself guided to ~$5.9B in operating cash flow less capex for 2025.

**Capital intensity.** SPGI is one of the most capital-light businesses in the world. PP&E sits at roughly $265M — a rounding error against a $14B+ annual revenue base. Capex was $195M in FY2025 and was guided to $160–170M in 2024. For comparison, the company generates more free cash flow in a single week than it spends on capex in a year. This means that essentially all operating profits are available to shareholders — as dividends, buybacks, or acquisitions — without needing to plow money back into physical assets just to stay competitive.

**Revenue quality.** This is perhaps SPGI's most underappreciated quality improvement over the past decade. Subscription and recurring revenue now represents over 55% of total revenue, up from ~40% in 2016. The Market Intelligence segment is almost entirely subscription-based (financial data terminals, regulatory feeds, research platforms). The Indices segment earns fees on assets under management tied to its benchmarks — as long as passive index funds exist, SPGI earns a toll. Ratings revenue is more cyclical (tied to debt issuance volumes), but even here, surveillance fees on outstanding ratings provide a recurring floor. The structural shift toward subscriptions was a major strategic goal of the IHS Markit merger, and the numbers confirm it is working.

**Operating leverage.** The 2024–2025 results confirm strong operating leverage: revenue grew 8% in 2025, but operating margin expanded 300 basis points (from 39% to 42%), meaning profits grew materially faster than revenue. Adjusted EPS grew 14% in 2025 on 8% revenue growth. Management has guided for an additional 50–75 bps of adjusted margin expansion in 2026. This pattern — moderate revenue growth compounding into faster earnings and FCF growth — is the hallmark of a high-quality franchise with a scalable cost structure.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** SPGI is a tollbooth on global capital markets and data infrastructure; as debt issuance volumes recover, passive investing grows, and financial data demand increases, the company compounds FCF at double-digit rates for a decade with minimal capital requirements.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged debt market freeze (similar to 2008–2009) crushes Ratings revenue, while the high fixed-cost Market Intelligence segment proves less flexible in a downturn than the subscription model suggests.
- **Confidence:** High — S&P Global is one of the most thoroughly documented public companies in the world, earnings releases are comprehensive, and the fundamental business model is straightforward to evaluate.

## Red Flags

- ROIC as reported is suppressed by IHS Markit goodwill; if the acquired businesses underperform, goodwill impairment risk is real (though management has not signaled this concern).
- Ratings revenue remains cyclical — FY2023 was notably weaker than FY2022, and another credit market shutdown would hit the highest-margin segment hardest.
- The Market Intelligence segment carries lower margins and higher costs, and competition from Bloomberg, Refinitiv/LSEG, and FactSet remains intense — pricing power here is meaningfully less durable than in Ratings or Indices.
- Amortization of IHS Markit intangibles (~$1.1B annually) will persist for years, depressing GAAP earnings relative to cash earnings and making comparisons to pre-merger peers difficult.

## Score: 9 / 10

SPGI operates two near-monopoly franchises (Ratings and Indices) alongside a strong subscription data business, generates extraordinary free cash flow with almost no capital reinvestment requirement, and is demonstrating clear operating leverage as the integrated post-merger business scales — the only reason it is not a 10 is the cyclicality of Ratings revenue and the moderate margin profile of Market Intelligence.
