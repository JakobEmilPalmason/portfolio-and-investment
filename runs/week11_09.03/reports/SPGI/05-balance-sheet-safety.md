## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and structure.** At fiscal year-end 2025, SPGI carried approximately $12.4B in long-term debt and $1.7B in cash, for a net debt position of roughly $10.7B. Total assets were approximately $59.7B, of which the vast majority is goodwill and intangibles from the IHS Markit acquisition. Stockholders' equity stood at approximately $33.1B (as of Q3 2025) and $37.7B in the year-prior period. The debt-to-equity ratio is approximately 0.40x (FY2025), down sharply from prior years. Most of the debt is fixed-rate and long-duration — in December 2025, SPGI priced new notes at 4.25% (due 2031) and 4.80% (due 2035), extending maturities and locking in rates before any potential refinancing stress. The company actively manages its maturity ladder, which is the correct posture for a business generating $5B+ in annual FCF.

**Interest coverage.** This is the most important metric for assessing debt safety, and SPGI's numbers here are exceptional. Interest coverage (EBIT / interest expense) is reported at approximately 21x by GuruFocus, with the Simply Wall St metric showing EBIT coverage of 21.3x. Multiple sources converge on a range of 20–23x. At this level, EBIT would need to fall more than 95% before interest payments became a concern. Even in SPGI's worst modern revenue environment (2009 or 2022 debt market slowdown), earnings did not approach anything near that decline. Interest is effectively a non-issue given current debt levels and earnings power.

**Liquidity.** Cash on hand of $1.7B at FY2025 is modest in absolute terms — this is a company that generates over $5B in FCF annually and returns the vast majority of it to shareholders, so holding excess cash is not the strategy. SPGI had a revolving credit facility in place (size not confirmed in available search results), and the business generates substantial operating cash flow every quarter. The current ratio was not directly found in search results, but the company's ability to cover short-term obligations from ongoing operations is clear given the scale of quarterly FCF. Short-term debt as of Q3 2025 was only $3M — effectively zero. There is no material near-term refinancing cliff.

**Refinancing risk.** Minimal. SPGI actively manages its maturity schedule by issuing long-duration notes (the December 2025 deal extended to 2031 and 2035) and by generating sufficient FCF to retire or repay debt without relying on capital markets. The company does not depend on market access for survival — it generates enough cash to self-fund operations and debt service even in a credit crunch. The broader debt maturity wall that SPGI's Ratings business tracks (with peaks expected in 2026 and 2028) actually benefits SPGI's Ratings revenue, creating an ironic hedge: a period of heavy corporate debt refinancing is simultaneously good for Ratings and manageable for SPGI's own obligations.

**Off-balance-sheet obligations.** Operating leases are now substantially on-balance-sheet under ASC 842. The company has contingent liabilities from regulatory actions — notably, in September 2024 the SEC charged S&P Global Ratings (along with Moody's, Fitch, and others) with recordkeeping failures, resulting in fines. These fines, while reputationally sensitive, were not of a scale that threatens financial health. Pension obligations are not a material disclosed concern from available sources. No significant off-balance-sheet commitments were identified.

**Stress test.** If SPGI's revenue fell 30% for two years — a scenario worse than the Global Financial Crisis — here is what would happen: Revenue would drop from ~$15.3B to roughly $10.7B. Operating margins would compress sharply, perhaps to 25–30% from 42%, given some fixed costs. That implies operating income of roughly $2.7–3.2B. Interest expense is approximately $500–600M annually. Interest coverage would still be well above 5x. The company would generate $2–3B in FCF under this scenario, more than enough to service $12B in debt with years of cushion remaining. There is no equity dilution risk, no survival risk, and no covenant risk in this scenario. SPGI's balance sheet can survive a severe downturn.

**Capital return commitment.** The company targets returning approximately 85% of adjusted FCF to shareholders via dividends and buybacks. In 2025, that implies roughly $5.1B returned, with quarterly dividends raised to $0.97/share (an annualized $3.88/share, representing 53 consecutive years of dividend increases). A new 30-million-share buyback program was announced at the 2025 Investor Day. This level of capital return is only possible because the balance sheet is well-managed — a heavily leveraged company could not return 85% of FCF while also maintaining investment-grade debt.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** SPGI's ~$10.7B net debt is modest relative to $7.7B in annual EBITDA and $5.5B in FCF; the balance sheet poses zero constraint on capital returns, acquisitions, or weathering a deep downturn.
- **Bear case:** A severe, multi-year ratings market freeze (like 2008–2009) compresses EBITDA materially, and while the company would still service debt comfortably, the leverage ratio would temporarily spike and buybacks would slow.
- **Confidence:** High — multiple sources confirm interest coverage above 20x, debt levels are well-documented, and the FCF generation provides an overwhelming buffer.

## Red Flags

- Cash on hand ($1.7B) is low relative to total debt ($12.4B); in a severe disruption, the company would rely on credit facility access rather than cash reserves — this is a minor flag, not a serious concern given FCF generation.
- Goodwill and intangibles represent the vast majority of total assets (~$59B in assets but only ~$265M in PP&E); if IHS Markit integration fails to deliver expected returns, a goodwill impairment could substantially reduce book equity — this is an accounting event, not a cash event, but it is worth monitoring.
- The December 2025 new debt issuance suggests SPGI was adding to gross debt rather than reducing it, consistent with a strategy of maintaining leverage for returns optimization; debt reduction is not a management priority at current leverage levels.
- Moody's issuer credit rating for SPGI was not confirmed in available sources; the company's own implied creditworthiness (given 21x interest coverage and ~1.3x Net Debt/EBITDA) would typically warrant an A-range rating.

## Score: 8 / 10

SPGI's balance sheet is conservatively structured relative to its earnings power — with interest coverage above 20x, net leverage below 1.5x EBITDA, no near-term maturities, and $5.5B in annual FCF — and the company could survive a deep, prolonged downturn without stress; the score stops short of 9 because the balance sheet is asset-heavy with intangibles from the IHS Markit acquisition and cash reserves are modest relative to gross debt.
