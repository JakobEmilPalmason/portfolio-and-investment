## Detailed Analysis

Cadence Design Systems maintains what can fairly be called a fortress balance sheet relative to its industry and earnings power. As of September 30, 2025, the company held $2.93B in cash and short-term investments against $2.48B in total debt, producing a net cash position of approximately $451M. For a company generating $1.4B+ in annual EBIT and $1.5B in free cash flow, this is a conservative capital structure. The total debt-to-EBITDA ratio is comfortably below 2x (estimated at roughly 1.4-1.5x based on ~$1.7B TTM EBITDA), well within the covenant limit of 3.5x under the 2024 Credit Facility.

The debt structure is clean and well-managed. Cadence's borrowings consist entirely of fixed-rate senior notes in three tranches: $500M of 4.200% notes due September 2027, $1.0B of 4.300% notes due September 2029, and $1.0B of 4.700% notes due 2034. These were issued in September 2024, likely to fund the acquisitions Cadence completed that year. The maturities are well-laddered — no single year presents a refinancing cliff, and the first maturity (2027) is only $500M, which Cadence could repay from a single quarter's free cash flow if needed. There is zero floating-rate exposure, eliminating interest rate risk on the existing debt. Total annual interest expense on these notes is approximately $110-115M (calculated from the coupon rates), which is easily covered by the 18.1x interest coverage ratio.

Liquidity is strong from multiple angles. Beyond the $2.93B cash pile, Cadence has an undrawn revolving credit facility (the 2024 Credit Facility) maturing in August 2029. The current ratio is healthy: $2.93B cash plus $846M receivables against $1.41B in short-term liabilities due within one year. Total shareholder equity stands at $4.7B against total assets of $9.0B, giving a reasonable equity ratio of over 50%. The balance sheet does carry significant goodwill and intangibles from acquisitions, which is typical for a software company that has grown through M&A, but the underlying cash generation makes the goodwill manageable.

Off-balance-sheet obligations are not a major concern for Cadence. The primary items would be operating lease commitments and purchase obligations, which are standard for a company of this size. There are no pension liabilities, no significant contingent liabilities flagged in recent filings, and no complex structured finance arrangements.

Running a stress test: if Cadence's revenue dropped 30% for two consecutive years (from ~$5.3B to ~$3.7B), what happens? Gross margins would likely compress somewhat but remain above 75% given the high fixed-cost nature of software. Assuming operating margins compress to 15% GAAP (from 28%), that would still produce ~$555M in operating income — enough to cover ~$115M in annual interest expense nearly 5x. The company would still generate positive free cash flow, and the $2.9B cash reserve provides an enormous buffer. Cadence would not need to issue equity or take on additional debt in this scenario. The business survived the 2008-2009 downturn and the 2020 pandemic without financial distress, and it was a much smaller company then.

The one consideration is that Cadence's $2.5B in debt is relatively new (issued September 2024), and the company historically operated with minimal debt. The debt was taken on strategically for acquisitions, and the fixed-rate, long-dated structure shows discipline. At current FCF generation rates, Cadence could retire the entire debt balance in under two years if it chose to, though it will likely maintain the leverage given the low cost relative to its returns on capital.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Net cash position, 18x interest coverage, and $1.5B annual FCF mean Cadence has essentially zero financial risk and retains full flexibility for M&A, buybacks, or organic investment.
- **Bear case:** The $2.5B debt load is new and acquisition-driven; if acquired businesses underperform, the goodwill could face impairment while debt remains, though the staggered maturities and FCF generation make this a mild concern.
- **Confidence:** High — Balance sheet data is well-documented across SEC filings and multiple financial data sources, and the capital structure is straightforward with no hidden complexities.

## Red Flags

- None identified — the balance sheet is conservatively managed with net cash, fixed-rate debt, staggered maturities, strong interest coverage, and ample liquidity

## Score: 9 / 10

Cadence carries a net cash position, 18x interest coverage, entirely fixed-rate debt with staggered maturities through 2034, and generates enough free cash flow to retire all debt in under two years — this is a fortress balance sheet that would comfortably survive a severe downturn without equity issuance.
