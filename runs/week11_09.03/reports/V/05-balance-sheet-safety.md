## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** As of September 30, 2025, Visa carried $25.2B in total debt (aggregate principal of senior unsecured notes: $25.4B), up from $20.8B in FY2024. All debt is in the form of senior unsecured notes — fixed-rate, long-duration obligations with no floating-rate exposure. Against FY2025 EBITDA of approximately $27–28B (operating income of $26.6B plus D&A), the net debt-to-EBITDA ratio is approximately 0.2x (net debt ~$5.2B / EBITDA ~$27B). Even on gross debt, the ratio is under 1x. This is one of the most conservative leverage profiles among large-cap US companies.

**Interest Coverage.** FY2025 interest expense was approximately $589M. Operating income (EBIT) was $26.6B. Interest coverage is therefore approximately 45x — well beyond any stress scenario. It bears emphasis that Visa's cash and investment portfolio generates interest income that partially or fully offsets this expense; the company is effectively net interest income positive. Debt service is not a financial risk at any foreseeable level of earnings decline.

**Liquidity.** Cash and cash equivalents plus available-for-sale investment securities totaled $20.0B at fiscal year-end (FY2025). Visa also maintains revolving credit facilities. Annual FCF generation is $21.6B. Even assuming zero revenue, Visa's $20B cash position, combined with minimal mandatory cash outflows (its infrastructure is largely cloud and network, not physical plant), would sustain operations for multiple years. The company could fund 12–24 months of operations with existing cash with enormous margin of comfort.

**Refinancing Risk.** Near-term refinancing obligations are manageable: $5.3B due in FY2026 (including the $4.0B December 2025 maturity and ~$1.6B June 2026 Euro notes) and $2.75B in FY2027. With $20B in liquid assets and $21.6B annual FCF, Visa could retire all near-term maturities from cash on hand without accessing capital markets. The company has consistent investment-grade ratings (AA-/Aa3) and has never faced difficulty accessing debt markets. Refinancing risk is negligible.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations and Litigation.** The most material off-balance-sheet exposure is litigation. Visa maintains a U.S. retrospective responsibility plan with an escrow account funded by Class B share conversions. In September 2025, Visa deposited $500M into this escrow. The long-standing MDL interchange fee litigation reached a proposed settlement in November 2025. FY2025 GAAP operating expenses rose 30% YoY, primarily due to a large litigation provision. While the MDL settlement is pending court approval and does not yet fully eliminate litigation exposure, the trajectory is toward resolution. Client incentive contracts are on-balance-sheet but require careful monitoring: $15.8B in FY2025 represents commitments to financial institution partners and merchants that partially constrain future revenue flexibility.

**Stress Test.** If Visa's revenue dropped 30% for two consecutive years — an extreme scenario implying a collapse in global payment volumes unprecedented in the modern era — FY2025 revenue would fall from $40B to ~$28B, and net income might decline to roughly $10–12B (given high fixed costs, though much of Visa's cost base is variable through client incentive contracts). Even in this scenario, Visa would remain solidly profitable with strong positive cash flow. There is no scenario in which Visa faces solvency risk or requires equity issuance unless litigation liability were to massively exceed reserves, which the retrospective responsibility plan is specifically designed to prevent.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Visa's balance sheet is a fortress: near-zero net leverage (~0.2x net debt/EBITDA), 45x interest coverage, $20B liquid assets, and $21.6B annual FCF that dwarfs all near-term debt obligations.
- **Bear case:** The 20.8% YoY increase in gross debt and growing client incentive commitments represent mild structural pressures; litigation exposure remains the primary (and manageable) tail risk.
- **Confidence:** High — FY2025 10-K data is fully reported; debt schedule, cash position, and interest expense are clearly disclosed; litigation escrow structure is well-documented.

## Red Flags

- Gross debt increased 20.8% in FY2025 to $25.2B — bear watching over the next 2–3 years to confirm this does not become a sustained leverage-building trend.
- Ongoing MDL interchange fee litigation: while the November 2025 proposed settlement is a major positive step, final court approval is pending and additional claims may persist.
- Client incentive liabilities (~$15.8B annual outflow) are quasi-contractual obligations that limit financial flexibility if volumes decline sharply.

## Score: 9 / 10

Visa's balance sheet is one of the strongest among large-cap global companies: ~0.2x net debt/EBITDA, 45x interest coverage, $20B in liquid assets, and $21.6B annual FCF that makes near-term refinancing obligations trivially manageable; the sole deduction reflects growing gross debt and unresolved (though settling) litigation tail risk.
