# Compact Checklist — V

**Date:** 2026-03-12

1. **Business**: Visa sells access to a two-sided payment network — routing authorization messages between issuing and acquiring banks on every card transaction — earning a small fraction of a percent on $14 trillion in annual payment volume with no credit risk, no inventory, and near-zero marginal cost per additional transaction.

2. **Moat**: A bilateral network effect spanning 44 million merchant locations and billions of cardholders across 200+ countries makes the acceptance footprint impossible to replicate, reinforced by switching costs so high that re-issuing cards and rebuilding merchant contracts would take issuing banks years and hundreds of millions of dollars, as confirmed by Visa's ~70% global card volume share holding stable for a decade despite billions invested in fintech alternatives.

3. **Economics**: Visa generates $21.6B in free cash flow on $40B in net revenue (54% FCF margin), sustains ~28% ROIC against an ~8% WACC, and converts over 100% of GAAP net income to free cash — on a capital base requiring only $1.5B in annual capex.

4. **Management**: Management returns capital with discipline — $18.3B in buybacks in FY2025 alone has reduced diluted share count ~12% since FY2020, governance is standard single-class with no founder entrenchment, and communication is metrics-driven and consistent — but with only 0.09% insider ownership, these are professional stewards of a great business, not owner-operators.

5. **Debt risk**: Net debt is only ~$5.2B against $21.6B in annual free cash flow (net debt/EBITDA ~0.2x), interest coverage is approximately 45x, and $20B in liquid assets means Visa could retire all near-term debt maturities from cash on hand without touching capital markets.

6. **Price**: At $310/share (~24x forward FY2026 consensus EPS of ~$13.10), the current price requires approximately 8–10% annual EPS growth over 10 years — below Visa's historical 13–15% delivery rate — meaning the market is pricing in a meaningful slowdown but not pricing in perfection, and this math only holds if the DOJ antitrust case does not produce a structural debit remedy.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself provides the primary protection — no credit risk, a moat that survived COVID and the 2008 crisis intact, and the ability to sustain profitability through a 30% revenue decline — because at $310 the price-based margin of safety is thin, with three independent DCF models placing fair value 10–27% below the current price.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if the DOJ antitrust trial (expected 2027–2028) produces a structural remedy forcing open debit routing access and management revises net revenue growth guidance materially downward as a result, or if net revenue growth falls below 6% for two consecutive quarters alongside FCF margin compression below 45%.
