## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels**

Total debt as of fiscal year-end October 2025 was approximately $6.6B (book value), with total assets of $37.6B and total equity of $21.7B. This gives a debt/equity ratio of approximately 30%, and a debt/total assets ratio below 18%. Net debt is negative — AMAT holds roughly $1.9B more cash than debt. EBITDA for FY2025 was approximately $9.5–10B (operating income ~$8.5B plus ~$1B D&A), implying a gross debt/EBITDA ratio of approximately 0.65–0.70x. This is far below even the most conservative thresholds for concern (2x). The debt maturity schedule was not granularly available in public sources, but interest expense of ~$269M annually on $6.6B of debt implies a blended coupon of approximately 4%, consistent with investment-grade long-term bonds — likely laddered across 5–30 year maturities. No evidence of refinancing urgency or near-term maturity cliff was found.

**Interest Coverage**

EBIT for FY2025 was approximately $8.5B. Interest expense was approximately $269M. This implies an interest coverage ratio of approximately 32x — among the strongest in the S&P 500 industrial-technology sector. Even if interest rates were to double on the entire debt stack upon refinancing, coverage would remain above 15x. There is no scenario under normal business conditions where interest payments represent any financial stress.

**Liquidity**

Cash and short-term investments of $8.5B, combined with an operating cash flow run rate of ~$8B annually, provides exceptional liquidity. The current ratio was reported at approximately 2.61x based on short-term assets of $21.0B against short-term liabilities of $7.8B. This means AMAT can fund over two years of operations from current assets alone, even before generating a single dollar of new revenue. The company also has access to credit facilities, though specific revolving credit facility capacity was not disclosed in public search results. Shareholders receive approximately $1.4B annually in dividends and $4–5B in buybacks — these would be the first items cut in a liquidity stress scenario, providing a large buffer before operational survival is threatened.

**Refinancing Risk**

Applied Materials carries an investment-grade credit rating and generates $8B+ in operating cash flow annually. The company has no material dependence on capital markets remaining open to fund operations. Even if the commercial paper and bond markets were entirely closed for 12 months, AMAT could continue operating, paying dividends, funding R&D, and servicing all debt from existing cash plus operating cash flow. Refinancing risk is negligible.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

The most significant off-balance-sheet items identified are operating leases: $565M in total operating lease obligations as of October 26, 2025, of which $104M is due within 12 months. This is a trivially small number relative to earnings (~3% of annual operating income). Pension liabilities were not specifically quantified in available public search results; for a U.S.-based technology manufacturer of this profile, pensions are typically modest defined-contribution structures. No evidence of material contingent liabilities was found outside the $253M export controls settlement with the U.S. Commerce Department (BIS), which has been paid/accrued as of Q1 FY2026.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Drop for Two Years**

FY2025 revenue was $28.4B. A 30% decline would reduce revenue to approximately $19.9B — roughly the FY2021 revenue level, which AMAT navigated comfortably. At that revenue level, assuming operating margins compress to approximately 20% (versus ~29% today), EBIT would be approximately $4.0B. After interest expense of $269M, pre-tax income would be ~$3.7B, generating approximately $2.8–3.0B in net income. Even in this severe scenario: annual debt service ($269M interest) is covered ~11x, dividend ($1.4B) is covered ~2x, and the company generates positive free cash flow after maintenance capex. AMAT would survive a 30% revenue decline for two years without issuing equity, drawing down cash, or violating any debt covenants. The balance sheet stress test passes comfortably.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Net cash position, 32x interest coverage, and $8B annual OCF mean the balance sheet is not a constraint on capital return, R&D investment, or M&A — AMAT has maximum strategic flexibility.
- **Bear case:** The $6.6B in long-term debt does represent a fixed obligation, and in an extreme WFE downturn combined with rising rates and a large refinancing event, costs would increase — though this remains a manageable scenario, not an existential one.
- **Confidence:** High — balance sheet data is directly sourced from the company's own earnings releases and widely reported third-party financial databases; the net cash position and coverage ratios are unambiguous.

## Red Flags

- The $253M BIS export controls settlement (Q1 FY2026) signals ongoing regulatory risk around China-related shipments; future restrictions could reduce revenue and indirectly pressure the financial profile, though they pose no balance sheet threat in isolation.
- Specific debt maturity schedule was not available in public sources; there could be lumpy near-term maturities not captured in this analysis, though the net cash position would offset any such risk.
- Operating cash flow declined from $8.7B (FY2023–FY2024) toward the $8B range in FY2025; if this trend continues, the FCF cushion narrows slightly, though it remains very large in absolute terms.

## Score: 9 / 10

Applied Materials holds more cash than total debt, carries a 32x interest coverage ratio, an Altman Z-Score of 9.74, and can survive a 30% revenue decline for two years without raising equity — this is a near-fortress balance sheet, with the single point deducted for the modest but non-zero regulatory/export-restriction overhang and unavailability of the full debt maturity schedule.
