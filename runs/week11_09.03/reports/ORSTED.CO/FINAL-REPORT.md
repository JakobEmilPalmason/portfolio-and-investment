## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Orsted builds, owns, and operates offshore wind farms — primarily in Europe — selling electricity for 20-25 years under government CfD contracts or long-term corporate PPAs with counterparties like BASF and Amazon, generating DKK 25.1 billion in adjusted EBITDA from a 10.2 GW operating fleet in 2025.

2. **Moat**: Orsted's competitive edge is a narrow, narrowing scale and execution advantage from 30+ years of offshore wind experience and approximately 24% global ex-China market share, but the UK's CfD Allocation Round 7 in 2025 awarded Orsted zero contracts while RWE won 6.9 GW, and ROCE sits at only 8-10% — not the 15%+ of a genuinely wide-moat business.

3. **Economics**: The underlying operating fleet generates stable EBITDA of DKK 24-25 billion annually, but ROIC has collapsed to approximately 1.67% against a WACC of 7.4%, gross capex runs at 65-75% of revenue, and the company has destroyed over DKK 44 billion in capital through US impairments in 2023-2024 — the business is currently earning well below its cost of capital.

4. **Management**: Management destroyed DKK 44+ billion in capital via the US expansion, forced a dilutive DKK 60 billion rights issue on shareholders, suspended dividends for three years, and then replaced the CEO with his own Deputy — an insider who was part of the same decision-making team — with no financial clawback mechanism for those responsible.

5. **Debt risk**: Orsted carries a BBB- credit rating (one notch above junk) after three successive downgrades, with FFO-to-net-debt of only 13.9% versus S&P's 25% floor for investment-grade maintenance, and survives a capital markets shutdown only because the Danish state owns 50.1% and provides an implicit sovereign backstop.

6. **Price**: At DKK 139 on 1.32 billion post-rights-issue shares (market cap ~DKK 184 billion, EV/EBITDA ~9x on 2026 guidance), the market is pricing roughly correct execution of the 8.1 GW construction pipeline and EBITDA growth toward DKK 28 billion by year-end 2026, with no premium for recovery and no discount large enough to absorb a repeat impairment cycle.

7. **Margin of safety**: There is no meaningful margin of safety at DKK 139 — the discounted bear-case intrinsic value is DKK 62-91 per share, meaning the current price sits above the pessimistic-but-plausible scenario, Morningstar's fair value of DKK 160 implies only ~15% upside, and a 20% miss on Year-5 EBITDA growth sends the base-case intrinsic value to around DKK 100 per share.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell immediately if Orsted announces a new impairment of DKK 5 billion or more on the 8.1 GW construction portfolio, because that would confirm the pattern of optimistic project underwriting has not changed under the new CEO and the entire recovery thesis depends on those assets delivering as promised.

## Key Risks

1. **Construction execution on the 8.1 GW pipeline.** This is the company's highest-probability, highest-severity risk. Orsted has demonstrated a repeating pattern of booking development-stage assets at optimistic values and writing them down when reality intervenes — DKK 50 billion in cumulative impairments across 2023-2025. The 8.1 GW currently under construction represents the entirety of Orsted's future EBITDA growth, and every project in it carries the same execution risks (turbine supply, installation vessel availability, cable delays, cost inflation) that derailed the US portfolio. A single major project running 30-50% over budget would trigger another impairment and potentially another dilutive capital raise.

2. **Credit rating trajectory — one notch from junk.** The three-notch downgrade from BBB+ to BBB- in three years is not a rounding error. FFO-to-net-debt of 13.9% is more than 11 percentage points below S&P's 25% investment-grade floor. A further downgrade to BB+ would force selling by investment-grade-constrained bond holders, spike Orsted's borrowing costs, and — given the company's structural dependence on capital markets for DKK 25-30 billion in annual net funding — could cascade into a genuine liquidity crisis that the Danish state would then be compelled to address on its own terms, not shareholders'.

3. **US political risk and residual US exposure.** Orsted exited US development but retained full ownership of Sunrise Wind after the farm-down stalled. If the Trump administration escalates hostility to offshore wind through executive orders, federal permitting holds, or IRA credit phase-outs, Sunrise Wind is at risk of another impairment. More broadly, the US experience is not merely a sunk cost — it revealed that regulatory and macroeconomic assumptions embedded in Orsted's development-stage valuations can be catastrophically wrong, and this pattern of optimistic underwriting has not yet been demonstrably corrected under the new CEO.

4. **European auction pipeline deterioration.** Orsted was shut out of the UK's Allocation Round 7 in 2025 — receiving zero awards while RWE won 6.9 GW — in what was once Orsted's strongest market. If this reflects a structural cost disadvantage in auction bidding rather than a one-off, the long-term EBITDA trajectory beyond the existing construction portfolio is far weaker than management projects. With onshore wind and bioenergy being exited, there is no alternative growth engine.

5. **Insider leadership and state ownership misalignment.** The replacement of CEO Nipper with Deputy CEO Errboe — who participated in the decisions that produced the US disaster — without any financial clawback mechanism signals that the governance structure has not been fundamentally reformed. The Danish state's 50.1% ownership means capital will periodically be deployed for policy rather than commercial reasons, and minority shareholders cannot override those decisions. This combination of weak stewardship accountability and political ownership is a permanent structural discount on the equity.

# Circle of Competence — ORSTED.CO

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-10

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Orsted sell, and to whom?** Orsted develops, constructs, owns, and operates large offshore wind farms — arrays of turbines anchored in shallow coastal waters, typically 10-100 km offshore. The electricity generated is sold to grid operators or end-customers under regulated government auction contracts (Contracts for Difference in the UK, equivalent schemes in Denmark, Germany, Taiwan, etc.) or corporate power purchase agreements. The company also operates a smaller onshore wind, solar, and bioenergy portfolio. Offshore wind is overwhelmingly the core business: it generated approximately DKK 24.3 billion of DKK 25.1 billion in total EBITDA (excluding partnerships) in 2025. The company does not sell a consumer product — its counterparties are electricity grids, utilities, and large industrial corporations.

**How does Orsted actually make money?** The revenue model is asset-heavy and long-duration. Orsted raises capital (equity, debt, green bonds, tax equity in the US, farm-down partnerships) to finance construction of offshore wind farms costing DKK 15-50+ billion each. Once operational, the farm sells electricity for 20-25 years at a fixed or CfD-indexed price set at the time of the auction contract award, generating predictable cash flows against which debt is serviced. Orsted also earns from farm-down transactions — selling stakes in operational or under-construction wind farms to institutional partners (pension funds, infrastructure funds) — which generates periodic large lumpy cash items classified as "new partnerships." In 2025, revenues of DKK 73.2 billion reflected power sales plus a material contribution from the 50% farm-down of Hornsea 3 offshore transmission assets.

**What are the key drivers?** Four variables dominate Orsted's financial outcomes: (1) Wind resource — actual wind speeds versus the long-run average determine how much electricity is generated in any given year; Orsted flagged below-normal wind speeds in H2 2025 and had to reduce guidance mid-year. (2) Auction contract prices and policy environment — the government-set "strike price" in CfD auctions determines long-run revenue; the inflationary surge of 2021-2023 temporarily made many existing contracts economically marginal. (3) Interest rates and cost of capital — offshore wind is highly capital intensive and long duration; rising rates in 2022-2023 destroyed project IRRs, caused massive US impairments (DKK 28+ billion in 2023 and a further DKK 15.6 billion in 2024), and drove the US exit. (4) Construction execution and supply chain — turbine availability, installation vessel capacity, cable and foundation costs. Supply chain constraints and construction delays were cited in every major negative update 2022-2025.

**How predictable is the revenue?** The operating portfolio is highly predictable — once a wind farm is commissioned, it generates power 24/7 under a 20-25 year government contract at a fixed or indexed price. This is bond-like cash flow. Variability comes from wind speeds (manageable, mean-reverts) and occasional grid availability issues. The unpredictable elements are: (a) the development pipeline, where auction outcomes, regulatory approvals, and construction timelines are uncertain; (b) farm-down / partnership income, which is lumpy and event-driven; (c) impairments and cancellation fees, which have been massive and unpredictable (DKK 28 billion in 2023, DKK 15.6 billion in 2024). Reported net income has therefore been highly volatile — zero profit in 2024, DKK 3.2 billion in 2025 — but underlying operating cash flow from the installed base is stable.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?** The primary customers in practical terms are national electricity markets through CfD contracts — e.g., the UK's National Grid, German grid operators, the Danish state. These are effectively sovereign counterparties, making default risk negligible but subject to regulatory and political risk. Corporate PPAs are a growing secondary channel: named customers include Amazon (250 MW from Borkum Riffgrund 3), BASF (186 MW, 25-year PPA from Borkum Riffgrund 3), REWE Group (100 MW from Borkum Riffgrund 3), Nestlé UK, Danfoss, Johnson & Johnson, and Google. Customer concentration is not structurally concerning — no single corporate buyer approaches a meaningful share of total revenues.

**Can you explain the business in two minutes?** Orsted is an offshore wind utility. It bids in government auctions to build huge wind farms at sea. When it wins a contract, it raises ~DKK 15-50 billion in capital, spends 3-5 years constructing turbines offshore, then earns a fixed or government-indexed electricity price for 20-25 years. The installed base is now 10.2 GW with 8.1 GW under construction. Once farms are running, cash flows are stable and predictable. Orsted also sells partial stakes in its farms to pension funds and infrastructure investors to recycle capital. The company went through a severe crisis in 2022-2024 when rising interest rates made its US pipeline uneconomic — it wrote off over DKK 40 billion and exited the US market. It is now refocused on European offshore wind, has repaired its balance sheet through a DKK 60 billion rights issue, and is targeting a return to dividends from 2026. The business model itself is understandable; the capital cycle complexity and recent execution failures add difficulty.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A simple, long-duration, contract-backed infrastructure model with a clean operating portfolio generating ~DKK 25 billion EBITDA annually and growing as 8.1 GW of new capacity is commissioned through 2027.
- **Bear case:** The business depends on government auction contracts that are vulnerable to political risk (the US exit proved this), requires constant massive capital raises to fund construction, and reported earnings have been deeply obscured by serial impairments.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The core operating business model is clear and simple; the complexity lies in understanding the capital cycle, auction pipeline economics, and geopolitical exposure, which are genuinely hard to predict.

## Red Flags

- DKK 28.4 billion impairment in 2023 and additional DKK 15.6 billion in 2024 from a single geography (US) reveal the limits of management's ability to underwrite long-dated capital commitments.
- Reported net income has been zero or negative for multiple consecutive years — only adjusted/underlying earnings are meaningful, which requires analyst trust that adjustments are honest.
- The business requires continuous access to capital markets at scale; any deterioration in credit rating or market confidence creates acute funding risk during the construction phase.

## Score: 6 / 10

The core operating model — build offshore wind farms, sell power under long-term contracts — is genuinely straightforward to understand, but the capital cycle complexity, massive recent impairments, lumpy farm-down income, and geographic pivot make the total picture moderately complex rather than crystal clear.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ORSTED.CO

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-10
**Data Sources:** Web search results (Orsted corporate website, Morningstar research notes, MIT Technology Review, PortersFiveForce analysis, S&P Global Ratings, offshore wind industry news 2024-2026, Orsted annual reports 2024 and 2025); training knowledge used for structural analysis of infrastructure industry economics. Data current as of early 2026.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Orsted has installed approximately one-third of all offshore wind turbines globally and holds ~24% global market share (ex-China), giving it genuine scale and operational experience advantages that competitors cannot quickly replicate. | 5 |
| 2 | The moat is primarily a cost/execution advantage from accumulated project experience, supply chain relationships, and institutional knowledge — not a traditional brand or switching-cost moat. | 4 |
| 3 | Regulatory moat from seabed lease positions in core European markets provides a meaningful but time-limited barrier — licenses are scarce but awarded by governments who can adjust the terms. | 4 |
| 4 | Orsted received nothing from the UK's CfD Allocation Round 7 (AR7) in 2025, where RWE emerged as the largest winner with 6.9 GW — evidence that the competitive advantage is eroding as European peers catch up. | 5 |
| 5 | The moat does not generate pricing power in the traditional sense — auction contracts are price-competitive; the advantage is in winning auctions at competitive prices while maintaining better project economics than rivals. | 3 |
| 6 | Margin evidence is mixed: adjusted ROCE of ~10% in 2024-2025, targeting ~13% through 2030 — not the 15-20%+ returns that characterize truly wide-moat businesses. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What type of moat exists?** Orsted possesses a narrow-to-moderate moat built primarily from three overlapping sources: (1) **Scale economics and learning curve advantages** — with over 30 years of offshore wind experience, more installed capacity than any non-Chinese competitor, and a fully integrated development-to-operations value chain, Orsted has institutional knowledge about site assessment, turbine installation logistics, cable routing, and operations that newer entrants lack. It has built relationships with the scarce supply of specialized installation vessels and turbine manufacturers. (2) **Regulatory/license positioning** — offshore wind requires governments to grant seabed leases and grid connection rights in specific geographic zones. Orsted's existing license portfolio in the North Sea, Baltic, UK, and Taiwan represents positions that cannot simply be bought. (3) **Vertical integration** — covering development, EPC, and long-term operations within one company reduces transaction costs and creates end-to-end accountability, which has historically enabled Orsted to deliver projects that smaller or less experienced developers cannot.

**How durable is the moat?** The durability is medium and weakening at the margins. The experience advantage was overwhelming 10-15 years ago when Orsted was essentially the only large-scale offshore wind developer. Today, well-capitalized peers — RWE, Vattenfall, Equinor, BP, Shell, and several national utility champions — have accumulated meaningful track records. The UK AR7 result (Orsted received no new contracts while RWE won 6.9 GW) is a concrete signal that the competitive gap is narrowing. The seabed license positions remain valuable for the next 5-10 years in the existing portfolio, but as auctions shift to new geographies (US East Coast exited, focus now on Europe and APAC), Orsted must continuously re-earn its position through bidding. Technology disruption risk in offshore wind itself is low — the technology is mature — but the economics of floating offshore wind (which opens deeper water sites) could partially reset competitive rankings if Orsted does not maintain its development edge.

**Evidence of moat in numbers.** The clearest evidence is Orsted's 30+ year market-leadership position and its ~24% global market share (ex-China) despite the industry growing rapidly and attracting major competitors. On financials, the picture is less compelling: adjusted ROCE of 10.1% in 2024 and 8.4% in 2025, targeting ~13% in 2028-2030. These are decent infrastructure returns but fall short of the 15%+ that characterizes high-quality moats. EBITDA margins at the segment level (approximately 34% on group revenue, offshore wind EBITDA of DKK 24.3 billion in 2025) are reasonable for capital-intensive infrastructure but not exceptional. The company does not exhibit pricing power in the sense of raising prices on incumbents; rather, it demonstrates cost discipline relative to competitors in auction bidding environments. Importantly, Orsted's awarded contracts in its existing European portfolio pre-date the cost inflation of 2021-2023, meaning those returns were locked in at attractive economics.

**Where is the moat weakest?** Three specific weaknesses stand out. First, the **auction bidding process** is zero-sum and price-competitive — Orsted must bid at or near its cost of capital, so any cost disadvantage relative to a competitor (e.g., a state-backed rival with cheaper financing) eliminates the advantage in that tender. Second, the **US exit was a moat failure** — Orsted had committed first-mover capital to US seabed leases, an apparent moat position, but rising interest rates and adverse regulatory changes (lack of OREC adjustment, Jones Act complications, tax credit uncertainty) made the economics collapse. Moats that depend on regulatory benevolence and stable interest rates are fragile. Third, **supply chain constraints** affect Orsted and its competitors roughly equally — Orsted's scale gives some preferential access to key suppliers, but the constraint is industry-wide, limiting differentiation.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** Offshore wind is not a product-cycle business — it is a site-and-contract business where each project is unique and long-lived. There is no "next iPhone" moment. However, the shift from fixed-bottom to floating offshore wind (which opens deep-water sites globally) is a genuine technology transition over the next decade that could partially reset competitive positions. Orsted is investing in floating wind R&D, but so are all major competitors. The practical implication is that the current moat from fixed-bottom offshore wind experience does not fully transfer to floating wind, which remains pre-commercial at scale.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Orsted's three-decade head start in offshore wind, integrated value chain, and European seabed license portfolio represent a genuine cost and execution advantage that keeps it competitive in the government auction system that governs this industry for decades.
- **Bear case:** The moat is narrowing faster than expected — the UK AR7 zero allocation and the catastrophic US exit demonstrate that Orsted's competitive advantage does not reliably protect against adverse regulatory and macroeconomic environments, and peers are catching up rapidly.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The existence of some advantage is clear from the historical record, but quantifying its durability and width requires predicting future auction results and regulatory environments that are genuinely uncertain.

## Red Flags

- Zero UK CfD AR7 contract awards in 2025 to Orsted despite being the UK's largest existing offshore wind operator — competitors outcompeted it in its core home market.
- US market exit after DKK 40+ billion in impairments proves that accumulated expertise and license positions are not durable when the macroeconomic and regulatory environment turns adverse.
- Government-set auction prices mean Orsted cannot unilaterally raise prices; in an inflationary cost environment, this becomes an existential rather than a competitive issue.
- Returns (adjusted ROCE ~8-10% currently, targeting 13%) are ordinary infrastructure returns, not wide-moat returns.

## Score: 5 / 10

Orsted has a real but narrowing scale/experience advantage in offshore wind, supported by regulatory license positions, but it cannot demonstrate pricing power or above-normal returns over a sustained period, and the UK AR7 result shows that even in its core market its competitive edge is now contestable.

# Management & Capital Allocation — ORSTED.CO

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-10
**Data Sources:** Web search results (Orsted corporate announcements 2024-2026, Fortune Europe, Hart Energy, WindInsider, Orsted investor relations pages, Orsted 2024 and 2025 annual reports, Renewables Now, CNBC, LinkedIn announcements); training knowledge used for context on Danish corporate governance norms. ## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | The Danish state owns 50.1% of voting rights, making Orsted a quasi-government enterprise — capital allocation decisions are structurally subject to policy considerations beyond pure shareholder returns. | 5 |
| 2 | CEO Mads Nipper was ousted in January 2025 after the share price fell approximately 83% from peak — a direct accountability response to the US impairment disaster and capital allocation failures. | 5 |
| 3 | The new CEO Rasmus Errboe is a long-tenured Orsted insider (joined 2012), not an external reform agent — he was Deputy CEO and CCO, directly involved in the years of value destruction. | 4 |
| 4 | Orsted suspended dividends for 2023-2025 and conducted a DKK ~60 billion rights issue in 2025 to repair the balance sheet — shareholders bore the full cost of management's capital misallocation. | 5 |
| 5 | The 2025 capital plan cuts investment by ~25% versus prior guidance (DKK 210-230 billion vs. DKK 270 billion to 2030) and abandons the US market — a belated correction to years of over-expansion. | 4 |
| 6 | The company has committed to reinstating dividends from financial year 2026 and targets ROCE of ~13% on average through 2030 — specific and measurable forward commitments that can be held to account. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Meaningful personal insider ownership is essentially absent at Orsted in the traditional sense. The dominant owner is the Danish state (50.1% voting rights), which holds its stake through Energinet and the broader state holding mechanism. Professional management and board members hold small equity positions typical of Danish governance. The new CEO Rasmus Errboe does not hold a controlling or significant personal economic stake. This means management's incentives are not aligned with shareholders in the owner-operator sense that Buffett values — management is ultimately accountable to a state shareholder whose priorities include energy security, employment, and green transition policy objectives, not solely total shareholder return.

**Capital allocation track record.** The record of the past five years is materially poor. The decisive failure was the aggressive expansion into the US offshore wind market: Orsted committed DKK 28.4 billion in impairments in 2023 alone (cancellation of Ocean Wind 1 and 2 in New Jersey) and a further DKK 15.6 billion in 2024 (Sunrise Wind delays, seabed lease write-downs, interest rate impacts). Total US-related capital destruction exceeded DKK 44 billion across 2023-2024. The proximate causes were rising interest rates and adverse US regulatory conditions, but the root cause was management committing massive irreversible capital to a market where regulatory risk (Jones Act, OREC adjustment rules, permitting timelines) and macroeconomic sensitivity (long-duration IRR model at the margin vs. interest rates) were clearly elevated. Simultaneously, the prior CEO presided over a DKK 270 billion investment plan to 2030 that was subsequently cut by 25%. The counter-argument is that European operations continued to execute well — EBITDA from offshore sites was resilient at ~DKK 24 billion annually — but the US failure was large enough to wipe out years of European operating value creation.

**Compensation design.** Specific compensation details are not available in public web searches, but Danish governance norms and Orsted's prior disclosures indicate management compensation is tied to EBITDA, ROCE, and safety/sustainability metrics. The suspension of dividends and massive rights issue dilution (DKK ~60 billion) suggests there was no mechanism to claw back compensation from the executives responsible for the US capital misallocation. The ousting of CEO Nipper in January 2025 — after the share fell ~83% from its peak — was reputational accountability rather than financial clawback. The new CEO's appointment from within raises questions about whether the compensation and incentive structure has actually changed.

**Candor and consistency.** Credit where it is due: Orsted has been relatively transparent in acknowledging its failures. The company issued clear public statements on each impairment event, named the specific causal factors (interest rates, OREC adjustments, Jones Act vessel constraints, seabed lease write-downs), and did not attempt to obscure the scale of losses. Guidance has historically been met or beaten at the operating level — DKK 24.8 billion EBITDA in 2024 vs. guidance of DKK 24-26 billion; DKK 25.1 billion in 2025 vs. guidance of DKK 24-27 billion (though the range was narrowed mid-year after H2 wind speed guidance reduction). However, the company's multi-year strategic guidance (DKK 270 billion investment plan through 2030, capacity targets) has been significantly revised downward, which represents a consistency failure at the strategic level even if annual operating guidance is honest.

**Governance structure.** Orsted has a two-tier board structure: a Supervisory Board (Board of Directors) with majority independent members plus employee representatives, and an Executive Board (Group President/CEO and registered executives). The Danish state, as 50.1% voting rights holder, has de facto veto power over all major decisions including capital raises, dividend policy, and strategic direction. This is not a traditional dual-class structure (the state's shares are ordinary shares representing economic ownership), but the practical effect is that minority shareholders cannot override state preferences. The Chairman Thomas Thune Andersen stepped down following the 2024 annual report period — the February 2024 capital markets update noted he would not stand for re-election at the upcoming AGM. This was itself a governance accountability response to the impairment crisis. Employee board representation (as required by Danish corporate law) adds a further stakeholder voice that may not always align with shareholder returns.

**Do they talk like owners or politicians?** Largely like managers of a quasi-public utility. The language in strategic communications emphasizes "green transition," "energy security," "Denmark's contribution to European renewable targets" alongside financial metrics. This is not inherently wrong for a company with a 50% state owner, but it means investors should expect capital allocation to sometimes reflect policy priorities. The February 2026 announcement describing the year as a "defining year" after "navigating a challenging industry" is measured rather than bluntly accountable. The new CEO Errboe's public framing emphasizes "stricter value-driven capital allocation" and disciplined investment — appropriate given the failures, but it remains language rather than demonstrated track record.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The management crisis has been acknowledged and acted upon — CEO replaced, investment plan cut 25%, US market exited, balance sheet repaired via rights issue, dividends reinstated from 2026 — and the business is now managed by someone with deep institutional knowledge who has explicitly committed to capital discipline.
- **Bear case:** The new CEO was part of the same management team that made the US decisions, the state's 50% ownership means capital will occasionally be deployed for non-commercial reasons, and shareholders have already absorbed DKK 44+ billion in impairments and a massively dilutive rights issue with no clawback mechanism.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The governance structure and state ownership create inherent limits on how owner-operator aligned this management can be; whether the new regime actually practices the capital discipline it professes will only be visible over the next 2-3 years of investment decisions.

## Red Flags

- Orsted's share fell approximately 83% from its peak to the time of CEO replacement — one of the worst capital destruction records in the European utility sector in the 2020s.
- DKK 44+ billion in impairments over 2023-2024 from the US market — management committed irreversible capital to a geography where regulatory and macroeconomic risks were knowable.
- DKK ~60 billion dilutive rights issue in 2025 forced by management's prior over-leverage and over-investment — minority shareholders paid for the mistakes.
- CEO replacement comes from within the same leadership team; no external accountability mechanism.
- State ownership: the controlling shareholder has objectives (energy security, green transition targets, Danish industrial policy) that are not purely financial.

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Orsted's ROIC story is painful to look at. GuruFocus calculated an annualized ROIC of 3.14% for the quarter ending December 2023, declining further to approximately 1.67% on a trailing twelve-month basis through 2024. Both figures are well below the company's weighted average cost of capital of around 7.4%, meaning Orsted is not earning back what it costs to fund the business. **Margin Structure.** On an adjusted basis (stripping out partnership income and cancellation fee reversals), EBITDA margins have been reasonably stable: EBITDA of DKK 24 billion on roughly DKK 79 billion revenue in 2023 (~30%), and DKK 24.8 billion adjusted EBITDA on DKK 71 billion revenue in 2024 (~35%). What we know: gross capital investment ran at DKK 48-55 billion annually in 2023-2024, which dwarfs EBITDA of DKK 24-25 billion. By definition, Orsted is a net cash consumer in its development phase — it is building wind farms that will generate returns over 25+ year asset lives. The company intends to fund the gap through farm-down partnerships (selling minority stakes to partners like pension funds), tax equity, and debt. The DKK 60 billion rights issue completed in late 2025 was an emergency measure to plug a funding gap when the Sunrise Wind farm-down stalled. Owner earnings (net income + depreciation - maintenance capex) are likely positive for the existing operating fleet, but precise maintenance capex figures were not disclosed in the search results — Orsted does not separately report this figure publicly.

In practice, Orsted is in a period of massive negative operating leverage as the development cost curve runs far ahead of the operating revenue curve. Once the investment programme matures and Orsted transitions from developer to operator, operating leverage should turn sharply positive. The 2025 result (DKK 25.1 billion EBITDA) with rights issue proceeds applied to the balance sheet and capex declining sets the stage for this transition — but it is still ahead of us.

# Margin of Safety — ORSTED.CO

## Detailed Analysis

**Price Margin of Safety**

The classic Buffett margin of safety question: how much cheaper is the stock than a conservative, bear-case valuation? Using the bear-case intrinsic value estimated in Umbrella 6 (DKK 62-91 per share, discounted), Orsted at DKK 139 actually trades above the bear-case intrinsic value. This is a critical finding: there is negative margin of safety in the most pessimistic but plausible scenario. The base-case intrinsic value (DKK 124 per share, discounted) is also below the current price, meaning the stock is only attractive if you believe the bull case has significant probability. Morningstar's current fair value of DKK 160 does imply a modest discount to intrinsic value, but 15% is far below Buffett's preferred 30-50% margin of safety for a business with this level of uncertainty. If you are wrong on EBITDA growth by 20% (say DKK 22-23 billion in Year 5 instead of DKK 28 billion), the base-case intrinsic value falls to around DKK 100 per share — a 28% loss from today's price. You do not make money by being modestly wrong.

**Business Margin of Safety**

This is where Orsted scores better. The core European offshore wind business — serving the UK, Germany, Denmark, Netherlands, and Taiwan — has long-term government contracts (Contracts for Difference) that provide revenue certainty for 15-20 years on operating assets. These CfDs are real margin-of-safety assets: they cannot be easily cancelled, they reprice above market rates if power prices fall, and they sit with creditworthy counterparties (European governments). The existing ~10 GW operational fleet generates reliable, low-volatility EBITDA. The business safety is strong for what is already built and contracted. The danger is in what is being built: 8.1 GW under construction represents enormous execution risk, and construction-phase assets have no CfD protection against cost overruns, supply chain failures, or delays.

**Downside vs Upside Asymmetry**

Realistic downside: If the bear case materializes (US remains hostile, 1-2 major construction projects run significantly over budget, EBITDA misses guidance), the stock could fall to DKK 80-100, representing a 28-42% loss from DKK 139. This is a real, plausible scenario given the track record of impairments since 2023.

Realistic upside: If the bull case materializes (policy normalizes, construction completes on schedule, rates decline), the stock could reach DKK 200-250, representing 44-80% upside over 3-5 years.

The ratio of upside to downside is roughly 1.5-2x — not the 3x minimum Buffett looks for. This is a speculative special-situation thesis, not a high-conviction margin-of-safety investment. The asymmetry is insufficient for a large position.

**What Could Go to Zero (or Near-Zero)?**

A full loss is extremely unlikely given Danish state ownership and the strategic importance of Orsted's European offshore wind infrastructure. However, scenarios for 70-80%+ additional loss exist: (1) The 8.1 GW construction portfolio experiences systemic failures — multiple projects running 30-50% over budget simultaneously, requiring another massive rights issue at an even more dilutive price; (2) Interest rates spike back to 5-6%, making the economics of new offshore wind projects unworkable across Europe, triggering further impairments and a collapse in EBITDA guidance; (3) A major policy reversal in the UK (Orsted's largest market by EBITDA) strips contract protections or fails to award new capacity. None of these are base-case scenarios, but each has happened (partially or analogously) in the last three years. The stock fell 88% from peak — the near-zero scenario is not purely theoretical.

**Key Ways You Could Be Wrong**

1. Construction execution: Orsted's 8.1 GW under construction is the company's future. Any repeat of the Ocean Wind / Revolution Wind debacles would destroy equity value. Probability: Medium. Severity: Extreme. Early warning: quarterly construction update delays, cost guidance revisions, supply chain announcements from turbine suppliers (Siemens Gamesa, Vestas).

**Concentration Risks**

## Detailed Analysis

**Holding Period Suitability**

Orsted is not a company you buy and forget about for 10 years. It is a thesis-dependent special situation. The thesis has three legs: (1) the existing ~10 GW European operational fleet continues generating stable, contracted EBITDA; (2) the 8.1 GW construction portfolio is delivered on time and on budget, adding substantial EBITDA by 2027-2029; (3) the macro environment (interest rates, US policy) normalizes enough to allow the stock's multiple to re-rate upward. Legs 1 and 2 are mostly within management's control; leg 3 is not. If you can realistically hold for 4-6 years and monitor each leg closely, this thesis has a window to play out. If you need to see results within 12-18 months or cannot tolerate interim volatility, this is the wrong position. It is closer in temperament to a distressed-debt turnaround than a quality-compounder buy-and-hold.

**Volatility Tolerance**

The historical data is stark: this stock lost 88% peak-to-trough over roughly four years. Within just the last 52 weeks it ranged from DKK 102 to DKK 334 — a 3x swing. Anyone who bought near the rights issue low (~DKK 66-102) is already well ahead; anyone who bought at DKK 300+ in H1 2025 before the rights issue announcement is still deeply underwater. The October 2025 rights issue itself caused an immediate share price plunge.

If the stock drops 30% from DKK 139 (to ~DKK 97), the right question is: has the thesis changed, or is this noise? If it is noise (the construction portfolio is on track, no new impairments), buying more makes sense. But there is a real scenario where a 30% drop signals thesis deterioration — another impairment announcement, an auction failure, or a US policy escalation. This requires genuine vigilance and a written decision rule before entering the position. The volatility profile demands discipline: you must know the difference between "stock is down because sentiment is bad" and "stock is down because the business is deteriorating."

**Catalysts**

The thesis has identifiable potential catalysts, most of which are event-driven rather than time-driven:
- 2026 European offshore wind auction results (UK, Germany, Netherlands): could confirm or deny Orsted's ability to win new capacity at attractive returns.
- Sunrise Wind commissioning (US): Orsted retained full ownership; successful commissioning would demonstrate US assets still work and might improve the sentiment around residual US exposure.
- Q1-Q4 2026 construction updates: any quarter that shows 8.1 GW portfolio on track without cost overruns is a positive catalyst; any revision is a negative one.
- US policy shift: if the Trump administration signals openness to offshore wind (or new administration in 2029), the equity story gets significantly better. This is binary and unpredictable.
- EBITDA delivery vs 2026 guidance (above DKK 28 billion): hitting guidance after years of misses would begin to restore credibility.

Most catalysts are within a 12-36 month window. This is not a "permanent hold" type of investment; it is a time-boxed recovery bet.

**Position Sizing Logic**

Given the risk profile — extreme past volatility, multiple impairment cycles, a no-moat business, thin margin of safety, and thesis dependency on external macro factors — this should not be a core portfolio position for most investors. A starter position of 1-3% of portfolio is appropriate: large enough to matter if the thesis works, small enough that being completely wrong (stock back to DKK 80-100) does not damage the overall portfolio materially. A core position (5-10%+) would require: (1) much higher confidence in construction execution, (2) some evidence of US policy normalization, and (3) a lower entry price (ideally DKK 100-110 or below). At DKK 139, the risk-reward does not justify a large position. The position should be reviewed every quarter against the three thesis legs; if two of the three legs break, exit regardless of price.

**Correlation and Portfolio Overlap**

Orsted is a pure-play offshore wind developer. If your portfolio already has exposure to renewable energy utilities (e.g., Iberdrola, RWE, EDP Renováveis, Vestas), adding Orsted increases concentration in the same macro factors: interest rates, energy policy, European electricity prices, and green subsidy regimes. If you hold no renewable energy exposure, Orsted adds genuine diversification from technology or consumer stocks. However, Orsted's specific risk factors (construction execution, US political risk, Danish state control) are not fully correlated with generic renewable energy sector risk — it has idiosyncratic risk that makes it feel different from Iberdrola even if the correlation in calm markets is high.
