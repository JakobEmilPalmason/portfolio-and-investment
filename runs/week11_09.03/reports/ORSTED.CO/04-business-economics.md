## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Orsted's ROIC story is painful to look at. GuruFocus calculated an annualized ROIC of 3.14% for the quarter ending December 2023, declining further to approximately 1.67% on a trailing twelve-month basis through 2024. Both figures are well below the company's weighted average cost of capital of around 7.4%, meaning Orsted is not earning back what it costs to fund the business. The destruction was driven primarily by DKK 28.4 billion in impairments on US offshore wind projects in 2023 (the Ocean Wind 1 and 2 cancellations in New Jersey), followed by an additional DKK 15.6 billion of US-related impairments in 2024. Strip out the impairments and look at underlying operating cash generation, and the picture is less catastrophic — but the capital base has been permanently damaged. The company's own forward guidance targets ROCE of approximately 13% over 2024-2030, which would represent a genuine recovery, but depends on executing a DKK 210-230 billion investment programme without further write-offs.

**Margin Structure.** On an adjusted basis (stripping out partnership income and cancellation fee reversals), EBITDA margins have been reasonably stable: EBITDA of DKK 24 billion on roughly DKK 79 billion revenue in 2023 (~30%), and DKK 24.8 billion adjusted EBITDA on DKK 71 billion revenue in 2024 (~35%). The 2025 full-year result showed DKK 25.1 billion adjusted EBITDA. Operating margins at the EBIT level are much lower because of high depreciation on the massive fixed asset base — reported operating margin was only 8.7% in 2024 even before the impairments. Net margins are near zero in both 2023 and 2024 due to the impairment cascade. The revenue decline from DKK 79-108 billion in earlier years to DKK 71 billion in 2024 partly reflects the divestiture of energy trading and retail activities, which had high revenue but thin margins. The remaining business — offshore wind generation — actually has better-quality margins.

**Cash Generation and FCF.** Free cash flow is difficult to assess in isolation for Orsted because the company does not cleanly separate maintenance capex from development capex in its public reporting. What we know: gross capital investment ran at DKK 48-55 billion annually in 2023-2024, which dwarfs EBITDA of DKK 24-25 billion. By definition, Orsted is a net cash consumer in its development phase — it is building wind farms that will generate returns over 25+ year asset lives. The company intends to fund the gap through farm-down partnerships (selling minority stakes to partners like pension funds), tax equity, and debt. The DKK 60 billion rights issue completed in late 2025 was an emergency measure to plug a funding gap when the Sunrise Wind farm-down stalled. Owner earnings (net income + depreciation - maintenance capex) are likely positive for the existing operating fleet, but precise maintenance capex figures were not disclosed in the search results — Orsted does not separately report this figure publicly.

**Capital Intensity.** This is the central issue with Orsted as a Buffett-style investment. Offshore wind is one of the most capital-intensive industries on earth. Each gigawatt of offshore wind capacity requires billions in upfront investment (foundations, turbines, cabling, grid connections) before generating a single unit of electricity. Orsted's plan calls for DKK 210-230 billion in gross investment through 2030. Even with farm-downs covering a portion, this is an enormous capital appetite. An analyst at Kepler upgraded Orsted specifically on the thesis of a "looming capex cliff" — meaning the investment cycle peaks and then falls sharply after 2026-2027 toward DKK 37 billion by 2027 (29% of sales vs 75% in 2025), which would finally allow free cash flow to materialize. This remains a thesis, not yet reality.

**Revenue Quality.** Once built, offshore wind farms have very high revenue quality. Power Purchase Agreements with corporate customers (BASF 25-year, Amazon 250 MW, Google in the US) lock in fixed prices for a decade or more. Government subsidy contracts (Contracts for Difference in the UK, similar structures elsewhere) provide further price floors. Load factors for offshore wind are high (40-50%) and there are no fuel costs. Customer "retention" is not a useful frame here — the counterparties are sovereign governments and investment-grade corporations, and contracts run 10-25 years. The weakness is that the Offshore segment's earnings are subject to wind resource variability year to year; Orsted noted in 2025 that lower-than-average wind speeds reduced generation despite higher availability rates.

**Operating Leverage.** In theory, offshore wind has strong operating leverage once assets are built: fixed costs dominate (maintenance, insurance, debt service) while incremental revenue from wind generation has near-zero variable cost. In practice, Orsted is in a period of massive negative operating leverage as the development cost curve runs far ahead of the operating revenue curve. Once the investment programme matures and Orsted transitions from developer to operator, operating leverage should turn sharply positive. The 2025 result (DKK 25.1 billion EBITDA) with rights issue proceeds applied to the balance sheet and capex declining sets the stage for this transition — but it is still ahead of us.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Orsted successfully completes its reduced DKK 210-230 billion investment programme, capex drops sharply after 2027, the existing offshore fleet generates stable high-margin cash flows under long-term PPAs, and ROCE reaches 13%+ — transforming from a capital-consuming developer into a cash-generating operator.
- **Bear case:** Further project write-offs, continued inability to execute farm-downs at acceptable valuations, persistent below-WACC returns, and an interest rate environment that makes offshore wind economics structurally unattractive, forcing additional equity dilution.
- **Confidence:** Low — The business economics are in a transitional state where current metrics (ROIC of 1.67%, near-zero net income) look terrible but the forward case depends on execution of a multi-year capital programme that has already experienced significant setbacks.

## Red Flags

- ROIC of 1.67% is materially below WACC of 7.4% — the company has been destroying value at the capital level for multiple years.
- DKK 44 billion in cumulative US impairments (2023-2024) demonstrates poor judgment on capital allocation into a market that proved unworkable under current economics.
- No clearly disclosed maintenance vs development capex split makes it impossible to calculate true owner earnings or assess the health of the existing fleet independently.
- Capex at 65-75% of revenue is extraordinary — the company is consuming cash at a rate that required an emergency DKK 60 billion rights issue in 2025.
- Management guidance of 13% ROCE over 2024-2030 has already been undermined by the 2024 impairments and rights issue; forward guidance credibility is damaged.

## Score: 3 / 10

Orsted's business economics are deeply challenged: returns are below the cost of capital, the business is massively capital-intensive with no near-term free cash flow, and the company has demonstrated poor capital allocation judgment on its US expansion; a higher score requires actually observing the return on capital recovery management is projecting.
