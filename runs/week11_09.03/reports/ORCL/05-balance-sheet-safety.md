## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure**

Oracle's debt load is the dominant balance sheet concern. Total debt as of Q4 FY2025 (May 31, 2025) was approximately $95.5B on an annual basis, rising to approximately $100-113B in more recent quarters (estimates differ by source, with the quarter ending November 30, 2025 showing long-term debt of ~$100B). This debt was largely accumulated in two waves: the 2022 Cerner acquisition (~$28B financed with debt) and the ongoing OCI infrastructure buildout. Total assets were ~$205B against total liabilities of ~$174.5B as of recent filings, leaving stockholders' equity of approximately $20-30B — itself a recovery from a prior negative equity position driven by aggressive buybacks. Debt-to-EBITDA: Oracle's FY2025 EBITDA is approximately $24-26B (operating income ~$17.6B + D&A). At $100B+ debt, Net Debt/EBITDA is approximately 3.5-4.0x, firmly in "elevated" territory (above the 4x threshold that begins to raise serious concerns). Debt structure: Oracle issues predominantly fixed-rate investment-grade notes. The company has an established track record of accessing bond markets, but the sheer volume required ($45-50B in 2026 alone) is extraordinary for any single issuer.

**Interest Coverage**

EBIT-based interest coverage has followed a concerning trajectory: from approximately 7.1x in FY2020, declining to a worrying 3.65x in FY2023 (the year the Cerner debt fully hit), and recovering to approximately 5.0-6.0x by FY2025 as operating income grew. The FY2025 EBIT of approximately $17.6B against interest expense (estimated $3-3.5B based on $100B+ debt at ~3% average coupon) yields a ratio in the 5-6x range. This is above the 5x "comfortable" threshold, but the upward trajectory of capex-funded debt could push interest expense materially higher. As new bonds are issued in 2026 at higher rates (current investment-grade rates are significantly above Oracle's historical low coupon debt), the interest burden will rise. There is a plausible scenario where interest coverage tightens back below 4x if operating income growth does not outpace the rising interest expense.

**Liquidity**

Oracle's current ratio stands at approximately 0.75-0.91 as of recent quarters — below 1.0, meaning current liabilities exceed current assets. This is a persistent structural condition for Oracle, not a new development: the company has historically managed this via its revolving credit facility and robust operating cash flow ($20.8B in FY2025). Cash on hand is not publicly broken out cleanly in the search results, but Oracle historically holds $5-15B in cash and equivalents. The immediate liquidity concern is the gap between operating cash flow (~$20-22B TTM) and the planned $50B capex in FY2026 — a gap of ~$28-30B that must be funded externally. Oracle has explicitly acknowledged this by announcing its $45-50B fundraise for 2026. Months of operating expenses covered by cash: with operating expenses (excluding capex) of ~$10-12B/quarter, even $10B in cash would cover only 2-3 months — thin.

**Refinancing Risk**

This is the single most important risk on the balance sheet. Oracle's explicit announcement that it plans to raise $45-50B of gross cash in calendar year 2026 — with approximately half via a single investment-grade bond issuance — represents an extraordinary dependence on capital markets. Any meaningful widening of credit spreads, a downgrade from investment-grade, or a freeze in debt markets could leave Oracle unable to fund its capex program as planned. The company is not in distress (investment-grade rated, strong cash flows), but it is operating with very little buffer. If capital market access is disrupted, Oracle would face stark choices: slash capex (forfeiting the OCI buildout and potentially RPO commitments), sell assets, or seek emergency financing. This is not a theoretical risk — it is the defining balance sheet vulnerability.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

Oracle has significant operating lease obligations for data center space, which are largely on-balance-sheet under current GAAP (ASC 842). Pension obligations are not material for Oracle (it is not a legacy industrial company). Contingent liabilities include the usual software IP litigation (Oracle has a long history of aggressive IP enforcement and litigation). The most significant off-balance-sheet consideration is the $523B RPO — these are contracted future revenues, but they also imply contracted future capital expenditure obligations. Oracle must build the infrastructure to deliver on these contracts; failure to do so would expose the company to customer claims and reputational damage. This is a contingent liability of sorts that is not visible on the face of the balance sheet.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years**

FY2025 revenue: ~$57B. A 30% decline takes revenue to ~$40B. Operating margin would compress — assume margins fall from ~30% to ~15-18% (fixed cost base) — producing EBIT of ~$6-7B. Interest expense would be $3-4B (and growing as new debt is issued). Operating cash flow might fall to ~$8-10B. Against planned capex of $35-50B, the company could not sustain its investment program. However, Oracle would almost certainly slash capex severely in a revenue shock scenario, reducing it back to maintenance levels of perhaps $5-8B. In that scenario, operating cash flow of $8-10B minus $5-8B capex produces minimal but positive FCF. Debt covenants could become an issue at this revenue level if EBITDA fell below thresholds. The company would survive without issuing equity if it cut capex — but the OCI buildout would be effectively abandoned. This is a survivable stress scenario, but only with major strategic capitulation. The Cerner acquisition (healthcare IT) provides some defensiveness since hospital revenue is relatively recession-resistant.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Oracle executes the $45-50B 2026 fundraise without issue, OCI revenue growth accelerates to justify the investment, and by FY2028 operating cash flow grows to $35B+, rapidly deleveraging the balance sheet toward 2x Net Debt/EBITDA.
- **Bear case:** Credit market disruption or a rating downgrade derails the 2026 fundraise, forcing Oracle to slash capex and forfeit RPO contracts, creating a cascade of customer defections and a balance sheet crisis.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Oracle's investment-grade status and strong operating cash flows provide real cushion, but the scale of capital markets dependency in 2026 is genuinely unprecedented for the company, and the margin for error is thin.

## Red Flags

- Total debt of $100B+ is extraordinary for a software company; Net Debt/EBITDA of ~3.5-4.0x is elevated
- Planned $45-50B fundraise in calendar year 2026 represents explicit, acknowledged dependence on capital markets being open
- Current ratio below 1.0 indicates structural short-term liquidity tightness
- Interest coverage recovered to ~5x but faces pressure as new debt is issued at higher rates
- S&P BBB rating with negative outlook: one downgrade away from the edge of investment grade, which would widen spreads and raise funding costs meaningfully
- $523B RPO creates implicit capex obligations that are not visible on the balance sheet
- No clean separation of maintenance vs. growth capex makes true financial health harder to assess

## Score: 4 / 10

Oracle's balance sheet carries elevated leverage (~3.5-4x Net Debt/EBITDA), a current ratio below 1.0, explicit capital market dependency for a $45-50B 2026 fundraise, and an S&P BBB negative outlook — the strong operating cash flows prevent a lower score, but this is far from a fortress balance sheet and the refinancing risk is a genuine and material concern.
