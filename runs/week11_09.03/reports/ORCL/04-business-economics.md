## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Oracle's ROIC has followed a volatile path over the past five years. It peaked at approximately 17.6% in FY2021, collapsed to ~9.7% in FY2022 following the Cerner acquisition (~$28B), and has since partially recovered to ~10.3% in FY2023, ~11.7% in FY2024, and ~12.3% in FY2025. The current ROIC of roughly 12% sits barely above the estimated WACC of ~10.25%, meaning Oracle is generating only marginal economic value at the consolidated level today. ROE appears elevated at ~60-61% due to the negative book equity base (from accumulated buybacks and the Cerner debt load), making it a misleading metric. The ROIC trend is the more informative signal: pre-Cerner Oracle consistently generated 15-20%+ ROIC; post-acquisition and mid-infrastructure-buildout, it has reverted to a mediocre band. Recovery of ROIC above 15% depends entirely on OCI scaling and the massive RPO backlog converting to high-margin revenue.

**Margin Structure**

FY2025 GAAP operating margin was approximately 30.8% on total revenue of ~$57B (with cloud and license contributing the bulk). Non-GAAP operating margin runs higher, around 41%. These headline margins are impressive for a company simultaneously absorbing a $28B acquisition and running $20B+ annual capex. The cloud transition is a dual-edged dynamic: OCI is infrastructure-heavy and lower-margin at early scale, while SaaS (Fusion ERP, NetSuite, health records via Cerner) carries software-level margins. Management has consistently guided that OCI gross margins are improving even amid the GPU buildout. Net margin in FY2025 was approximately 21.7% on a GAAP basis. Margin trajectory will depend on whether cloud revenue scales faster than infrastructure cost — early signals from Q2 FY2026 (non-GAAP operating income $6.7B on $16.1B revenue = ~42% non-GAAP margin) suggest operational leverage is beginning to show.

**Cash Generation**

Operating cash flow in FY2025 was $20.8B, up 11.5% from $18.7B in FY2024. This is strong and real — Oracle's software subscription base generates clean cash. The problem is capex: $21.2B in FY2025 (a 209% increase from $6.9B in FY2024) erased all free cash flow, producing -$394M FCF for FY2025. Q2 FY2026 FCF was -$10B (capex $12B vs. operating cash flow not specified but TTM operating cash flow ~$22B). Guidance for FY2026 capex has escalated to ~$50B, implying FCF will remain deeply negative through FY2026. Owner earnings (net income + D&A - maintenance capex) cannot be cleanly separated because maintenance vs. growth capex is not broken out — a significant analytical limitation. The FCF deficit is a deliberate strategic choice to capture AI infrastructure demand, not a sign of operational weakness, but it carries real balance sheet risk given the existing debt load.

**Capital Intensity**

Oracle has undergone a fundamental shift from a structurally asset-light software business to a capital-intensive cloud infrastructure operator. FY2023 capex was ~$4.7B; FY2024 was ~$6.9B; FY2025 was $21.2B; FY2026 guidance is ~$50B. This is a 10x increase in capex over two years. The driver is OCI data center buildout, GPU procurement (for AI workloads), and fulfilling the massive $523B RPO. This capital intensity is not indefinitely sustainable without either (a) the OCI business generating substantially higher returns, or (b) Oracle taking on more debt or issuing equity. Management has announced plans to raise $45-50B of gross cash in calendar year 2026, including a planned investment-grade bond issuance. The business is now meaningfully capital-heavy during this investment cycle.

**Revenue Quality**

Revenue quality is high and improving. Cloud services and license support — the recurring revenue streams — represent approximately 77% of total revenue in FY2025. Cloud revenue grew 27% in Q4 FY2025, and as of Q2 FY2026, cloud revenue reached $8B/quarter (50% of total), growing 33% YoY. The $523B RPO as of Q2 FY2026 (up 433% YoY, including contracts with Meta and NVIDIA) represents exceptional forward visibility. Cerner's healthcare IT segment contributes meaningful recurring SaaS revenue from hospital systems — these are extremely sticky customers with long switching cycles. License support (maintenance) is also sticky, though declining as customers migrate to cloud. The pivot from perpetual license to cloud subscription is well advanced, and the revenue quality profile has improved substantially over the past five years.

**Operating Leverage**

The operating leverage story is mixed during this investment cycle. In the underlying software business, operating leverage is clear: as cloud revenue scales, incremental margins should improve materially because the fixed cost base (data centers built) will support more revenue. The Q2 FY2026 result — non-GAAP net income up 57% YoY on 14% revenue growth — demonstrates this leverage in the P&L once you exclude the capex drag. However, the sheer scale of planned capex ($50B in FY2026) means the cash flow statement will not show operating leverage for at least 1-2 more years. Long-term, OCI's architecture (more software-defined than competitors) should yield structurally better unit economics than AWS or Azure at comparable scale. Near-term, the investment cycle compresses visible cash returns.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** OCI achieves hyperscaler scale by FY2028-2030, converting the $523B RPO backlog into high-margin revenue and restoring FCF to $20B+, with ROIC recovering above 20% as the capex cycle normalizes.
- **Bear case:** OCI fails to win market share at the pace required to justify $50B annual capex, debt service consumes an increasing share of operating cash flow, and Oracle is forced into dilutive equity issuance.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Oracle's underlying software economics are genuinely strong and the RPO backlog is real, but the capex bet is enormous relative to the balance sheet, and execution risk is high.

## Red Flags

- Free cash flow is deeply negative (-$394M FY2025, estimated -$25B+ in FY2026) at a time when the company already carries $100B+ in debt
- Capex guidance has repeatedly been revised upward (from $35B to $50B in FY2026 alone), raising questions about cost discipline and execution predictability
- ROIC of ~12% barely clears WACC — the Cerner acquisition has not yet been accretive to returns
- No clear breakout of maintenance vs. growth capex, making true owner earnings impossible to calculate
- Dependence on capital markets remaining open: the announced $45-50B fundraise for 2026 assumes benign credit conditions

## Score: 6 / 10

Oracle's underlying software economics are strong (77% recurring revenue, 30%+ operating margins, $20B+ operating cash flow), but the aggressive capex cycle has destroyed near-term free cash flow and the ROIC has barely cleared WACC since the Cerner deal; a 6 reflects genuine operational quality offset by real capital intensity concerns during this transition.
