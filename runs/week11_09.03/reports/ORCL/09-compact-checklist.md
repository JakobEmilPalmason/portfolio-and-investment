# Compact Checklist — ORCL

**Date:** 2026-03-11

1. **Business**: Oracle sells enterprise database software, cloud infrastructure (OCI), and SaaS applications to large corporations and governments, and customers keep buying because migrating off Oracle Database is a multi-year, tens-of-millions-of-dollars undertaking that most enterprises refuse to attempt.

2. **Moat**: The switching cost on Oracle's installed database base — embedded in mission-critical transaction and financial systems at 40%+ market share sustained through decades of open-source competition — is the moat; even technically inferior, customers will not migrate.

3. **Economics**: Operating cash flow is $23.5B TTM and GAAP operating margins are 30.8%, demonstrating genuine software-level cash generation, but $50B in FY2026 capex has turned free cash flow deeply negative (~-$13B) and pushed ROIC to ~12%, barely above WACC — the underlying economics are strong, the investment cycle is destroying near-term cash returns.

4. **Management**: Ellison's 41% ownership stake is the dominant alignment fact, but buybacks were largest ($21B) in FY2021 at peak multiples and are now near zero, the $28.3B Cerner acquisition has not justified its price, and CEO Catz exercised and sold $2.5B in options in one recent year — the alignment is real, the execution is mixed.

5. **Debt risk**: With $108B in debt, Net Debt/EBITDA of ~3.5-4x, a current ratio below 1.0, an S&P BBB negative outlook, and an announced need to raise $45-50B in calendar 2026 via bond markets, Oracle cannot survive a credit market disruption without slashing capex and forfeiting OCI contracts — the operating cash flow ($23.5B) comfortably covers interest, but the refinancing dependency is the single biggest risk in a recession scenario.

6. **Price**: For $164/share to make sense, Oracle must hit $90B in FY2027 revenue (34% growth), sustain 12-15% growth for several years beyond, expand non-GAAP operating margins from 43% toward 48%, normalize capex from $50B to ~$10-15B by FY2028-2029, and convert the $553B RPO at high rates without customer renegotiations — the base case yields roughly fair value, not a margin of safety.

7. **Margin of safety**: The $553B RPO backlog and the legacy database annuity (~$8-10B annually in near-zero-capex license and support revenue) protect the business from demand collapse, but at $164 there is zero price margin of safety — the bear case intrinsic value of $75-90/share sits 45-54% below today's price, and the upside/downside asymmetry is below 2:1.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell or materially reduce if FY2027 revenue guidance is cut below $82B, if RPO growth stalls for two consecutive quarters, or if Oracle's credit rating falls below investment grade — any of these signals that the AI infrastructure buildout thesis is impaired, not just delayed.
