## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

IQVIA's reported ROIC of approximately 6-7% is misleading as a standalone measure. The company carries ~$17-18B in goodwill and intangible assets from the 2016 merger of Quintiles and IMS Health, which inflates the invested capital denominator. On a cash-on-cash basis — using EBITDA vs. tangible invested capital — returns are substantially higher and more in line with a 15-20% range, comparable to other high-quality data/services businesses. Return on equity runs around 19% given the leveraged capital structure. The 5-year trajectory on operating margins (10.2% in 2020 → 14.7% in 2024) shows the business is expanding margins steadily as scale benefits accrue, though growth has decelerated.

**Margin Structure**

Gross margins have been relatively stable at approximately 33-35% over 2020-2024, reflecting the mixed business model: TAS (technology and data analytics) carries higher margins while R&DS (CRO/clinical trial management) is more labor and passthrough-heavy. Operating margins have expanded materially — from 10.2% in 2020 to 13.7% in 2023 to 14.7% in 2024 — driven by operating leverage in the TAS segment and disciplined cost management. Adjusted EBITDA margin of approximately 24% is more representative of steady-state cash earnings power given the non-cash amortization drag (~$900M+ annually). Net margins on a GAAP basis were approximately 8.6% in 2023 ($1,358M / $14,984M) and 8.9% in 2024 ($1,373M / $15,405M).

**Cash Generation**

FCF quality is excellent. Operating cash flow grew from $2,149M (2023) to $2,716M (2024), and FCF from $1,500M to $2,114M — a 41% jump driven by improved working capital management and reduced restructuring payments. In 2025, FCF was $2,051M representing ~99% of Adjusted Net Income, confirming that reported adjusted earnings are largely cash-backed. Owner earnings (Net Income + D&A − estimated maintenance capex) are significantly higher than GAAP net income, as D&A from the merger-era intangibles adds back roughly $900-1,000M in non-cash charges annually. Capital expenditures run approximately $600M annually (roughly 4% of revenue), of which a portion is growth-oriented (technology platform development, data infrastructure). Maintenance capex is estimated at $300-400M.

**Capital Intensity and Operating Leverage**

IQVIA is moderately asset-light relative to manufacturing businesses but carries meaningful capex for technology platform upkeep and data asset maintenance. The TAS segment (40% of revenue) is close to pure data/software economics — subscription-based, high recurring revenue, low variable cost. The R&DS segment (55% of revenue) is more labor-intensive with large pass-through reimbursements that inflate reported revenue but not margins. Total capex of roughly $600M on $15.4B revenue (~4%) is manageable. Operating leverage has been demonstrated: from 2020 to 2024, revenue grew ~11% cumulatively while operating income grew ~61%, implying meaningful fixed cost absorption. However, recent revenue deceleration (2024: +2.8%) has limited further margin expansion.

**Revenue Quality**

Revenue quality is genuinely high. The TAS segment generates subscription-based recurring revenues from pharmaceutical companies buying data, analytics, and software access — these are sticky, multi-year contracts. R&DS operates on long-term project backlogs: the $31.1B contracted backlog as of end-2024 provides 3.6 years of R&DS revenue cover at recent run rates, and the $7.9B next-twelve-months conversion is highly predictable. CSMS (5% of revenue) is more transactional. Overall, IQVIA has a blend of approximately 40% highly-recurring TAS revenue and 55% contracted-backlog-supported R&DS revenue, giving the business exceptional top-line visibility relative to most industrial or consumer companies.

**Operating Leverage and Segment Dynamics**

The TAS segment, growing at 5-9% annually, has strong operating leverage characteristics — incremental margins on data license renewals and software add-ons are high. R&DS growth has been constrained by a biotech funding slowdown (2022-2024) which compressed new bookings, but the backlog build rate resumed in 2025 (R&DS revenue +4.3%). The 2025 full-year revenue of $16,310M (+5.9%) with EBITDA of $3,788M (+2.8%) shows that revenue growth outpaced EBITDA growth slightly, reflecting investment in platform infrastructure and some margin compression from pass-throughs. Longer-term, management has guided toward high-single-digit adjusted EPS growth as the business normalizes.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A durable data moat combined with a $31B CRO backlog and 40% TAS recurring subscriptions creates highly visible, compounding revenue with accelerating FCF as the debt load shrinks.
- **Bear case:** Low GAAP ROIC (~7%), modest revenue growth (3-6%), and high debt service costs mean the business must sustain execution over a long period before returns become compelling on an absolute basis.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Revenue, FCF, and margin figures are well-sourced from press releases and confirmed across multiple databases; ROIC interpretation requires judgment given the large intangible base.

## Red Flags

- GAAP ROIC of ~6-7% is below the cost of capital when taken at face value, though the intangible-heavy balance sheet is the primary driver.
- Revenue growth decelerated to 2.8% in 2024 — below long-term targets — driven by biotech funding constraints weighing on CRO bookings.
- Heavy D&A (~$900M+ annually) inflates the gap between GAAP earnings ($1.37B) and Adjusted Net Income ($2.04B), requiring investors to accept management's adjusted figures as more representative.
- Pass-through reimbursements inflate R&DS revenue without equivalent economic value; adjusted revenue excluding pass-throughs would show lower reported growth rates.

## Score: 7 / 10

Solid business economics anchored by high-quality recurring data subscriptions (TAS) and a large contracted CRO backlog (R&DS), with strong FCF conversion and expanding operating margins, offset by modest GAAP ROIC due to acquisition-era intangibles and recent top-line growth deceleration.
