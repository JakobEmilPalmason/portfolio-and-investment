# Investment Analysis: ROP — Roper Technologies

**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Verdict:** Own

> Roper Technologies is a high-quality vertical software compounder trading at historically depressed multiples (15x forward P/E, 14.5x EV/EBITDA) due to a transient guidance miss and cyclical headwinds at Deltek and DAT. With 28+ sticky niche software businesses generating 31% FCF margins, 95% gross retention, and a proven M&A engine, the business quality is exceptional — and the market is pricing in permanently impaired growth and zero value from $5B+ in acquisition capacity. For a patient investor with a 5-10 year horizon, this is a compelling entry point into a durable compounder at a rare discount.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Roper owns 28+ niche vertical software businesses (legal, insurance, government contracting, freight) that serve as mission-critical systems of record, generating $7.9B in revenue with 85%+ recurring and 31% FCF margins.
2. **Moat**: System-of-record switching costs — migrating off Deltek, Vertafore, or Aderant means months of data migration, retraining, and compliance risk — deliver 95% gross retention and 105-115% net revenue retention across the portfolio.
3. **Economics**: ROIC ex-goodwill is ~35%, FCF conversion is 158% of net income, and CapEx is just 1.3% of revenue, making this one of the most capital-efficient businesses in the market despite a misleading headline ROIC of 6%.
4. **Management**: CEO Neil Hunn has deployed $10B+ in vertical software M&A with strong returns and transformed Roper from an industrial conglomerate into a software compounder, though insider ownership at 0.17% is genuinely low.
5. **Debt risk**: Net Debt/EBITDA of 2.8x on $9.3B of investment-grade debt is elevated but serviceable — $2.4B in annual FCF covers interest 7x over, and even a 30% revenue decline (near-impossible with 95% retention) would not threaten solvency.
6. **Price**: At 15x forward earnings and 14.5x EV/EBITDA — a 33% discount to its 13-year median multiple — the market is pricing in permanently impaired growth and zero value creation from $5B+ in M&A capacity.
7. **Margin of safety**: Diversification across 28+ sticky software businesses with 31% FCF margins means cash generation is resilient even if growth disappoints, and the probability-weighted fair value of ~$405 provides a 15% cushion at $351.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if organic growth stays below 4% for three consecutive quarters with management lowering long-term targets, signaling the vertical software compounding model has structurally broken rather than facing cyclical headwinds.

## Key Risks

1. **Organic growth structural deceleration** (Medium likelihood, High impact): Q4 2025 organic growth decelerated to 4%, below the 5.5% expected. If this reflects a permanent step-down rather than cyclical headwinds (government shutdown, freight recession), the premium valuation framework breaks.

2. **Debt constrains the M&A engine** (Medium likelihood, High impact): At 2.8x Net Debt/EBITDA with $9.3B in total debt, Roper has less flexibility to pursue large acquisitions at a time when the pipeline is described as "super active." Rising interest costs (4-5% on new issuances) are a growing drag on equity returns.

3. **Government spending cuts hit Deltek** (Medium-High likelihood, Medium impact): Deltek is the largest single business and is disproportionately exposed to U.S. federal contracting budgets. DOGE-style spending cuts or prolonged government shutdowns could suppress this key contributor for an extended period.

4. **AI disruption of vertical software moats** (Low-Medium likelihood, Very High impact): If generalist AI agents can replicate the workflow automation that Roper's vertical software provides, switching costs could erode. This is slow-moving but represents the most existential long-term threat.

5. **Goodwill impairment risk** (Low likelihood, Medium impact): $19.3B in goodwill (56% of total assets) creates latent impairment risk if any major acquisition underperforms. While non-cash, write-downs could affect credit metrics and investor confidence.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - Q1 2026 organic growth comes in above 5%, confirming Q4 2025 weakness was transient
  - Stock tests $300-$315 range (near 52-week low) with no fundamental deterioration beyond current guidance
  - Large, well-priced M&A announcement that signals the compounding engine is intact

- **Sell triggers**:
  - Organic growth below 4% for three consecutive quarters with management lowering long-term growth targets
  - Debt/EBITDA exceeds 4.0x without a clear deleveraging path
  - Material churn increases (>200bps) across multiple verticals, signaling AI-native competitors gaining traction

# Circle of Competence — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release, company investor presentations, Morningstar, Seeking Alpha, company website (ropertech.com)

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Roper is a vertical software compounder: ~77% of revenue from Application Software (57%) and Network Software (20%), with Technology Enabled Products at 23% | 5 |
| 2 | Over 85% of revenue is recurring (subscriptions + term licenses), with net revenue retention of 105-115% across the portfolio | 5 |
| 3 | Revenue grew from $5.4B (FY2022) to $7.9B (FY2025), a 14% CAGR driven roughly equally by organic growth (~5%) and acquisitions (~7-10%) | 4 |
| 4 | Portfolio is highly diversified across 25+ niche software businesses serving distinct verticals (legal, insurance, government contracting, freight, healthcare, education, etc.) — no single customer or end market dominates | 4 |
| 5 | The business model is capital-light: only $105M CapEx on $7.9B revenue (1.3% of sales), enabling $2.4B in free cash flow (31% FCF margin) | 5 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Roper sell, and to whom?** Roper Technologies is a diversified technology company that operates a portfolio of over 25 niche vertical software businesses. These businesses sell mission-critical software — practice management, compliance, billing, logistics, workflow automation — to specific end markets. Application Software (57% of revenue, $4.5B) includes Deltek (project management for government contractors), Vertafore (insurance agency management), Aderant (law firm management), Clinisys (laboratory information systems), and others. Network Software (20%, $1.6B) includes DAT Solutions (freight matching network), ConstructConnect (construction bidding), iPipeline (life insurance distribution), and SoftWriters (pharmacy management). Technology Enabled Products (23%, $1.8B) includes Neptune (water meters), Verathon (medical devices), and Northern Digital (precision measurement). Customers are typically mid-market enterprises in regulated or specialized industries.

**How does it actually make money?** The revenue model is predominantly recurring software subscriptions and term licenses. Over 85% of total revenue is recurring or reoccurring. The Application Software segment runs at ~43% EBITDA margins, Network Software at ~53% EBITDA margins, and Technology Enabled Products at ~35% EBITDA margins. The compounding engine works as follows: existing businesses generate substantial free cash flow (FCF conversion of 158% of net income in FY2025), which Roper reinvests into acquiring additional niche vertical software businesses. Roper deployed $3.3B in M&A during 2025 alone (CentralReach, Subsplash, bolt-ons). The company does not aggressively cross-sell across its portfolio — each business operates autonomously with its own management team.

**What are the 2-4 key drivers?** (1) Organic revenue growth in existing software businesses, driven by price increases, cross-sell/upsell within verticals, and new customer wins — management guides 5-6% organic growth for 2026. (2) M&A deployment — Roper has $6B in acquisition capacity and a large pipeline; the quality and pace of deals is the single biggest swing factor for long-term value creation. (3) Margin expansion from operating leverage in software businesses and mix shift away from lower-margin Technology Enabled Products. (4) FCF conversion efficiency — the capital-light model means almost all earnings convert to deployable cash.

**How predictable is the revenue?** Very predictable. With 85%+ recurring revenue, 95% gross retention, and 105-115% net revenue retention, the base business is highly visible. The software is deeply embedded in customers' daily workflows — switching costs are enormous (see Umbrella 2). The main source of unpredictability is the M&A cadence: Roper's growth depends on finding and closing acquisitions at reasonable prices. Organic growth alone (5-6%) is solid but not exceptional; the M&A engine is what makes Roper a compounder. Cyclical exposure exists in DAT (freight markets, currently weak) and Deltek (government spending, currently under pressure from DOGE-style budget cuts), but these are minority contributors.

**Customer concentration?** Extremely low. With 25+ independent businesses serving dozens of distinct verticals, no single customer, contract, or end market represents a material share of consolidated revenue. This is a deliberate feature of the Roper model — diversification across niches reduces idiosyncratic risk.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** Yes. Roper Technologies is a holding company that buys and permanently holds niche vertical software businesses. Each business sells mission-critical, deeply embedded software to a specific industry (law firms, insurance agencies, government contractors, freight brokers, etc.). Customers rarely switch because the software is their system of record. This creates highly recurring revenue with fat margins. Roper takes the free cash flow from existing businesses and buys more of the same. It is essentially a perpetual compounding machine: buy sticky software businesses, harvest cash, reinvest in more sticky software businesses. The model is simple and understandable.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper is one of the highest-quality serial acquirers in the market. 85%+ recurring revenue, 31% FCF margins, capital-light model, and a proven M&A playbook make it a durable compounder. The business is firmly within circle of competence — the model is transparent and each piece is understandable.
- **Bear case:** The complexity of 25+ businesses means no single unit gets deep scrutiny from outside investors. M&A dependency means growth is lumpy and acquisition-price discipline is the key risk. Some segments (DAT freight, Deltek government) face cyclical headwinds that cloud near-term visibility.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is clear, well-documented, and has a long track record. The vertical software niche-acquirer model is straightforward to understand.

## Red Flags
- Organic growth of only 4-5% is not exceptional for a software company; the premium valuation historically relied on M&A-driven growth acceleration.
- Deltek (government contracting software) faces headwinds from federal budget uncertainty and DOGE-style spending cuts — this is a meaningful contributor to Application Software.
- DAT freight network remains in a cyclical downturn as the broader freight recession persists.

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper's business model is highly understandable — a capital-light, recurring-revenue software holding company with a clear compounding flywheel — and falls comfortably within circle of competence for any investor who understands software economics and serial acquirers.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release, Morningstar (wide moat rating), Seeking Alpha, PitchGrade Research, BeyondSPX, company investor presentations

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Roper's portfolio companies serve as systems-of-record in their verticals, creating deep switching costs that are economic and operational, not merely technical | 5 |
| 2 | 95% enterprise gross retention and 105-115% net revenue retention across portfolio companies demonstrate the moat in hard numbers | 5 |
| 3 | Morningstar upgraded Roper to "wide moat" status, citing switching costs and niche market dominance as primary sources | 4 |
| 4 | 69% gross margins and 28% operating margins sustained over multiple years indicate durable pricing power across the portfolio | 4 |
| 5 | Each vertical software business holds a #1 or #2 position in its niche, creating scale advantages that are difficult for new entrants to replicate | 4 |
| 6 | Moat weakest in Technology Enabled Products (23% of revenue) where competitive dynamics are more traditional hardware/device competition | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What type of moat?** Roper's primary moat is switching costs — and specifically the deepest, most durable variety: system-of-record entrenchment. When a law firm runs its entire practice on Aderant, when an insurance agency manages all policies through Vertafore, or when a government contractor handles project accounting through Deltek, the cost of switching is enormous. It includes data migration risk, staff retraining, workflow disruption, regulatory compliance gaps, and months of parallel running. These are not cosmetic switching costs — they are existential operational risks for the customer. The second moat source is niche market dominance. In most of its verticals, Roper's subsidiary is the #1 or #2 player in a market too small to attract large horizontal software companies (Salesforce, SAP, Microsoft). Vertafore dominates insurance agency management software. Deltek dominates government contractor project management. DAT operates the largest spot freight matching network in North America. These niche leadership positions create a self-reinforcing cycle: market leader attracts more customers, generates more data, invests more in R&D, and pulls further ahead. A third emerging moat is the data advantage. Portfolio companies with years of domain-specific data (DAT's freight pricing data, ConstructConnect's construction bid data, Vertafore's insurance transaction data) are building AI/ML capabilities that new entrants cannot replicate without equivalent data scale.

**How durable — will it exist in 10 years?** The switching cost moat is among the most durable in software. System-of-record software in regulated, specialized industries does not get disrupted quickly. Law firms did not abandon their practice management systems for cloud-native alternatives overnight — transitions in these verticals take decades, not years. The vertical software markets Roper targets are also inherently protected from big-tech disruption: the total addressable market for "insurance agency management software" or "pharmacy long-term care software" is too small and too specialized for Microsoft or Google to pursue. This structural protection should persist for 10+ years. The main durability risk is AI disruption: if generalist AI agents can replicate the workflow automation that Roper's vertical software provides, some moats could erode. However, the data moats and deep regulatory/compliance features in these verticals make this a slow-moving risk at most. The Technology Enabled Products segment has a less durable moat — Neptune water meters and Verathon medical devices face more traditional competitive dynamics, though they maintain strong positions within their niches.

**Evidence in numbers.** Gross margins of 69% and operating margins of 28% have been sustained and expanding over multiple years — a hallmark of pricing power. Application Software EBITDA margins at 43% and Network Software at 53% are elite. The 95% gross retention rate means customers almost never leave. Net revenue retention of 105-115% means existing customers spend more each year — Roper grows even without winning a single new customer. The 82% annual contract renewal rate with average partnership duration of 7.3 years further demonstrates stickiness. FCF margins of 31% on $7.9B revenue, with only 1.3% CapEx intensity, reflect a business whose competitive position allows it to convert nearly all earnings to cash without heavy reinvestment to maintain its position.

**Where is the moat weakest?** Three areas: (1) Technology Enabled Products (23% of revenue) — Neptune water meters, Verathon medical devices, and similar hardware businesses have competitive advantages but face more traditional rivalry, shorter product cycles, and lower switching costs than the software portfolio. Roper has been strategically divesting these assets (sold its industrial businesses in 2019-2022) but still retains a meaningful TEP segment. (2) DAT freight network — while DAT is the largest freight matching network, the freight-tech space has attracted significant venture capital and competitive intensity (though Convoy's failure and DAT's acquisition of its platform strengthened DAT's position). (3) Any business where the customer is a small enterprise with limited IT complexity — switching costs diminish as the customer shrinks.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** No — this is a key strength. Vertical software is not like consumer electronics or even enterprise SaaS where platform shifts (on-premise to cloud, for example) can reset competitive positions. Roper's businesses are already largely cloud/SaaS-native or have completed transitions. The data accumulates, the integrations deepen, and the compliance certifications compound over time. There is no "next version" that levels the playing field. The moat compounds rather than resets.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper possesses one of the widest moats in the software industry, built on system-of-record switching costs in dozens of niche verticals. 95% gross retention and 105-115% NRR prove the moat empirically. The combination of switching costs + niche dominance + data moats creates a triple-layered defense that should persist for a decade or more.
- **Bear case:** The Technology Enabled Products segment (~23% of revenue) has a narrower moat. AI disruption could theoretically erode switching costs if generalist AI agents replicate vertical workflow automation. Debt of $9.3B (3.0x EBITDA) used for acquisitions means the moat must generate reliable cash flow to service obligations.
- **Confidence:** High — Switching costs in system-of-record vertical software are among the most empirically validated moat sources in the market. The retention metrics and margin profile confirm the moat is real and durable.

## Red Flags
- Technology Enabled Products still represents 23% of revenue with narrower competitive advantages — Roper's moat story is cleaner if this segment shrinks further.
- $9.3B in debt (3.0x EBITDA) means the moat must generate consistent cash to service obligations; a severe downturn across multiple verticals simultaneously could stress the balance sheet.
- AI risk is real but slow-moving — generalist AI tools could eventually reduce the specialization premium that underpins vertical software switching costs.

## Score: 8 / 10
Roper's moat is wide and durable, built on deep switching costs and niche market dominance across 25+ vertical software businesses, backed by elite retention metrics and sustained high margins. The score reflects the remaining exposure to narrower-moat hardware businesses and the theoretical long-term AI disruption risk.

# Management & Capital Allocation — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 2025 10-K, Q4 2025 earnings release, proxy statement, company investor presentations, Seeking Alpha, Morningstar, Bloomberg executive profiles, SEC filings

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | CEO Neil Hunn (since 2018) has executed a disciplined strategic transformation — exiting industrial businesses and deploying $10B+ in vertical software M&A over his tenure | 5 |
| 2 | Insider ownership is very low at 0.17%, though executive compensation is 96% at-risk and tied to adjusted EBITDA growth, net earnings CAGR, and relative TSR | 4 |
| 3 | M&A track record is strong: Vertafore ($5.35B, 2020), Deltek ($2.8B, 2016), CentralReach, Subsplash — all performing within or above acquisition theses | 5 |
| 4 | First-ever $3B share repurchase authorization deployed opportunistically — $500M repurchased in Q4 2025 at avg. ~$446/share, well above current price of $351 | 3 |
| 5 | Decentralized operating model with autonomous subsidiary management — Roper is a "home of choice" for acquisition targets, supporting deal pipeline | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership — skin in the game?** This is Roper's weakest governance metric. Insider ownership stands at only 0.17% of shares outstanding as of April 2025. For a $38B company, this is not unusual in absolute terms, but it means management's personal wealth is not primarily concentrated in Roper stock. That said, all named executive officers are in compliance with share ownership guidelines, and the compensation structure creates meaningful alignment (see below). Institutional ownership is 94%, with mutual funds holding 77% — this is a widely held, professionally monitored company. The low insider ownership is a yellow flag but not a red flag given the compensation structure.

**Capital allocation track record.** This is where Roper's management truly distinguishes itself. The company's capital allocation strategy has four prongs, in priority order: (1) M&A — the primary growth engine, (2) organic reinvestment in existing businesses, (3) share repurchases (new, opportunistic), and (4) modest dividends. On M&A, the track record is exceptional. Major acquisitions include Deltek ($2.8B, 2016), Vertafore ($5.35B, 2020), CentralReach (2025), and Subsplash ($800M, 2025). Roper deployed $3.3B in M&A in 2025 alone. Critically, the company has also been a disciplined seller — divesting its industrial businesses (Gatan, Scientific Imaging, etc.) between 2019 and 2022 at attractive multiples to fund higher-return software acquisitions. This portfolio reshaping under Hunn's leadership has transformed Roper from a diversified industrial conglomerate into a focused vertical software compounder. The company now maintains $6B in acquisition capacity with a robust pipeline. The introduction of the $3B share repurchase program adds a new capital return tool — $500M was deployed in Q4 2025 at an average price of ~$446/share. With the stock now at $351, that particular deployment looks poorly timed in the short run, though it signals management's confidence in intrinsic value.

**Compensation design.** Roper's executive compensation is well-aligned with shareholders. For the CEO, 96% of target direct compensation is at-risk (performance-based or stock-linked), with only 4% in base salary. Short-term incentives are tied to adjusted EBITDA growth. Long-term incentives use performance-based restricted stock units (PRSUs) that are 100% performance-contingent — no pure time-based restricted stock. The PRSUs vest over 3 years and are tied to adjusted net earnings CAGR with a relative TSR modifier. In 2025, the company raised performance rigor thresholds with higher entry and target hurdles, demonstrating a board willing to tighten the bar. This is a well-designed comp structure that rewards durable earnings growth rather than short-term stock manipulation.

**Candor and consistency.** Roper's management communication is notably candid for a serial acquirer. On the Q4 2025 earnings call, management acknowledged the Deltek headwinds from government spending uncertainty and the DAT freight market weakness without sugarcoating. Guidance for 2026 (8% total revenue growth, 5-6% organic) was described as conservative, with management noting they expect stronger second-half performance. The company provides segment-level detail and organic vs. acquisition growth breakdowns, which is above-average transparency for a conglomerate. Neil Hunn consistently frames capital allocation in terms of "deploying cash into the highest-return opportunities" and speaks like an owner-operator rather than a bureaucratic CEO. The management presentation style is data-driven and operationally focused.

**Governance structure.** Roper's board is composed primarily of independent directors. The decentralized operating model — where each portfolio company retains its own management team and operates autonomously — is both a governance feature and an acquisition advantage. Acquisition targets see Roper as a "permanent home" rather than a private equity-style flip, which gives Roper preferential access to deal flow. This is a genuine strategic moat in the M&A market. The board has shown willingness to evolve — the recent introduction of share repurchases and the tightening of compensation hurdles demonstrate active governance.

**Do they talk like owners or politicians?** Owners. Neil Hunn's communication style is direct, metrics-oriented, and focused on free cash flow and returns on deployed capital. There is minimal jargon and no excessive promotional language. The Q4 2025 call acknowledged disappointing areas (Deltek, DAT) while explaining the structural thesis. The company's investor presentations focus on FCF conversion, organic growth rates, and M&A pipeline quality — the metrics that actually matter for a serial acquirer. This is a management team that understands its business model and communicates it clearly.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper's management has executed one of the most successful strategic transformations in industrial/tech history — from diversified industrial conglomerate to focused vertical software compounder. The M&A track record is excellent, compensation is well-aligned, and the decentralized model creates a sustainable deal-flow advantage. Capital allocation is the primary value driver, and this team has proven it can compound capital at high returns.
- **Bear case:** Insider ownership at 0.17% is genuinely low — management's personal financial exposure to Roper is modest. The $500M buyback at $446/share in Q4 2025 (now trading at $351) raises questions about short-term capital allocation timing, though the long-term thesis may vindicate it. $9.3B in debt from M&A means any acquisition misstep is magnified.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The track record is strong and the compensation structure is well-designed, but low insider ownership and the recent buyback timing prevent a full "High" rating.

## Red Flags
- Insider ownership of 0.17% is well below what Buffett-style investors prefer; management does not have outsized personal stakes in the business.
- Q4 2025 buyback of $500M at ~$446/share is now underwater by ~21% at current $351 price — suggests management may have overestimated near-term intrinsic value or was slow to recognize macro headwinds.
- Debt of $9.3B (3.0x EBITDA) is elevated for a serial acquirer and limits margin of safety if M&A execution falters or multiple portfolio companies underperform simultaneously.
- Decentralized model means corporate has limited operational control if a subsidiary underperforms — the fix is typically patience or divestiture, not active intervention.

## Score: 7 / 10
Roper's management has an excellent capital allocation track record and well-aligned compensation, but low insider ownership and elevated debt from aggressive M&A temper the score. The strategic transformation from industrial conglomerate to vertical software compounder has been masterfully executed, earning a strong but not exceptional stewardship rating.

# Business Economics — ROP

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-16
**Data Sources:** Roper Technologies 10-K (FY2025, filed Feb 2026), quarterly earnings releases (Q1-Q4 2025), Yahoo Finance, GuruFocus, StockAnalysis.com, company investor presentations

## Key Findings
| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Headline ROIC of ~6% is misleading; ROIC ex-goodwill is ~35%, reflecting the true economics of Roper's asset-light software businesses | 5 |
| 2 | FCF margin of 31% with 158% FCF conversion (FCF/NI) demonstrates exceptional cash generation relative to reported earnings | 5 |
| 3 | ~75% of revenue is software, with ~80% recurring; gross retention ~95%, net retention 105-120% across the portfolio | 5 |
| 4 | Capital intensity is minimal: only $105M CapEx on $7.9B revenue (1.3% of sales), making this one of the most asset-light industrial conglomerates in existence | 4 |
| 5 | Consistent operating leverage: organic growth of 5-8% annually translates to faster EBITDA growth, with EBITDA margin stable at ~40% | 4 |
| 6 | Revenue has compounded from $5.4B (2022) to $7.9B (2025), a ~14% CAGR, driven by ~6% organic growth plus acquisitions | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: The Goodwill Distortion.** Roper's headline ROIC of 5-7% appears mediocre but is one of the most misleading numbers in large-cap investing. The company's balance sheet carries ~$19.3B in goodwill and several billion more in acquired intangibles, reflecting over two decades of serial acquisitions. Total assets are $34.6B, but tangible operating assets are a tiny fraction of that. When goodwill is stripped out, ROIC jumps to approximately 35% on a 3-year average basis, per GuruFocus data. This is the correct way to evaluate Roper's operating economics: the underlying businesses require almost no tangible capital to operate. ROE of ~8% is similarly depressed by the enormous equity base inflated by acquisition goodwill. Management uses a proprietary "Cash Return on Investment" (CrROI) metric internally, which better captures the cash-on-cash returns of deployed acquisition capital and has historically run in the mid-to-high teens, well above Roper's cost of capital.

**Margin Structure and Trends.** Roper's margin profile is exceptional for a company of its scale and diversity. Gross margin is 69%, operating margin 28%, EBITDA margin 40%, and net margin 19%. These margins have been stable to improving over the past several years as the company has divested lower-margin industrial businesses (TransCore, CIVCO Radiotherapy, etc.) and replaced them with higher-margin vertical software acquisitions. The Application Software segment (~57% of revenue) and Network Software segment (~20%) both exhibit software-like margin profiles. Even the Technology Enabled Products segment (23% of revenue) runs gross margins near 59%. Operating leverage is visible within each segment: as organic revenue grows 5-8%, SG&A as a percentage of revenue has declined in both Application Software and Network Software in recent quarters.

**Cash Generation: The Crown Jewel.** Roper generated $2.4B in FCF on $7.9B in revenue for a 31% FCF margin in FY2025, up 12% year-over-year. FCF conversion to net income is 158%, meaning Roper generates far more cash than reported earnings. This is driven by high non-cash amortization of acquired intangibles ($898M in D&A vs. only $105M in CapEx), plus favorable working capital dynamics from upfront subscription billing. Owner earnings (NI + D&A - CapEx) were $2.3B. Stock-based compensation of $166M is modest at ~2% of revenue, so FCF is not materially inflated by SBC. The company has been able to fund $500M in buybacks, pay dividends, and continue acquisitions while maintaining this cash flow engine.

**Capital Intensity: Ultra-Light Model.** CapEx of $105M represents just 1.3% of revenue, one of the lowest ratios among large-cap industrials or software companies. There is virtually no maintenance CapEx requirement — the software businesses have no factories, no heavy equipment, and minimal physical infrastructure. Nearly all CapEx is discretionary growth spending. This asset-light model is the foundation of Roper's compounding flywheel: acquisitions generate immediate cash flow that can be recycled into the next acquisition without needing to reinvest heavily in the existing business.

**Revenue Quality: Deep Recurring Moats.** Approximately 75% of Roper's revenue comes from software, with roughly 80% of that being recurring (subscriptions, SaaS, maintenance contracts). This translates to over 60% of total company revenue being recurring software revenue. Enterprise gross retention rates are approximately 95%, meaning very low churn, while net retention rates of 105-120% indicate healthy expansion from existing customers through upselling, price increases, and growing transaction volumes. The vertical-specific nature of Roper's software (legal management, insurance distribution, construction data, K-12 education, healthcare diagnostics) creates deep switching costs, as these products are often mission-critical and deeply embedded in customer workflows.

**Operating Leverage: Profits Growing Faster Than Revenue.** Roper demonstrates meaningful operating leverage. From 2022 to 2025, revenue grew from $5.4B to $7.9B (~47% cumulative), while EBITDA grew from ~$2.1B to $3.2B (~52% cumulative) and FCF grew from ~$1.9B to $2.4B (~26% cumulative). The slight lag in FCF growth relative to EBITDA reflects higher interest expense from acquisition-related debt.
