## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** As of December 31, 2025, Roper carried total debt of $9,355.9 million, consisting entirely of senior unsecured notes plus borrowings on its revolving credit facility. The company exited FY2025 with $297M in cash and $850M drawn on the revolver, resulting in net debt of approximately $9.0B. Net Debt/EBITDA stands at 2.8x, which is above the typical 1-2x comfort zone for conservative balance sheets but is deliberately maintained to fuel the acquisition-driven compounding model. All debt is investment-grade rated — Roper maintains BBB/Baa2 credit ratings from S&P and Moody's respectively. The debt is entirely fixed-rate senior unsecured notes plus the variable-rate revolver, with the fixed-rate notes comprising the vast majority. The August 2025 offering of $2B in new notes ($500M at 4.25% due 2028, $500M at 4.45% due 2030, $1B at 5.10% due 2035) was used to refinance notes maturing in September and December 2025, demonstrating proactive maturity management. Weighted-average cost of debt is likely in the 4-5% range.

**Interest Coverage.** EBIT-based interest coverage is 7.0x ($2.27B operating income / $325M interest expense). EBITDA-based coverage is a more comfortable 9.8x ($3.2B / $325M). FCF-based coverage is 7.4x ($2.4B / $325M). These are adequate levels for an investment-grade credit, though not wide margins of safety by Buffett standards. Interest expense has risen as Roper has added debt for acquisitions — the $325M annual interest burden is a real cost that reduces equity returns. However, given the non-cyclical, recurring nature of Roper's revenue streams, the risk of cash flows dropping below debt service levels is low in any normal economic scenario.

**Liquidity Position.** Roper's liquidity position is strong despite the optically weak 0.5x current ratio. The low current ratio is a structural feature of subscription software businesses: deferred revenue (cash collected upfront for multi-period contracts) sits as a current liability, making the ratio misleadingly low. Actual liquidity includes $297M in cash plus approximately $2.65B of undrawn capacity on the $3.5B unsecured revolving credit facility, totaling roughly $2.95B of available liquidity. This is more than sufficient to cover near-term obligations, fund bolt-on acquisitions, and manage any temporary disruptions. The revolver matures in 2029 based on the 5-year facility structure.

**Refinancing Risk.** Refinancing risk is low-to-moderate. Roper demonstrated its ability to access debt markets on favorable terms with the August 2025 $2B offering. The maturity ladder is now well-staggered: nothing major due before 2027 (the 2025 maturities were retired), with the $500M notes due 2028, $500M due 2030, and $1B due 2035 providing a smooth maturity profile. Additional older notes likely mature in the 2027-2029 window based on prior issuances. The remaining ~$5-6B in outstanding notes (beyond the 2025 issuance) are spread across multiple maturities. As an investment-grade issuer with $2.4B in annual FCF, Roper has the flexibility to either refinance or retire maturing debt. The company could, in theory, eliminate all debt within 4 years if it paused acquisitions entirely — though this is not the strategic intent.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Roper's primary off-balance-sheet items include operating lease obligations (modest given the software-heavy business model) and contingent consideration from acquisitions (earnouts). Neither is material relative to the company's scale. There are no unconsolidated entities, complex derivatives, or hidden guarantees that would pose a risk. The $19.3B goodwill balance is on-balance-sheet but represents a risk in its own right — it is tested annually for impairment, and any significant write-down, while non-cash, would reduce book equity and increase leverage ratios.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years.** Under a severe scenario where revenue drops 30% (from $7.9B to ~$5.5B) and stays there for two years, Roper would still likely generate positive FCF. Here is the logic: at 40% EBITDA margin, a 30% revenue decline would produce ~$2.2B in EBITDA (assuming some margin compression to ~40% given the high fixed-cost software model — margins might actually hold better given software's inherent operating leverage). Interest expense of ~$325M would still be covered 6.8x. FCF would likely fall to ~$1.3-1.5B, still positive and sufficient to service all debt obligations. The company would survive, though leverage would temporarily spike to ~4.2x Net Debt/EBITDA — uncomfortable but not covenant-breaking for a BBB-rated credit. Importantly, a 30% revenue decline is extremely unlikely given that 60%+ of revenue is recurring software with 95% gross retention and mission-critical vertical applications. Even in the 2008-2009 financial crisis, Roper's revenue declined only modestly. The true downside scenario is more like a 10-15% revenue decline, which would barely dent the balance sheet.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Roper's balance sheet is deliberately leveraged to fuel a proven acquisition compounding model. All debt is investment-grade, fixed-rate, with staggered maturities and no near-term refinancing cliffs. $2.95B in available liquidity provides ample cushion. The recurring, non-cyclical revenue base makes the 2.8x leverage far less risky than it would be for a cyclical industrial.
- **Bear case:** 2.8x Net Debt/EBITDA is elevated by conservative standards. Interest expense of $325M is a meaningful drag on equity returns. If acquisition multiples increase or integration stumbles, the leverage becomes harder to justify. A rising rate environment (the 5.1% rate on the 2035 notes vs. lower legacy rates) is gradually increasing the cost of the debt stack. Goodwill impairment, while non-cash, could trigger covenant or ratings concerns.
- **Confidence:** High — Roper's debt structure is transparent, the maturity schedule is public, and the cash flow coverage is well-documented. The recurring revenue base provides high confidence in debt service ability.

## Red Flags
- 2.8x Net Debt/EBITDA is above conservative thresholds; leaves limited room for a large acquisition without further leveraging
- $19.3B goodwill (56% of total assets) creates latent impairment risk that could affect credit metrics
- Interest expense trending higher as legacy low-rate debt is refinanced at 4-5% rates
- Current ratio of 0.5x, while structurally explained, could concern creditors in a stress scenario
- Acquisition pace requires ongoing capital market access; a credit market freeze would constrain the growth model

## Score: 7 / 10
Roper's balance sheet is intentionally leveraged but well-managed, with investment-grade ratings, staggered maturities, strong liquidity, and recurring cash flows that comfortably cover all obligations. The 2.8x leverage and rising interest costs prevent a higher score, but the risk of financial distress is genuinely low given the business model's resilience.
