## Detailed Analysis

**What does Merck sell and to whom?** Merck & Co., Inc. (headquartered in Rahway, NJ) is one of the world's largest pharmaceutical companies. It discovers, develops, manufactures, and markets prescription medicines, vaccines, biological therapies, and animal health products. Its primary customers are hospitals, oncology clinics, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), government health programs (Medicare, Medicaid, VA), and wholesale drug distributors — not individual patients directly. Internationally, it sells through local affiliates and distributors to national health systems. The Animal Health segment sells to veterinarians, livestock producers, and pet owners via a parallel distribution channel.

**Revenue model.** Merck makes money by obtaining regulatory approval for branded drugs, pricing those drugs at a premium during the patent-protected exclusivity window, and maintaining volume through physician relationships, clinical data, and formulary negotiations. Once patents expire, generic or biosimilar competition collapses prices by 80-90%. The model therefore requires continuous R&D investment and business development (M&A) to replace revenue as drugs cycle off patent. Royalties, licensing income, and contract manufacturing are minor contributors. Animal Health operates on a similar model — proprietary branded products sold at premium margins.

**Key revenue drivers.** There are four variables that matter most right now: (1) KEYTRUDA volume and pricing — it drives nearly half of total revenue and any slowdown matters enormously; (2) New launch ramp — WINREVAIR (PAH), CAPVAXIVE (pneumococcal vaccine), OHTUVAYRE (COPD), and the subcutaneous KEYTRUDA QLEX need to build scale before 2028; (3) GARDASIL recovery — China remains disrupted and represents a multi-billion-dollar swing factor; (4) IRA/government pricing headwinds — Medicare drug price negotiation and rebate rules represent an estimated ~$2.5B headwind to 2026 revenues per management guidance.

**Revenue predictability.** Within the patent window, pharmaceutical revenue is moderately predictable — KEYTRUDA carries multi-year formulary contracts, and oncology drugs rarely lose patients mid-course. However, this predictability has a hard expiry date: the 2028-2029 KEYTRUDA patent cliff is the single largest known risk overhang in the portfolio. Vaccine revenue (GARDASIL) has proven more volatile due to geopolitical factors. Animal Health is the most stable segment — recurring, low-event-risk, tied to livestock economics and companion animal trends. Quarterly seasonality is modest.

**Customer concentration.** Merck sells into highly fragmented end markets (millions of patients, thousands of hospitals) but is dependent on a small number of U.S. wholesale distributors (McKesson, AmerisourceBergen, Cardinal Health) who collectively handle the vast majority of pharmaceutical logistics. Payer concentration adds another layer — large PBMs and government programs have significant leverage in formulary and pricing negotiations. No single customer likely exceeds 10-15% of revenue, but the payer/distributor channel structure does limit direct pricing power.

**Two-minute explanation.** Merck is a large drug company that makes money by selling patented medicines at premium prices. Its star product, KEYTRUDA, is the world's top-selling cancer drug — a checkpoint inhibitor that teaches the immune system to attack tumors — and it alone generates nearly half the company's revenue. Merck also sells vaccines (GARDASIL against HPV), a new pulmonary hypertension drug (WINREVAIR), and a broad portfolio of other prescription drugs. It has an animal health division that sells medicines for livestock and pets. The fundamental business challenge is that KEYTRUDA's patents expire in 2028-2029, and Merck is racing to build a pipeline of new drugs, acquire companies with promising assets, and transition patients to a new subcutaneous version of KEYTRUDA that has patent protection to 2042 — before generic competition arrives and collapses revenue.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** KEYTRUDA's subcutaneous formulation captures 30-40% of the patient base before IV biosimilars arrive, new launches (WINREVAIR, CAPVAXIVE, cardiometabolic pipeline) compound to replace most of the lost revenue, and Animal Health grows steadily.
- **Bear case:** KEYTRUDA IV biosimilars erode revenue faster than expected, new launches disappoint or face trial failures, and Merck enters a multi-year revenue decline that compresses earnings and the multiple.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model, product revenues, and near-term risks are very well documented in public filings and earnings releases. The uncertainty is about execution post-2028, not about understanding the business.

## Red Flags

- Nearly 49% of revenue from a single drug facing patent expiry in 2028-2029
- GARDASIL revenue down 39% in 2025 — China disruption risk is not resolved
- "Subcutaneous product hop" strategy for KEYTRUDA faces litigation and political scrutiny
- IRA drug price negotiation is an emerging structural headwind for high-revenue products

## Score: 7 / 10

The core business is understandable — a classic Big Pharma cash machine built on a dominant oncology franchise — but the single-product concentration and the known 2028 cliff mean understanding the business requires understanding a complex pipeline transition, which introduces moderate analytical complexity.
