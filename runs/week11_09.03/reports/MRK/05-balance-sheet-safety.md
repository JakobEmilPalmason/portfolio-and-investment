## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure:** As of end-2024, Merck carried approximately $37.1B in total debt against stockholders' equity of ~$46.3B, implying a debt-to-equity ratio of ~0.80x — meaningfully improved from 0.93x in 2023 and down from a peak of over 1.0x. Total debt/EBITDA using 2024 EBITDA of ~$24.7B equals approximately 1.5x — well within the 2x comfort zone and far below the 4x danger threshold. By TTM 2025, with EBITDA around $27B, the leverage ratio improved further toward ~1.3x. In December 2025, Merck issued approximately $8.0B in new senior unsecured notes across eight tranches with maturities ranging from 2029 to 2065 to partially fund the planned $9.2B acquisition of Cidara Therapeutics. This issuance temporarily increases gross debt but with coupons of 3.85%–5.70% and very long-dated maturities, the structure is not threatening. The balance sheet reported carrying value of long-term debt at approximately $35.4B as of mid-2025 (fair value ~$31.3B, reflecting rising rates environment).

**Interest Coverage:** This is a clear strength. Search results confirm EBIT of approximately $23.9B yielding an interest coverage ratio of approximately 29x. Even applying a more conservative EBITDA-to-interest metric, coverage is comfortably above 15x. For context, 5x is the standard "comfortable" threshold and 3x is a warning signal — Merck is operating at nearly 10x above the warning threshold. Interest expense on ~$35–38B in debt at blended rates of approximately 3.5–4.5% (mix of older low-rate paper and newer higher-coupon issuances) runs approximately $1.2–1.7B annually. With annual FCF of $13–18B, Merck could theoretically service its entire debt load principal and interest from approximately two years of free cash flow.

**Liquidity:** Cash on hand was approximately $8.6B as of Q2 2025, then rose sharply to approximately $18.2B by Q3 2025, likely reflecting the proceeds from the December 2025 bond issuance combined with FCF generation. Even at the lower Q2 figure, $8.6B in cash against annual interest obligations of ~$1.5B and annual capex of ~$2.2B provides ample runway. Merck also has access to credit facilities (specific terms not publicly confirmed in this search, but large-cap pharma companies of Merck's size routinely maintain $3–10B revolving credit facilities). Current ratio is healthy — Merck can fund at least 24 months of operations without accessing capital markets. The company's investment-grade credit rating (S&P A+, Moody's A1 as of last available data) ensures that capital markets access is effectively guaranteed even in stressed conditions.

**Refinancing Risk:** Low. Merck does not need capital markets to be open to survive. With FCF generation of $13–18B annually and relatively modest debt service requirements, the company generates more than enough internal cash to retire debt as it matures. The December 2025 bond issuance pushed maturities out to as far as 2065 — this is a proactive liability management move that reduces near-term refinancing pressure. The one scenario where refinancing risk increases is if Keytruda revenue were to decline sharply post-biosimilar (2028+), materially compressing FCF. Even in that scenario, Merck would still likely generate $8–12B in FCF annually — enough to service debt comfortably.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations:** Like all major pharmaceutical companies, Merck carries several off-balance-sheet obligations that merit attention. Pension and post-retirement benefit obligations are standard in large pharma and likely run in the multi-billion-dollar range (specific funded status not found in current searches; historical 10-K filings typically show net pension obligation of $2–4B for companies of this size). Operating lease obligations are present but not material at this scale. Contingent liabilities from pharmaceutical litigation are the more significant wildcard — Merck has historically faced product liability suits (Vioxx settlement was >$4.85B, fully resolved). Current litigation exposure related to Gardasil lawsuits and potential Keytruda adverse event claims exists but is not quantified publicly in this research window. These are industry-standard risks and do not change the safety assessment at current debt levels.

**Stress Test — 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years:** Merck reported 2025 revenue of approximately $65B. A 30% decline would reduce revenue to ~$45.5B. At current cost structure, gross margins would likely compress from ~77% to perhaps 70–72% (fixed manufacturing costs spreading over lower volumes), yielding gross profit of ~$32–33B. Applying operating expense leverage conservatively, EBITDA might fall from ~$27B to approximately $12–15B. Even at $12B EBITDA, debt service of ~$1.5B and capex of ~$2.2B leaves ~$8B in free operating cash flow. Merck could survive this scenario without equity issuance and while continuing to fund its dividend (~$7B/year at current rates). R&D spend ($15–17B) would likely be cut materially in such a scenario, providing further protection. The company would survive — this is not an overleveraged balance sheet.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Fortress-like interest coverage (~29x), debt/EBITDA of ~1.3–1.5x, and $13–18B annual FCF mean Merck's balance sheet is a competitive advantage — it can fund large acquisitions, maintain dividends, and absorb pipeline failures without financial stress.
- **Bear case:** The December 2025 debt issuance added ~$8B for Cidara; if Keytruda biosimilar entry in 2028 compresses FCF significantly and additional large acquisitions are needed to fill the revenue gap, leverage could ratchet up toward 2–3x at a time when coverage ratios are under pressure.
- **Confidence:** High — the current balance sheet metrics are transparent, well-documented, and consistent across multiple data sources; the stress scenario is survivable with comfortable margin.

## Red Flags

- Absolute debt level of ~$35–43B (post-Cidara issuance) is large, though well-covered by earnings
- December 2025 issuance of ~$8B for Cidara acquisition increases gross leverage before that asset generates meaningful cash flow
- Pension and pharmaceutical litigation contingent liabilities are not fully quantified from current search data — require 10-K review for completeness
- Gardasil litigation risk (lawsuits over alleged adverse effects) is an emerging contingent liability not fully reserved on the balance sheet
- Post-2027 FCF visibility is reduced due to Keytruda biosimilar entry, which could change leverage dynamics materially

## Score: 8 / 10

Merck's balance sheet earns a strong 8: debt/EBITDA of ~1.3–1.5x, interest coverage of ~29x, and $13–18B in annual FCF constitute a genuinely conservative leverage profile — the deduction from a 9+ reflects the recent step-up in gross debt from acquisition activity and the potential for leverage to increase if the 2028 Keytruda cliff is worse than expected.
