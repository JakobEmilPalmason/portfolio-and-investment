## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital:** Merck's ROIC sits in the 21–26% range depending on the reporting window and methodology. GuruFocus calculates ROIC at 20.92% on a trailing twelve-month basis through mid-2025; AlphaSpread reports 25.91%. ROE is approximately 36.9%, elevated partly by the meaningful debt load taken on to fund acquisitions (Prometheus, Harpoon, Acceleron, and the pending Cidara deal). By any measure, returns on capital are well above the ~6.5% WACC, demonstrating that Merck deploys capital into genuinely value-creating assets. The trend over the past five years has been upward — ROIC has expanded as Keytruda moved from a growth drug to a dominant franchise generating operating leverage.

**Margin Structure:** Gross margins have expanded meaningfully and steadily: from roughly 70.6% in 2022 to 73.2% in 2023, 76.3% in 2024, and ~77.7% on a TTM basis through Q3 2025. This is exceptional for a large-cap pharma and reflects Keytruda's extremely high-margin biologic profile — manufacturing costs are modest relative to realization. Operating margins recovered to ~31% in 2024 after being compressed in prior years by in-process R&D charges from acquisitions. Net margin reached 26.7% in 2024 and expanded to ~29.6% by Q3 2025 TTM. These are high-quality pharmaceutical margins, on par with peers like Eli Lilly and AbbVie.

**Cash Generation:** FCF generation is the standout metric. After years of being suppressed by large upfront acquisition charges (Acceleron in 2021, Prometheus in 2023), Merck generated approximately $18.1B in FCF in 2024 — nearly doubling from 2023. On a TTM basis through late 2025, FCF was approximately $13–17B depending on the window, with some variation due to timing of capex and working capital. Operating cash flow is approximately $16.5B (TTM). Capital expenditures have been running at ~$4.1B (reported in the FCF search), suggesting the FCF conversion rate relative to net income is robust — at a net income of roughly $15–18B and FCF around $13–18B, conversion is healthy and above the 80% threshold that signals quality earnings. Owner earnings (net income + D&A - maintenance capex) would be somewhat higher still, given that Merck's D&A is substantial from acquisition-related intangible amortization.

**Capital Intensity:** Merck is a moderately asset-light business for its size. Guided capex of ~$2.2B against ~$65B in revenue represents a capex-to-revenue ratio of ~3.4% — very low for a manufacturing company, reflecting that drug discovery and marketing drive economics more than physical plant. The heavier "investment" in Merck's model is R&D spending, which runs in the $15–17B range annually and is expensed through the income statement. Maintenance capex is likely in the $1.5–2B range; growth capex has been elevated in recent years as Merck builds manufacturing capacity for Winrevair (sotatercept) and next-generation pipeline assets. The business does not require continuous reinvestment in physical assets to maintain its competitive position — durable advantage comes from patents, regulatory approvals, and physician relationships.

**Revenue Quality and Concentration Risk:** Merck's revenue quality is mixed. On the positive side, Keytruda is deeply embedded in oncology treatment protocols across 40+ indications globally, making it highly recurring and resistant to switching. The drug represents repeat-purchase institutional demand (hospitals, oncology clinics) rather than discretionary consumer spending. Revenue has grown at double-digit rates for years. On the negative side, product concentration is extreme: Keytruda alone accounted for ~$31.7B of ~$65B total 2025 revenue (~49%), and this share has been growing. Gardasil's collapse in 2025 (from $8.6B in 2024 to $5.2B in 2025, a $3.4B decline) underscores how a single market disruption (Chinese domestic vaccine competition) can materially impair a secondary franchise. The pipeline is generating new revenues — Winrevair launched in 2024 and generated ~$1.4B in 2025, and Capvaxive contributed ~$759M — but neither yet approaches the scale needed to offset Keytruda dependency.

**Operating Leverage:** Merck has demonstrated meaningful operating leverage. As revenue grew from ~$59B (2023) to ~$64B (2024), gross margins expanded, EBITDA grew to ~$24.7B in 2024 and ~$27.0B on a TTM basis through 2025. This indicates that incremental Keytruda revenue flows through at very high margins, given that the drug is already approved, manufactured, and marketed at scale. The 2026 outlook is more cautious: management offered modest guidance below Wall Street expectations, citing the looming Keytruda biosimilar entry in the U.S. (mid-2028) and near-term patent expirations on other products, which will require the pipeline to fill the gap.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Keytruda dominance generates $30B+ in annual cash flows through mid-2028, funding a deep pipeline (including MK-1084, subcutaneous Keytruda, Winrevair, and numerous oncology combinations) that could sustain the franchise post-biosimilar entry.
- **Bear case:** Keytruda's U.S. biosimilar entry in 2028 creates a fiscal cliff — Merck faces a potential $10–15B revenue disruption over 2028–2030 just as legacy products (Januvia, Janumet) already off patent, and no single pipeline asset yet de-risked enough to replace the lost cash flow.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Core profitability metrics and FCF generation are clearly high-quality; the uncertainty is concentrated in the post-2027 revenue trajectory, where we are dependent on pipeline execution.

## Red Flags

- Keytruda constitutes ~49% of total revenue; U.S. patent expiry and biosimilar entry expected mid-2028 represents a concentrated cliff risk
- Gardasil China franchise collapsed in 2025 (–39% revenue), demonstrating that geographic/regulatory risk can materialize rapidly at scale
- 2026 guidance came in below Wall Street expectations — management signaling near-term headwinds before pipeline fills the gap
- R&D spending (~$15–17B/year) is a large, opaque cash outflow; pipeline success rates are inherently uncertain
- Acquisition-driven intangible asset amortization masks true cash earnings for accounting purposes but owner earnings remain strong

## Score: 8 / 10

Merck earns a strong 8 due to exceptional ROIC (21–26%), expanding gross margins (~77%), robust FCF (~$13–18B), and an asset-light operating model — offset by extreme Keytruda concentration and a visible revenue cliff approaching in 2028.
