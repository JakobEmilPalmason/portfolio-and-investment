## Detailed Analysis

**What does Alphabet sell, and to whom?** Alphabet is primarily an advertising company built on top of consumer attention. Its core product — Google Search — is free to end users; advertisers pay per click or per impression to reach those users. The advertiser base spans millions of businesses globally, from Fortune 500 brands buying display inventory to the corner restaurant buying local search keywords. No single advertiser comes close to representing a meaningful percentage of revenue. Beyond advertising, Alphabet sells cloud computing services (Google Cloud Platform, Workspace) to enterprise and government customers, consumer hardware (Pixel phones, Nest), YouTube Premium subscriptions, and Google One storage subscriptions. Other Bets (Waymo, Wing, Verily) generate negligible revenue today.

**How does it actually make money?** Roughly 79% of 2025 revenue — approximately $318 billion — came from advertising: Google Search text ads (the largest piece), YouTube video ads, and Google Network (Display & Video 360, AdSense). The mechanism is an auction: advertisers bid for placement against keywords or audiences; Google collects a tax on every transaction. Full-year 2025 advertising revenue was approximately $264 billion for Search & other, $35.7 billion for YouTube ads, and ~$22 billion for Google Network. Google Cloud contributed approximately $43.2 billion for the full year ($17.7 billion in Q4 alone, up 48% YoY). Subscriptions, platforms, and devices — Google One, Pixel, Play Store — grew 17% in Q4, contributing a smaller but fast-growing ~$13 billion annually.

**Key drivers (2-4 variables).** The four variables that matter most are: (1) total global search query volume and advertiser CPCs (cost per click) — these two together essentially determine Search revenue; (2) YouTube watch time and video ad pricing, which determines YouTube ad revenue; (3) enterprise cloud spend and Google Cloud's win rate vs. AWS and Azure, which determines cloud trajectory; and (4) macroeconomic conditions, which directly compress or expand advertiser budgets. Of these, (1) is the existential variable given AI search threats.

**How predictable is the revenue?** Google Search advertising is highly recurring and has compounded at double digits for two decades, but it is not contractual. Enterprise cloud revenue (Workspace, GCP) is increasingly contractual and subscription-based — that backlog of $240 billion provides high forward visibility. Advertising revenue correlates with GDP and is cyclically sensitive; the 2022 slowdown demonstrated this when Search growth decelerated sharply. On balance, revenue is moderately predictable over a 2-3 year horizon but carries meaningful cyclical variance, and is facing a structural inflection risk from AI-native search interfaces.

**Who are the customers, and how concentrated are they?** Alphabet has two distinct customer classes. End users (billions of people globally using Search, YouTube, Chrome, Android, Gmail, Maps) are effectively free users whose attention is sold to advertisers. Advertisers — the paying customers — number in the millions globally. The top 100 advertisers likely represent less than 10% of total revenue, making customer concentration negligible. Enterprise cloud customers are individually larger but still diversified across thousands of enterprises and governments. This combination of enormous scale, no customer concentration, and sticky end-user behavior makes the customer base extremely robust.

**Can you explain the business in 2 minutes?** Google owns the front door of the internet — every time someone types a question, Google shows the answer and charges businesses to appear next to it. The more people use Google, the more advertisers pay. The more advertisers pay, the more Google invests in making Search, Maps, and YouTube better, which keeps users coming back. On top of that, Google built a cloud computing business renting its AI and infrastructure to enterprises, which is now growing faster than Amazon Web Services in percentage terms. The whole machine generates roughly $73 billion in free cash flow per year on $403 billion in revenue. The one real question is whether AI chat interfaces (ChatGPT, Perplexity) will erode Google's ownership of the search moment — and so far, the data shows query volumes still growing, though market share has slipped ~2 points in the past year.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Google Search query volumes continue growing even as AI competition intensifies, YouTube and Cloud compound at 15%+ annually, and $175-185 billion in 2026 AI capex solidifies Google's position as a top-tier AI platform with $240 billion cloud backlog underpinning revenue visibility.
- **Bear case:** AI-native search interfaces (ChatGPT, Perplexity) structurally reduce search query volume or advertiser CPCs over 3-5 years, permanently impairing the ~$200 billion Search advertising business that funds everything else.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is straightforward, the financials are thoroughly disclosed, and the key risk (AI disruption to Search) is well-defined and observable.

## Red Flags

- Advertising remains ~79% of total revenue — significant cyclical and structural concentration risk.
- Google's global search market share declined ~2 percentage points in the past year — the most significant annual drop in a decade — a trend worth monitoring closely.
- Planned 2026 capex of $175-185 billion (roughly double 2025's $91.4 billion) is massive; if cloud or AI revenue growth disappoints, this creates severe free cash flow compression.
- Regulatory risk is elevated globally: EU Digital Markets Act enforcement, U.S. DOJ antitrust actions on Search and ad tech are ongoing.

## Score: 8 / 10

Alphabet's business is straightforward to understand — attention aggregation monetized through advertising, supplemented by fast-growing cloud — with a clear set of key drivers, minimal customer concentration, and well-disclosed financials; the sole reason for not scoring higher is the genuine structural uncertainty around AI disruption to the Search model.
