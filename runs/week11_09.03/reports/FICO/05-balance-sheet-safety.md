## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Capital Structure.** As of September 30, 2025 (fiscal year-end), FICO had total debt of $3.075B, comprised of long-term debt of $2.656B and short-term/current maturities of $399.5M. Cash and equivalents stood at $134.1M, giving a net debt position of approximately $2.94B. This level of leverage is the direct consequence of an aggressive, multi-year capital return program: FICO has repurchased $5.2B in shares over FY2021–FY2025, funded by a combination of operating cash flows and debt issuance. The company has been willing to operate with negative book equity — total shareholders' equity was ($1.75B) at year-end FY2025 — because management views the business's earnings power as the relevant solvency metric, not GAAP book value. Total liabilities of $3.61B against total assets of $1.87B yields a technically insolvent-looking balance sheet on a liquidation basis; however, assets are predominantly intangible (goodwill of $783M) and the earnings engine is what matters.

**Interest Coverage.** Interest expense was $146.2M in FY2025, up from $133.6M in FY2024 and $105.6M in FY2023 — a trend reflecting increasing debt load. EBIT of $925M in FY2025 yields an interest coverage ratio of approximately 6.3x on an EBIT basis. Gurufocus calculates this at 6.8x using slightly different figures. EBITDA coverage (EBITDA $940M / interest $146M) is approximately 6.4x. These ratios are adequate by investment-grade standards — they are above the typical 3–4x threshold for concern — but they are not comfortably conservative. The trajectory matters: interest expense has grown 38% over two years as the company has added debt to fund buybacks. If interest rates on refinanced debt stay elevated (the new 2034 notes carry 6.250% vs. the retired 5.25% 2026 notes), the interest burden will continue to rise modestly.

**Liquidity Position.** The current ratio of 0.83 and quick ratio of 0.78 reflect the fact that FICO operates with a structurally thin current asset base. The company holds minimal cash ($134M), carries accounts receivable (~$200M range), and has short-term debt maturities of ~$399M in FY2025. This would be alarming for a capital-intensive or cyclically sensitive business, but FICO generates $770M+ in annual FCF with high predictability. The business does not require large working capital buffers because it has no inventory, no significant receivables lag in its Scores business (financial institutions pay promptly), and no meaningful prepaid supply chain. The revolving credit facility provides an additional liquidity backstop, though the size post-March 2026 refinancing has not been fully disclosed in available sources.

**Refinancing Risk and Debt Maturity Profile.** The most significant near-term balance sheet event was the retirement of $400M in 5.25% senior notes due 2026. On March 11, 2026 — one day before this report's date — FICO priced a $1.0B offering of 6.250% unsecured senior notes due 2034. Proceeds will retire the 2026 notes and repay revolving credit facility borrowings. This transaction substantially extends the maturity profile. The other major tranche is $1.5B in 6.000% senior notes due 2033, issued May 2025. Combined, FICO's post-March 2026 fixed-rate debt stack looks approximately like: $1.5B due 2033, $1.0B due 2034, with the revolving credit facility providing flexible shorter-term capacity. No major maturities before 2033 — this is a material improvement in refinancing risk. The remaining risk is the floating-rate revolving facility, the drawn balance of which fluctuates with buyback activity.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** No significant off-balance-sheet financing structures are identified in available sources. FICO's business model does not require operating lease-heavy real estate (the company is largely remote-work compatible), no joint venture funding obligations, and no take-or-pay commodity contracts. Pension and post-retirement obligations are minimal given the company's relatively young employee base concentrated in knowledge workers. Stock-based compensation ($86M range annually) is a dilution and cost-of-capital consideration but not a balance sheet solvency item.

**Stress Test.** In a severe recession scenario — assume a 30% decline in Scores revenue (comparable to the 2007–2009 mortgage origination collapse), with Software flat — revenue would fall from $1.99B to approximately $1.64B. Applying current cost structure (operating costs of ~$1.07B, relatively fixed), operating income would compress from $925M to approximately $570M. Interest coverage would fall to approximately 3.9x ($570M / $146M), still above distress thresholds. FCF would decline from ~$770M to perhaps $450–500M — still well above annual interest obligations of $180–190M (post refinancing). The company would remain solvent and cash flow positive even in a severe credit origination downturn. The primary risk is not insolvency but rather a forced halt to buybacks, which would disappoint the market but not impair the business.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Post-March 2026 refinancing eliminates all near-term maturities; FCF of $770M+ more than covers interest obligations, and the debt load is purposeful leverage on a high-ROIC asset, not a distress signal.
- **Bear case:** Debt-to-EBITDA of 3.3x with rising interest costs and a structurally thin liquidity buffer leaves limited cushion if Scores revenue contracts sharply in a mortgage downturn, and the company cannot easily deleverage while maintaining its buyback program.
- **Confidence:** High — balance sheet figures are well-sourced from filings and financial data providers; the March 2026 refinancing is confirmed by BusinessWire press release.

## Red Flags

- Interest expense has grown 38% in two years ($106M → $146M); further debt-funded buybacks will continue this trend
- Current ratio of 0.83 — any short-term cash need (e.g., large acquisition, unexpected litigation) would require revolver drawdown or new issuance
- Negative book equity renders traditional leverage ratios (D/E) meaningless; debt-to-EBITDA of 3.3x is the correct anchor and it is elevated for a pure software/data company
- New 2034 notes at 6.250% are more expensive than the 5.25% 2026 notes being retired — refinancing adds ~$10M/year in incremental interest cost on the $400M tranche

## Score: 6 / 10

The balance sheet carries real but manageable leverage (net debt ~$2.94B, D/EBITDA 3.3x, interest coverage 6.3x) with the March 2026 refinancing materially reducing near-term maturity risk; the FCF engine provides ample debt service cushion, but the thin liquidity ratios, rising interest burden, and absence of a safety-first capital structure prevent a higher score.
