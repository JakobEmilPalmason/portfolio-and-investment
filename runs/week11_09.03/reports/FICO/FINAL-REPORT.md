# Investment Analysis: FICO — Fair Isaac Corporation

**Date:** 2026-03-13
**Verdict:** Watch

> Fair Isaac Corporation is the owner of the FICO Score standard — effectively a toll-booth on US consumer credit with near-monopoly pricing power, 88% segment operating margins, and $770M in annual free cash flow on minimal capital investment. The opportunity and the concern are the same event: in 2025, FHFA's "lender choice" model formally ended the GSE-mandated monopoly in mortgage originations, and the credit bureaus (who jointly own competitor VantageScore) immediately began offering it at $0.99/score as a subsidized alternative to FICO's multi-dollar fees. At $1,285/share after a 9% single-day drop, the business is exceptional but the stock is priced for base-case execution with no margin of safety against a genuine and escalating competitive challenge — the right posture is watchlist at current prices with a defined entry point near $950–1,050.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: FICO operates a near-monopoly credit scoring toll-booth (59% of revenue, ~88% operating margins) charging per-inquiry royalties on every US loan origination, plus a transitioning enterprise decision-management software segment (~41% of revenue) with $263.6M Platform ARR growing 16%.
2. **Moat**: The regulatory-enforced GSE monopoly that was FICO's deepest protection is now formally cracked — FHFA's July 2025 lender choice model allows VantageScore 4.0 as a mortgage alternative, and the bureaus are weaponizing it at $0.99/score to seed lender adoption against FICO's multi-dollar fees.
3. **Economics**: ROIC of ~48–54%, gross margins of 82%, FCF of $770M on just $8.9M capex, and 118% FCF-to-net-income conversion confirm an extraordinary royalty-like business that drops nearly every incremental Scores dollar to the bottom line.
4. **Management**: CEO Lansing has reduced shares outstanding 48% since 2010 via buybacks funded by $739M FY2025 FCF, but simultaneously sold ~$32M in personal shares over six months while the board authorized a new $1.5B corporate repurchase program during a live competitive inflection — an optics conflict that warrants scrutiny.
5. **Debt risk**: Net debt of ~$2.94B (3.3x EBITDA) with interest coverage of 6.3x is manageable given $770M FCF, and the March 2026 $1B refinancing at 6.25% extends all major maturities to 2033–2034, but a 30% Scores revenue shock would compress coverage to ~3.9x and force a halt to buybacks.
6. **Price**: At ~$1,285 post-9% drop, FICO trades at 43x trailing P/E and ~41x P/FCF — base-case intrinsic value is $1,350–1,500 (5–17% upside) while the bear case of coordinated VantageScore adoption implies $650–750 (42–49% downside), leaving probability-weighted fair value near or slightly below the current price.
7. **Margin of safety**: There is no margin of safety at $1,285 — the stock requires 13–14% FCF CAGR for 10 years to justify current price, the upside/downside ratio is at least 1:3 in a realistic bear scenario, and a genuine entry with 25–30% discount to base-case intrinsic value would require the stock to fall to ~$950–1,050.
8. **Thesis breaker**: If VantageScore 4.0 captures more than 25–30% of conforming mortgage origination volume within three years — enabled by the $0.99 bureau pricing and FHFA lender choice — FICO will be forced into structural Scores fee rollbacks that permanently impair the pricing model driving 60% of revenue and the majority of FCF.

## Key Risks

1. **VantageScore pricing war (highest likelihood, highest near-term impact)**: The three major credit bureaus — who both supply FICO's infrastructure and jointly own VantageScore — are pricing the competing score at $0.99 for mortgage lenders, in some cases free when bundled with a FICO purchase. This is structurally predatory from a counterparty with a direct conflict of interest. Even without volume loss, this creates a ceiling on FICO's continued per-unit price increases and could force rollbacks. Mortgage originations represent 55% of B2B Scores revenue, and scores pricing has risen 1,600%+ since 2022 — generating exactly the political and economic motivation for lenders to switch.

2. **FHFA regulatory deregulation (structural, medium-term)**: The July 2025 lender choice model formally ends FICO's de facto government-backed monopoly in conforming mortgage underwriting. The November 2025 Fannie Mae decision to eliminate minimum FICO score requirements goes further, potentially reducing mandatory score pulls per origination. FICO's moat was never purely economic — it was regulatory-reinforced. That reinforcement is being systematically dismantled. This is a 3–7 year structural shift, not a 12-month event.

3. **Leverage amplification in a revenue shock**: With $2.94B net debt, negative book equity, and interest expense growing 38% over two years, a sustained Scores revenue decline would quickly compress financial flexibility. A 30% revenue shock (plausible in a combined mortgage downturn and VantageScore market share gain) reduces FCF to ~$480M and interest coverage to ~3.9x — above distress thresholds but too tight to sustain the buyback program. This means leverage amplifies the equity downside disproportionately.

4. **Software transition risk**: FICO Platform ARR of $263.6M is only 32% of total software ARR, and total software ARR grew just 4% in FY2025. The SaaS transition is mid-stream — legacy revenue is declining while platform revenue grows, with no certainty the offset completes cleanly. If the Scores segment faces headwinds at the same time the software transition stalls, FICO loses its two-segment thesis simultaneously.

5. **Political and regulatory tail risk**: FICO's 1,600%+ price increases since 2022 have generated bipartisan political attention. The Mortgage Bankers Association has publicly framed FICO fees as "a tax on homeownership." This creates non-trivial risk of legislative fee caps, CFPB enforcement action, or further FHFA policy changes. Any one of these would be negative; a combination would be severely negative.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - Stock declines to $950–1,050, providing genuine 25–30% discount to base-case intrinsic value ($1,350–1,500) and a reasonable margin of safety against competitive headwinds
  - FICO 10T is formally adopted by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac as a required or preferred standard for conforming mortgages, re-establishing regulatory embedding with an upgraded product
  - Verifiable VantageScore adoption data (from FHFA quarterly reporting) shows minimal lender switching after 12 months of "lender choice" availability, demonstrating the moat's practical durability

- **Sell triggers**:
  - Scores revenue growth turns negative for two consecutive quarters, confirming that VantageScore market share gains are outpacing FICO's pricing power
  - FHFA or Fannie Mae formally expands score-agnostic underwriting across all agency lending categories, rendering the per-inquiry score pull structurally optional
  - VantageScore captures verifiably more than 25–30% of conforming mortgage origination volume within three years of FHFA lender choice implementation

# Circle of Competence — FICO

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** FICO FY2025 Annual Report (10-K filed 11/07/2025), FICO Q4 2025 earnings release, PitchGrade business model analysis, DCFModeling financial health review, web searches on FICO revenue segments and business model

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | FICO operates two distinct businesses — a near-monopoly score franchise (60% of revenue, 88% operating margins) and a transitioning enterprise software segment (40% of revenue, lower margins) — with very different economics | 5 |
| 2 | The Scores segment is entirely B2B: lenders pay per-inquiry fees to pull FICO scores from credit bureaus; pricing power has driven 27% revenue growth in FY2025 with essentially no volume investment required | 5 |
| 3 | Financial services represents 92% of total revenue and the Americas 87%, making FICO highly concentrated by customer type and geography | 4 |
| 4 | The Software segment is in active SaaS transition: FICO Platform ARR grew 16% to $263.6M (35% of total software ARR) while non-platform legacy ARR declined 2%; net software ARR growth was only 4% | 3 |
| 5 | B2C scores (myFICO.com) grew only 8% versus B2B's 29%, confirming that the lucrative business is institutional not consumer | 3 |
| 6 | FY2025 revenue of $1.991B (+16% YoY), record free cash flow of $739M (+22%), and FY2026 guidance of $2.35B (+18%) confirm a business with durable and accelerating cash generation | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

FICO sells two fundamentally different things to different buyers under one brand. The first — and overwhelmingly more powerful — business is the FICO Score franchise. Lenders across the United States (and increasingly internationally) pay per-inquiry fees each time they pull a FICO score from one of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) to evaluate a consumer's creditworthiness. FICO does not own the credit data — the bureaus do — but FICO owns the algorithm, the brand, and historically the regulatory mandate. This is a toll-booth model: every mortgage application, auto loan, credit card approval, and personal loan originates a score query, and FICO collects a royalty. B2B scores revenue was $1.019B in FY2025, growing 29%, with mortgage originations representing 55% of that B2B revenue.

The second business is enterprise analytics software — decision management platforms that financial institutions, insurers, telecoms, and retailers use to automate decisions (fraud detection, customer onboarding, collections, risk modeling). FICO Platform is the cloud-native SaaS successor to legacy on-premise products. Platform ARR was $263.6M at September 30, 2025, growing 16% YoY, but total software ARR growth is only 4% because non-platform legacy revenue is declining. The software segment generated ~$822M in FY2025 revenue at lower operating margins than Scores. The transition from one-time license deals to recurring SaaS is strategically correct but creates near-term revenue drag.

The revenue model's predictability differs sharply between segments. Software ARR is highly predictable — once installed in a bank's decision infrastructure, churn is extremely low. The Scores segment is more variable because it is tied to credit origination volume (mortgage refinancing activity, consumer credit demand). However, FICO has offset volume sensitivity with aggressive per-unit price increases — the company has reportedly raised mortgage score pricing by more than 1,600% since 2022, which is the primary driver of the 27-29% Scores growth despite a soft mortgage origination environment.

Customer concentration risk is meaningful. Financial services represents 92% of total revenue. Within Scores, the top revenue driver is mortgage originations, and within mortgage, FICO is dependent on the Federal Housing Finance Agency's (FHFA) regulatory framework for Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. This creates a single policy-risk nexus: any FHFA rule change that meaningfully shifts volume to VantageScore would disproportionately impact Scores segment revenue. The Americas account for 87% of revenue, limiting international diversification.

The business can be explained in two minutes: FICO owns the credit score standard that every major lender in the United States uses to determine whether to approve a loan. Banks pay a small fee per score pull. Because regulators mandated FICO scores for decades, and because every lender underwrites to the same standard, there is an enormous switching cost embedded in the entire financial infrastructure — retooling risk models, retraining underwriters, and managing second-order network effects with other lenders is prohibitively expensive. FICO also sells the software platforms that automate lending decisions, a slower-growth but sticky recurring revenue business.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A regulatory toll-booth on US credit with 88% operating margins, pricing power that has grown per-unit fees by over 1,600% since 2022, and a software segment transitioning to durable SaaS ARR — all compounding into record free cash flow.
- **Bear case:** FHFA's 2025 lender choice model allows VantageScore 4.0 as a mortgage alternative, and bureaus are offering it at $0.99 vs. FICO's much higher price, threatening the pricing monopoly that drives 60% of revenue.
- **Confidence:** High — FICO's two-segment structure, revenue model, and key drivers are well-documented in public filings and straightforward to understand.

## Red Flags

- 92% revenue concentration in financial services; any credit cycle downturn or regulatory pivot hits the entire business simultaneously
- Mortgage originations represent 55% of B2B Scores revenue — a highly cyclical and interest-rate-sensitive sub-segment
- Software SaaS transition is mid-stream; legacy ARR declining while Platform ARR grows, with no certainty the offset completes cleanly
- FICO has no dividend; all returns are via buybacks, making it entirely dependent on sustained price appreciation for shareholder return

## Score: 8 / 10

The business model is exceptionally clear and understandable — a dominant IP-based toll-booth on US credit with a secondary software franchise — with the only complexity being the transition-era software segment and the emerging competitive threat to score pricing.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — FICO

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** FICO FY2025 10-K and Annual Report, MarketMinute/FinancialContent reporting on VantageScore pricing war (March 12, 2026), FHFA credit scores policy page, National Mortgage News, ABA Banking Journal on FHFA lender choice transition, Housing Wire on VantageScore pricing, FICO newsroom on FICO Score 10T adoption, BeyondSPX moat analysis

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | FICO's primary moat is a regulatory-embedded network standard: for decades, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac required Classic FICO scores for conforming mortgage underwriting, creating a mandated monopoly backed by the US housing finance system | 5 |
| 2 | The FHFA adopted a "lender choice" model in late 2025, formally allowing VantageScore 4.0 as an alternative — the single biggest structural threat to FICO's moat in the company's history | 5 |
| 3 | The bureaus are pricing VantageScore 4.0 at $0.99/score for mortgage lenders (vs. FICO's substantially higher price), with all three bureaus offering it free when bundled with a FICO score purchase — a direct predatory pricing assault on FICO's B2B Scores revenue | 5 |
| 4 | FICO Score 10T is being positioned as a next-generation answer: 40+ lenders have joined the adopter program covering $264B in annualized originations, and FICO reached a December 2025 agreement with FHFA to release historical 10T data enabling GSE adoption | 4 |
| 5 | The software segment's moat is primarily switching-cost based — decision management infrastructure embedded in bank workflows — but this is a common enterprise software moat, not FICO's distinctive advantage | 3 |
| 6 | Despite the competitive threat, the Scores segment posted 88% operating margins and 27% revenue growth in FY2025, suggesting the moat remains largely intact in the near term | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

FICO's Scores segment moat is best described as a regulatory-enforced network standard that became self-reinforcing through decades of lender habituation. From the early 2000s through 2025, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac — which guarantee roughly 70% of US mortgage originations — required Classic FICO scores for conforming loan underwriting. This mandate did not emerge from FICO's lobbying alone; it reflected the genuine network benefit of a shared credit standard. When every lender underwrites to the same scale, secondary markets can price risk efficiently, investors can compare loan pools, and regulators can supervise with consistent metrics. FICO became the shared language of US credit risk.

This regulatory embedding created three compounding layers of moat. First, switching costs are enormous: a lender that transitions from FICO to VantageScore must remodel its entire risk infrastructure, recalibrate loss rate forecasts, and potentially requalify with counterparties and investors who are conditioned to FICO thresholds. Second, network effects reinforce the standard — lenders, servicers, rating agencies, and capital markets participants all "speak FICO," so any individual defection imposes non-trivial coordination costs. Third, brand recognition with consumers (myFICO, credit monitoring) creates a demand-side pull that ensures consumers also frame their creditworthiness in FICO terms.

The moat is now under its most serious regulatory challenge in decades. In late 2025, the FHFA officially adopted a lender choice model allowing VantageScore 4.0 alongside Classic FICO for conforming mortgage loans. This removes the mandatory regulatory backstop that made switching unthinkable. The bureaus — who jointly own VantageScore — immediately weaponized this opening with a predatory pricing strategy: VantageScore 4.0 is priced at $0.99/score by both Experian and TransUnion, and $1.00 by Equifax, compared to FICO's mortgage score pricing that the Mortgage Bankers Association has described as "a tax on homeownership." Critically, bureaus are offering VantageScore 4.0 for free to any lender who purchases a FICO score bundle — effectively a zero-marginal-cost alternative designed to seed adoption and build a usage track record.

FICO's response is FICO Score 10T — a next-generation model using trended data that demonstrably outperforms Classic FICO and VantageScore 4.0. More than 40 lenders with $264B in annualized originations have signed onto the 10T adoption program. In December 2025, FICO struck a deal with FHFA to release historical 10T datasets, which had been the final barrier to GSE adoption. This positions 10T as FICO's moat-reinforcing move: if the GSEs formally adopt FICO 10T alongside or instead of VantageScore, FICO retains regulatory embedding with an upgraded product. This is the bull case within the competitive threat: FICO is trying to obsolete Classic FICO before VantageScore can claim the Classic FICO vacancy.

The weakest point of FICO's moat is the pricing itself. The 1,600%+ price increase since 2022 is unusual behavior for a regulated quasi-utility, and it has created political motivation for change. The Mortgage Bankers Association's framing of FICO fees as a "tax on homeownership" aligns with FHFA director Bill Pulte's public support for scoring competition. If FICO's pricing is perceived as extractive, regulatory bodies have both the motivation and the mechanism (lender choice, mandatory VantageScore acceptance) to discipline it. FICO's pricing power is real, but it has been exercised so aggressively that it created its own most credible competitive threat.

Whether the advantage resets depends on how quickly lenders actually switch. Adopting VantageScore 4.0 at $0.99 requires lenders to run dual-score workflows during transition, validate their models, update investor agreements, and manage basis risk. This takes time — likely 2-5 years for material volume shift. FICO's 10T adoption momentum suggests it may retain the high end of the mortgage market. The mid-market (smaller lenders, correspondent channels) is where VantageScore's price advantage is most dangerous.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** FICO 10T supplants Classic FICO as the GSE standard and creates a new decade of regulatory embedding, while VantageScore's $0.99 price erodes margins only at the margin as lenders find switching operationally burdensome.
- **Bear case:** FHFA's lender choice model drives material volume to VantageScore within 3 years, compressing Scores segment pricing power and triggering a structural re-rating of FICO's moat and valuation multiple.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the moat is real and still largely intact, but March 12, 2026 is a pivotal day with FICO shares already down 9% on this precise concern; near-term trajectory is genuinely uncertain.

## Red Flags

- FHFA lender choice model is live as of late 2025 — the regulatory backstop that has been FICO's deepest protection is formally gone
- Bureau-backed VantageScore 4.0 at $0.99 is a structurally predatory price from counterparties (the bureaus) who both supply FICO's infrastructure and compete with FICO's product — a dangerous vertical conflict
- FICO raised prices 1,600%+ since 2022, creating political and economic motivation for lenders and regulators to support alternatives
- Mortgage originations are 55% of B2B Scores revenue — if this sub-segment migrates even partially to VantageScore, the impact is outsized
- VantageScore 4.0 has been independently validated by FHFA as comparable to Classic FICO for conforming mortgages

## Score: 7 / 10

FICO's moat was a 9-10 as recently as 2024 — a regulatory monopoly with extraordinary switching costs — but the FHFA lender choice mandate and bureau-sponsored $0.99 VantageScore pricing materially erode that rating; the moat is still wide and durable in the near term, but its long-term durability is now genuinely contested.

# Management & Capital Allocation — FICO

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** FICO 2025 Proxy Statement, FICO FY2025 Annual Report, FICO newsroom buyback announcement (February 25, 2026), Yahoo Finance/Gurufocus on $1.5B buyback program, Panabee executive compensation data, Quiver Quant CEO pay, SharesOutstandingHistory.com, SimplyWallSt management analysis, FinancialContent on buyback-driven stock surge

## Key Findings

## Detailed Analysis

William Lansing has been President and CEO since 2012, making him one of the longer-tenured CEOs among large-cap technology companies. The longevity matters: FICO's transformation from a diversified analytics business into a focused, two-segment cash machine with elite margins happened on his watch. When Lansing took over, FICO was a far more complex business with consulting revenues, diversified verticals, and lower margins. The pruning of non-core businesses, the deliberate shift toward recurring software revenue, and the aggressive monetization of the Scores franchise all reflect consistent strategic vision over more than a decade.

The capital allocation record is straightforward and investor-friendly in its logic, if aggressive in execution. FICO generates substantial free cash flow — $739M in FY2025 — and returns virtually all of it to shareholders through buybacks, supplemented by debt. There is no dividend. Since 2010, shares outstanding have fallen from approximately 46.5M to approximately 24M, a 48% reduction. This means that even if FICO's total earnings had grown only modestly, earnings per share would have compounded significantly from denominator reduction alone. In FY2025, GAAP EPS was $26.54, up 30% from FY2024, and a meaningful portion of that growth reflects buyback accretion rather than purely operational improvement.

The February 25, 2026 announcement of a new $1.5B buyback program is consistent with this pattern. The program replaced a $1.0B authorization approved in June 2025, indicating FICO exhausted $1.0B in buybacks in approximately 8 months. Notably, the announcement came in the context of a stock that had been under pressure from VantageScore concerns — and shares surged 13% in the two days following the announcement, suggesting management timed the disclosure deliberately to signal confidence and provide price support. This is sophisticated use of buyback authorization as a communication tool, not merely capital deployment.

Whether the buyback program is value-creating depends on price. FICO's shares have historically traded at high multiples — the stock traded above $2,000/share before recent pressure. Buying back stock at very high earnings multiples is only value-accretive if those multiples are sustained or if the earnings power being retired at those multiples is genuinely exceptional. The record ($739M FCF on ~24M shares = ~$30 FCF per share) supports the case that FICO's intrinsic value per share genuinely warrants premium pricing. However, repurchasing stock at 50x+ earnings when a regulatory threat to 60% of revenue is emerging introduces meaningful timing risk.

CEO compensation structure is heavily equity-linked — approximately 98% of Lansing's $36M total compensation was stock awards in FY2025. On one hand, this aligns his wealth with shareholder outcomes and ensures he participates in the same downside risk shareholders face. On the other hand, stock awards that vest on time rather than performance targets can dilute the disciplinary effect of equity compensation, particularly when the CEO can simultaneously sell $32M in shares over six months while the board approves new buyback programs. There is nothing illegal or unusual about this — it is standard executive equity management — but the juxtaposition of insider selling and corporate buybacks warrants scrutiny. Lansing's remaining stake of ~1.77% worth ~$617M is still a meaningful personal anchor.

Governance quality is adequate. The board has an independent chairman (Braden Kelly), is majority independent, and is a compact 8-member body. Annual director evaluations are conducted without individual attribution, which is standard practice. FICO does not publish a traditional Buffett-style shareholder letter — the annual report letter is functional rather than philosophically candid. Lansing communicates via earnings calls, which are generally direct about the FICO Platform transition progress, competitive environment, and pricing strategy. There is no evidence of earnings management or guidance manipulation; the company has beaten or met guidance consistently and provided FY2026 guidance of $2.35B revenue with characteristic confidence.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A long-tenured, equity-aligned CEO with a 14-year track record of disciplined buybacks, margin expansion, and strategic focus; the $1.5B buyback announced during competitive turbulence signals management confidence in the intrinsic value of the business.
- **Bear case:** Insider selling of $32M while the company simultaneously repurchases shares is a structural conflict; and buybacks at premium multiples during an emerging regulatory threat are a capital allocation bet that must prove out.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the capital allocation track record is factual and well-documented; the forward judgment on whether current buybacks are value-creative is the uncertainty.

## Red Flags

- CEO sold ~$32M in shares over the prior 6 months while the board authorized $1.5B in corporate buybacks — creates an optics conflict, though legally unrelated
- 98% variable compensation is equity-aligned, but the 368:1 CEO-to-median-employee ratio is an outlier and may invite governance scrutiny
- FICO uses debt to fund buybacks; while appropriate given the FCF generation, rising interest rates increase the cost of this leverage strategy
- No dividend and no stated acquisition strategy means the entire capital return story depends on sustained share price premium — if FICO's moat erodes and multiple compresses, the buyback strategy retroactively becomes value-destructive
- Lansing is 13+ years into the role; succession planning is not publicly disclosed

## Score: 7 / 10

Management has an excellent 14-year track record of capital allocation, strategic focus, and equity alignment, but the simultaneous insider selling and corporate buyback program during a competitive inflection point, combined with above-market pay, slightly reduces confidence that shareholder interests are perfectly prioritized.

# Business Economics — FICO

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** StockAnalysis.com income statement and cash flow data (FY2021–FY2025), Gurufocus ROIC data, Alpha Spread ROIC summary, DCFModeling financial health report, FICO Q4 2025 earnings call highlights (Yahoo Finance), FICO 2025 Annual Report (investors.fico.com), company press releases

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC of ~48–54% in FY2025, well above WACC of ~12%, reflects a genuine durable economic moat | 5 |
| 2 | Operating margin expanded from 38.4% (FY2021) to 46.5% (FY2025) on revenue growing from $1.32B to $1.99B — powerful operating leverage | 5 |
| 3 | Gross margin reached 82.2% in FY2025 (up from 74.8% in FY2021), driven by high-margin Scores segment scaling | 4 |
| 4 | Free cash flow of $769.9M in FY2025 on net income of $652M = FCF conversion of 118%, confirming high-quality earnings with minimal capex drag | 5 |
| 5 | Scores segment ($1.169B, 59% of revenue) carries ~88% segment margins and is structurally tied to US mortgage, auto, and card originations — a transactional toll structure | 4 |
| 6 | Software segment (~$822M, 41% of revenue) is in active platform transition; legacy products declining while FICO Platform ARR of $263.6M represents only 32% of total software ARR — transition risk remains | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**ROIC and Return Structure.** FICO's reported ROIC for FY2025 is approximately 48–54% depending on the calculation methodology, versus a WACC of approximately 12%. The gap of ~35–40 percentage points is extraordinary and indicative of a business with genuine pricing power and structural switching costs. The high ROIC is not merely an artifact of negative book equity from buybacks — it reflects the underlying economics of the Scores franchise: a near-zero marginal cost per incremental score query layered on top of fixed infrastructure. ROIC has improved materially from approximately 35% three years ago to 48% today, meaning the moat is widening, not narrowing. ROE is technically undefined (or infinitely negative) due to negative shareholder equity, but this is a mechanical artifact of the buyback program and conveys no information about the underlying business economics.

**Margin Structure.** Gross margin was 82.2% in FY2025, up from 74.8% in FY2021. The primary driver is revenue mix shift: the Scores segment, which requires almost no incremental delivery cost once infrastructure is in place, grew 27% to $1.169B in FY2025 and now represents ~59% of total revenue. Cost of revenue grew from $332M to $354M over four years — a 6.5% cumulative increase — while revenue grew 51%, demonstrating the near-fixed nature of the delivery cost base. Operating margin is 46.5% in FY2025, among the highest in enterprise software. R&D ($188M, 9.5% of revenue) and SG&A ($513M, 25.8% of revenue) are the dominant operating cost lines; both have grown in absolute terms but declined as a percentage of revenue.

**Free Cash Flow and Capital Intensity.** Capital expenditures were just $8.9M in both FY2024 and FY2025 — approximately 0.4% of revenue. Operating cash flow of $778.8M in FY2025 exceeded net income of $652M by $127M, reflecting strong non-cash charges (primarily stock-based compensation and amortization) and favorable working capital. **Portfolio Overlap.** FICO should be evaluated alongside existing holdings in financial data and analytics. A portfolio that already holds one or more of these names at meaningful weight has significant concentration in the "information monopoly" thesis. If that thesis faces structural pressure (as FICO's now does with Scores pricing), co-movement risk is high. FICO's specific risk — government-mandated use of its product being relaxed — is unique and not fully shared by MCO/SPGI/MSCI, making it somewhat complementary rather than fully duplicative.
