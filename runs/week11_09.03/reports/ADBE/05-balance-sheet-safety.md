## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels**

As of November 30, 2025 (fiscal year-end), Adobe carries $6.21B in long-term debt and $5.4B in cash and short-term investments, yielding a net debt position of approximately $0.8B. When measured against EBITDA (GAAP operating income of $8.71B plus D&A of approximately $600–700M, implying EBITDA near $9.3–9.4B), the gross Debt/EBITDA ratio is approximately 0.66x and net Debt/EBITDA is approximately 0.09x. Both figures are deeply conservative — the 2x "comfortable" threshold is not in sight. Total shareholder equity stands at $11.6B, giving a debt-to-equity ratio of approximately 53.5%, which sounds more elevated but is misleading because the denominator (equity) has been reduced by years of share buybacks. The operative measure — cash flow coverage — is exceptional.

**Debt Maturity Schedule**

Adobe managed its maturity profile actively in January–February 2025. In February 2025, $1.5B of previously outstanding senior notes matured and were fully repaid from existing cash. In January 2025, Adobe simultaneously issued $2.0B in new notes: $800M at a fixed rate due January 17, 2028; $700M due January 17, 2030; and $500M due January 17, 2035. The remaining pre-existing notes include tranches maturing in 2027 and beyond (the exact outstanding breakdown from prior issuances would require the full 10-K debt schedule). The January 2025 refinancing activity demonstrates that Adobe can access capital markets on favorable terms and has deliberately spread maturities across a decade. There is no single year where an unusually large maturity cliff creates refinancing pressure.

**Interest Coverage**

FY2025 interest expense was $263M. GAAP EBIT (operating income) was $8.71B. This yields an interest coverage ratio of approximately 33x — extraordinary and far above the 5x "comfortable" threshold. Even using a more conservative earnings figure (e.g., GAAP net income of $7.13B, which includes taxes), interest coverage remains extreme. The company generates enough operating income in roughly four days to cover its entire annual interest bill. Adobe earns material interest income on its cash balances, which further offsets gross interest expense.

**Liquidity**

Cash and equivalents/short-term investments of $5.4B at fiscal year-end (Nov 2025) provides a substantial cash buffer. In addition, Adobe maintains a $2.0B revolving credit facility (with $500M committed tranches noted in the Q3 10-Q) and a $3B commercial paper program. As of August 29, 2025, neither facility had any outstanding borrowings. Combined, Adobe has access to over $10B in liquidity (balance sheet cash plus undrawn credit lines), against annual interest obligations of only $263M. The current ratio is not a primary concern for a business generating $10B+ in annual operating cash flow with minimal seasonal working capital swings.

**Refinancing Risk**

Refinancing risk is negligible. Adobe is a single-A-rated issuer (S&P: A) with consistent investment-grade access to bond markets. The January 2025 issuance demonstrated its ability to issue at favorable spreads even in an elevated rate environment. With no commercial paper outstanding, no drawn revolving credit, and $5.4B in cash, there is no scenario in which Adobe would need to access capital markets on an emergency basis. The company could fund all debt maturities through the end of this decade from existing cash and a single quarter of free cash flow.

**Stress Test: Revenue Down 30% for Two Years**

Under a severe stress scenario — revenue declining 30% for two consecutive years — Adobe's revenue would drop from $23.8B to approximately $16.6B in year one and $11.6B in year two. Assuming proportional cost reductions and similar margin compression to 2012–2015 pre-subscription levels (operating margins falling to 20–25%), operating cash flow might drop to approximately $2.3–$2.9B in year one and $1.5–$2.0B in year two. Even in this severe scenario, Adobe would generate roughly $3.8–$4.9B in cumulative operating cash flow over two stressed years, against total debt of $6.2B and annual interest of $263M. Survival would not be in question. No equity issuance would be necessary. This scenario is also extremely implausible given that over 90% of Adobe's revenue is recurring subscription and a 30% revenue decline would require simultaneous mass cancellations across Creative Cloud, Document Cloud, and Experience Cloud.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Near-net-cash balance sheet, 33x interest coverage, no drawn credit facilities, $10B+ annual FCF — one of the most financially impregnable companies in the S&P 500.
- **Bear case:** Leverage increased meaningfully in 2022–2023 related to the attempted Figma acquisition (which was ultimately terminated); management has demonstrated willingness to lever up for large M&A, which remains the primary theoretical risk to balance sheet safety.
- **Confidence:** High — multiple independent sources confirm the balance sheet figures, and the Q1 FY2026 earnings release (March 12, 2026) provides current confirmation.

## Red Flags

- Management has shown appetite for large acquisitions (Figma at $20B, though terminated in Dec 2023 after regulatory block). If Adobe pursues another large deal, leverage could increase substantially.
- Debt increased from approximately $4.1B (pre-2022) to $6.2B (post-refinancing) partly to finance the Figma deal costs and partially in anticipation of M&A. The net debt figure has remained manageable, but M&A ambition warrants monitoring.
- None of these are near-term solvency or liquidity concerns.

## Score: 8 / 10

Adobe's balance sheet is strong but not a net-cash fortress (net debt of ~$0.8B vs. peers like Apple with net cash), and management has demonstrated M&A appetite that could temporarily stress leverage; however, with 33x interest coverage, $5.4B in cash, no drawn credit lines, and $10B in annual FCF, the company could survive virtually any realistic adverse scenario without raising external capital — a score of 8 (Strong) is warranted.
