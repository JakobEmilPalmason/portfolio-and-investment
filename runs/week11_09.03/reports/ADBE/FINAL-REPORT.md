# Investment Analysis: ADBE — Adobe Inc

**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Verdict:** Watch

> Adobe is one of the highest-quality software businesses ever built: a near-pure subscription model with $25.2B in ARR, 41% FCF margins, 89% gross margins, and switching costs embedded so deeply in professional workflows that competitive disruption would take years — not months — to impair cash flows materially. At ~$249 (~15x forward earnings, 11x P/FCF), the market is pricing Adobe as a stagnating business, yet fundamentals continue to compound at double digits. The Watch verdict reflects not doubt in the business, but the thin price margin of safety (~35% to bear case), an unresolved CEO transition with no named successor, and an AI disruption narrative that requires 12–24 more months of evidence before it can be dismissed — making this a high-quality name at an intriguing but not yet unambiguous price.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Adobe sells software subscriptions — primarily Creative Cloud (Photoshop, Premiere, Illustrator) and Experience Cloud — to creative professionals and enterprises who embed these tools so deeply into daily workflows that recurring revenue of $25.2B ARR and 96%+ subscription mix make the cash flow machine nearly automatic.

2. **Moat**: Switching costs are structural and multi-layered: professionals spend years building muscle memory, proprietary file formats (.psd, .ai, .pdf, .indd) create hard dependency on legacy work, and enterprise workflows are rebuilt around Adobe specifications — evidenced by 89% gross margins held stable for over five consecutive years and price increases in 2023 that produced no material subscriber exodus.

3. **Economics**: Adobe generated $9.85B in free cash flow on $23.77B in FY2025 revenue (41% FCF margin), achieved ROIC of ~33%, maintained 87–89% gross margins for five consecutive years, and requires only $179M in annual capex — placing it in the top tier of capital efficiency among all S&P 500 companies.

4. **Management**: Narayen's 18-year capital allocation track record is strong — a $25B buyback executed at well-below-peak valuations reduced share count ~7% in four years — but the abrupt CEO departure announced simultaneously with Q1 FY2026 earnings, with no successor named, is a material stewardship uncertainty at a critical AI strategy pivot.

5. **Debt risk**: Adobe carries net debt of only ~$0.8B against $10B+ in annual operating cash flow, maintains 33x interest coverage, holds $5.4B in cash with no drawn credit lines, and could retire all outstanding debt from approximately seven weeks of cash generation — the balance sheet is effectively impregnable in any plausible recession or industry downturn.

6. **Price**: At ~$249 (~15x forward earnings, 11x P/FCF on $9.85B FY2025 FCF), the market is pricing Adobe as a business with flat-to-declining owner earnings, implying zero benefit from 10.9% ARR growth, AI-first ARR tripling YoY, or any multiple normalization — conditions that require sustained fundamental stagnation to be fair value.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself provides the primary cushion: $26B in recurring ARR with high switching costs, 90%+ gross margins, and $10B annual FCF mean competitive erosion would take years to impair cash flows materially, but the price margin of safety is thin with a bear-case DCF of ~$160–$180 only 28–36% below the current ~$249 — this is not a Graham deep-value entry.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Creative Cloud ARR growth falls below 5% for two consecutive quarters alongside rising disclosed churn, as that combination would confirm that AI-native alternatives are structurally eroding the subscriber base rather than merely creating narrative uncertainty.

## Key Risks

1. **AI disruption to Creative Cloud (High likelihood, High impact):** Canva's 260M MAUs and AI-native generation tools (Midjourney, Runway) are demonstrably eroding the lower end of Adobe's addressable market. If this migration accelerates into the professional tier, Creative Cloud ARR growth — which drives ~70% of revenue — could decelerate structurally rather than cyclically. This risk has no near-term resolution; investors must live with it for 2–4 years.

2. **CEO transition vacuum (Medium likelihood, High impact):** Narayen's departure with no named successor and no timeline creates a genuine leadership and strategy-execution risk at the exact moment Adobe needs to execute its AI platform pivot. A poor successor choice or prolonged search could allow competitors to capture enterprise share during the transition. This is the most acute near-term risk.

3. **Price margin of safety is thin (Realized risk, Medium ongoing impact):** At ~$249, the bear-case DCF of $160–$180 represents ~35% downside. This is a real and measurable loss scenario, not a tail risk. The stock has already declined 41% from its 52-week high — additional deterioration in the AI narrative or leadership confidence could push prices toward the bear-case range.

4. **Firefly monetization opacity (Medium likelihood, Medium impact):** AI-first ARR "tripling YoY" is compelling directionally but the absolute base is undisclosed. If Firefly fails to generate $2B+ in ARR by FY2027, the AI augmentation thesis loses credibility and the stock risks further multiple compression independent of core business performance.

5. **Reduced segment transparency in FY2026 (Lower likelihood, Medium impact):** Management's consolidation of reportable segments eliminates public visibility into Digital Media vs. Experience Cloud performance — reducing the analyst community's ability to detect early deterioration in either segment. This opacity warrants monitoring at each earnings call.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - A high-credibility CEO successor is named and the transition is orderly, removing the leadership risk discount; combined with continued ARR growth above 10%, this would justify building to a core position.
  - AI-first ARR reaches disclosed scale of $2B+ (or Creative Cloud ARR growth re-accelerates above 12%), confirming Firefly is a genuine growth driver rather than a defensive hedge.
  - Price declines to $200 or below, where bear-case downside narrows to ~15–20% and probability-weighted upside becomes unambiguous.

- **Sell triggers**:
  - Creative Cloud ARR growth falls below 5% for two consecutive quarters with rising churn disclosures — confirms structural erosion, not narrative fear.
  - CEO successor announcement reveals poor strategic fit, or key product/AI leadership departs within 6 months of succession — thesis execution materially impaired.
  - Price reaches $450+ (approaching bull case) without fundamental improvement in AI metrics — take partial profits and reassess whether intrinsic value justifies the multiple.

**Date:** 2026-03-15

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Adobe sell, and to whom?**

Adobe sells software subscriptions to creative professionals, marketing teams, students, and enterprises. Its flagship suite, Creative Cloud, encompasses over 20 applications including Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere Pro, After Effects, InDesign, and Acrobat. These tools are used by individual designers, video editors, photographers, marketing agencies, media companies, and corporate creative departments. The Digital Experience segment (marketed as Adobe Experience Cloud) sells data, analytics, and marketing automation software to large enterprises — competing with Salesforce, Oracle, and SAP in the marketing technology stack. Adobe also generates a smaller revenue stream from its legacy Publishing & Advertising segment.

**How does it actually make money?**

Adobe's revenue model is almost entirely subscription-based. Customers pay monthly or annual fees for Creative Cloud (individual, team, or enterprise plans), Acrobat (Document Cloud), and Experience Cloud (enterprise SaaS). Subscription revenue accounts for roughly 96–97% of total revenue, making Adobe one of the most purely recurring-revenue software businesses at scale. A small portion of revenue comes from perpetual licenses (legacy) and services. The subscription model generates reliable, front-loaded cash flows — Adobe reported $2.96B in operating cash flow in Q1 FY2026 alone, up 19% YoY.

**Key revenue drivers**

The four variables that matter most are: (1) Digital Media net new ARR — the single most-watched metric, representing new subscriber adds and price increases; (2) Creative Cloud ARPU expansion through tier upsells and AI feature add-ons; (3) Experience Cloud enterprise contract values, which are driven by sales cycles with Fortune 500 companies; and (4) Acrobat/Document Cloud growth, which has been accelerating through the Acrobat AI Assistant. A fifth emerging driver is AI-first ARR (Firefly-driven), which crossed $250M in FY2025 and tripled YoY in Q1 FY2026.

**Revenue predictability**

Revenue predictability is very high. The RPO of $22.2B as of Q1 FY2026 (up 13% YoY) provides more than two years of revenue visibility at current run rates. Churn rates are low due to deeply embedded workflows. Annual subscription contracts lock in revenue, and enterprise agreements are often multi-year. The business has produced consistent double-digit percentage revenue growth for over a decade. FY2026 guidance midpoint of $26.0B represents ~9% growth on a now-large base, which management has historically met or exceeded.

**Customer concentration**

Adobe has no material customer concentration. Its Digital Media subscriber base numbers in the tens of millions of individual and team users globally. The Experience Cloud customer base is large enterprises, but even there, no single client represents a meaningful percentage of revenue. This diversification significantly reduces revenue risk.

**Can you explain the business in 2 minutes?**

Adobe makes the software that the world's creative and marketing professionals use to do their jobs. If you design a logo, edit a video, build a website, or run a digital advertising campaign, you almost certainly use Adobe software. You pay a monthly subscription — roughly $55/month for an individual Creative Cloud plan — and Adobe recognizes that revenue ratably. The company also sells analytics and marketing automation tools to large corporations. Adobe's revenue is predictable, grows consistently, and generates extraordinary cash flows due to near-zero marginal cost of software delivery. The main questions for investors today are: (1) Can AI-native competitors erode their subscriber base? and (2) Is the AI opportunity big enough to sustain double-digit growth?

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A near-pure subscription model with $22B in RPO, 12% top-line growth, and $250M+ AI-first ARR growing 3x YoY makes the revenue trajectory clear and durable.
- **Bear case:** Canva's 260 million MAUs and AI-native tools are commoditizing the lower end of the market, which could constrain new subscriber growth and limit ARPU expansion.
- **Confidence:** High — Adobe's business model is one of the most legible in large-cap software; the subscription mechanics, segment structure, and key drivers are all well-disclosed and straightforward to analyze.

## Red Flags

- Management is consolidating its three reportable segments into one in FY2026, which will reduce external visibility into segment-level performance.
- Growth rate guidance (~9% for FY2026) is decelerating from FY2025's ~11% and FY2024's ~11%, though still healthy at this revenue scale.

## Score: 9 / 10

Adobe is among the most understandable software businesses at scale: a subscription model with clearly defined segments, well-disclosed ARR metrics, and revenue drivers that require no specialized knowledge to track.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ADBE

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Data Sources:** Web search results (March 2026); Alpha Spread moat analysis; GuruFocus moat score and financial data; Futurum Group Q4 FY2025 analysis; ainvest.com competitive analysis; simplywall.st narrative analysis; MacroTrends gross margin history; training knowledge on creative software competitive dynamics. Financial figures from Adobe's publicly reported results through Q1 FY2026.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Adobe benefits from multiple reinforcing moat sources simultaneously: switching costs (embedded workflows and proprietary file formats), brand/trust (industry-standard status), and partial network effects (Creative Cloud libraries, shared assets, collaboration). | 5 |
| 2 | Gross margins of ~89% are near-best-in-class for enterprise software and have been stable for years, providing strong numerical evidence of pricing power. | 5 |
| 3 | AI-native competitors (Canva, Figma, and generative AI tools) are credibly eroding Adobe's hold on the lower end of the market — 260 million Canva MAUs is not a rounding error. | 4 |
| 4 | Adobe's file format dominance (.psd, .ai, .pdf, .indd) creates hard dependency: converting away from Adobe means years of legacy files become harder to edit. PDF alone is a global standard with no realistic replacement. | 4 |
| 5 | Adobe's enterprise moat is stronger and more durable than its consumer moat — large enterprise contracts with deeply integrated Experience Cloud workflows are not easily ripped out. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Moat type and sources**

Adobe's moat is primarily switching costs, reinforced by intangible assets (brand and file format standards). The switching cost argument is multi-layered: (1) professionals invest years learning Adobe's tools and build muscle memory that does not transfer to alternatives; (2) files saved in .psd, .ai, .indd, or .pdf formats may technically open in other software but lose fidelity, layers, and editability; (3) enterprise workflows — from print production pipelines to broadcast post-production — are built around Adobe specifications; (4) the PDF standard, while open, is synonymous with Adobe Acrobat in enterprise settings. These switching costs are real and measurable: churn rates are low, and customer lifetimes are long.

**Moat durability over 10 years**

The enterprise and professional tier moat is likely durable over a 10-year horizon. Enterprises that have embedded Adobe Experience Cloud, Adobe Sign, or fully integrated Creative Cloud into their production workflows face meaningful disruption cost to switch. The PDF standard is entrenched globally and is not under threat. For professionals who earn their living with Photoshop or Premiere Pro, the productivity loss of switching to an inferior or different tool is a real economic cost.

The consumer and prosumer tier is under more genuine pressure. Canva has demonstrated that for a wide range of use cases — social media graphics, presentations, marketing collateral — a browser-based, template-driven tool is good enough. Figma (UI/UX design) has already become the default for product design teams. These are real moat erosions at the margin, not hypothetical risks.

**Moat evidence in the numbers**

Gross margin of ~89% in FY2025 is the most powerful evidence of moat. A software business with commoditized offerings cannot sustain 89% gross margins and 47% non-GAAP operating margins over decades. Adobe has raised Creative Cloud prices multiple times (most recently in 2023) with no material subscriber exodus, which is a direct test of pricing power. Return on equity of ~61% reflects both the high-quality business economics and the capital-light model. The RPO of $22.2B confirms that customers are willing to commit future cash flows for Adobe's services.

**Where the moat is weakest**

The moat is weakest in the "prosumer" and entry-level creative segments. Users who need basic image editing, design for social media, or simple document creation have an increasingly good set of alternatives. Canva's 260 million monthly active users (compared to Adobe's roughly 30-35 million paying Creative Cloud subscribers) illustrates the scale of the opportunity being contested. Additionally, AI-native image generation tools (Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion) challenge the value proposition of Photoshop and Illustrator for users who previously needed those tools only for content generation, not professional editing. Adobe's Figma acquisition was blocked on antitrust grounds in 2023, leaving that competitive threat unaddressed.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?**

This is the central AI question. Adobe's moat is workflow-and-format-based, not feature-based — which means it is more durable than a product-cycle moat. However, if a paradigm shift occurs where the dominant creative workflow moves from "open application, edit file, save" to "prompt an AI model, receive output, ship," then Adobe's workflow embedment becomes less relevant. Adobe is aggressively integrating Firefly into its core products, and Firefly is a genuine technical asset (enterprise-safe, trained on licensed content). The outcome is not decided: Adobe could successfully transition its platform, or it could see the margin of the moat narrow. Current evidence (AI-first ARR growing 3x) suggests transition is progressing, but the story is still being written.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Decade-long switching costs embedded in global professional workflows, 89% gross margins, and Firefly's early monetization traction suggest the moat is intact and adapting to the AI era.
- **Bear case:** Canva's massive consumer base and Figma's UI/UX dominance demonstrate that Adobe's moat has a real ceiling at the lower end of the market, and AI generation tools could further compress the addressable premium tier.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the professional and enterprise moat evidence is strong and well-documented; the consumer/prosumer competitive dynamics are genuinely uncertain and evolving.

## Red Flags

- The failed Figma acquisition ($20B attempted, blocked on antitrust grounds in late 2023) means Figma remains an independent competitor with growing enterprise penetration.
- Multiple analysts have downgraded ADBE on AI disruption concerns (HSBC, Piper Sandler, Jefferies), and the stock has declined over 22% in recent months — market is pricing real doubt.
- Canva's IPO rumored for 2026; public market scrutiny will likely accelerate competitive pressure and capital inflow into Adobe's market.

## Score: 7 / 10

Adobe has a genuine wide moat in its professional and enterprise tiers, backed by hard switching costs and 89% gross margins, but the lower-end creative market is under real competitive siege from Canva and AI-native tools, preventing a top score.

# Management & Capital Allocation — ADBE

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-15

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership**

Specific insider ownership data was not definitively retrieved from search results. Based on SEC filings and proxy data patterns for Adobe, insider ownership (including Narayen) is relatively low as a percentage of total shares — typical for a large-cap company where executive wealth is concentrated in vested RSUs rather than accumulated founder-level stakes. Narayen is a professional manager, not a founder-owner. This is a neutral observation: it means incentives are more compensation-driven than ownership-driven. Investors should review the most recent proxy statement for current figures.

**Capital allocation track record**

Adobe's capital allocation over the past several years is strong by most measures. The company approved a $25B buyback authorization in March 2024 and executed approximately $20.3B of that program by end of FY2025, retiring roughly 10% of shares. This was done at prices well below Adobe's 2021 peak (~$700/share), which means capital was deployed at better valuations — a reasonable indicator of management's price awareness. Share count fell from approximately 460M in 2022 to ~427M by November 2025, a ~7% reduction in four years. The company does not pay a dividend, preferring to return capital through buybacks.

M&A has been a mixed area. The company attempted to acquire Figma for ~$20B in 2022, which was blocked by regulators in late 2023. While the strategic rationale was sound (eliminate the leading UX design competitor), the attempted deal at that price ($20B for a then ~$10B revenue-multiple target) raised questions about discipline. The deal collapse ultimately cost Adobe the $1B breakup fee but avoided arguably overpaying. More recently, Adobe announced an acquisition of Semrush Holdings for approximately $1.9B — a far more modest, focused deal in digital marketing analytics.

**Compensation design**

Adobe's compensation structure ties executive pay to non-GAAP revenue growth, non-GAAP EPS, and digital media ARR — metrics that track business performance and per-share value. SBC of $1.94B in FY2025 is elevated as a percentage of revenue (8.2%), which is higher than ideal for a mature business. However, the $25B buyback program more than offsets SBC dilution over the measurement period. Net dilution has been negative (share count declining), which is the correct outcome. CEO compensation of $51M (FY2025) is high in absolute dollars, though Narayen's 18-year track record of compounding revenue from sub-$1B to $25B+ and navigating the SaaS transition provides a basis for the figure.

**Candor and communication quality**

Narayen and CFO Dan Durn have generally been clear and consistent in communicating the business's strategic priorities. Adobe provides detailed ARR metrics, segment breakdowns, and non-GAAP performance bridges that give investors meaningful visibility. One concern: the decision to consolidate reportable segments into one in FY2026 reduces external transparency at a time when investors most want segment granularity (Digital Media vs. Experience Cloud performance). Whether this is administrative simplification or information management is unclear — it warrants monitoring.

**CEO transition risk**

The March 12, 2026 announcement is the most critical near-term management variable. Narayen joined Adobe in 1998 and served as CEO for 18 years. He was the architect of the SaaS transition and the Creative Cloud subscription pivot — one of the most successful business model transformations in software history. His departure removes a known, trusted leader at a moment of heightened competitive and AI uncertainty. The successor search is open to internal and external candidates. Until a successor is named and has established credibility, there is a genuine leadership vacuum risk. The board's decision to retain Narayen as Chair provides some continuity.

**Governance structure**

Adobe has a conventional single-class share structure. The board is majority independent. Frank Calderoni, Lead Independent Director, is chairing the CEO search. No material related-party transactions or governance controversies are in the public record.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A decade of proven capital allocation (massive buybacks, disciplined SaaS transition), consistent per-share value creation, and a board-led succession process led by an experienced lead director suggest stewardship is institutionally sound, not dependent on one person.
- **Bear case:** The abrupt CEO announcement mid-earnings, lack of a named successor, and Narayen's outsized personal contribution to Adobe's transformation create real uncertainty about who will lead the AI-era strategy.
- **Confidence:** Medium — capital allocation history and financial track record are well-documented; the CEO transition introduces genuine uncertainty that cannot be assessed until a successor is named.

## Red Flags

- Narayen departure announced simultaneously with Q1 earnings and a "tepid" FY2026 guidance tone — the optics are poor regardless of whether the timing was intentional.
- No successor identified or timeline provided as of March 15, 2026.
- SBC at 8.2% of revenue is above the threshold where it becomes a meaningful drag on per-share economics without continued aggressive buybacks.
- The failed Figma acquisition attempt at ~$20B highlighted a moment of potentially undisciplined capital allocation, though the breakup fee outcome mitigated the damage.

## Score: 6 / 10

Narayen's 18-year track record of capital allocation and business transformation deserves recognition, and the buyback program shows price awareness; however, the unresolved CEO transition, elevated SBC, and the Figma episode prevent a stronger score at this moment.

# Business Economics — ADBE

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Data Sources:** Web search results (March 2026) covering Adobe's FY2025 annual earnings release (Dec 2025), Q1 FY2026 earnings release (March 12, 2026), MacroTrends historical margin data, GuruFocus ROIC data, FinanceCharts ROIC historical series, SEC filing (Q3 FY2025 10-Q). Training knowledge used for context and cross-checks. All data considered current as of March 15, 2026.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Adobe generated $9.85B in free cash flow in FY2025 on $23.77B revenue — an FCF margin of ~41%, one of the highest in enterprise software | 5 |
| 2 | ROIC expanded sharply from ~21% in 2023-2024 to ~33% in FY2025, driven by net income surging 28% on 11% revenue growth | 5 |
| 3 | Gross margin is structurally high and stable at 87–89% across five years, demonstrating pricing power and near-zero incremental cost of software delivery | 4 |
| 4 | Capex of $179M against $10.03B in operating cash flow represents a 1.8% capex-to-revenue ratio — an extremely asset-light model | 4 |
| 5 | Total ARR of $25.20B exiting FY2025 confirms that effectively all revenue is recurring subscription, providing exceptional revenue visibility | 5 |
| 6 | GAAP operating margin reached 36.6% in FY2025 and 37.8% in Q1 FY2026, with non-GAAP operating margin near 47% — operating leverage is alive and working | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Adobe's ROIC trajectory over the past five years reflects a business compounding at exceptional rates. ROIC was approximately 21.7% in FY2020, expanded to 29.4% in FY2021 (peak Creative Cloud leverage), dipped to 26.9% in FY2022 and 21.7% in FY2023 as the aborted Figma acquisition created uncertainty and the company incurred transaction-related costs, then recovered to 21.2% in FY2024 before surging to approximately 33% in FY2025. The FY2025 expansion reflects both higher net income ($7.13B GAAP, up 28%) and disciplined control of invested capital. Return on equity is even higher given moderate leverage on the balance sheet. By any standard, ROIC consistently above 20% — and now approaching 35% on some measures — indicates a business that earns far more than its cost of capital.

**Margin Structure**

Gross margins have held in the 87–89% band for at least five consecutive years: 88.2% (FY2021), 87.7% (FY2022), 87.9% (FY2023), 89.0% (FY2024), 89.3% (FY2025). This stability reflects Adobe's software-only delivery model where the marginal cost of serving an additional subscriber is negligible. GAAP operating margins have also expanded: from roughly 34% in FY2020 to 36.6% in FY2025, and 37.8% in Q1 FY2026. Non-GAAP operating margin reached ~46% in FY2025 and 47.4% in Q1 FY2026. The spread between GAAP and non-GAAP (~10 percentage points) is primarily stock-based compensation, which is a real cost but reflects the labor market for engineers rather than structural inefficiency. Net margin was approximately 30% GAAP in FY2025 ($7.13B net income / $23.77B revenue).

**Cash Generation**

Free cash flow of $9.85B in FY2025 — up 25% year-over-year — represents a FCF conversion rate well above 100% relative to GAAP net income ($7.13B), driven by meaningful D&A add-back and modest working capital requirements. Operating cash flow was $10.03B. Owner earnings (net income + D&A − maintenance capex) are close to operating cash flow given minimal physical asset base. Capex of $179M was almost entirely growth/infrastructure (data centers, facilities) with a very small maintenance component. This cash generation profile places Adobe in the top tier of all public companies globally.

**Capital Intensity**

Adobe is one of the most asset-light businesses in the S&P 500. At $179M capex on $23.77B revenue (0.75% of revenue), the business requires almost no reinvestment of capital to sustain or grow. The bulk of investment is in human capital (R&D and sales), which flows through the income statement. R&D spending runs at approximately 18–19% of revenue, which is recurring but discretionary in terms of pace. This asset-light model means that free cash flow tracks closely to profit and that returns on capital will remain high unless Adobe dramatically overpays for acquisitions.

**Revenue Quality**

Adobe's revenue quality is among the highest in enterprise software. Total ARR exiting FY2025 was $25.20B (up 11.5% YoY), meaning the entire business is effectively on subscription. Digital Media ARR (Creative Cloud + Document Cloud) reached $19.2B; Digital Experience (Experience Cloud) provides the remainder. Over 75% of net new ARR in Digital Media came from cross-sell, upsell, and subscription activity rather than one-time pricing actions. Revenue visibility is extremely high: the company entered FY2026 with $25.2B in ARR underpinning guidance of $25.9–$26.1B for the full year. Churn is low structurally — Creative Cloud is embedded in professional workflows worldwide, and switching costs are high.
