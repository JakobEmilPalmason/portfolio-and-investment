## Detailed Analysis

**New CEO: Maziar Mike Doustdar**

Doustdar took over as President and CEO on August 7, 2025, succeeding Lars Fruergaard Jørgensen, who departed amid investor pressure following a series of negative clinical and commercial surprises. Doustdar is an unusual choice: Austrian–Iranian by background, born in Iran in 1970, raised in the US, educated at Webster University Vienna and through an executive programme at Harvard Business School (2012). He joined Novo Nordisk in 1992 as an office clerk in Vienna — a bottom-of-the-hierarchy entry point — and spent over three decades progressing through finance, logistics, operations, and marketing across the Near East, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East. Since 2014, he was EVP for International Operations, responsible for all markets outside Europe and North America. He is deeply experienced in Novo's emerging-market commercial engine; his weakness, at least on paper, is that his background is commercial and operational rather than R&D or US market leadership.

His selection signals the Novo Nordisk Foundation's diagnosis of what went wrong: execution and operational discipline, not science. The company over-invested in peripheral programs, lost cost control relative to revenue growth, and was caught flat-footed when US pricing pressure and tirzepatide competition accelerated simultaneously.

**Restructuring Plan**

Doustdar's restructuring is concrete and moving fast. At JPM26 (January 2026), he outlined the strategic priorities: (1) cut costs to save DKK 8 billion ($1.3B) per year by end of 2026 through elimination of approximately 9,000 positions globally (~11.5% of the 78,400 workforce), with roughly 5,000 cuts in Denmark; (2) sharpen focus to diabetes and obesity, exiting programmes and therapeutic areas outside the core; (3) accelerate commercial execution — specifically rolling out higher-dose semaglutide 7.2mg, launching oral Wegovy, expanding cash-pay channels in the US, and improving product availability; (4) progress late-stage pipeline (CagriSema NDA approval, amycretin Phase 3). Transformation costs are explicitly expected to weigh on 2026 profitability. The restructuring is aggressive by Novo Nordisk's historical standards — this is a company that has never previously cut 11.5% of its workforce — and it reflects genuine strategic urgency.

The Morningstar analysis is constructive: the restructuring sets the stage for longer-term investment in diabetes and obesity by right-sizing the cost base. The counterargument is that cutting 9,000 people during a period of rapid pipeline transitions and competitive pressure introduces operational risk: institutional knowledge loss, morale disruption, and execution gaps.

**Governance: Foundation Board Takeover**

The most unusual governance event is the Novo Nordisk Foundation's near-complete replacement of the company's board of directors. Board chair Helge Lund and six other board members resigned after the Foundation determined the incumbent board was moving too slowly on leadership transition and strategic reset. Lars Rebien Sørensen — former CEO of Novo Nordisk (2000–2016) and the architect of the original GLP-1 growth era — has taken the Chair role, intending to hold it for 2–3 years before stepping back. The Foundation simultaneously proposed new directors to be elected at the AGM on March 26, 2026.

This is structurally unusual in ways both positive and negative. The Foundation's voting control (77% of votes through Novo Holdings, representing ~28% of share capital) means there is no corporate governance resistance possible from minority shareholders. The concentration of control eliminates the risk of activist short-termism — the Foundation has a 100-year investment horizon — but it also means minority shareholders have no recourse if the Foundation makes decisions they disagree with. The return of Sørensen to the Chair role is a credibility signal — he presided over Novo's most successful period — but it also raises questions about whether the company is looking backward for answers when the competitive landscape has fundamentally changed.

**Response to CagriSema and FDA Warning Letter**

Management's public response to CagriSema's REDEFINE 4 failure was measured: the company emphasised that CagriSema still achieved 23% weight loss (a clinically meaningful result), that it showed superiority over tirzepatide in blood sugar control for diabetic patients, and that it plans to explore higher-dose formulations. The NDA filed in December 2025 proceeds. This is honest framing — the company did not spin a failure into a victory, which is a point in management's favour for candour.

The FDA warning letter response (issued March 5, 2026, the same day as this prior analysis cycle began) is: Novo states it has been in regular communication with the FDA about its corrective action plan since the January–February 2025 inspection and is confident it will resolve the requests to the FDA's full satisfaction. The company has 15 business days from the letter to formally respond. The FDA warning letter involves process failures in adverse event reporting (MedWatch/IND reporting timelines) at Novo's pharmacovigilance operations, likely involving a contractor. This is a manageable compliance issue if the processes are fixed; it is a credibility problem if it reflects a deeper cultural attitude toward safety reporting. Doustdar's response must be unambiguous and corrective in tone. The inherited nature of the violation (inspection was in early 2025, before his tenure as CEO) is a mitigating factor, but accountability belongs to current management.

**Capital Allocation**

In a financially difficult year, management's capital allocation signals are a mixed-but-acceptable picture. The DKK 15B share buyback (resumed February 4, 2026 after being suspended through much of 2025) at current prices of ~250 DKK is arguably the best use of capital Novo has access to — repurchasing a de-rated franchise at distressed-multiple prices. A DKK 3.75 interim dividend represents a ~7% increase year-on-year and signals dividend commitment even under pressure. The more significant capital allocation decision is the DKK 45B+ planned capex for 2026, overwhelmingly directed at manufacturing expansion for peptide API and fill-finish — this is the right long-term allocation even though it weighs on near-term free cash flow. R&D spending is maintained at approximately 20% of revenue, appropriate for a pipeline-dependent franchise that must continuously innovate to stay relevant.

The prior management era's capital allocation was imperfect: the multi-billion acquisition of Catalent assets (2025) to address supply constraints was necessary but expensive, and the company's guidance accuracy record in 2025 was poor (multiple cuts). Under Doustdar, the priority is explicit focus rather than diversification. Whether this produces better results depends heavily on CagriSema's commercial launch trajectory and amycretin's Phase 3 outcomes.

**Compensation and Candor**

Executive compensation at Novo is structured with performance-linked components aligned to financial results and pipeline milestones, consistent with sector norms. Compensation levels are not exceptional by large-cap pharma standards. The company's candor on the CagriSema head-to-head outcome and on the FDA warning letter has been straightforward — neither management team denied adverse facts. The foundation's governance overhaul, while turbulent, is a transparency signal in itself: the Foundation publicly stated it had irreconcilable differences with the incumbent board, which is unusually candid disclosure.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A new CEO with 33 years of institutional knowledge but fresh outside perspective, backed by a long-horizon Foundation controlling shareholder and a returning legend as Chair, provides a credible platform for strategic reset; the restructuring cuts are decisive and the capital return programme signals confidence.
- **Bear case:** Management is executing a painful restructuring while simultaneously navigating a product portfolio transition, a US pricing collapse, an FDA warning letter, and governance turbulence — this is a high number of simultaneous execution risks for a new CEO with no prior CEO experience at a company this size.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Doustdar's moves are directionally correct, but his track record as CEO is six months old, governance turbulence is real, and the FDA warning letter response quality (due imminently) will be an important early data point.

---

## Red Flags

- FDA warning letter (March 5, 2026): inherited process failure in adverse event reporting; response due within 15 business days of the letter — this is an immediate management quality test.
- Board near-complete replacement by Foundation in late 2025/early 2026; governance turbulence signal, even if the outcome (Sørensen as Chair) may prove positive.
- New CEO has never run a company of this size; his background is commercial/operational in emerging markets, not US pharma or R&D — a profile gap in exactly the areas most stressed.
- 2025 guidance was cut multiple times; current −5% to −13% 2026 guidance should be treated as a floor until management re-establishes forecasting credibility.
- 9,000-person workforce reduction during a critical pipeline transition period introduces execution and institutional knowledge risk.

## Score: 5 / 10

*A new CEO with the right instincts and strong institutional backing is executing a necessary restructuring with appropriate candour. But six months of CEO tenure, governance turbulence, an inherited FDA compliance violation requiring immediate remediation, and a multi-year record of guidance misses by the prior team leaves the stewardship score below average. The foundation is in place for recovery; execution proof points are still pending.*
