## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Capital Structure**

Novo Nordisk's balance sheet has undergone a structural transformation. Two years ago the company was effectively debt-free with a net cash position. By end of 2025, total debt stood at approximately DKK 122.4 billion against cash of DKK 27.0 billion, implying net debt of roughly DKK 95 billion. Total assets are DKK 542.9 billion; total liabilities DKK 348.9 billion; shareholder equity DKK 194.0 billion. The debt-to-equity ratio of ~63% is elevated relative to Novo's own history but moderate by broad corporate standards.

The debt is well-structured. It consists primarily of fixed-rate Eurobonds issued under the EUR 20 billion Euro Medium Term Note Programme, with approximately EUR 16.3 billion outstanding by end of 2025. Maturities are laddered: EUR 1.3 billion in 2026, EUR 1.9 billion in 2027, EUR 1.45 billion in 2028, EUR 1.0 billion in 2029, and further tranches to 2034 and beyond. No single maturity year presents a refinancing cliff. The fixed-rate structure insulates the interest cost from further rate rises. Long-term debt as of Q3 2025 was approximately USD 13.97 billion (DKK equivalent ~DKK 97B), up 84% year-on-year.

**Interest Coverage**

Despite the debt build-up, earnings power keeps coverage comfortably wide. FY2025 operating profit was DKK 127.7 billion. Net financial items in 2025 included a loss of approximately DKK 9 billion (currency hedging losses plus increased interest on Catalent-related debt). Interest expense in isolation is estimated at DKK 3–4 billion annually. EBIT-to-interest coverage of approximately 35–45x means Novo would need to lose 95%+ of its earnings before debt service became a concern. Even under the 2026 guidance of a 5–13% decline, interest coverage remains above 30x. This is the strongest element of Novo's balance sheet — the earnings base so vastly exceeds debt obligations that leverage is not an immediate financial risk.

**Liquidity**

Cash and short-term investments as of end-2025 are approximately DKK 27.0 billion. The company has access to its EUR 20 billion EMTN programme (EUR 3.7 billion remaining capacity after EUR 16.3B drawn) and capital markets at AA/Aa3 credit ratings. Operating cash flow was approximately DKK 85–90 billion in FY2025 (before capex and acquisitions). Annual EUR 1–2 billion debt maturities are easily serviceable from operating cash flow. The company continues a DKK 15 billion share buyback program initiated February 2026 — discretionary capital that could be paused if conditions deteriorate. Dividends (approximately DKK 3.75/share interim) add a further DKK 10–12 billion of discretionary outflow that could also be reduced.

**The FDA Warning Letter Risk — Material New Factor Since Prior Report**

This is the most significant change since the 2026-03-05 report. Two FDA warning letters are now outstanding simultaneously:

1. **PADE Warning Letter (issued 2026-03-05):** Following a January–February 2025 inspection of Novo Nordisk's Plainsboro, New Jersey headquarters, FDA cited serious violations of postmarketing adverse drug experience (PADE) reporting requirements. Specific failures included unreported deaths and cases of suicidal ideation, a policy allowing rejection of adverse event reports if the reporter believed the event was unrelated to the product (inconsistent with federal regulations), and a consent requirement for follow-up that has no basis in federal regulations. Novo has responded; the letter requires a remediation plan.

2. **GMP Warning Letter (Bloomington, Indiana):** A second warning letter was issued related to Good Manufacturing Practice violations observed during a late-2025 inspection at the Bloomington, Indiana manufacturing site.

For balance sheet safety, the direct financial risk from warning letters is not immediate — they rarely result in facility shutdown without extended non-compliance. However, the regulatory risk adds a tail scenario: if FDA escalates to a consent decree or import alert affecting a major manufacturing site, product supply disruption becomes a live risk. Given that Novo is already under capex stress with a large manufacturing build-out, a facility action would simultaneously impair revenue and require additional remediation spending. This is a low-probability, high-magnitude risk that was not present at the prior report date.

**Free Cash Flow Under Impaired Revenue**

FY2025 FCF guidance was DKK 20–30 billion against net income of DKK 102.4 billion. The gap is almost entirely DKK 60 billion in capex. In 2026, revenue is expected to decline 5–13% at CER. If revenue falls to approximately DKK 270–290 billion and operating margins compress further (as 70% U.S. price cuts flow through at full annualized impact), net income could fall to DKK 80–90 billion. If capex also falls from DKK 60B to DKK 55B (as guided), FCF would remain approximately DKK 25–35 billion — barely above the 2025 guidance range despite lower net income. This means the balance sheet is not self-funding debt reduction in any meaningful sense in 2026. Net debt will be approximately stable or could rise marginally if buybacks and dividends absorb residual FCF.

**Off-Balance-Sheet and Other Obligations**

Capital lease obligations are approximately DKK 6.8 billion — modest relative to scale. Novo operates primarily defined-contribution pension plans, limiting defined-benefit pension risk. No material off-balance-sheet liabilities have been identified. Contingent liabilities from litigation (generic challenges, pricing litigation) are not publicly quantified but are within normal pharma ranges.

**Stress Test: 30% GLP-1 Revenue Decline**

FY2025 GLP-1 revenues (Ozempic + Wegovy + related) constitute approximately 70%+ of DKK 309 billion total revenue — roughly DKK 215–220 billion. A 30% decline in GLP-1 revenue would reduce total revenue by approximately DKK 65 billion, or ~21% of total, implying total revenue of ~DKK 244 billion. Assuming gross margins compress to ~75% and operating cost structure partially variable, operating profit could fall to approximately DKK 60–75 billion (25–31% margin) — down from DKK 127.7 billion.

At DKK 60–75 billion EBIT versus DKK 3–4 billion interest expense, coverage is still approximately 15–20x. Annual debt maturities of EUR 1–2 billion are ~DKK 8–16 billion — fully serviceable from operating cash flow. However: capex remaining at DKK 50–55 billion against operating cash flow of DKK 65–75 billion would yield near-zero FCF. The company would need to pause the DKK 15 billion buyback and potentially reduce dividends to avoid net debt increasing. The balance sheet would remain solvent — but it would not be reducing leverage, and covenant headroom, while not contractually defined for Eurobonds, would tighten optically. Net debt-to-EBITDA would rise to approximately 1.5–2.5x, which is high for a company previously running at near-zero leverage but still well within investment-grade territory.

Conclusion: The balance sheet survives a 30% GLP-1 revenue decline without equity issuance, but shareholder returns would need to be curtailed and capex would require reduction. The AA credit rating would likely be maintained, though negative outlook pressure would be expected.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** AA/Aa3 credit ratings, 35–45x interest coverage, fixed-rate well-laddered debt, and DKK 85–90 billion annual operating cash flow mean even a severe GLP-1 revenue decline leaves the balance sheet solvent and debt-service unimpaired; FDA warning letters are compliance remediable and unlikely to escalate to supply disruption.
- **Bear case:** Near-zero FCF conversion (20–29%) in 2025 persists into 2026 as capex remains elevated while revenue declines; dual FDA warning letters introduce a low-probability tail risk of facility action; declining earnings trajectory arriving at peak leverage means no balance sheet cushion is being rebuilt; if 2026 proves worse than guided, the company faces flat-to-rising net debt with no near-term FCF offset.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Debt structure, credit ratings, and operating cash flow scale are well-sourced; the FDA warning letter severity and capex trajectory in 2026 introduce meaningful uncertainty on the downside tail.

---

## Red Flags

- Total debt rose from near-zero to ~DKK 122 billion in 24 months; this is a permanent balance sheet shift, not a temporary acquisition bridge
- FCF conversion at 20–29% of net income in FY2025 — historically one of pharma's best cash converters; now one of the worst due to DKK 60B capex
- Two FDA warning letters outstanding simultaneously: PADE reporting failures (including unreported deaths) and GMP violations — dual regulatory pressure creates a non-trivial tail risk of supply disruption
- 2026 revenue and operating profit both declining 5–13% at CER at the same time net debt is near its all-time high — no de-leveraging occurring
- DKK 15 billion buyback program initiated February 2026 during a period of peak debt, declining earnings, and constrained FCF — capital allocation priority questionable
- The 70% U.S. GLP-1 price reduction is a permanent cash flow headwind that was not fully reflected in end-2025 balance sheet figures but will flow through 2026 cash generation
- New CEO and two senior executive departures coinciding with the first revenue decline guidance in a decade — management continuity risk is higher than in prior periods

## Score: 6 / 10

Novo Nordisk retains genuine balance sheet strengths: AA credit rating, extraordinary interest coverage (35–45x), fixed-rate well-laddered debt, and massive operating cash generation that keeps debt service trivially covered even under stress. However, the prior report's 7/10 is lowered to 6/10 for this REFRESH because: (1) two simultaneous FDA warning letters introduce a tail supply risk that did not exist at the prior report date; (2) near-zero FCF conversion means the balance sheet is not being repaired despite enormous headline earnings; (3) the 70% U.S. price cut is confirmed permanent and will structurally impair future FCF generation; and (4) management continuity risk is elevated at precisely the moment of maximum complexity. This remains a financially sound company, but the margin of safety on the balance sheet is materially narrower than two years ago.
