## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Rocket Lab has never generated positive ROIC. As of mid-2025, GuruFocus reported annualized ROIC of approximately -30% to -31%. The company has been operating since 2006, went public via SPAC in 2021, and has consistently destroyed capital in accounting terms throughout its public history. This is unsurprising for a capital-intensive aerospace startup in build-out mode, but it is essential to acknowledge: there is no historical period where Rocket Lab demonstrated capital efficiency. The ROE picture is similarly negative, with persistent net losses. Net loss for FY2025 was approximately $198M. Until Neutron launches and scales, and until the core business reaches operating breakeven, ROIC will remain negative.

**Margin Structure**

The gross margin trajectory is the most encouraging signal in Rocket Lab's financials. GAAP gross margin improved from roughly 25-27% in 2022-2023 to 34.4% for full-year 2025 and hit a record 38% in Q4 2025. Non-GAAP gross margin reached 44.3% in Q4. Management has articulated long-term gross margin targets of ~50% for Launch Services (Electron) and low-40s for Space Systems — the former driven by volume and reuse, the latter by SDA contract delivery cadence. Operating margins remain deeply negative. GAAP operating expenses in Q1 2026 are guided at $120M–$126M per quarter — meaning even at $200M/quarter revenue, the company produces a substantial operating loss. Adjusted EBITDA loss for FY2025 was $101M. Net margins are approximately -33% for FY2025 ($198M net loss / $602M revenue). The trend direction on gross margin is positive and real, but the distance to operating profitability is still considerable.

**Cash Generation**

Free cash flow for FY2025 was approximately -$321M, a significant deterioration from -$103M in FY2024 and -$150M in FY2023. This acceleration in cash burn is almost entirely attributable to Neutron R&D and related capex. Non-GAAP FCF in Q4 2025 alone was -$114M. The Neutron program consumed cumulative R&D and capex of approximately $360M through year-end 2025, up from the original $250-300M budget due to timeline slippage (the Neutron Stage 1 tank failure delayed first launch to Q4 2026). FCF conversion is meaningless to calculate in this context — there is no positive net income to convert. Owner earnings are substantially negative. The core Electron/Space Systems business may be approaching cash flow breakeven on its own economics, but it is impossible to cleanly isolate this from the Neutron spend in reported figures.

**Capital Intensity**

Rocket Lab is a highly capital-intensive business. Capex was approximately $55M in 2023, surged to an estimated $169M in 2024, and reached approximately $200M in 2025 — primarily driven by Neutron launch complex construction in Virginia, factory buildout, and vehicle development hardware. Q3 2025 alone saw $45.9M in capex. The distinction between maintenance and growth capex is blurred: Electron manufacturing is relatively low-capex on a per-launch basis, but building Neutron is a multi-hundred-million dollar infrastructure project. Space Systems is the more asset-light segment, relying on acquired component manufacturing capabilities (SolarFlux, Advanced Space, Sinclair Interplanetary, etc.) rather than entirely new plant construction. Until Neutron is operational and flying commercially, the company's overall capex profile will remain extremely heavy relative to revenue.

**Revenue Quality**

Revenue quality has meaningfully improved over the past two years. The $1.85B backlog (up 73% YoY) represents approximately 3x annual revenue — strong forward visibility. The Space Systems segment ($403M in FY2025) is driven primarily by U.S. Space Development Agency (SDA) satellite manufacturing contracts (Tranche 2 and Tranche 3), which are multi-year, government-funded programs with defined delivery milestones. This is recurring, contract-backed revenue rather than spot market sales. Electron Launch Services ($199M in FY2025) reached 21 launches in 2025 with a 100% mission success rate — the consistent cadence builds customer confidence and repeat business. Q1 2026 revenue guidance of $185M-$200M implies ~57% YoY growth and confirms the backlog is converting to recognized revenue. The main risk to revenue quality is SDA contract concentration — if the government reduces satellite procurement, Space Systems revenue could decline sharply. One-time contract cancellations were noted in 2025 (Q3), with a 100% margin recognition on a cancelled contract that inflated that quarter's margins.

**Operating Leverage**

Operating leverage is not yet visible at the reported level. As gross profit grows (improving gross margins on rising revenue), the gains are absorbed by growing SG&A, R&D (Neutron), and stock-based compensation. R&D spending in 2025 was substantial and growing. Adjusted EBITDA loss improved modestly YoY but remains deeply negative at -$101M for FY2025. Management guided an Adjusted EBITDA loss of $21M-$27M for Q1 2026 — if maintained, that would imply annualized improvement. The operating leverage thesis depends entirely on: (1) Neutron R&D peaking and declining post-launch, and (2) Electron and Space Systems scaling without proportional opex growth. Neither has been demonstrated in reported results yet. Peak Neutron spending was flagged for Q1 2026 by management, suggesting some opex relief in H2 2026 if the launch timeline holds.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Gross margins are inflecting toward 40%+ as Electron reaches 20+ launches/year and Space Systems scales SDA deliveries; once Neutron R&D peaks (Q1 2026) and tapers, the business could approach EBITDA breakeven by 2027-2028 on an existing backlog of $1.85B.
- **Bear case:** The business is structurally capital-intensive and cash flow negative, with ROIC deeply negative across its entire public history, a cost overrun-prone Neutron program, and no demonstrated path to FCF generation even if Neutron launches successfully.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The revenue and gross margin data are from audited/reported 2025 results and are reliable. The path to profitability involves multiple future execution steps (Neutron launch, ramp, pricing power) that are not yet proven.

## Red Flags

- ROIC has been negative for the company's entire public history, with no sign of inflection.
- FY2025 FCF of approximately -$321M represents an acceleration of cash burn, not improvement.
- Neutron development cost overruns: original budget ~$250M-$300M, actual ~$360M cumulative through 2025, with the first launch delayed to Q4 2026.
- Stock-based compensation is a material non-cash drag that inflates reported losses and dilutes shareholders continuously.
- Space Systems gross margins (targeting low-40s) are not exceptional for defense/government contracts; the segment is not a software-like margin profile.
- Operating leverage has not materialized despite years of revenue growth — opex grows nearly as fast as revenue.
- Q4 2025 FCF of -$114M in a single quarter signals peak cash consumption is not yet behind the company.
- Heavy dependence on equity issuance to fund operations: Q4 2025 saw $280.6M raised via ATM offering, further diluting shareholders.

## Score: 3 / 10

Rocket Lab's business economics are currently poor by all conventional measures: deeply negative ROIC, accelerating FCF burn, no demonstrated operating leverage, and a capital structure that requires continuous equity dilution to fund a pre-profitability aerospace program — the score acknowledges improving gross margin trends but cannot score higher until capital returns turn positive and FCF shows a credible path to breakeven.
