## Detailed Analysis

**What ServiceNow sells and to whom.** ServiceNow sells workflow automation software to large enterprises and governments. Its core product is a cloud platform that digitizes and automates manual, approval-heavy processes — primarily in IT service management (ITSM), HR service delivery, customer service, and increasingly security operations. The platform sits inside large organizations and acts as the connective tissue between departments: when an employee needs a new laptop, files an HR request, or reports a security incident, ServiceNow routes, tracks, and resolves it. Customers are almost exclusively large enterprises — 85% of the Fortune 500 use it. There are roughly 8,800 customers worldwide as of end-2025. The company does not sell to small businesses or individual consumers.

**How it makes money.** ServiceNow's revenue model is almost entirely subscription-based SaaS. Customers sign multi-year contracts (typically 3 years) to license access to the platform, paying annually. Subscription revenue accounted for 97% of total 2025 revenue ($12.88 billion out of $13.28 billion). The remainder is professional services — implementation consulting and training fees that ServiceNow collects when customers onboard or expand. The subscription model means revenue is contracted in advance, recognized ratably over the contract term, and highly predictable. ServiceNow does not sell advertising, take transaction fees, or rely on one-time license sales.

**Key revenue drivers.** There are four variables that matter most. First, customer retention: at a 98% renewal rate, almost no revenue is at risk in any given year from existing customers walking away. Second, expansion within existing accounts: customers typically start with ITSM and add HR, customer service, security, or AI modules over time — "land and expand" is the growth engine. The jump from 420 to 603 customers above $5 million ACV in two years shows this is working. Third, new customer acquisition: the company adds new logos (organizations) every quarter, though this is a smaller driver than expansion. Fourth, pricing: new AI-powered modules (Now Assist) carry higher price points than legacy ITSM workflows, lifting average contract values without requiring entirely new customers.

**How predictable is the revenue.** Very predictable. Multi-year subscription contracts mean most revenue for any given year is already committed before January 1. The cRPO of $12.85 billion (representing contracted revenue expected to be recognized within the next 12 months) at year-end 2025 effectively pre-funds 2026 subscription revenue. Seasonal patterns are modest — Q4 tends to be the strongest quarter for new bookings, which is typical of enterprise software. There are no commodity inputs, no advertising cycles, and no spot-price exposure.

**Customer concentration and switching behavior.** No single customer appears to represent a material percentage of revenue — ServiceNow's 8,800 customers are spread across sectors (finance, healthcare, government, technology, manufacturing). The company has never disclosed a customer exceeding 10% of revenue, suggesting low concentration risk. Switching behavior is sticky but not impossible. Leaving ServiceNow requires migrating custom workflows, integrations, and years of accumulated process data to a competitor's platform. Third-party migration guides describe this as a multi-month project requiring significant IT resources — essentially a mini enterprise IT transformation. That said, some vendors (Atlassian Jira, Freshservice) have built migration tooling specifically to capture disaffected ServiceNow customers, showing the lock-in is real but not impenetrable.

**Two-minute plain-English summary.** ServiceNow is the enterprise workflow system of record for large companies. When a big corporation needs to track, route, and resolve internal requests — from IT tickets to HR onboarding to security incidents — ServiceNow is usually the platform running that process. Customers pay a subscription each year, almost always renew, and tend to buy more modules over time. The business is essentially a toll road: once a Fortune 500 company has built its workflows on ServiceNow, leaving is painful and expensive enough that most just stay and pay more. That is a straightforward, legible business model.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** ServiceNow compounds revenue at 20%+ annually for the next five years as enterprises adopt AI-powered workflow automation and expand average contract values, while free cash flow margins widen toward 40%.
- **Bear case:** Aggressive AI competition from Microsoft (Copilot embedded in existing enterprise workflows), Salesforce, and new entrants erodes ServiceNow's pricing power, slowing net new ACV growth and forcing customer discounting.
- **Confidence:** High — the revenue model, customer base, and retention metrics are clearly documented in public filings and quarterly earnings reports, leaving little ambiguity about the fundamentals of how the business works.

## Red Flags

- The AI module revenue ($600M ACV) is still a small fraction of total ACV; the $1B 2026 target remains a forward projection that needs to materialize.
- Professional services revenue is shrinking as a percentage of the business, which is healthy, but implementation complexity means ServiceNow relies on a large third-party partner ecosystem (Accenture, Deloitte, KPMG) — creating indirect customer acquisition costs not fully visible in financials.
- No single customer concentration risk identified, but the heavy Fortune 500 weighting means macro slowdowns in enterprise IT spending disproportionately affect bookings.

## Score: 9 / 10

ServiceNow has one of the clearest and most legible enterprise SaaS business models available — recurring subscription revenue, nearly perfect retention, a well-defined customer base, and four identifiable growth levers that are all measurable quarter-to-quarter.
