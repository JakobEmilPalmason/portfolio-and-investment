## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** McDermott's direct ownership is low in absolute percentage terms: approximately 57,475 shares as of August 2025, worth roughly $6–10 million depending on share price (the stock has been in the $700–$1,100+ range on a pre-split basis in recent years). For a company with a market cap well above $100 billion, a CEO holding $6–10 million in stock is modest. However, the compensation structure — 96.4% in equity and performance-linked bonuses — means McDermott's personal wealth is almost entirely a function of ServiceNow's long-term stock performance. The February 2026 RSU award of 40,014 shares was performance-based, certified by the Compensation Committee after a two-year performance period (January 2024 to December 2025), which is a good structural design. The December 2025 contract extension through 2030, combined with McDermott's public commitment to tie future pay "more closely to ServiceNow's stock performance over the next three years," is a positive signal of alignment.

**SBC as a percentage of revenue.** At 15.9% of 2024 revenue, ServiceNow's SBC is high by absolute standards but is typical of high-growth enterprise SaaS. For context, comparable companies in the $10B+ ARR SaaS tier generally run SBC at 10–20% of revenue. The more important trend is the direction: as revenue grows toward $15.5 billion in 2026 and SBC grows more slowly, this ratio should compress. Diluted share count growing only 0.44% in 2025 — despite granting over $1.7 billion in new equity annually — shows the buyback program is doing its job. However, investors should be clear: the buybacks are not shareholder-enriching capital returns; they are a treadmill running to offset employee compensation. The net effect is roughly neutral dilution, not share count reduction.

**Buyback discipline.** ServiceNow's buyback history is focused on dilution management rather than opportunistic value capture. The company has not historically purchased large blocks of shares when the stock is depressed. The $2 billion accelerated share repurchase announced in early 2026 was launched immediately after a strong Q4 2025 earnings report — the stock was not at a distressed valuation. The Board's stated rationale is consistently "managing the impact of dilution," not "we believe the shares are undervalued." This is honest but not the Buffett-style capital allocation an owner-operator ideally pursues.

**M&A: a significant shift to watch.** This is the most important capital allocation question for ServiceNow right now. The company has historically grown organically, acquiring small technology tuck-ins to accelerate specific product roadmaps. In 2025 that changed sharply: approximately $12 billion in acquisitions and investments were committed, anchored by the $7.75 billion all-cash acquisition of Armis (cybersecurity, expected to close H2 2026) and Moveworks ($1.5 billion, closed December 2025). The Armis deal is the largest in ServiceNow history and represents a strategic bet that security operations is the next large adjacent market. The rationale — creating a unified security exposure and operations platform — is coherent: ServiceNow already manages IT workflows, so adding asset visibility and vulnerability management is a logical extension. The concern is the price. $7.75 billion in cash for a pre-revenue or early-revenue cybersecurity startup is an aggressive multiple, and the integration complexity of a deal this size is non-trivial. Armis is expected to contribute roughly 1 percentage point to 2026 subscription revenue with a 50 basis point operating margin headwind — meaning the deal is dilutive to margins in the short term, with the long-term payoff dependent on cross-sell execution into ServiceNow's installed base.

**Compensation design.** The structure is directionally correct: the bulk of executive pay is performance-based equity that vests over multi-year periods. The 2024–2025 RSU award to McDermott was certified only after the Compensation Committee confirmed performance targets were met. The recent restructuring to link pay "more closely to stock performance over the next three years" is an improvement. The weakness is that total compensation of $37.56 million in a year where the company is spending $12 billion on M&A — and asking shareholders to absorb substantial integration risk — raises questions about whether the board is exercising sufficient discipline. Compensation that is large when the company is doing well is not differentiated.

**Candor and consistency.** McDermott's communication style is effusive and marketing-heavy. Earnings call language tends toward grandiose framing ("AI control tower," "intelligent platform of platforms"), which is common for enterprise software CEOs but makes it harder to extract plain-English business assessments. The company does not prominently discuss risks or acknowledge mistakes in public communications. On the other hand, guidance has been consistently beat-and-raised across multiple consecutive quarters, which is a form of structural conservatism — management does not over-promise. The contract extension through 2030 and public commitment to personal equity purchases are credible ownership signals.

**Governance.** ServiceNow has a single-class share structure (no dual-class stock), which is positive for shareholder rights. The Board includes independent directors. No material governance red flags were identified in the search results.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** McDermott executes the Armis and Moveworks integrations cleanly, creating a bundled security and AI workflow platform that raises average contract values, while continued buybacks keep share count roughly flat over the next five years.
- **Bear case:** The $12 billion M&A cycle proves to be empire-building at peak valuations, integration costs and cultural complexity drag margins, and SBC remains elevated while deal synergies disappoint — a pattern seen at other enterprise software companies that over-acquired.
- **Confidence:** Medium — McDermott's track record at ServiceNow has been strong (revenue has compounded well since his 2019 arrival), but the sudden shift to large-scale M&A introduces a new variable that has not yet been tested through integration.

## Red Flags

- $37.56 million CEO compensation in 2025 is high relative to the modest direct share ownership of ~$6–10 million; the alignment is better than it looks on paper (given equity structure) but ownership is not deep.
- The $12 billion in 2025 M&A commitments is an abrupt shift from an organic growth model — the Armis deal in particular is large enough that a poor integration would be materially damaging.
- SBC at ~16% of revenue is a genuine ongoing cost to shareholders that buybacks only partially offset; free cash flow is real, but it is meaningfully lower on a per-share basis than if SBC were smaller.
- Buybacks are done at market prices (not when shares are cheap) and are explicitly a dilution-management tool, not capital allocation in the Buffett sense.
- McDermott's communication style prioritizes excitement over candor — investors who want CEO frankness about headwinds will not find it in ServiceNow earnings calls.

## Score: 6 / 10

ServiceNow has a structurally aligned compensation model and a capable, committed CEO, but the combination of elevated SBC, buybacks that merely tread water on dilution rather than create per-share value, and a sudden shift to large-scale M&A at high valuations prevents a strong stewardship score — management is solid but not exceptional capital allocators by Buffett standards.
