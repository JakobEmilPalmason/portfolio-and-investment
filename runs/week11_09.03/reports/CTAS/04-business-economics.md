## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital — High, Stable, and Accelerating**

Cintas has delivered ROIC of 21.4% (FY2022), 23.0% (FY2023), 25.1% (FY2024), and 27.2% (FY2025). ROE has similarly climbed from 37.4% to 40.3%, and ROA — a more conservative metric because it includes goodwill from acquisitions — from 15.2% to 18.4%. Invested capital grew from $6.1B to $7.1B over the same period, yet returns on that capital kept rising. This is the defining characteristic of a genuine compounder: the business earns more on every incremental dollar deployed, not less. The question for any high-ROIC business is whether competition eventually erodes returns; Cintas has maintained 20%+ ROIC for well over a decade, a strong signal that the route-density and switching-cost moat is durable. Q2 FY2026 results (gross margin 50.4%, EPS +11%) confirm the trajectory continues into FY2026. The $5.5B UniFirst acquisition announced March 11, 2026 will temporarily dilute ROIC as the combined invested capital base grows faster than integrated earnings — management has guided net leverage at close to ~1.5x Debt/EBITDA with $375M in identified synergies — but the long-term effect on ROIC depends on integration execution.

**Margin Structure — Improving Across All Levels Without Exception**

Gross margin expanded from 46.2% in FY2022 to 50.0% in FY2025, reaching 50.4% in Q2 FY2026 — 420 basis points of expansion over four-plus years with no reversal in any single year. Operating margin improved from 20.2% to 22.8% (+260 bps). Net margin rose from 15.7% to 17.5%. EBITDA margin reached 27.7%. In Q2 FY2026, the Uniform Rental segment's revenues grew 8.3% while its cost of sales grew only 6.6%, expanding segment gross margin 70 bps — direct evidence of operating leverage at the unit level. Technology is amplifying this: the SmartTruck AI routing platform has reportedly cut annual mileage by 12% and increased customer-facing time by 8%, compressing per-route costs while revenue per route grows. Every margin line moving in the same direction every year for four consecutive years, in a business that is actively adding routes and automation, is not a coincidence — it is the output of a structurally advantaged cost model.

**Cash Generation — FCF Is Real and Compounding**

Operating cash flow grew from $1.5B (FY2022) to $2.2B (FY2025), a 47% increase over four years. Free cash flow grew from $1.3B to $1.75B (using the $409M CapEx figure). FCF conversion — FCF divided by net income — has been 105%, 93%, 106%, and 97% across FY2022–FY2025, averaging essentially 100%. This is a business where reported earnings are cash earnings. Owner earnings (net income + D&A − CapEx) grew from $1.4B to $1.9B. SBC of $128M is 7% of net income — modest, and the dilution is largely offset by aggressive buybacks ($935M in FY2025, $700M in FY2024). There is no meaningful wedge between GAAP earnings and economic earnings, which is a rare and important quality. Full-year FY2026 guidance of $11.15–$11.22B in revenue implies FCF will grow further absent the UniFirst transaction, which may temporarily absorb capital.

**Capital Intensity — Moderate, Not Minimal, But Appropriate**

CapEx has run at $241M, $331M, $410M, and $409M over FY2022–FY2025, or roughly 3–4% of revenue. Cintas is not asset-light in the pure sense — it owns and operates industrial laundry plants, a large fleet of delivery vehicles, and inventories of rental garments. However, 4% CapEx intensity is modest by any industrial comparison (manufacturers typically 5–10%, heavy industrials 10–15%). The maintenance CapEx on the existing revenue base is lower than the headline figure because much of CapEx is growth investment in new plants and automation. D&A of $494M exceeds CapEx of $409M, suggesting the company is actually under-depreciating or over-investing versus a pure-maintenance baseline — both favorable interpretations for FCF quality. The route-based model's key advantage is that each marginal customer on an existing route requires minimal incremental CapEx: the van, plant, and route already exist.

**Revenue Quality — Contracted, Recurring, and Non-Discretionary**

Approximately $7.98B (77% of FY2025 revenue) came from Uniform Rental & Facility Services — uniform rental, laundering, floor mats, restroom supplies, and hand sanitizer, all provided via recurring route-based contracts with regular scheduled visits. The First Aid and Safety Services segment ($1.22B, +14.1% growth in FY2025) operates on the same model for compliance-driven safety products and inspections. Fire Protection Services rounds out the remainder. Customers sign multi-year contracts; switching requires re-sourcing an entire uniform program, retraining employees, and disrupting an embedded operational routine. This is not discretionary spending — a manufacturing plant cannot operate without appropriate workwear, and an employer cannot pass an OSHA audit without maintained first aid cabinets. Revenue behavior approximates a utility: slow to add, even slower to lose. Full-year FY2026 guidance implies continued ~8–9% revenue growth, consistent with Cintas's multi-year track record.

**Operating Leverage — Structurally Embedded and Accelerating**

The clearest summary: revenue CAGR of ~9.3% over four years produced EPS CAGR of ~15.6%. Operating income grew from $1.6B to $2.4B — a 50% increase on 30% revenue growth. In FY2025 alone, operating margin expanded 120 bps on 7.7% revenue growth. The route-based model creates natural operating leverage: fixed costs (laundry plants, corporate overhead, route infrastructure) are spread over a growing volume base, while variable costs (labor hours, materials) grow proportionally with volume but more slowly than revenue due to density gains. Technology investment in routing and sorting automation is compounding this leverage. The result is a business where each additional percentage point of revenue growth produces more than one additional percentage point of earnings growth — and based on four years of data, that relationship is strengthening, not weakening.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A route-density compounder with 27% ROIC, structurally improving margins, ~100% FCF conversion, and contracted recurring revenue — the economics get better as scale increases, and UniFirst adds ~$1.8B in revenue with $375M in guided synergies.
- **Bear case:** UniFirst integration complexity and required leverage (~1.5x at close vs. current 0.8x) could compress ROIC and margins for 2–3 years; the valuation (~50x FCF) leaves no room for execution missteps.
- **Confidence:** High — the four-year financial record is internally consistent, corroborated by Q2 FY2026 results and segment disclosures, and the business model is well-understood.

## Red Flags

- The $5.5B UniFirst acquisition (March 2026) introduces meaningful integration risk and temporarily increases leverage from 0.8x to ~1.5x Net Debt/EBITDA — ROIC will be diluted while synergies are captured
- CapEx doubled from $241M to $409M in four years; while growth-driven, the trend should be monitored to ensure capital intensity does not structurally shift upward post-acquisition
- FCF yield of ~2.2% at current valuation means the market already prices in continued excellence — any stumble on execution is punished disproportionately
