## Detailed Analysis

**What does this company sell, and to whom?** Cintas provides B2B facility services to businesses of all sizes across North America. Its core offering is uniform rental: it supplies, launders, and delivers uniforms to companies whose employees need work clothing — factories, restaurants, auto shops, healthcare facilities, hotels, and thousands of other businesses. On the same weekly delivery route, Cintas also restocks restroom supplies, first aid kits, floor mats, shop rags, and fire extinguishers. The customer is almost always a business, not a consumer, and the relationship is ongoing, not transactional.

**How does it actually make money?** The revenue model is recurring rental and service fees paid under multiyear contracts. A business signs up, Cintas embeds its products into the customer's operation (uniforms fitted to employees, first aid cabinets installed, fire suppression systems inspected), and invoices regularly — typically weekly. The key insight is that switching costs are baked into the delivery cadence. Cintas does not sell products once; it rents them in perpetuity. Once a business is on a Cintas route, it almost never leaves, because rebuilding those programs from scratch across multiple vendors is operationally disruptive and not worth the headache for modest savings.

**Revenue segments in FY2025:** Uniform Rental & Facility Services ($7.98B, ~77% of total) is the engine. First Aid and Safety Services ($1.22B, ~12%) is a faster-growing adjacent vertical. Fire Protection Services and Uniform Direct Sale together make up the remaining ~11%. The three growth levers are: (1) add new customer logos, (2) cross-sell additional product lines into existing accounts, and (3) raise prices annually. All three are working simultaneously, which explains the consistent 7–10% organic revenue growth.

**What are the key drivers?** The four key variables are: (1) number of business locations served (currently over one million), (2) revenue per stop (driven by uniform wearers per location plus cross-sold services), (3) route efficiency (cost per delivery stop), and (4) employment levels at customer locations. The employment link is real — if customers lay off workers, Cintas loses uniform wearers. This creates mild cyclicality, but it is modest in practice because Cintas's diversified customer base (no sector is dominant) mutes the effect. During COVID-19, Cintas lost revenue when customers closed, but recovered quickly and has grown through every subsequent period.

**How predictable is the revenue?** Very predictable. Multiyear contracts with auto-renewal provisions, physical products embedded at the customer site, and weekly service visits create high stickiness. Annual revenue can be modeled with confidence at roughly 7–10% growth; management raised FY2026 guidance in December 2025 to $11.15–$11.22B. FCF conversion has been 93–106% over four years. The business generates cash like clockwork. The main source of uncertainty in FY2026 is the pending $5.5B acquisition of UniFirst (announced March 11, 2026), which introduces integration and regulatory risk — though this is a familiar kind of complexity, as Cintas has digested acquisitions before.

**Can you explain it in two minutes?** Yes, easily. Cintas is essentially the world's largest business laundry and B2B convenience store, delivered by truck. Every week, a Cintas driver pulls up to a factory, restaurant, or hospital, swaps out dirty uniforms for clean ones, tops off the first aid cabinet, replaces the floor mat, and checks the fire extinguisher. The customer pays a recurring fee for all of it. Nobody cancels because switching vendors means refitting every employee, reinstalling equipment, and managing multiple vendors instead of one. Cintas does this for over a million business locations, has been doing it since 1968, and gets a little better every year at packing more revenue into each route stop.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A simple, recurring B2B service model with over a million locked-in customers, compounding at 8% organically, with multiple upsell vectors and minimal customer concentration.
- **Bear case:** The UniFirst acquisition introduces $5.5B in integration complexity and meaningful antitrust risk, which could distract management and create headline uncertainty for 12–24 months.
- **Confidence:** High — Cintas is one of the most transparent, well-documented, and consistently described businesses in public markets. The model has not changed in decades.

## Red Flags

- UniFirst acquisition (announced March 2026) represents a step-change in balance sheet leverage and introduces genuine FTC scrutiny; outcome uncertain
- Mild cyclicality tied to employment levels at customer sites
