## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure — Conservative and Declining Pre-Acquisition**

Cintas carries total debt of $2.7B against FY2025 EBITDA of $2.86B, a Debt/EBITDA ratio of 0.9x. Net debt (total debt minus cash of $264M) is $2.2B, giving Net Debt/EBITDA of 0.8x. The trend has been steadily improving: Net Debt/EBITDA was 1.5x in FY2022, 1.1x in FY2023, 0.8x in FY2024, and 0.8x in FY2025. Total debt/equity has declined from 89.7% to 56.7% over the same period. The known debt structure includes: a $1B senior note at 3.746% maturing in 2027; $400M at 4.2% maturing in 2028; additional tranches including notes maturing in 2032; and a revolving credit facility maturing March 2027 (with no outstanding balance at Q2 FY2026 end). The company refinanced senior notes in Q4 FY2025, issuing at higher rates — FY2026 interest expense guidance has been raised to ~$104M from ~$101M in FY2025. Disclosed coupon rates on existing instruments are fixed, reducing exposure to interest rate volatility on the current debt stack. The critical caveat: the $5.5B UniFirst acquisition (announced March 11, 2026), financed with cash on hand, committed credit lines, and bridge financing from Morgan Stanley/KeyBank/Wells Fargo, will raise net leverage to approximately 1.5x Debt/EBITDA at close. This is a meaningful temporary step-up from the current 0.8x, though management has explicitly guided to this level and pre-arranged full bridge financing, indicating the transaction is not leverage-constrained.

**Interest Coverage — Exceptional, With Modest Post-Acquisition Erosion**

FY2025 interest expense was $101M against operating income of ~$2.35B, yielding interest coverage of 23.4x. This ratio has improved from 17.9x in FY2022 to 23.4x in FY2025, tracking the operating margin expansion story. Post-UniFirst, interest expense will increase substantially as the company takes on incremental debt to fund the cash portion of the deal. However, even if interest expense tripled to $300M — a conservative proxy for the post-close debt service burden — coverage would remain above 7–8x on the combined entity's guided earnings power. FY2026 guidance from Cintas already prices in modestly higher interest ($104M for the standalone entity); the acquisition-related interest layered on top will be visible in FY2027 results. In no scenario does the interest coverage drop to levels that threaten operational flexibility.

**Liquidity — Adequate, With Bridge Financing Pre-Arranged**

At FY2025 year-end, Cintas held $264M in cash with a current ratio of 2.1x and working capital of $1.8B. The company generates approximately $1.75B in annual FCF, meaning it regenerates its entire cash balance every 7–8 weeks from operations. For the UniFirst acquisition, Cintas secured "fully committed bridge financing" from three major banks (Morgan Stanley Senior Funding, KeyBank, Wells Fargo) — the transaction is explicitly described as not contingent on financing availability. The revolving credit facility (expiring March 2027) carried no outstanding balance at Q2 FY2026, providing additional liquidity headroom. The company is not dependent on equity issuance or capital market conditions to fund current operations or close the pending acquisition; the bridge financing de-risks the liquidity transition.

**Refinancing Risk — Low on Current Portfolio, Moderate Post-Acquisition**

S&P Global Ratings affirmed Cintas at A- with a stable outlook in March 2025 (one source also references a prior BBB+ rating — treated conservatively, Cintas is solidly investment-grade at either level). Moody's affirmed A3 with stable outlook in May 2025. Both agencies assessed the credit profile as stable despite macroeconomic uncertainty. At these ratings, Cintas has continuous and broad access to investment-grade debt markets. The most material near-term refinancing event is the $1B bond due 2027; at current FCF levels, Cintas could retire this entirely from operations in approximately seven months without tapping debt markets. Post-UniFirst, the expanded debt load will require disciplined refinancing, but the company's guided post-close leverage of ~1.5x and track record of deleveraging (from 1.5x in FY2022 to 0.8x in FY2025 — three years) suggest they have both the intent and the capacity to reduce leverage post-integration. The revolving credit facility also matures in March 2027 and will need renewal; given the credit profile, this is a routine event.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations — Minimal and Well-Disclosed**

Under ASC 842, operating lease liabilities are captured on-balance-sheet. Cintas's disclosed long-term operating lease liabilities are approximately $179M — modest relative to its $2.86B EBITDA. The company leases certain service centers, vehicles, and equipment. Cintas has historically operated with defined contribution (401k-style) retirement plans rather than defined benefit pension plans, meaning there is no material unfunded pension obligation creating off-balance-sheet risk. The 10-K discloses contingent liabilities and routine legal matters as ordinary for a company of its size and scope. There is no evidence of special-purpose vehicles, synthetic leases, or securitization structures that would artificially reduce reported debt. The balance sheet is clean and transparent by industrial services standards.

**Stress Test — Survive a 30% Revenue Drop Without Equity Issuance**

Assuming a severe two-year stress: FY2025 revenue of $10.3B falls 30% to $7.2B in Year 1 and remains depressed in Year 2. At this revenue level and using a conservative EBITDA margin of 18–20% (reflecting fixed-cost deleverage from lower volume — well below the FY2025 27.7% level), EBITDA would be approximately $1.3–1.4B. Pre-UniFirst debt service runs at ~$101M in annual interest; total debt is $2.7B with no imminent covenant triggers publicly disclosed at these leverage levels. Debt/EBITDA in the stress case rises to approximately 1.9–2.1x — still investment-grade and manageable. The company would likely suspend buybacks and reduce discretionary CapEx, preserving substantial cash from FCF. Dividends (paid for 40+ consecutive years with annual increases) might face pressure in the deepest scenario but are not existentially threatened. Historical evidence is consistent: during the 2008–2009 recession Cintas saw revenue decline but maintained its investment-grade rating and continued dividends without equity issuance. During COVID-2020, the impact was temporary and recovery was rapid. The recurring, contracted nature of revenue means Cintas does not face the binary customer-loss scenarios that affect more cyclical businesses. Post-UniFirst, leverage starts higher (~1.5x) but the same conclusion holds: the combined entity's FCF power means solvency is not at risk even under extreme scenarios.
