## Detailed Analysis

### Owner Earnings Estimate

Owner earnings, in the Buffett sense, equals net income plus depreciation and amortization minus the capital expenditure needed to maintain the existing business (not grow it). The tricky part with HIMS is separating maintenance capex from growth capex. In 2025, the company spent $243M in total capex — the vast majority of which was building out new pharmacy and fulfillment capacity to handle the GLP-1 surge. D&A for 2025 was approximately $35–40M (annualizing the H1 2025 figures of ~$18.7M for property/software plus ~$9.8M for intangibles). Maintenance capex for a platform business of this type is likely in the $30–50M range.

Working estimate of 2025 owner earnings: Net income $128M + D&A ~$38M - maintenance capex ~$40M = **approximately $126M**. This is a rough figure; the company does not disclose a maintenance vs. growth capex split. If maintenance capex is higher (say $70M), owner earnings drop to ~$96M. Call the realistic range **$90–130M** for 2025.

At ~$5B market cap, you are paying roughly **38–55x current owner earnings**. That is not cheap in absolute terms, but whether it's justified depends entirely on how fast those earnings grow.

### Scenario Analysis (5-Year)

All scenarios use a 10% discount rate and assume shares outstanding remain roughly stable at ~230M.

**Bear Case — GLP-1 compounding revenue evaporates, brand shift underwhelms:**
- Revenue plateaus at ~$2.5B by 2030; EBITDA margins compress to 10% = ~$250M EBITDA
- Owner earnings ~$150M; apply 15x multiple = $2.25B enterprise value
- Minus net debt (company carries modest net cash, call it ~$200M net cash) = ~$2.45B equity value
- Discounted back 5 years at 10%: **~$1.52B**, or **~$6.60/share** — a loss of roughly 70% from today's ~$22

**Base Case — Novo partnership generates meaningful volume; HIMS reaches ~$3.5B revenue by 2030:**
- Revenue $3.5B; EBITDA margin improves to 15% = $525M EBITDA
- Owner earnings ~$350M; apply 20x multiple = $7.0B enterprise value
- Add ~$200M net cash = $7.2B equity value
- Discounted back 5 years at 10%: **~$4.47B**, or **~$19.50/share** — roughly in line with today's price, suggesting fair value, not bargain

**Bull Case — Novo partnership + organic growth; HIMS reaches $5B revenue by 2030:**
- Revenue $5B; EBITDA margin 18% = $900M EBITDA
- Owner earnings ~$600M; apply 22x multiple = $13.2B enterprise value
- Add net cash $300M = $13.5B equity value
- Discounted back 5 years at 10%: **~$8.38B**, or **~$36.50/share** — roughly 65% upside from today

**Summary:** The range of outcomes is extraordinarily wide — from $6.60 to $36.50. The base case barely justifies the current price. You are not buying with a margin of safety here unless you have high conviction in the bull case.

### Multiples in Context

At approximately $22/share:
- **Trailing P/E:** ~39x (net income $128M, market cap ~$5B)
- **EV/EBITDA:** ~14–15x on 2026 guided adjusted EBITDA of $337M midpoint
- **EV/FCF (trailing):** ~87x (FCF of $57M — distorted by peak growth capex)
- **P/S:** ~2.1x 2025 revenue

For context, Teladoc at its peak traded at 15–20x revenue and destroyed value. Consumer health platforms with durable competitive advantages (think a mid-growth SAAS analog) typically warrant 15–25x EBITDA when the business model is proven. HIMS at 14–15x 2026 EBITDA is not wildly stretched if you believe the 2030 targets — but those targets require near-perfect execution through significant regulatory and competitive headwinds.

The current multiple **implies** the market is pricing roughly 20–25% annual EBITDA growth for 4–5 years with moderate confidence. That's an aggressive but not insane assumption for a company growing revenue at 59% in 2025. The problem is the GLP-1 compounding revenue stream — which drove a huge portion of recent growth — has been eliminated, creating real uncertainty about what the organic growth rate looks like without it.

### What Must Be True to Justify Today's Price

For $22/share to be a fair price (not a bargain, just fair), roughly the following must be true:
1. The Novo Nordisk partnership generates enough branded GLP-1 volume to partially offset the compounded revenue loss
2. HIMS can sustain 15–20% revenue CAGR through 2030 on its non-GLP-1 core (hair loss, ED, skincare, mental health) plus new branded drug partnerships
3. Capex normalizes to a level where free cash flow converges toward EBITDA (i.e., the infrastructure build is largely complete)
4. No further major regulatory actions, DOJ referral consequences, or branded drug supplier defections
5. EBITDA margins expand from the current ~14% toward 18–20% as the business scales

That's a lot of things that need to go right. This is a show-me story, not a sit-and-wait story.

### Implied Expectations vs. Likely Reality

The stock's collapse from $73 to $15 and partial recovery to $22 tells you the market went from pricing perfection (100x forward earnings in early 2025) to pricing disaster (regulatory shutdown scenario) and is now pricing something in between. At $22, the market is saying: "We think there's a viable business here, but we're uncertain about the path." That's more honest than the February 2025 peak, but it's still not a bargain.

The Novo Nordisk partnership is a genuine positive — it converts a legal enemy into a distribution partner and replaces illegal compounded revenue with legal branded revenue. But branded drug distribution is a lower-margin business than proprietary compounding. The economics almost certainly worsen on the GLP-1 side. HIMS's differentiation now depends more on its platform (customer acquisition, retention, brand trust) than on product exclusivity.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The Novo Nordisk partnership and HIMS's 2M+ subscriber base positions it as the go-to digital front door for branded GLP-1 access, driving the business toward its $6.5B/2030 target and making today's price look cheap.
- **Bear case:** Branded GLP-1 margins are thin, competitors (Ro, Teladoc, Walgreens) access the same drugs at the same prices, the DOJ referral creates legal overhang, and HIMS's non-GLP-1 business can't sustain the current multiple.
- **Confidence:** Low — the near-term outlook changed materially in the past 30 days (DOJ referral → partnership → dismissal), the earnings trajectory is genuinely uncertain, and the fair value range spans $7 to $37.

## Red Flags

- Free cash flow ($57M) is 82% below EBITDA ($318M) due to massive capex — either capex normalizes or the business is far less cash-generative than it appears
- DOJ referral for potential federal law violations (February 2026) — outcome and timeline unknown
- Novo Nordisk reserves right to refile patent suit — partnership could unwind
- Company growth in 2024–2025 was substantially driven by compounded GLP-1 sales, which are now banned — the "clean" organic growth rate is unknown
- Capex of $243M in 2025 to build pharmacy infrastructure that may be over-sized if GLP-1 volumes decline
- 2026 EBITDA guidance ($300–375M) implies potential EBITDA decline or flat performance vs 2025's $318M — even the company isn't sure revenues will translate to profits
