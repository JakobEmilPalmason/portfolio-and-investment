## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** HIMS has been a negative-ROIC business for most of its existence, bottoming at -50.5% ROIC in September 2022. The company only turned profitable in 2022 and crossed into meaningful positive territory in 2024, reaching an estimated peak ROIC of 48.5% at end of 2024. However, by Q3 2025 the annualized ROIC had fallen to approximately 4.4%, with TTM figures variously reported between 6% and 17% depending on the methodology. Return on equity has followed a similarly volatile trajectory. The honest picture is that HIMS has not demonstrated sustained high-quality returns on capital — it achieved a burst of very high returns during the semaglutide-shortage window (2024), but those are now compressing rapidly. There is no 5-10 year track record of consistent 20%+ ROIC that a Buffett framework would require.

**Margin Structure.** The gross margin profile is genuinely impressive for a healthcare/consumer company — running at 75–79% from 2021 through 2024. This high gross margin is the hallmark of a platform business: the company is primarily distributing branded and compounded medications plus telehealth services, not manufacturing heavy physical goods. However, 2025 showed the first meaningful compression, with gross margin declining to 74%. This is not catastrophic, but it is directionally concerning and likely reflects the cost of scaling compounded semaglutide manufacturing in-house. Operating margins tell a different story: the company only crossed into positive operating territory in 2022 (approximately 4–5% operating margin), and despite strong revenue growth has not yet demonstrated the operating leverage one would hope for at this revenue scale. Net margin in 2025 was approximately 5.5% ($128M / $2,348M) — thin, and essentially flat from 2024's $126M / $1,477M = 8.5%, meaning margins actually contracted year-over-year in net terms.

**Cash Generation — the Real Story.** This is where the economics become deeply concerning. In 2024, operating cash flow was $251M and free cash flow was $198M against net income of $126M — an excellent FCF conversion ratio above 150%. In 2025, operating cash flow was $300M (up modestly), but free cash flow collapsed to $57M because capex jumped to approximately $226M (vs. $42M in 2024). FCF as a percentage of net income fell from approximately 157% in 2024 to just 45% in 2025 — below the 50% warning threshold. The business is not generating the kind of owner earnings one would expect from a 74% gross margin platform. The $226M capex figure is almost certainly heavily weighted toward growth capex in pharmacy infrastructure, but the company does not cleanly disclose maintenance vs. growth capex split. D&A for the first half of 2025 was $13.8M (annualizing to roughly $28M). Owner earnings estimate: $128M net income + ~$28M D&A – unknown maintenance capex = likely $130–$150M. Against $2.35B in revenue, this is a ~5.5–6.4% owner earnings yield on revenue — not exceptional.

**Capital Intensity.** HIMS is in transition. Its core business — matching patients with prescribers and fulfilling medications — is inherently asset-light. But the company has been investing heavily in in-house compounding pharmacy operations to supply its GLP-1 weight loss products at scale. Capex went from $2.7M (2022) → $17.2M (2023) → $41.7M (2024) → $226M (2025). This is a 54x increase in capex in three years. That trajectory is not what asset-light looks like. Whether this capex is now stranded — given the FDA's move to restrict compounded semaglutide in 2026 — is a material risk. The company may have built infrastructure around a product it can no longer sell in its original form.

**Revenue Quality.** The subscription model provides surface-level recurring revenue characteristics: 2.51M subscribers paying approximately $83/month translates to approximately $2.5B in annualized run-rate revenue. The 85% annual retention rate cited by management is credible for the legacy hair loss/ED/skincare business. However, the critical question is what fraction of 2025 subscribers were retained on GLP-1/weight loss products vs. the legacy business. GLP-1 revenue was projected at $725M in 2025 — roughly 31% of total revenue. Those subscribers may face a very different retention experience as compounded semaglutide is restricted and branded Wegovy is materially more expensive. The FDA's February 2026 action against compounded semaglutide is a direct threat to the quality and durability of this revenue stream. Revenue growth in 2025 (+59%) was heavily volume-driven, pulled by the GLP-1 opportunity. Without that tailwind, the underlying organic growth rate of the legacy business was mid-to-high teens.

**Operating Leverage.** The company did demonstrate operating leverage through 2024 — revenues scaled faster than costs for several years, driving the ROIC improvement. In 2025, however, operating leverage broke down: 59% revenue growth produced essentially flat net income and dramatically lower FCF, with the difference eaten by capex and margin compression. Adjusted EBITDA of $318M on $2,348M revenue is a 13.5% margin — respectable but not exceptional for a business with 74% gross margins. The gap between EBITDA and actual free cash flow ($318M vs. $57M) is very wide and warrants scrutiny.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The legacy subscription business (hair loss, ED, skincare, mental health) has durable recurring revenue, high gross margins, and proven retention; if HIMS successfully pivots GLP-1 to branded or oral alternatives and maintains its subscriber base, the underlying economics are good.
- **Bear case:** Compounded semaglutide (~31% of 2025 revenue) is being restricted by the FDA and faces patent litigation from Novo Nordisk; capex invested in GLP-1 infrastructure may be partly stranded; FCF conversion has already broken down; and the business has no long track record of sustained high ROIC.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The revenue and margin numbers are well-documented for 2025; the key uncertainty is the magnitude of the GLP-1 revenue impairment in 2026 and beyond, which is still unfolding as of March 2026.

## Red Flags

- Free cash flow collapsed from $198M (2024) to $57M (2025) on 59% revenue growth — FCF conversion fell to 45% of net income, below the 50% warning threshold.
- Capex surged from $42M to $226M in a single year, raising serious questions about capital intensity trajectory and whether this infrastructure is now partly stranded.
- Gross margin compressed 500 basis points year-over-year (79% → 74%), breaking a multi-year trend of stable high margins.
- GLP-1 compounded semaglutide (~31% of 2025 revenue) has been restricted by FDA as of early 2026, creating a major revenue cliff in 2026.
- Net income was essentially flat ($126M → $128M) despite 59% revenue growth, meaning scale is not yet translating to earnings growth.
- ROIC peaked at ~48% in Q4 2024 and has since fallen to low single digits — this is not the durable high-ROIC profile Buffett looks for.
- No meaningful track record of positive ROIC before 2022; only 3–4 years of data to assess.

## Score: 4 / 10

The underlying platform economics are genuinely attractive — high gross margins, recurring subscriptions, growing revenue per subscriber — but FCF conversion has broken down, capex is now material and rising fast, a critical revenue segment faces regulatory extinction, and there is no long-term track record of sustained high returns on capital.
