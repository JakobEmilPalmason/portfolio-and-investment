## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Hims & Hers has one primary debt instrument: approximately $870M–$1B in convertible senior notes issued in May 2025, bearing a 0% interest rate and maturing on May 15, 2030. This is an unusual structure — zero-coupon convertible debt is effectively equity-adjacent financing that allows the company to raise capital without cash interest cost, at the cost of potential future dilution if the stock price is above the conversion price at maturity or if holders convert early. As of approximately Q3 2025, total debt is reported at approximately $971M and total liabilities at approximately $1.7B. With $2.2B in total assets, the company's leverage ratio in balance sheet terms is substantial but not extreme. The debt-to-equity ratio of approximately 167% is elevated. For context, total debt / trailing EBITDA ($318M) is approximately 3x — within the "moderate concern" range (2–4x) on the rubric. Total debt / actual free cash flow ($57M) is approximately 17x — alarming if FCF remains suppressed.

**Interest Coverage.** Because the convertible notes carry 0% interest, there is no contractual interest expense on the primary debt instrument. This makes the traditional interest coverage ratio (EBIT / interest expense) essentially meaningless in its standard application — EBIT is several hundred million dollars while interest expense approaches zero, yielding an extremely high but uninformative coverage ratio. The real test is whether cash flows can service or eventually retire the principal at maturity. With $1B due in May 2030 and current annual FCF of only $57M, the company would need to either refinance, convert the notes to equity, or substantially grow its FCF over the next four years. The 0% coupon is a gift for near-term cash flow, but it obscures the medium-term repayment challenge.

**Liquidity.** As of approximately Q3 2025, Hims & Hers held approximately $630M in cash and short-term investments. Total assets of $2.2B against liabilities of $1.7B suggest a current ratio that is at least workable, though the exact figure is not confirmed from available sources (current ratio not explicitly reported). The company generated $300M in operating cash flow in 2025. At zero revenue, monthly burn would be driven by fixed costs — at current opex levels, the $630M cash position likely funds 12–18 months of operations without revenue, meeting the minimum threshold. However, if 2026 revenue drops materially due to the GLP-1 restriction, operating cash flow could turn sharply negative, reducing this runway. Management has guided 2026 revenue of $2.7B–$2.9B — which, if achieved, would imply continued strong operating cash generation. That guidance was issued before the full scope of the February 2026 FDA enforcement actions became clear.

**Refinancing Risk.** The single 2030 maturity with zero coupon means the company faces no near-term refinancing pressure from the debt itself. The convertible structure provides the company optionality: if the stock trades above the conversion price, holders may convert rather than demand cash repayment, effectively recapitalizing the debt as equity. The risk is that if the stock remains depressed (down 35–40% from highs as of early 2026 based on available information), the notes will not convert and must be refinanced or repaid in cash at maturity. By 2030, the company will need to either have sufficient cash on hand, access to credit markets, or enough stock price appreciation to encourage conversion. This is a medium-term, not immediate, risk.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** The company has operating leases (pharmacy facilities, office space) that constitute off-balance-sheet commitments. The Q2 2025 10-Q shows long-term debt and capital lease obligations of approximately $1.04B, suggesting some lease capitalization. Specific operating lease totals are not extracted from available sources; these are likely material given the company's investment in physical pharmacy infrastructure in 2025. Pension obligations are not applicable (no defined benefit plans). Contingent liabilities are a real and growing concern: Novo Nordisk filed a sweeping patent infringement lawsuit against HIMS in February 2026 over its "personalized" compounding formulations, and a DOJ referral for potential FDCA violations was reported as of early 2026. These contingent liabilities cannot be quantified but represent genuine financial risk.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** 2025 revenue was $2,348M; a 30% decline would bring revenue to approximately $1,643M. For context, this is close to 2024 revenue levels ($1,477M), at which point the company generated $198M in FCF and $251M in operating cash flow. So a 30% revenue decline in isolation — maintaining the cost structure at 2024 levels — would likely allow the company to survive without issuing equity, since 2024 demonstrated profitable operations at that revenue level. The complications are: (1) the 2025 cost structure is materially higher than 2024, particularly with $226M in capex that has added to the fixed asset base and associated depreciation; (2) a revenue decline driven by the GLP-1 restriction means the highest-margin product lines are being lost, not volume across the board; and (3) ongoing legal expenses from the Novo Nordisk suit and DOJ investigation add unpredictable cash outflows. A 30% revenue decline in 2026 followed by a further 30% decline in 2027 would be more existential, but this scenario (to approximately $800–900M revenue by 2027) would likely require equity issuance. The company is not at acute survival risk, but is not stress-test-proof either.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The 0% convertible note maturing in 2030 gives the company five years of zero cash interest burden, substantial liquidity ($630M+ cash), and no near-term debt wall — the balance sheet is structured to survive a transition period.
- **Bear case:** Net debt of ~$341M against only $57M in 2025 FCF implies the company is deeply reliant on either recovering FCF or refinancing by 2030; the Novo Nordisk litigation and DOJ investigation create unquantifiable contingent liabilities at the worst possible time.
- **Confidence:** Medium — Debt structure and maturity are clearly documented; the uncertainty is around 2026 FCF trajectory given the GLP-1 revenue impairment and whether legal liabilities will require material cash outflows.

## Red Flags

- Net debt of approximately $341M against 2025 FCF of only $57M = approximately 6x net debt / FCF ratio; deeply uncomfortable if FCF does not recover.
- Novo Nordisk patent infringement lawsuit (filed February 2026) and DOJ referral create unquantifiable contingent liabilities that could require material cash settlements.
- The $1B convertible note matures in May 2030; with current FCF, the company cannot repay this from operations — it is dependent on either FCF recovery, stock price appreciation enabling conversion, or capital market access for refinancing.
- 2025 capex of $226M may be substantially stranded investment if compounded semaglutide infrastructure cannot be repurposed.
- Total liabilities of $1.7B against total assets of $2.2B leaves limited equity cushion; goodwill or asset impairments could erode book value significantly.
- 2026 revenue guidance ($2.7B–$2.9B) was issued before the full scope of FDA enforcement became clear; downside scenario could see revenue fall meaningfully below guidance, pressuring the operating cash flow that underlies balance sheet resilience.

## Score: 5 / 10

The 0% coupon and 2030 maturity provide meaningful breathing room, and current cash reserves are adequate for near-term operations; however, net debt relative to actual free cash flow is dangerously high, contingent legal liabilities are material and growing, and the company's path to repaying or converting $1B of notes by 2030 is dependent on a significant FCF recovery that is not yet assured.
