## Detailed Analysis

### Debt Levels and Structure

As of FY2025 (fiscal year ended September 2025), Qualcomm carries total debt of approximately $14.8B. This figure has been remarkably stable over the past five years, fluctuating in the range of $14.6B–$15.8B since 2020, with modest reductions from $15.7B (2020) to $14.6B (2024) before a marginal increase to $14.8B (2025). This stability suggests Qualcomm is comfortable maintaining a roughly fixed absolute debt load rather than aggressively deleveraging or taking on materially more leverage.

Debt-to-equity has improved substantially over the same period, declining from 2.59 in 2020 to approximately 0.64-0.70 in 2024-2025, reflecting equity accumulation through retained earnings rather than debt reduction. At a debt-to-EBITDA ratio of approximately 1.06x, Qualcomm's leverage profile is conservative for a large-cap technology company and well within investment-grade norms (typically below 2x for "A"-rated issuers).

The debt maturity profile was described in search results as "strategically staggered" with no significant near-term maturities that could strain liquidity. However, specific maturity tranches (e.g., dollar amounts maturing in FY2026, FY2027, FY2028) were not found via search — full verification requires the 10-K or 10-Q. Qualcomm's debt is predominantly fixed-rate, which is typical for investment-grade issuers of its scale and limits interest rate exposure.

### Interest Coverage

Interest coverage is one of Qualcomm's clearest balance sheet strengths. EBIT of approximately $12.4B against annual interest expense of approximately $664M yields coverage of approximately 18.6x. A ratio above 5x is generally considered comfortable; above 10x is exceptional. Qualcomm's 18.6x is comfortably in the latter category.

For context, this means Qualcomm would need to see operating income fall by roughly 94% before it could not cover its interest expense. Even in the severe FY2023 downturn — when revenues fell approximately 17% and earnings dropped sharply — interest coverage remained well above minimum safety thresholds. Interest expense on $14.8B of debt at approximately 4.5% average coupon is a modest annual burden relative to the earnings power of the business.

### Liquidity

Qualcomm's liquidity position is strong. As of the quarter ending March 2025, cash and equivalents were approximately $13.8B. The current ratio for the December 2025 quarter was 2.51, meaning current assets are 2.5x current liabilities — a healthy buffer. Short-term assets of $24.6B versus short-term liabilities of $9.8B provide approximately $14.8B of current net liquidity.

The cash-to-debt ratio was approximately 0.95 as of March 2025, meaning cash on hand could retire approximately 95% of total debt — a near-cash-covered debt position that is unusual in its strength. It is worth noting that Qualcomm's practice of returning nearly all free cash flow to shareholders means cash balances are actively managed rather than accumulating, so cash fluctuates with timing of buyback programs and dividend payments.

Specific revolving credit facility details were not found via web search. Qualcomm has historically maintained revolving credit access (noted in prior 10-K filings), but current capacity and terms require SEC filing verification.

### Refinancing Risk

Qualcomm's refinancing risk is low but not zero. The company carries $14.8B in debt with a staggered maturity profile and does not appear to be retiring principal in bulk — instead relying on refinancing as tranches mature. Given its A/A2 credit ratings, access to capital markets at competitive rates is not in question under normal market conditions.

The key refinancing risk scenario is a prolonged credit market dislocation combined with a simultaneous business deterioration. With $12.8B in annual FCF and $13.8B in cash, Qualcomm could theoretically cover near-term refinancing needs from internal resources if markets were temporarily inaccessible. The risk of a true refinancing crisis is assessed as very low.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations

Operating leases are the primary off-balance-sheet item of note. As a fabless company, Qualcomm does not own manufacturing facilities, so property-related operating leases (primarily for office and R&D facilities) are the main commitment. Specific lease obligation totals were not found via search — these are disclosed in the 10-K and 10-Q. Pension liabilities for a US-listed technology company of this profile are typically modest. No significant contingent liabilities were identified in the search results, though Qualcomm has a history of patent litigation (both offensive and defensive) that creates episodic contingent legal exposures.

### Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years

Under a severe stress scenario — revenues falling 30% for two consecutive years — Qualcomm's revenues would drop from $44.3B to approximately $31B in year one and approximately $21.7B in year two. Applying historical operating leverage ratios, operating income could fall to perhaps $6-8B in year one and $3-5B in year two.

Even in year two, with revenues at $21.7B:
- Interest expense remains approximately $664M, easily covered.
- The company's $13.8B cash pile and ongoing (reduced) FCF generation would likely fund operations for several years without accessing equity markets.
- Debt-to-EBITDA would rise to perhaps 3-4x at the trough, which is elevated but not distressed for an A-rated issuer.
- R&D commitments ($9B currently) would need to be cut, which would impair the competitive position but not constitute a solvency threat.

Conclusion: Qualcomm survives a 30% revenue decline for two years without needing to issue equity. The balance sheet provides adequate buffer for a cyclical semiconductor downturn of significant severity.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** An A/A2-rated balance sheet with 18.6x interest coverage, $13.8B in cash, $14B EBITDA against only $14.8B in debt, and $12.8B in annual FCF — the balance sheet is a source of strength, not risk.
- **Bear case:** Qualcomm returns nearly all FCF to shareholders rather than deleveraging or building a cash buffer, creating some dependency on capital market access for debt refinancing — manageable in normal conditions but a potential vulnerability in a deep, prolonged market dislocation.
- **Confidence:** High — balance sheet data is well-covered by multiple independent sources consistent with Qualcomm's public FY2025 filings. Credit ratings confirmed by S&P and Moody's directly.

---

## Red Flags

- Cash-to-debt ratio of 0.95 (as of March 2025 quarter) means cash alone does not fully cover debt — though FCF generation more than compensates for this.
- Specific debt maturity schedule details not confirmed via search — investors should verify concentration of near-term maturities against the 10-K.
- Operating lease totals and specific contingent legal liability reserves not found via search — these are typically material disclosures in Qualcomm's filings given its history of IP licensing disputes.
- Returning ~100% of FCF to shareholders is aggressive capital allocation that limits the balance sheet's organic capacity to absorb a sudden, large strategic need (e.g., a major acquisition).

Qualcomm's balance sheet is conservatively leveraged (1.06x debt/EBITDA), exceptionally covered (18.6x interest coverage), well-rated (A/A2), and liquid ($13.8B cash, 2.51 current ratio) — minor deductions for the aggressive capital return policy that limits balance sheet buildup and the absence of confirmed revolving credit facility details.
