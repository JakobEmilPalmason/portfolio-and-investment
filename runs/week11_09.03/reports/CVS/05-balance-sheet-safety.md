## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure**

CVS's long-term debt stood at approximately $60.5B as of September 30, 2025, with total debt estimated at $64–65B including current maturities and short-term borrowings. Total assets are approximately $255.3B and total liabilities $182.2B, implying shareholder equity of approximately $73.1B. The debt-to-equity ratio is approximately 88%, and the balance sheet remains heavily leveraged five years after the Aetna acquisition closed in 2019. The original intent was to use operating cash flow to rapidly delever, but this plan has repeatedly stalled — first due to COVID disruptions, then elevated medical cost ratios at Aetna, then goodwill impairments. Rather than paying down debt in 2025, CVS issued new long-term debt in both August 2025 and (carrying forward from December 2024), which caused interest expense to rise. The net debt position (total debt minus cash) is approximately $53–54B, a figure that dwarfs annual free cash flow of $7.8B, implying roughly 7x net debt/FCF. On a Debt/EBITDA basis, given adjusted EBITDA is difficult to calculate precisely from available data but is estimated at approximately $14–16B (adjusted operating income was up 20.6% in 2025, and the company guided $9B+ operating cash flow for 2026), the ratio is approximately 4–5x — elevated, though not at distress levels.

**Interest Coverage**

Interest coverage has been a concern. Based on GuruFocus data, EBIT-to-interest coverage dropped to approximately 3.1x in 2024 — uncomfortably close to the 3x warning level. It recovered to approximately 4.3x in Q1 2025 as the Aetna segment's medical cost ratios normalized. A separate calculation using $10.1B EBIT produces a coverage ratio of ~14.6x, likely reflecting adjusted/normalized EBIT rather than GAAP. The GAAP picture is more concerning: the $5.7B goodwill impairment in Q3 2025 and $1.2B in litigation charges severely suppressed GAAP EBIT in 2025, meaning GAAP interest coverage in 2025 was very poor. Full-year interest expense is estimated at approximately $3.1–3.2B (the 5.4% increase over 2024 levels). Against $10.6B operating cash flow, the cash coverage is more comfortable but the accrual-based GAAP coverage is not.

**Liquidity**

CVS held approximately $11.2B in cash and short-term investments. Combined with an undrawn revolving credit facility (specific size not confirmed in search results but historically $4–6B for a company of this size), near-term liquidity appears adequate. However, the current ratio of approximately 0.92 means that current liabilities exceed current assets — a persistent feature of insurance businesses where medical claims payable are a large current liability. CVS's insurance subsidiary (Aetna) holds significant regulated reserves that cannot be freely remitted to the parent company. Effectively, not all $11.2B in cash is freely available to corporate treasury for debt service.

**Refinancing Risk**

CVS is dependent on capital markets remaining open. With ~$60B in long-term debt, maturities coming due each year require ongoing refinancing. The company's issuance of new debt in August 2025 (rather than net repayment) demonstrates continued reliance on market access. CVS carries investment-grade credit ratings, which limits acute refinancing risk — but if ratings were to be downgraded to sub-investment-grade (which would require a severe deterioration), the cost of refinancing would increase materially. Given the debt level, CVS does not have the option to allow a credit market disruption without significant pain.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

CVS operates approximately 9,000 retail pharmacy locations, generating operating lease right-of-use assets of approximately $17.3B (2023 10-K data). When operating leases are capitalized, effective total obligations increase by this amount — adding roughly $17B to the liability side of the balance sheet. This is material. Adjusted for operating leases, total leverage metrics worsen further. Pension obligations are present but not reported as a major concern in recent filings. The Omnicare Entities (a CVS subsidiary related to long-term care pharmacies) voluntarily entered Chapter 11 proceedings in September 2025, suggesting isolated but real off-balance-sheet legacy liabilities that needed to be ring-fenced.

**Stress Test: Revenue Down 30% for Two Years**

If CVS revenue fell 30% (approximately $120B reduction from $402B to ~$282B), the consequences would be severe but the immediate survival question depends on the nature of the shock. The PBM segment is largely a pass-through; a 30% revenue decline there would proportionally reduce costs. The real danger is in the insurance segment: a 30% premium revenue decline alongside elevated claims would compress margin brutally. At reduced revenue, operating income might fall by 50–70% or more, potentially leaving operating cash flow at $3–5B against $3.1B+ in annual interest expense — meaning coverage could fall below 1.5x. The company would likely have to cut capital expenditures aggressively, reduce the dividend (currently ~$0.665/quarter), and access revolving credit facilities. Equity issuance would probably not be required in the first year, but would become likely in the second year of such a scenario. CVS would survive a severe recession but not without financial distress, credit rating downgrades, and significant shareholder dilution risk.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Investment-grade ratings, $10.6B operating cash flow, and a long history of meeting debt obligations suggest near-term survival is not in question, and $7.8B FCF can steadily reduce debt.
- **Bear case:** At 4–5x Debt/EBITDA with volatile earnings, rising interest expense, and continued goodwill impairments signaling poor acquisition returns, a prolonged medical cost spike or recession could create real stress.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The debt load is well-documented and real; the key uncertainty is CVS's ability to generate stable operating cash flow through insurance and PBM margin cycles.

---

## Red Flags

- Total debt of ~$60–65B is nearly unchanged from 2019 Aetna acquisition levels — the promised deleveraging has not materialized
- Interest expense increased in 2025 due to new debt issuances — moving in the wrong direction
- GAAP interest coverage was very low in 2024 (~3.1x) and severely distorted in 2025 by impairment charges
- Current ratio of 0.92 means current assets do not cover current liabilities
- $17.3B operating lease obligations represent significant off-balance-sheet leverage from ~9,000 retail locations
- Omnicare subsidiary entered Chapter 11 in September 2025 — a signal that legacy liabilities lurk in the corporate structure
- Cash-to-debt of 0.16–0.17 provides minimal cushion against debt servicing demands

---

## Score: 4 / 10

CVS carries approximately $60–65B in long-term debt with interest coverage that fluctuates near warning levels, a current ratio below 1.0, and has failed to meaningfully reduce leverage since the Aetna acquisition — the balance sheet is the primary structural vulnerability of this business and cannot be considered safe by Buffett-style standards.
