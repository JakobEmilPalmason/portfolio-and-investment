# Compact Checklist — CVS

**Date:** 2026-03-11

1. **Business**: CVS sells pharmacy retail, pharmacy benefit management (through Caremark's 100M+ members), and health insurance (through Aetna's ~25M members) — all three generate recurring revenue but interact in ways that are genuinely difficult to model as a single enterprise.

2. **Moat**: Caremark's PBM switching costs are real — changing a PBM requires employers to renegotiate formularies, notify all covered members, and retrain HR over months — but the rebate-retention economics underpinning that moat are being systematically legislated away by the PBM Reform Act of 2026 and active FTC enforcement.

3. **Economics**: CVS generates $7.8B in free cash flow annually, but ROIC of ~5.2% is below its cost of capital, gross margins have declined 470 basis points since 2020, and a $5.7B Oak Street goodwill impairment in 2025 confirms the acquisitions have destroyed rather than created economic value.

4. **Management**: CEO David Joyner (appointed October 2024) is pursuing the right near-term priorities — integration over acquisition, debt paydown, operational focus — but the prior leadership spent ~$90B on acquisitions that required a $5.7B write-down within two years and failed to warn investors of the Medicare Advantage cost spiral that halved the stock from 2022 to 2025.

5. **Debt risk**: At ~$60–65B total debt (4–5x Debt/EBITDA), with interest coverage that touched 3.1x in 2024 and a current ratio below 1.0, CVS would survive a recession but would face genuine distress — likely requiring dividend cuts, credit downgrades, and possible equity issuance — if Medicare Advantage medical costs spike again for two consecutive years.

6. **Price**: At ~$78 (~11x 2026 forward EPS of $7.00–$7.20), the market is pricing in a continued Medicare Advantage margin recovery reaching 3–4% by 2028 and flat-to-modest PBM earnings — modest execution, not perfection, but the $52B net debt load means equity holders capture only a thin sliver of any enterprise value recovery.

7. **Margin of safety**: The primary protection is the $7.8B annual free cash flow from three durable recurring-revenue businesses that are unlikely to lose their customer base overnight, but at ~$78/share with a bear-case analyst target of $79, there is almost no price buffer — a base-case DCF net of debt yields equity value of $41–$62/share, suggesting the stock is fairly valued to modestly overvalued rather than offering a classic margin of safety.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if the April 2026 final Medicare Advantage rate announcement is punitive (extending the margin recovery beyond 2028), or if adjusted EPS guidance is cut more than 15% in a single year, as either event would confirm that the central recovery thesis has failed and the $52B debt load has no earnings base to absorb.
