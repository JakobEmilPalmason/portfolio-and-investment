## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital — Deteriorating and Below WACC**

CVS Health's ROIC has declined to approximately 5.2%–5.3% as of the most recent measurement periods. This compares poorly against a reasonable WACC estimate in the 7–9% range for a company carrying this level of financial leverage. Pre-Aetna acquisition (before 2019), CVS generated ROIC closer to 9–10%, meaning the transformative acquisition structurally damaged returns. The root cause is the large goodwill and intangible asset base (over $80B post-Aetna) in the denominator of invested capital. ROE data is not cleanly comparable given the repeated goodwill write-downs but is similarly depressed. The trend is not yet clearly reversing: the $5.7B Health Care Delivery goodwill impairment in Q3 2025 is an additional acknowledgment that the acquired asset base was over-capitalized.

**Segment Economics — Three Very Different Businesses**

Health Services (Caremark PBM): Revenue of approximately $186.8B in 2024. The PBM is a pass-through model: revenues are enormous but gross margins are extremely thin because drug costs flow through to clients. Adjusted operating income grew 17.6% year-over-year in Q1 2025 driven by improved purchasing economics and drug mix, but declined 17.8% in Q2 2025 due to client pricing concessions. The PBM faces structural pressure as employers and government payors renegotiate contracts and demand greater transparency. This business is capital-light but earnings are volatile.

Health Care Benefits (Aetna): This is a medical insurance business. It generates float income and premium revenue. Performance was severely stressed in 2023–2024 due to elevated medical cost ratios (MCR). The segment reported an adjusted operating loss of $924M in Q3 2024, then recovered to positive $314M in Q3 2025 — a dramatic swing of over $1.2B in a single year. In Q2 2025, adjusted operating income grew 39.4% year-over-year driven by favorable individual exchange risk adjustment estimates and improved Government business margins. The MCR remains elevated relative to historical norms.

Pharmacy & Consumer Wellness (retail pharmacy): This segment showed resilience with adjusted operating income growing 11.6% in Q1 2025 and 7.6% in Q2 2025, driven by prescription volume and drug purchasing economics. However, the retail pharmacy faces structural headwinds from mail-order, Amazon Pharmacy, and reimbursement rate pressure from PBMs. This is the highest-touch, most capital-intensive segment with ~9,000 retail locations.

**Margin Structure — Sustained Compression**

CVS's consolidated gross margin fell from 18.0% (2020) to 16.7% (2022) to 14.9% (2023) to 13.3% (2024). This reflects the growing revenue weight of the ultra-low-margin PBM, which passes through hundreds of billions in drug spend with margins measured in basis points. Operating margin followed: 5.2% in 2020, dropping to 2.4% in 2022, recovering slightly to 3.9% in 2023, then falling again to approximately 2.3% in 2024 and 2.1% in mid-2025. These are the margins of a commoditized distribution/pass-through business, not a high-quality compounder.

**Cash Generation — The Bright Spot**

Despite ugly GAAP margins, CVS generated $10.6B in operating cash flow in 2025 and $7.8B in free cash flow (after $2.8B capex). This is real cash. Free cash flow exceeds GAAP net income by a wide margin because depreciation/amortization of acquisition-era intangibles is a large non-cash charge. Owner earnings (net income + D&A - maintenance capex) are substantially higher than headline net income. FCF conversion relative to adjusted net income appears healthy at approximately 85–90%. For 2026, management guided at least $9.0B in operating cash flow, suggesting continued strong cash generation.

**Capital Intensity and Operating Leverage**

CVS is moderately capital-intensive. The $2.8B capex in 2025 (~0.7% of revenue) is relatively modest for a company of this size but substantial in absolute dollars. Approximately 60–70% of capex is estimated to be maintenance (store upkeep, technology, pharmacy systems), with growth capex largely paused after the Oak Street Health clinic rollout was curtailed. The business does not exhibit strong operating leverage — revenue grew 7.85% but GAAP operating income grew much more slowly due to cost pressures. Adjusted operating income grew 20.6% in 2025 as Aetna's medical cost ratio normalized, which is more encouraging.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** $7.8B in annual free cash flow at a depressed valuation, improving Aetna MCR, and a debt-paydown flywheel could generate substantial shareholder value over 5–7 years.
- **Bear case:** Structurally thin margins, declining ROIC, PBM pricing pressure, and unresolved healthcare delivery write-downs suggest capital continues to be misallocated.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The FCF figures are credible and auditable, but segment-level margin trajectory and long-run ROIC recovery remain uncertain.

---

## Red Flags

- ROIC of ~5.2% is below estimated WACC — CVS is destroying economic value incrementally
- Gross margin declined 470 basis points from 2020 to 2024 with no sign of reversal
- $5.7B goodwill impairment in 2025 signals the Oak Street Health / Health Care Delivery strategy has failed to create value
- Operating margin of 2.1–2.3% provides almost no cushion against volume or pricing shocks
- PBM revenue pass-through model inflates top-line revenue without corresponding profit substance

---

## Score: 4 / 10

CVS generates substantial free cash flow (~$7.8B) but its ROIC is below WACC, margins are structurally thin and declining, and the Aetna/Oak Street acquisitions have demonstrably destroyed capital — characteristics inconsistent with the economics of a high-quality Buffett-style business.
