## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Home Depot is a high-ROIC business at its core. ROIC ran between 34–37% from FY2021 to FY2022 as the pandemic home-improvement boom compressed the capital denominator and inflated earnings. Post-peak, ROIC has declined every year: 34.5% (FY2022), 29.2% (FY2023), 24.8% (FY2024), 18.3% (FY2025). The FY2025 level still exceeds any reasonable WACC estimate, but the trajectory matters. The primary driver is not margin collapse — it is balance-sheet expansion. The $18.25B SRS Distribution acquisition (completed June 2024) added roughly $18B of goodwill and intangibles to invested capital while contributing SRS's lower-margin distribution economics. ROE figures are distorted by the near-zero and at times negative book equity that characterized Home Depot before SRS; with equity now at $12.8B post-acquisition, ROE has normalized to ~113% on an NOPAT basis — still extraordinary. ROA has declined in parallel with ROIC, from 24.5% in FY2021 to 15.0% in FY2025.

**Margin Structure.** Gross margin has been Home Depot's calling card for stability: 33.6% in FY2021, 33.5% in FY2022, 33.4% in FY2023, 33.4% in FY2024, 33.3% in FY2025. This near-perfect stability across a full commodity and freight cycle reflects the company's category curation, private label penetration, and pricing discipline. Operating margin tells a different story: the peak of 15.2–15.3% in FY2021–FY2022 has eroded to 12.7% in FY2025. The gap (approximately 250 bps) is driven by higher SG&A from the SRS integration, elevated interest expense now running at $2.4B annually, and operating deleverage on flat-to-modest comparable sales growth (+0.3% in FY2025). Net margin contracted from 10.9% (FY2021) to 8.6% (FY2025), with approximately 100 bps attributable to higher interest costs alone.

**Cash Generation.** Home Depot's cash engine remains powerful despite margin compression. Operating cash flow was $16.3B in FY2025, down from a peak of $21.2B in FY2023, partly due to working capital timing related to SRS. Free cash flow (OCF minus capex) was $12.6B in FY2025 versus $16.3B in FY2024; the $3.7B decline reflects $194M higher capex and a $3.5B OCF reduction. Owner earnings — approximated as net income ($14.2B) plus D&A ($4.1B) minus estimated maintenance capex ($2.2B, roughly 60% of total capex) — come to approximately $16.1B. This is a very high earnings quality score: D&A substantially exceeds maintenance capex, and the business requires modest reinvestment in property to sustain its operations. FCF conversion (FCF/net income) was 89% in FY2025 and averaged 93% over the five-year period, confirming that reported earnings are closely aligned with actual cash generation.

**Capital Intensity and Capex.** Total capex was $3.7B in FY2025 ($3.5B in FY2024, $3.2B in FY2023). Management guided FY2026 capex at approximately 2.5% of revenues, or roughly $4.1–4.2B at the midpoint of revenue guidance. The core store fleet is mature: Home Depot operates ~2,340 stores, and the vast majority of capex goes to technology, supply chain modernization (fulfillment centers, interconnected retail infrastructure), and store refresh rather than gross new store openings. Estimated maintenance capex (excluding growth investment) is approximately $1.8–2.2B annually. Growth capex thus represents about 40–50% of total spend — a moderate capital intensity profile for a retailer of this scale.

**Revenue Quality and Operating Leverage.** Revenue has compounded at a modest pace since the pandemic peak: $157.4B (FY2022) → $152.7B (FY2023, a contraction) → $159.5B (FY2024) → $164.7B (FY2025). The addition of SRS contributed roughly $6–7B of incremental revenue. Organic comparable sales growth of +0.3% in FY2025 reflects the housing market headwind — existing home sales at multi-decade lows suppress project spending. The Pro customer segment (contractors, tradespeople) now accounts for approximately 50% of company sales, up from ~45% pre-SRS. Pro revenue is more recurring and relationship-driven, with lower return rates and larger basket sizes, but it is also more sensitive to new construction activity. Operating leverage is structurally favorable: with ~55% fixed or semi-fixed cost structures (occupancy, store labor, IT), each incremental dollar of comparable sales growth flows at high incremental margins. The flip side is meaningful deleverage on negative comps, which the company experienced during the housing downturn of FY2022–FY2023.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Core retail model is a capital-light compounder with durable gross margins; when housing activity normalizes, operating leverage and FCF should rebound sharply toward the $16–18B range.
- **Bear case:** SRS acquisition has structurally diluted ROIC and operating margins, and continued housing weakness or tariff-driven material cost inflation could keep margins compressed for multiple years.
- **Confidence:** Medium — FY2025 results are well-documented; SRS integration trajectory and steady-state margin structure carry genuine uncertainty.

## Red Flags

- ROIC cut nearly in half (36.6% → 18.3%) over four years; while still above cost of capital, the trend requires monitoring
- Operating margin erosion of ~250 bps from peak; meaningful portion may be structural (SRS mix), not purely cyclical
- OCF declined 17.6% year-over-year in FY2025; FCF declined 22.5%; management attributes this partly to working capital timing
- SRS Distribution contribution and margin profile not separately disclosed post-acquisition, limiting segment-level visibility

## Score: 7 / 10

Home Depot's core business economics remain excellent — stable gross margins, strong FCF conversion, and a capital-light store model — but ROIC compression from the SRS acquisition, operating margin erosion, and ongoing housing headwinds prevent a higher score pending evidence that returns stabilize or recover.
