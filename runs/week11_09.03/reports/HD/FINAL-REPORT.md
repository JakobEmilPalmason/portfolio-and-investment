# Investment Analysis: HD — The Home Depot, Inc.

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Verdict:** Watch

> Home Depot is one of the most understandable, competitively advantaged retailers in America — a business with 51% market share in home improvement, durable scale economics, and $12-16B in annual free cash flow. The concern is not the business; it is the price and timing. At ~$339, the stock sits at or slightly above a conservative estimate of intrinsic value (~$310-360), carries no meaningful margin of safety, and requires both a housing market recovery and a successful $23B acquisition integration to deliver above-average returns. This is a high-quality franchise to own at the right price — that price is closer to $270-295, not today's level.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Home Depot sells building materials, tools, and home improvement products to both DIY consumers and professional contractors through ~2,350 large-format stores plus the SRS/GMS specialty distribution network, generating $165B in annual revenue with the Pro contractor channel (now ~50% of sales) as the primary growth engine.

2. **Moat**: HD's moat rests on unmatched retail scale (51% of the home improvement specialty channel), which compounds into superior supplier leverage, denser store coverage, and lower unit costs than any competitor — reinforced in the Pro segment by high switching costs from commercial credit accounts, job-site delivery, and procurement integrations that would be genuinely painful for contractors to replicate elsewhere.

3. **Economics**: ROIC of 26% (down from 36% pre-SRS but still well above cost of capital), gross margins locked in at 33-34% for a decade, and $12-16B in annual free cash flow demonstrate a durable, high-quality economic engine — with the acquisition-related ROIC compression the key metric to watch for stabilization.

4. **Management**: Ted Decker's management team has a strong pre-acquisition track record of disciplined buybacks and 16 consecutive dividend increases, but the $23B SRS/GMS acquisition bet — which suspended buybacks, doubled the debt load, and is not yet accretive to EPS — is the defining capital allocation question mark of this era.

5. **Debt risk**: With $65B in total debt (net ~$64B), leverage at ~2.6x EBITDA, and $2.4B in annual interest expense, the balance sheet is no longer a fortress — but $16B+ in operating cash flow provides adequate debt service coverage, and management is credibly targeting a return to 2.0x leverage by end of FY2026 by halting buybacks and deploying FCF to paydown.

6. **Price**: At ~$339, the stock requires a genuine housing market recovery to begin in 2026-2027, SRS integration delivering $500M+ in incremental EBITDA, and continued EPS compounding at 5-7% annually to justify the current price — fair value assumptions, not optimistic ones.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business provides structural safety through irreplaceable scale and a non-discretionary repair/maintenance demand floor, but the price provides no safety at $339 (roughly at base intrinsic value of $310-360), with bear-case downside of 25-32% and upside/downside ratio of approximately 1:1 — a compelling buy only below $290.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if gross margins compress sustainably below 32% (signals structural pricing power loss), interest coverage falls below 5x (debt becoming dangerous), or if SRS/GMS ROIC is confirmed to be below Home Depot's cost of capital after full integration — any of these would indicate the Pro distribution bet destroyed value rather than created it.

## Key Risks

**1. Prolonged housing market freeze (highest priority):** The mortgage "lock-in effect" — millions of homeowners holding 2.5-3.5% mortgages refusing to move — is a structural headwind, not a cyclical blip. Comparable sales have been essentially flat for three years. If 30-year mortgage rates stay above 6.5% through 2028, the thesis collapses into multi-year earnings stagnation at elevated leverage. This is the dominant risk. *Early warning: mortgage rates staying above 6.5%; existing home sales below 4M annualized.*

**2. SRS/GMS integration failure:** HD paid $23B+ for distribution businesses it is still integrating. If SRS/GMS underperforms — through margin erosion, customer attrition, or operational complexity — ROIC could continue declining, EPS could remain depressed, and the capital allocation thesis reverses. *Early warning: SRS segment margins below 10% EBIT; revenue synergy guidance missed by >20%.*

**3. Leverage and financial flexibility constraints:** At $65B gross debt and 2.6x EBITDA, HD has limited room for error. A recession compressing EBITDA by 25-30% would push leverage toward 3.5-4x, potentially triggering rating agency concern, dividend pressure, or forced asset sales. *Early warning: EBITDA declining more than 10% YoY while debt remains elevated.*

**4. Tariff and input cost inflation:** Building materials (lumber, steel, imported appliances) face ongoing tariff risk. HD cannot fully pass through all cost increases in a weak housing demand environment. A 100-200 bps gross margin compression would be material. *Early warning: Gross margin declining below 32.5% for two consecutive quarters.*

**5. Competitive erosion in Pro segment:** Lowe's acquisition of Foundation Building Materials (FBM) and Amazon's continued attempt to penetrate job-site delivery represent direct threats to the Pro moat HD is trying to build. If the Pro segment stops growing or begins losing share, the entire SRS investment thesis unravels. *Early warning: HD Pro revenue growth lagging Lowe's Pro growth for 2+ consecutive quarters.*

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  1. Stock declines to $270-295 range, providing a genuine 15-20% discount to base intrinsic value
  2. 30-year mortgage rates decline below 6.0% for two consecutive months, signaling housing unlock beginning

- **Sell triggers**:
  1. Gross margin falls and sustains below 32% — indicating structural pricing power impairment, not just cyclical pressure

# Circle of Competence — HD

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** Web search (Home Depot IR Q4 FY2025 earnings release Feb 24 2026, MacroTrends revenue data, corporate.homedepot.com strategic update Dec 2025, PYMNTS Pro segment analysis Jan 2026, Digital Commerce 360 online sales analysis, Hardware Retailing Pro strategy 2026); training knowledge on retail business models. ## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Home Depot sell, and to whom?** Home Depot sells physical products for home construction, repair, and improvement — lumber, paint, plumbing fixtures, electrical supplies, appliances, lawn and garden, tools, and flooring. It also offers installation services (kitchens, flooring, roofing, windows) and tool rental. Customers are roughly split between retail DIY consumers (~50% of sales) and professional contractors — builders, remodelers, electricians, plumbers — who account for approximately 50% of revenue. The Pro share has been growing and is the company's stated strategic priority.

**How does it actually make money?** Home Depot is a physical retailer that buys products at wholesale prices and sells them at a markup. Gross margin runs around 33%, meaning for every dollar of sales, 33 cents covers operating costs before store expenses. With operating margins around 13-14% and net margins around 9%, it is a high-volume, moderately-thin-margin business — but the sheer scale ($165B revenue) produces enormous absolute profit. The SRS/GMS acquisitions add a B2B distribution model on top: HD subsidiaries deliver bulk materials directly to job sites for contractors, operating on a slightly different margin structure.

**What are the key drivers?** The four key variables are: (1) Housing market activity — when people buy, sell, or refinance homes, renovation spending spikes. The current high-rate environment has suppressed housing turnover, which directly hurt comparable sales from 2022 through 2025. (2) Pro contractor volumes — large remodeling and construction projects drive high-value ticket purchases. (3) Consumer confidence and discretionary spending — DIY projects are deferrable in downturns. (4) Big-ticket discretionary items (appliances, kitchens, bathrooms) vs. repair/maintenance — the latter is more recession-resistant.

**How predictable is the revenue?** Meaningfully predictable in structure but cyclical in magnitude. Home repair/maintenance has a baseline that doesn't disappear (roofs need replacing, pipes break), but discretionary projects are elastic. Comparable sales have been essentially flat for 3 years as the housing market froze. Revenue is NOT subscription or recurring in the software sense, but the repair/maintenance segment provides a relatively stable floor. Seasonality is mild and manageable.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?** No single customer represents a meaningful share of revenue — this is a fragmented retail and contractor base. The Pro contractor segment is the largest single channel, but no individual contractor is large. Customer switching costs for DIY are low (Lowe's is often nearby), but Pro contractors have higher stickiness due to credit accounts, job-site delivery, and procurement relationships.

**Can you explain this business in 2 minutes?** Yes, easily. Home Depot owns large-format stores where people buy things to build, fix, or improve their homes. Contractors use it as a primary supply source. It makes money by selling a lot of products at a modest markup. The SRS acquisition adds a wholesale distribution arm aimed specifically at professional trades. The business is straightforward, the competitive dynamics are well-understood, and the key risks (housing cycles, interest rates, Lowe's competition) are all identifiable.

The SRS/GMS layer does add complexity — a distribution business with 750 branches and 4,000 trucks is a different operational model than retail stores. This is a nuance to monitor, not a dealbreaker for understanding.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Housing market recovers as rates normalize, Pro distribution flywheel gains share in $450B addressable market, and HD returns to mid-single-digit comparable sales growth.
- **Bear case:** Prolonged high rates keep housing frozen, SRS integration stumbles, and DIY deflation + contractor competition from Amazon/Fastenal erodes the model.
- **Confidence:** High — this is one of the most understandable large businesses in the US. The new distribution layer adds some complexity, but the core model is transparent.

## Red Flags

- The SRS and GMS acquisitions, totaling ~$23-24B, meaningfully increased operational complexity and leverage — investors need to understand the distribution business model, not just the retail model.
- Comparable store sales have been essentially flat for 3 years; thesis depends on a housing recovery that has been repeatedly delayed.

## Score: 9 / 10

Home Depot's core business is among the most comprehensible in the market; the SRS distribution addition is a logical extension but adds one layer of new operational complexity, knocking it slightly below a perfect 10.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — HD

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** Web search (Morningstar HD moat analysis 2025, OpenBrand market share analysis, Nasdaq HD digital sales analysis, corporate.homedepot.com strategic update Dec 2025, HBS Dealer store count comparison, PYMNTS Pro segment analysis Jan 2026, Ainvest moat analysis Feb 2026); training knowledge for moat framework. Data current through February 2026.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Home Depot commands ~17% of the total $1.1T U.S. home improvement market, and roughly 64% of the home improvement specialty retail channel (vs. Lowe's ~35%) — a scale gap that compounds through better supplier terms, more SKUs, and denser store coverage. | 5 |
| 2 | Pro contractor switching costs are meaningfully higher than DIY: credit accounts, job-site delivery, dedicated Pro desks, and procurement integration create real lock-in for the contractor segment. | 4 |
| 3 | The SRS/GMS acquisitions added 750+ branches, 4,000 delivery trucks, and 2,500 sales reps — creating a last-mile distribution capability that competitors cannot replicate quickly at this scale. | 4 |
| 4 | Home Depot's scale economics produce better unit economics than Lowe's: more stores in better locations, lower cost per SKU from supplier leverage, and an interconnected retail/delivery model that shares infrastructure. | 4 |
| 5 | Online sales grew 8% YoY in Q1 2025; HD's "Magic Apron" AI tool and interconnected retail model (buy online, pick up in store or deliver to job site) strengthen multi-channel stickiness. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**What type of moat exists?** Home Depot's moat is primarily a cost advantage and scale economics moat, reinforced by modest switching costs in the Pro segment. It is not a network effect business and the brand, while strong, does not command meaningful price premiums over Lowe's for identical products. The real advantage is that HD can afford better locations, more inventory depth, and lower sourcing costs than any competitor — and that gap widens with scale.

**Switching costs:** For the DIY consumer, switching costs are low — Lowe's is often across the street. But for the Pro contractor, switching costs are real. A contractor with a Home Depot commercial credit account, established job-site delivery schedules, and procurement integrations has real friction to move. As SRS adds more Pro-specific branches and direct job-site delivery, these switching costs increase. The $450B Pro market is where HD is building its deepest moat.

**Cost advantage and scale:** At $165B in revenue vs. Lowe's ~$85B, HD is roughly twice the size. This scale translates to better negotiated supplier terms, more buying power, and the ability to stock more SKUs profitably. A smaller regional chain cannot match HD's purchasing leverage or distribution infrastructure investment.

**Brand/trust:** The HD brand is strong and trusted, particularly among contractors. But it does not function as a premium brand — customers are not paying more for "Home Depot" paint vs. identical paint elsewhere. The brand primarily means reliability, availability, and convenience — which are real but not pricing-power moats.

**How durable is the moat?** The scale and distribution moat should persist for 10+ years. The SRS/GMS buildout is now creating a B2B distribution infrastructure that took years to assemble and would require billions to replicate. Amazon has tried to penetrate building materials for a decade and still has minimal share in job-site delivery. The physical infrastructure of stores + branches + delivery trucks is inherently slow to replicate.

**Vulnerabilities:** (1) The DIY segment has low switching costs and is exposed to e-commerce disruption over time, though physical products with same-day needs limit this. (2) Lowe's is executing its own Pro strategy, reducing HD's Pro differentiation advantage over time. (3) Smaller specialty distributors (Fastenal, Ferguson, others) have deep Pro relationships in specific trades that HD doesn't fully penetrate.

**Evidence in the numbers:** ROIC of 26%+ (even after levering up for acquisitions), gross margins of 33%, and operating margins of ~14% all suggest real pricing and operational advantages. The business generates $16B+ in free cash flow annually on $165B in revenue — that is not a commodity retail margin profile.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** SRS/GMS integration creates a durable B2B distribution moat, Pro penetration drives market share gains in a $450B addressable market, and HD becomes the indispensable platform for professional construction.
- **Bear case:** Lowe's narrows the competitive gap with its own FBM acquisition, Amazon makes inroads in job-site delivery, and HD's scale advantage in DIY gradually erodes.
- **Confidence:** High — the scale and distribution moat are real and quantifiable. The Pro contractor switching cost moat is growing but not yet fully proven at scale.

## Red Flags

- Lowe's acquisition of Foundation Building Materials (FBM) is a direct competitive response to SRS — the moat is under active assault.
- DIY segment comparable sales were essentially flat through FY2025; the moat in consumer retail is narrower than in Pro.
- Integration of SRS and GMS remains unproven at full scale — the moat expansion thesis depends on execution.

## Score: 7 / 10

Home Depot has a clear, durable scale-economics moat in retail and a growing (but still-being-built) distribution moat in Pro contracting — strong enough to justify confidence, but not yet wide enough in the Pro vertical to score higher, given active Lowe's competition and integration risk.

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game:** HD's institutional ownership dominates; executive insider ownership is not large in absolute share count relative to the company's size. Ted Decker's compensation is heavily equity-based, which aligns his interests with shareholders to a degree, but the stock and option grants are salary-driven rather than the result of open-market purchases with personal capital. This is not unusual for a $400B market cap company, but it falls short of "owner-operator" status.

**Buybacks — timing quality:** Home Depot has historically been a consistent repurchaser of shares, shrinking the share count meaningfully over the prior decade. The $15 billion buyback authorization was announced alongside strong results. However, the SRS acquisition in 2024 forced a halt to repurchases — management took on significant leverage ($56B total debt by end of FY2025) and explicitly prioritized debt paydown over buybacks. The target is to return to 2.0x leverage ratio by end of FY2026 and then resume buybacks. The historical buyback record is solid; the current pause is a consequence of the M&A bet, not mismanagement per se.

**Dividends:** 16 consecutive years of dividend increases is a strong signal of capital discipline. The 1.3% increase in February 2026 (even while highly levered) reflects confidence in cash generation. At $9.32/share annually, the dividend is well-covered by $14.69 adjusted EPS. Payout ratio is around 63% — high but not concerning for a mature, cash-generative business.

**M&A — the big question:** The SRS ($18.25B) and GMS (~$5.5B enterprise value) acquisitions are the defining capital allocation decisions of the current management era. On the bull case: HD paid a fair price to acquire a distribution platform that would take a decade to build organically, accelerating access to the $450B Pro market. On the bear case: HD overpaid at a peak in the acquisition cycle, took on $23B+ in debt to buy businesses whose integration risk is high, and EPS has actually declined (adjusted EPS $14.69 in FY2025 vs. $15.24 in FY2024). The jury is still out. Management has a credible track record before these acquisitions, so this deserves benefit of the doubt — but it's a real risk.

**Share count trend:** Shares outstanding have been reduced meaningfully over the prior decade. The current pause in buybacks is explicitly tied to leverage reduction, and management has communicated a clear timeline (end of 2026) for resumption.

**Candor and consistency:** The December 2025 "strategic update" where HD explicitly provided a "market recovery case" alongside base guidance is a positive transparency signal. Management has been clear that the business is in a housing cycle trough and has avoided pretending otherwise.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** SRS/GMS prove to be value-creating acquisitions, leverage comes down as planned by end of 2026, buybacks resume aggressively, and management's Pro market bet pays off with multi-year earnings growth.
- **Bear case:** Integration proves harder than expected, housing market remains frozen through 2027, leverage stays elevated longer than guided, and the capital allocation bet destroys value.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the pre-acquisition management track record is strong, but the $23B M&A bet introduces genuine uncertainty about the quality of this specific capital allocation decision.

## Red Flags

- Total debt of ~$56B at end of FY2025 represents a dramatic change in balance sheet posture driven by M&A.
- Adjusted EPS declined from $15.24 (FY2024) to $14.69 (FY2025) — shareholders are not yet seeing accretion from the acquisitions.
- Dividend increase in a high-leverage year prioritizes optics over financial conservatism.

## Score: 7 / 10

Management has a long track record of disciplined capital allocation and honest communication, and the Pro distribution strategy has strategic logic — but the scale of the M&A bet, the resulting leverage, and the EPS dilution in the near term warrant a slight downgrade from the historically higher score this team would have earned.

# Business Economics — HD

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** Home Depot Q4 FY2025 earnings press release (February 24, 2026); StockAnalysis.com annual income statement, cash flow, and ratios (FY2021–FY2025); GuruFocus ROIC data; MacroTrends margin history; SRS Distribution acquisition filings (IR.homedepot.com, March/June 2024)

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ROIC has compressed from 36.6% in FY2021 to 18.3% in FY2025 — still well above cost of capital but half its peak, driven primarily by the $18.25B SRS Distribution acquisition doubling invested capital | 5 |
| 2 | Operating margin has declined steadily from 15.2% (FY2021) to 12.7% (FY2025), partly structural (SRS mix) and partly cyclical (housing downturn suppressing big-ticket Pro and DIY projects) | 4 |
| 3 | Free cash flow generation remains formidable at $12.6B in FY2025 despite the OCF decline, supporting a ~7.7% FCF yield on enterprise value | 4 |
| 4 | Owner earnings (net income + D&A − estimated maintenance capex) approximate $16.1B in FY2025, reflecting the capital-light nature of the core retail model despite recent acquisition spend | 3 |
| 5 | Gross margin has been remarkably stable for a decade, oscillating narrowly between 33.3%–33.6%, demonstrating pricing power and category mix discipline | 3 |
| 6 | Revenue quality has shifted meaningfully toward Pro customers (now ~50% of sales) and the SRS professional distribution channel, increasing recurring contract-driven revenue but also cyclicality tied to new construction | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Home Depot is a high-ROIC business at its core. ROIC ran between 34–37% from FY2021 to FY2022 as the pandemic home-improvement boom compressed the capital denominator and inflated earnings. Post-peak, ROIC has declined every year: 34.5% (FY2022), 29.2% (FY2023), 24.8% (FY2024), 18.3% (FY2025). The FY2025 level still exceeds any reasonable WACC estimate, but the trajectory matters. The primary driver is not margin collapse — it is balance-sheet expansion from the SRS acquisition.

**Margin Structure.** Gross margin has been Home Depot's calling card for stability: 33.6% in FY2021, 33.5% in FY2022, 33.4% in FY2023, 33.4% in FY2024, 33.3% in FY2025. This near-perfect stability across a full commodity and freight cycle reflects the company's category curation, private label penetration, and pricing discipline. Operating margin tells a different story: the peak of 15.2–15.3% in FY2021–FY2022 has eroded to 12.7% in FY2025. Net margin contracted from 10.9% (FY2021) to 8.6% (FY2025), with approximately 100 bps attributable to higher interest costs alone.

**Cash Generation.** Home Depot's cash engine remains powerful despite margin compression. Operating cash flow was $16.3B in FY2025, down from a peak of $21.2B in FY2023. Free cash flow (OCF minus capex) was $12.6B in FY2025 versus $16.3B in FY2024. Owner earnings — approximated as net income ($14.2B) plus D&A ($4.1B) minus estimated maintenance capex ($2.2B) — come to approximately $16.1B. FCF conversion averaged 93% over the five-year period, confirming that reported earnings are closely aligned with actual cash generation.

**Capital Intensity and Capex.** Total capex was $3.7B in FY2025. Management guided FY2026 capex at approximately 2.5% of revenues. Estimated maintenance capex is approximately $1.8–2.2B annually. The core store fleet is mature: Home Depot operates ~2,340 stores, and the vast majority of capex goes to technology, supply chain modernization, and store refresh rather than gross new store openings.

**Revenue Quality and Operating Leverage.** The Pro customer segment (contractors, tradespeople) now accounts for approximately 50% of company sales. Pro revenue is more recurring and relationship-driven, with lower return rates and larger basket sizes, but it is also more sensitive to new construction activity. Operating leverage is structurally favorable: with ~55% fixed or semi-fixed cost structures, each incremental dollar of comparable sales growth flows at high incremental margins.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Core retail model is a capital-light compounder with durable gross margins; when housing activity normalizes, operating leverage and FCF should rebound sharply toward the $16–18B range.
- **Bear case:** SRS acquisition has structurally diluted ROIC and operating margins, and continued housing weakness or tariff-driven material cost inflation could keep margins compressed for multiple years.
- **Confidence:** Medium — FY2025 results are well-documented; SRS integration trajectory and steady-state margin structure carry genuine uncertainty.

## Red Flags

- ROIC cut nearly in half (36.6% → 18.3%) over four years; while still above cost of capital, the trend requires monitoring
- Operating margin erosion of ~250 bps from peak; meaningful portion may be structural (SRS mix), not purely cyclical
- OCF declined 17.6% year-over-year in FY2025; FCF declined 22.5%

## Score: 7 / 10

Home Depot's core business economics remain excellent — stable gross margins, strong FCF conversion, and a capital-light store model — but ROIC compression from the SRS acquisition, operating margin erosion, and ongoing housing headwinds prevent a higher score pending evidence that returns stabilize or recover.

# Balance Sheet Safety — HD

**Analyst Role:** Balance Sheet Safety Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** Home Depot Q4 FY2025 earnings press release (February 24, 2026); StockAnalysis.com balance sheet and ratios (FY2021–FY2025); SRS Distribution acquisition investor slides (March 2024); MacroTrends long-term debt history; GuruFocus Debt/EBITDA; SEC/EDGAR 8-K filings

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Total debt reached $65.4B in FY2025 (including operating lease liabilities), largely reflecting the $18.25B SRS Distribution acquisition funded primarily with incremental debt; net debt is approximately $64.0B | 5 |
| 2 | Debt/EBITDA of 2.6x is elevated relative to Home Depot's historical range of 1.8–2.1x pre-SRS but management has committed to returning to a 2.0x leverage target by end of FY2026 | 4 |
| 3 | Interest coverage (EBIT/interest expense) of approximately 8.7x remains solid despite $2.4B annual interest expense, providing meaningful cushion above a stress threshold | 3 |
| 4 | Current ratio has tightened to 1.06x in FY2025 with only $1.4B cash on hand, but a multi-billion dollar commercial paper and revolving credit facility provides adequate liquidity backstop | 3 |
| 5 | Operating lease obligations add approximately $8.1–8.7B of fixed commitments (now on-balance-sheet under ASC 842) representing a significant fixed-cost structure | 3 |
| 6 | A 30% revenue stress test over two years would be severe but survivable without equity issuance, given $12–16B annual FCF baseline | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

Home Depot carried $65.4B in total debt at fiscal year-end FY2025. Long-term debt stood at $46.3B, with short-term debt at $4.5B. Net debt is approximately $64.0B as of February 1, 2026, with cash and equivalents of only $1.4B. The debt/EBITDA ratio, calculated on FY2025 EBITDA of approximately $25.0B, is 2.6x — elevated from the pre-SRS range of 1.8–2.1x.

Interest expense for FY2025 was approximately $2.4B. EBIT was $20.9B, implying interest coverage of 8.7x. Management guided FY2026 net interest expense at approximately $2.2B, reflecting incremental debt repayment and favorable refinancing.

Home Depot maintains a $4.0B commercial paper program and revolving credit facility. Operating cash flow of $16.3B in FY2025 is the primary liquidity source. The current ratio of 1.06x is lean but consistent with a business that operates on negative working capital dynamics.

Home Depot has an investment-grade credit profile (rated A2/A by Moody's/S&P). The debt maturity schedule from the SRS acquisition financing was structured with staggered maturities, limiting near-term cliff refinancing risk.
