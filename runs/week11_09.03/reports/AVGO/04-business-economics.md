## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Strong Underlying Economics Masked by Acquisition Accounting.** Broadcom's ROIC tells two different stories depending on whether you look through the VMware acquisition or not. Pre-acquisition, ROIC climbed steadily from roughly 5% in FY2019 to a peak of 23% in FY2023 — an excellent trajectory showing disciplined capital allocation. Post-VMware, reported ROIC cratered to ~8% in FY2024 and 6% as of early 2025 because the $69B acquisition ballooned invested capital with goodwill and intangible assets. ROE, which includes the leverage effects, has held up better at 31-33%. The honest assessment: the underlying business earns very high returns on tangible capital, but whether the VMware acquisition will earn adequate returns on the $69B price tag remains an open question that will take several more years to resolve. Historical ROE averaged 24% over the past decade.

**Margin Structure: Elite and Expanding.** Broadcom's margin profile is remarkable. Gross margins climbed from 55% (FY2019) to 69% (FY2023), then compressed to 63% in FY2024 as the lower-margin VMware perpetual license business blended in. The subscription transition is now pushing margins back up. Adjusted EBITDA margins reached 67% of revenue in FY2025, and GAAP operating margins recovered from 26% in FY2024 to 40% in FY2025. The FY2024 GAAP margin drop was driven primarily by massive amortization charges on VMware intangible assets (~$3.7B incremental) and restructuring costs — not operating deterioration. The gap between GAAP and non-GAAP results narrowed significantly in FY2025 (diluted EPS of $1.74 GAAP vs. $1.95 non-GAAP in Q4), signaling that integration distortions are fading. Net margins should continue normalizing upward as amortization rolls off over the next several years.

**Cash Generation: The Crown Jewel.** Free cash flow is where Broadcom truly shines. FCF has grown from roughly $12-13B (FY2020-FY2021) to $16.3B (FY2022), $17.6B (FY2023), $19.4B (FY2024), and $26.9B (FY2025). The five-year average FCF margin of ~48% is extraordinary — few companies of any size convert nearly half their revenue to free cash. Even in FY2024, the first full year with VMware and its integration costs, FCF still grew 10% year-over-year. In FY2025, FCF grew 39% to $26.9B. FCF conversion relative to GAAP net income was distorted in FY2024 (FCF was 3.3x net income due to non-cash amortization charges), but normalizing for that, cash conversion is consistently healthy. This is a business that generates enormous amounts of real, spendable cash.

**Capital Intensity: Remarkably Low.** Broadcom's capex was $452M in FY2023 and $548M in FY2024 — roughly 1% of revenue in both years. This is astonishingly low for a semiconductor company. Broadcom is fabless (outsources chip manufacturing to TSMC and others), so it doesn't carry the massive fab capital costs of Intel or Samsung. The software business is essentially zero-capex at the margin. Maintenance capex is minimal; virtually all capex is growth-oriented (design tools, lab equipment, data center infrastructure). This asset-light model is a key reason FCF margins are so high — operating cash flow converts almost dollar-for-dollar to free cash flow.

**Revenue Quality: Improving Rapidly.** The VMware acquisition transformed Broadcom's revenue composition. In FY2024, semiconductors contributed $30.1B (58%) and infrastructure software $21.5B (42%). Within semiconductors, AI revenue was $12.2B (up 220% YoY), driven by custom XPU chips for hyperscalers (Google, Meta, and others) and networking silicon. The software segment is transitioning to subscription/recurring revenue — over 87% of VMware's top 10,000 customers have migrated to VMware Cloud Foundation subscriptions. This shift from perpetual licenses to recurring subscriptions creates far more predictable revenue. However, customer concentration is a real concern: AI semiconductor revenue is driven by roughly five to six hyperscaler customers, and any pullback in their AI capex would hit Broadcom's fastest-growing segment directly. The non-AI semiconductor business (networking, broadband, storage, wireless) provides diversification but is growing more slowly.

**Operating Leverage: Present but Temporarily Obscured.** From FY2022 to FY2025, revenue grew at a 24% CAGR while adjusted EBITDA grew at 25% — modest positive leverage. The FY2024 year is a poor comparison period because of VMware integration distortions. Looking at FY2023 to FY2025 (skipping the noise), revenue grew 78% while adjusted EBITDA grew roughly 85%, and operating income went from $16.5B to $26.1B — clear operating leverage. As the VMware subscription transition completes (expected by mid-FY2026), software margins should expand further, and operating leverage should become more visible. The business model — high-margin design IP licensed across many chips, plus sticky enterprise software — is inherently high-leverage once the R&D investment is made.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Broadcom is a free-cash-flow machine with ~48% FCF margins, sub-1% capex intensity, an improving recurring revenue mix from VMware subscriptions, and an AI semiconductor business growing 100%+ with $100B revenue visibility by 2027 — the economics are elite and getting better.
- **Bear case:** ROIC on total invested capital (including VMware goodwill) is mediocre at ~6-8%, AI revenue is dangerously concentrated among five hyperscaler customers, and VMware's aggressive pricing has pushed 98% of surveyed customers to consider alternatives — creating long-term churn risk.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The financial data is clear and publicly reported; the uncertainty lies in whether VMware's subscription transition will hold against customer backlash and whether AI semiconductor demand from a handful of hyperscalers is sustainable.

## Red Flags

- ROIC on total capital has fallen to 6-8% post-VMware — the $69B acquisition must generate substantially more revenue and cash flow to justify the price, and the jury is still out
- AI semiconductor revenue ($12.2B in FY2024, growing rapidly) is concentrated among approximately five to six hyperscaler customers — losing even one would be material
- VMware pricing increases of 150-1,200% have created significant customer resentment; a Rimini Street survey found 98% of VMware customers are considering alternatives and 36% have already switched
- The wide gap between GAAP and non-GAAP results (narrowing but still present) means investors must carefully distinguish real economics from adjusted metrics — adjusted EBITDA of $43B in FY2025 versus GAAP operating income of $26B is a $17B difference, mostly stock-based compensation and amortization
- Broadcom carries substantial debt from the VMware acquisition; interest expense is a meaningful drag on GAAP profitability

## Score: 8 / 10

Broadcom's underlying business economics are exceptional — sub-1% capex intensity, ~48% FCF margins, and an improving recurring revenue mix are hallmarks of a truly elite business model. The score is held back from 9+ because ROIC on total invested capital is currently mediocre (the VMware acquisition inflated the capital base enormously), customer concentration in AI semiconductors is concerning, and VMware customer backlash introduces real long-term churn risk. If VMware integration succeeds fully and AI revenue diversifies, this could be a 9.
