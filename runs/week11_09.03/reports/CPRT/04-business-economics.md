## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Copart's ROIC has been consistently extraordinary. The 5-year average ROIC is approximately 31.9%, the 3-year average is approximately 30.5%, and the trailing twelve-month figure stands at roughly 29.9% (as of October 2025). Return on equity is approximately 18%, somewhat suppressed relative to ROIC because the company now holds a net cash position exceeding $5 billion — massive idle capital that dilutes the equity return denominator without destroying value. The ROIC-to-WACC spread of roughly 20 percentage points is among the widest in any capital-intensive services business, indicating durable competitive advantages translating directly into economic profit year after year.

**Margin Structure**

Gross margin dipped to 44.9% in FY2023, reflecting a post-pandemic cost surge in towing, labor, and land. It has since recovered: FY2024 gross margin was 45.8% (+1.9 pp), FY2025 was 47.2% (+1.4 pp), and Q1 FY2026 came in at 46.5% ($537M gross profit on $1.155B revenue). Operating margin has tracked between 36–39% in recent years, with FY2025 at approximately 36% and Q1 FY2026 showing improvement as operating income rose to $430.7M (37.2% margin) on a period when revenue grew only 0.7% year-over-year — demonstrating meaningful operating leverage on flat revenue. Net margin in FY2025 was approximately 34%. These are among the highest sustained net margins in the entire US commercial services sector.

**Cash Generation**

FY2025 operating cash flow was $1.83B — a 22% jump from the prior year. Capital expenditures were $569M (focused on yard acquisitions and facilities), yielding free cash flow of approximately $1.26B. FCF conversion of net income is approximately 79%, which is strong given that the company is simultaneously investing heavily in growth capex. Owner earnings are arguably higher still: depreciation and amortization on an asset base this age are not immaterial, and to the extent that existing yard infrastructure requires minimal ongoing maintenance spend (versus growth-driven capex), the true maintenance FCF is higher than the reported free cash flow figure. Q1 FY2026 net income grew 11.5% to $403.7M while revenue was nearly flat (+0.7%), indicating that the FCF trajectory is pointing upward as growth capex begins to moderate against a growing earnings base.

**Capital Intensity and Capex Nature**

Copart is land-intensive: it must own or lease large secured yards to store salvage vehicles, and this requires significant real estate investment. However, this capital intensity is primarily growth-driven, not maintenance-driven. Once a yard is built and operational, the marginal maintenance cost is low. The $569M FY2025 capex is essentially a strategic land grab — locking up scarce acreage near population centers that competitors cannot easily replicate — rather than sustaining existing revenue. This distinction matters for owner earnings: the core "maintenance capex" is likely in the range of $100–200M annually against an asset base of roughly $5–6B in property, implying maintenance FCF meaningfully higher than reported FCF. Exact maintenance vs. growth capex breakdowns are not disclosed by the company, so this estimate carries uncertainty.

**Revenue Quality and Operating Leverage**

Service revenue is 80–85% of total revenue. While Copart does not have traditional subscription contracts, the revenue is effectively recurring: insurance companies channel totaled vehicles to Copart under long-term exclusivity agreements, and once enrolled in the auction platform, buyers (salvage dealers, rebuilders, parts recyclers) return repeatedly. The take-rate model — collecting fees from both the seller (insurance company) and buyer side — means Copart benefits from rising vehicle values without owning inventory. Higher average selling prices (ASPs) on auctioned vehicles translate directly into higher service revenue per unit. As volumes and ASPs grow on a relatively fixed infrastructure base, operating leverage is evident: in Q1 FY2026, operating income grew 6.0% while revenue grew only 0.7%, illustrating the model's inherent scalability. The 15.1% 5-year compound annual revenue growth and 17.5% compound annual EPS growth confirm that earnings grow faster than revenue over time.

**Operating Leverage Quantification**

Over the 5-year period through FY2025, revenue grew at ~15% CAGR while EPS grew at ~17.5% CAGR, yielding an earnings-to-revenue growth ratio of approximately 1.15x — modest but consistent. The near-term compression (FY2023–FY2025) was driven by deliberate expansion investment, not structural deterioration. As the newly opened yards mature and reach utilization targets, operating leverage should reassert more visibly.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Copart is a quasi-monopoly toll-road on the growing salvage vehicle market, generating 30%+ ROIC on a growing capital base with compounding FCF — the economics of a world-class compounder.
- **Bear case:** Operating margins have compressed during the current expansion cycle, CapEx is structurally high at ~$570M/year, and free cash flow conversion could disappoint if land acquisition costs accelerate further.
- **Confidence:** High — Copart's multi-year financial record is well-documented and consistent; margin/capex trends are disclosed in recent earnings releases and 10-K filings.

## Red Flags

- Operating margin has declined ~300 basis points from FY2023 peak (39%) to FY2025 (36%) as facility operations expenses outpaced service revenue growth by 2.3 percentage points.
- Capex of $569M consumes ~31% of operating cash flow — elevated, though primarily growth-oriented.
- Exact maintenance vs. growth capex split is not disclosed, introducing uncertainty into owner earnings calculations.

## Score: 9 / 10

Copart earns a 9 for its consistently high ROIC (~30% 5-year average), massive and growing FCF ($1.26B in FY2025), asset-light service revenue model generating 34%+ net margins, and strong operating leverage that should reassert as the current expansion cycle matures; the minor deduction reflects the near-term margin compression and opacity around maintenance capex.
