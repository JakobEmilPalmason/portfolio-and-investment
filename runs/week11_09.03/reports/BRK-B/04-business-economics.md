## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Berkshire's ROE and ROIC figures are systematically distorted by two structural features: (1) the ~$373B cash/T-bill hoard earns modest yields (generating ~$12.5B in investment income in 2025) and suppresses percentage returns while depressing the ratio, and (2) the enormous equity base of $719.7B — grown through decades of retained earnings — dilutes percentage metrics. GuruFocus calculates ROIC at ~7%, while methodologies that exclude the cash drag imply 15–17%. Historical median ROE over the past decade is approximately 10–12%. On 2025 operating earnings of $44.49B on equity of $719.7B, operating ROE is approximately 6.2% on a headline basis. The correct interpretive frame is Berkshire's own: the company should be judged by growth in per-share intrinsic business value, not by ROIC on a balance sheet that carries hundreds of billions in uninvested capital as a deliberate strategic choice.

**Margin Structure**

Berkshire does not report a consolidated operating margin in any meaningful way. By segment in 2025: BNSF's operating margin improved to 34.5% (from 32.0% in 2024) — a high-quality result for a capital-intensive railroad. BHE generated ~$8.4B in operating cash flows, benefiting from lower wildfire loss accruals at PacifiCorp and improved earnings at the rail-adjacent businesses. Insurance underwriting profit was $7.26B against a $176B float base — a combined ratio below 100% that means Berkshire is paid to hold the float rather than paying for it. Manufacturing, Service & Retailing produced $13.6B in operating earnings across dozens of subsidiary businesses with varied but collectively healthy margin profiles. Insurance investment income of $12.51B (down from $13.67B in 2024) reflects the impact of moderating short-term rates on the T-bill hoard.

**Cash Generation and Owner Earnings**

The company generated ~$46B in operating cash flows in 2025 against capex of ~$20.9B, yielding FCF of ~$25.1B. This capex is predominantly at BNSF (~$3.5–4B maintenance and growth) and BHE (multi-billion infrastructure investments in renewable energy and grid infrastructure). The holding company and insurance operations are inherently asset-light with minimal maintenance capex. Owner earnings — net income plus depreciation/amortization minus maintenance capex — are estimated in the $30–35B range, reflecting BHE and BNSF's substantial D&A. Reported GAAP net income of approximately $67B in the trailing twelve months is highly volatile due to mark-to-market swings in the ~$300B equity investment portfolio; Berkshire explicitly instructs investors to disregard this figure.

**Capital Intensity**

The conglomerate is bifurcated. BNSF and BHE are capital-intensive regulated businesses requiring sustained reinvestment; BHE alone plans tens of billions in capex over 2024–2026 for its renewable energy buildout. The insurance operations, consumer subsidiaries, and investment operations are asset-light. Total capex intensity (~5–6% of ~$371B revenue) is moderate at the consolidated level, but nearly all of that burden sits in the two regulated utility/rail subsidiaries. The holding company itself deploys capital through acquisitions and equity investments rather than fixed assets — making it structurally different from an industrial company.

**Revenue Quality and Recurring Nature**

Insurance premiums are sticky and renewing; BNSF freight revenues are tied to long-term industrial and consumer rail transport demand; BHE utility revenues are regulated and predictable. Manufacturing and service subsidiaries generate repeat revenues across industrial, consumer, and specialty markets. Insurance investment income (~$12.5B in 2025) from the T-bill portfolio and equity holdings is recurring but rate-sensitive. The only significant non-recurring items are realized investment gains and mark-to-market unrealized gains/losses — both of which Berkshire explicitly asks investors to exclude from economic analysis.

**Operating Leverage**

Operating leverage is muted at the consolidated level given the diversified structure and significant fixed-cost components in rail and utilities. BNSF demonstrated meaningful positive operating leverage in 2025: modest revenue growth paired with margin expansion to 34.5% as efficiency gains dropped to the bottom line. Insurance underwriting, however, showed negative operating leverage when catastrophe losses hit: the $850M in after-tax Q1 2025 LA wildfire losses illustrates how concentrated events can impair segment earnings regardless of premium volume. The conglomerate structure dampens volatility in both directions — limiting dramatic positive leverage but also buffering against severe negative surprises.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** $44.5B in annual operating earnings compounding across diversified moat businesses, backed by $176B in near-free insurance float and $373B+ in deployable capital — when Berkshire deploys that cash, it will do so at scale into distressed opportunities that no other investor can match.
- **Bear case:** Headline ROE and ROIC appear modest (~7–10%), insurance underwriting is declining as Berkshire deliberately shrinks its P&C book, and the sheer scale makes high-percentage returns structurally impossible — the company may be at a permanent return plateau.
- **Confidence:** High — Berkshire's financial disclosures are among the most detailed of any public company; segment data is well-reported and the analytical framework for interpreting the conglomerate is well-established.

## Red Flags

- Insurance underwriting earnings fell 19% year-over-year to $7.26B in 2025; management indicated deliberate reduction in P&C volume "for a period of time," which will further suppress this earnings stream in 2026.
- Insurance investment income declined from $13.67B to $12.51B despite record cash levels — reflecting lower short-term interest rates; this trend will continue if rates fall further.
- No share repurchases occurred in 2025; with the stock near all-time highs, this implies management sees the stock as fairly to richly valued, which is itself a capital allocation signal worth noting.
- BHE wildfire liability remains partially unresolved; while 2025 earnings benefited from lower accruals, future claims from the 2020 Oregon fires and subsequent events could re-emerge.
- ROIC on a consolidated basis will remain low until the $373B cash hoard is deployed — opportunity cost drag is real and has been building for several years.

## Score: 7 / 10

Berkshire generates exceptional absolute owner earnings (~$25–35B FCF annually) from a diversified empire of moat businesses backed by $176B in near-free insurance float, but percentage returns on the massive equity base are structurally modest, insurance underwriting is declining deliberately, and the $373B idle cash pile represents both Berkshire's greatest strategic asset and its most visible drag on reported returns.
