## Detailed Analysis

**Owner Earnings Estimate.** Hermès reported 2025 net income of approximately EUR 4.5 billion (adjusted for France's exceptional tax on large company profits, which inflated the reported figure). Depreciation and amortization came in around EUR 1 billion. Capital expenditures were EUR 1.16 billion, though a meaningful share of this is growth capex (new stores, production capacity). Maintenance capex is likely EUR 600-800 million. That puts owner earnings in the range of EUR 4.7-4.9 billion, or about EUR 45 per share on roughly 105 million shares outstanding. At a share price of EUR 1,925, the stock trades at roughly 40-43x owner earnings. This is expensive by any traditional standard, but Hermès is not a traditional business.

**Scenario Analysis — Year 5 Owner Earnings (discounted at 10%).**

*Bear case (6% annual earnings growth):* Year 5 owner earnings of ~EUR 6.3B. Apply a 30x terminal multiple = EUR 189B enterprise value. Discount back 5 years at 10% = ~EUR 117B, or roughly EUR 1,120/share. Add net cash of EUR 120/share = **EUR 1,240/share.** This implies about 35% downside from today.

*Base case (10% annual earnings growth):* Year 5 owner earnings of ~EUR 7.7B. Apply a 35x terminal multiple = EUR 270B. Discount back at 10% = ~EUR 167B, or roughly EUR 1,590/share. Add net cash = **EUR 1,710/share.** This implies roughly 10% downside, suggesting the stock is approximately fairly valued.

*Bull case (14% annual earnings growth):* Year 5 owner earnings of ~EUR 9.4B. Apply a 40x terminal multiple = EUR 376B. Discount back at 10% = ~EUR 233B, or roughly EUR 2,220/share. Add net cash = **EUR 2,340/share.** This implies about 22% upside.

Note: the 10-year CAGR for Hermès net income has been roughly 16-17%, so 14% is actually slightly below historical trend. But past performance in a booming luxury cycle should not be extrapolated uncritically.

**Multiples in Context.** The trailing P/E of ~45x compares to LVMH at ~22-25x, Richemont at ~20-22x, and the broader European luxury sector at 20-30x. Hermès has historically traded at a substantial premium — its 5-year average P/E is roughly 50-55x — so today's multiple actually represents a modest de-rating from peak levels (the stock hit 60x+ during the 2021-2023 luxury boom). EV/EBITDA is approximately 26x on trailing figures, versus a luxury sector average closer to 15-18x. P/FCF sits around 40x.

**What Must Be True for Today's Price.** At EUR 1,925, the market is pricing roughly 10-12% compounded earnings growth for the next 7-10 years with no meaningful multiple compression. That requires: (a) continued volume-driven growth in leather goods and ready-to-wear, (b) pricing power sustained at 3-5% annually, (c) China/Asia recovery or at least stability, (d) no significant margin erosion from the current 41% operating margin. These are plausible but not guaranteed outcomes. The deceleration from 13% to 9% growth in 2025, while still strong, hints that the hyper-growth phase may be moderating.

**Implied Expectations vs Reality.** The stock has fallen ~24% from its peak, which has taken some froth out. But it remains priced for excellent execution. If growth slips to mid-single digits for a sustained period — possible if Chinese luxury demand weakens further — the multiple would likely compress simultaneously, creating a double hit. The stock is not priced for perfection, but it is priced for continued excellence.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Hermès is one of the few truly irreplaceable luxury franchises, and at a 5-year average P/E discount, the current de-rating could be a rare entry point if growth re-accelerates.
- **Bear case:** At 45x earnings with decelerating growth and significant China exposure, even modest disappointment could trigger further multiple compression toward 30-35x.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The business quality is extremely high, but valuation depends heavily on growth trajectory assumptions and terminal multiple, both of which carry wide uncertainty bands.

## Red Flags
- Growth decelerated from 13% to 9% in one year; if this trend continues, the multiple is too high
- China/Asia-Pacific represents roughly 45-50% of revenue — geopolitical or macro risks are concentrated
- At 45x earnings, there is minimal valuation cushion against negative surprises
- Currency headwinds (EUR strength) compressed reported growth from 9% constant FX to 5.5% reported

## Score: 5 / 10
Hermès is approximately fairly valued under base-case assumptions — not clearly overvalued, but offering no meaningful discount to intrinsic value, and requiring continued strong execution to justify the current price.
