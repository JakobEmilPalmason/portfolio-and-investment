## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Danaher carries $19.7B in total debt ($18.4B long-term, remainder short-term) against $4.6B in cash, yielding net debt of $13.8B. At 2.0x Net Debt/EBITDA, this is a moderate level of leverage for a company with Danaher's earnings stability and cash generation. The debt/equity ratio of 37.5% (down from 57.3% five years ago) reflects disciplined deleveraging even as the company has pursued large acquisitions. Danaher's debt is predominantly investment-grade senior notes with maturities spread across 2026, 2027, 2028, 2030, 2031, 2039, and 2049. In October 2025, Danaher Switzerland issued CHF-denominated bonds due 2027-2045 raising ~$1.6B, which diversifies currency exposure and extends duration. The weighted average maturity appears well-managed with no single year presenting an outsized refinancing cliff.

**Interest Coverage.** At 17.0x EBIT/interest (with interest expense of only $265M against EBITDA of $6.9B), Danaher has extremely comfortable debt service capacity. Even using a more conservative EBITDA-less-capex measure (~$5.7B), coverage would be ~21x. This level of coverage provides a massive cushion against earnings deterioration. The company could absorb a very significant decline in operating income before debt service becomes a concern.

**Liquidity.** The current ratio of 1.9x indicates healthy short-term liquidity. Cash of $4.6B is substantial, though the company also has access to commercial paper programs and revolving credit facilities. FCF of $5.3B per year means Danaher generates enough cash to cover its entire annual interest expense (~$265M) roughly 20 times over, and could theoretically pay down all net debt ($13.8B) in under 3 years from free cash flow alone (net debt/FCF of ~2.6x).

**Masimo Acquisition Impact.** The most significant near-term risk to balance sheet safety is the pending $9.9B Masimo acquisition expected to close in H2 2026. Assuming a mix of cash and debt financing, pro forma net debt could reach $20-23B, pushing Net Debt/EBITDA toward 2.5-3.0x (assuming Masimo adds ~$2B revenue and modest EBITDA initially). This would still be manageable given Danaher's FCF power and DBS margin improvement track record, but it narrows the safety margin materially. Danaher has navigated similar leverage spikes before — the ~$20B GE Biopharma (Cytiva) acquisition in 2020 temporarily pushed leverage higher before being worked down. Management has historically demonstrated discipline in deleveraging post-acquisition.

**Stress Test: Revenue -30% for 2 Years.** Under a severe stress scenario where revenue drops 30% (to ~$17.2B), assuming gross margins compress 300bps and operating costs are partially variable, EBITDA could fall to roughly $3.0-3.5B. At current debt levels, Debt/EBITDA would rise to ~5.5-6.5x — uncomfortable but not existential. Interest coverage would still be ~11-13x. More importantly, FCF would likely remain positive at $1.5-2.5B even in this scenario given Danaher's variable cost structure and low maintenance capex. The company would not need to raise equity. However, with the Masimo debt added, a 30% revenue decline would push leverage to 6-8x — tight but survivable given the long maturity schedule and no near-term refinancing cliff. The 80%+ recurring revenue base makes a sustained 30% decline highly unlikely in any case; even during COVID-era destocking, the peak-to-trough decline was roughly 10%.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Danaher has standard operating lease obligations and pension liabilities, but nothing unusual or outsized for a company of this scale. The guaranteed subsidiary debt (Danaher International, Danaher Switzerland, Danaher Japan) is already consolidated and reflected in the total debt figures.

**Refinancing Risk.** With maturities spread across 15+ years and investment-grade credit ratings, refinancing risk is low. The diversification into CHF-denominated bonds reduces exposure to any single credit market. Even in a credit crunch, Danaher's FCF could cover near-term maturities from operating cash flow without accessing capital markets.

## Signal Summary
- **Bull case:** Danaher's 17x interest coverage, 2.0x net leverage, and $5.3B annual FCF provide a fortress-like balance sheet that can absorb the Masimo acquisition and still weather severe economic stress.
- **Bear case:** The Masimo deal will temporarily push leverage to the upper end of comfort, reducing financial flexibility precisely when the bioprocessing recovery is still uncertain.
- **Confidence:** High — The current balance sheet is unambiguously strong; the only meaningful risk is the pending Masimo acquisition's leverage impact, which is quantifiable and temporary based on Danaher's track record.

## Red Flags
- Pending $9.9B Masimo acquisition will meaningfully increase leverage (pro forma Net Debt/EBITDA likely 2.5-3.0x)
- Large share buybacks ($3.1B in FY2025, $6.0B in FY2024) reduce cash buffers ahead of a major acquisition — raises question about capital allocation prioritization
- Total debt of $19.7B is substantial in absolute terms, though well-supported by cash flows

## Score: 7 / 10
Danaher's current balance sheet is conservatively leveraged with excellent interest coverage, ample liquidity, and well-laddered maturities — solidly in the 8/10 range. However, the pending $9.9B Masimo acquisition will temporarily elevate leverage and reduce the safety margin, warranting a one-point haircut to reflect near-term execution risk.
