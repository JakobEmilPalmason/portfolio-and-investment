## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Eli Lilly sells patent-protected branded prescription drugs — primarily tirzepatide (Mounjaro for diabetes, Zepbound for obesity), which together generated $36.5B of $65.2B in 2025 revenue — and customers keep buying because these drugs are the most clinically effective treatments for two of the largest chronic disease markets in the world, and physicians have no comparable alternative until patents expire in 2036–2041.

2. **Moat**: Competitors are blocked by a compound patent running to 2036 with follow-ons to 2041, a $50B manufacturing investment program that has grown incretin production 1.8x and cannot be replicated quickly, and the clinical credibility of tirzepatide as the reference efficacy standard in its class — with Novo Nordisk as the only credible rival and one that trails on weight-loss outcomes.

3. **Economics**: Gross margins of 83%, operating margins of 40%, and ROIC of 25% in FY 2025 are world-class for any sector, with operating income growing 307% on 91% revenue growth between 2023 and 2025 confirming exceptional operating leverage; reported FCF of $9B is temporarily suppressed by $7.8B in growth capex, with normalized owner earnings estimated at ~$20B annually.

4. **Management**: CEO David Ricks owns ~$683M of personal LLY stock (and bought more at $645 in August 2025), has delivered 571% total shareholder return over his tenure, prioritizes internal reinvestment at high ROIC before buybacks and dividends, and has made only targeted bolt-on acquisitions ($3.2B Morphic, $1.4B Point Biopharma) — not empire-building mergers.

5. **Debt risk**: At net debt/EBITDA of ~1.2x and interest coverage of ~14–19x, Lilly can service its $42.5B gross debt even under a 30% sustained revenue decline without equity issuance; operating cash flow of $16.8B in 2025 provides ample coverage, and the debt is laddered into long-term investment-grade instruments with no near-term maturity wall.

6. **Price**: At ~$1,001/share, the market requires Lilly to compound owner earnings at roughly 15% annually for five years — achievable in the base and bull cases given $80–83B 2026 guidance — but the stock is priced at approximately base-case intrinsic value (~$960–1,025) with no cushion, meaning even modest execution shortfalls on GLP-1 growth will produce capital loss.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business has real safety embedded in 83% gross margins, patents to 2041, and a pipeline of successor molecules (orforglipron, retatrutide), but there is effectively no price margin of safety — the bear case intrinsic value of ~$450–700 is 30–55% below the current price, and the bull-to-bear risk/reward ratio of ~1.5–2:1 falls well short of the 3:1 threshold needed for a high-conviction safe entry.

8. **Thesis breaker**: The thesis breaks if IRA Medicare negotiations select tirzepatide for price cuts AND Lilly simultaneously guides revenue below $80B — signaling that volume growth can no longer offset net pricing decline — or if a tirzepatide safety signal emerges requiring a black-box warning or market withdrawal.

## Key Risks

1. **No price margin of safety.** At ~$1,001, the stock is priced at the base case intrinsic value (~$960–1,025). Any revenue growth miss — even a modest 20% shortfall versus guidance — produces 40–55% capital loss. There is no cushion. This is the most actionable risk: the business quality is real, but the price removes the protection Buffett-style investing requires.

2. **IRA Medicare drug price negotiation of tirzepatide.** Jardiance was already cut January 1, 2026 with "low-to-mid teens" price headwinds. Mounjaro and Zepbound are logical next targets. A 30–40% mandated net price cut on the company's dominant product (56% of revenue) would materially compress earnings and the multiple simultaneously — a double blow.

3. **Oral GLP-1 competitive disruption.** Novo Nordisk's next-generation oral semaglutide, Pfizer's danuglipron, and Viking Therapeutics' pipeline represent a credible competitive wave of pill-form alternatives. If any of these achieve non-inferiority to tirzepatide in Phase 3 trials, the moat narrative weakens materially — particularly for Zepbound in obesity, where patient preference for an oral formulation is a real factor.

4. **Tirzepatide concentration risk.** At 56% of 2025 revenue from a single active ingredient, Lilly is more exposed to a single-product risk than most large-cap peers. A safety signal, label restriction, or competitive substitution hits 56% of the revenue base, not a modest fraction.

5. **Manufacturing capex as a potential stranded asset.** Lilly has committed $16B+ in GLP-1 manufacturing since 2023, with $7.8B capex in FY 2025 alone. This spending is justified by the demand thesis. If GLP-1 demand disappoints — whether due to pricing, competition, or regulatory caps — this manufacturing infrastructure becomes a costly burden on cash flow rather than a competitive moat.

# Circle of Competence — LLY

**Date:** 2026-03-11

## Detailed Analysis

**What does Eli Lilly sell, and to whom?** Eli Lilly is a global pharmaceutical company that discovers, manufactures, and sells branded prescription drugs. Its primary customers are pharmacies, hospitals, and pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) who purchase drugs on behalf of patients and health insurers. End patients are prescribed Lilly drugs by physicians. Lilly operates in three therapeutic areas: cardiometabolic/obesity (dominant), oncology, and neuroscience/immunology. As of early 2026, the cardiometabolic franchise — specifically the GLP-1/GIP dual agonist tirzepatide — is the overwhelming driver of revenue.

**How does it actually make money?** Lilly earns revenue through product sales of branded drugs. The model is: manufacture a drug under patent protection, price it at a premium (Mounjaro/Zepbound list prices are ~$1,000/month), collect gross revenue from wholesalers and PBMs, then subtract rebates and discounts to arrive at net realized revenue. Gross margins are very high — reported at approximately 83% in the high-growth phase of 2025 — because incremental manufacturing costs for a proven molecule are low relative to its pricing power. R&D and SG&A are the primary cost buckets, alongside the amortization of any acquired intangibles.

**Key revenue drivers:** (1) Tirzepatide volume: demand for GLP-1 therapies for diabetes and obesity is structurally large, with an estimated 170 million potential U.S. patients; (2) Net pricing: Lilly must balance list price with rebates paid to PBMs to maintain formulary access — a real pressure as payer pushback grows; (3) Pipeline launches: orforglipron (oral GLP-1, FDA decision expected 2026), retatrutide (triple agonist GLP-1/GIP/glucagon, Phase III readouts through 2026), and Kisunla (Alzheimer's, EU approval Q3 2025) are the next legs; (4) International: outside-U.S. Mounjaro revenue grew from $899M in Q4 2024 to $3.3B in Q4 2025, showing massive international runway.

**Revenue predictability:** Near-term (1–3 years) revenue is high-visibility given tirzepatide's compounding volume and Lilly's 2026 guidance of $80–83B. However, pharmaceutical revenue has inherent binary risks: a negative Phase III outcome, an FDA rejection, or an unexpected safety signal can abruptly end a product's trajectory. Patent expiry also creates a hard cliff — tirzepatide's core compound patent expires in 2036, with follow-on patents extending to 2041. Beyond 2030, the revenue picture depends heavily on whether retatrutide and orforglipron achieve blockbuster status as predicted ($30B and $16B in peak sales respectively by 2031). The revenue is less predictable over 5–10 year horizons than, say, a consumer staples company.

**Customer concentration and explainability:** Lilly's direct customers are a small number of large wholesale distributors (McKesson, AmerisourceBergen, Cardinal Health) and PBMs. This is industry-standard structure but creates dependency on a few counterparties for access and pricing. Lilly's business can be explained to an intelligent layperson in about two minutes: Lilly makes the most effective injectable drugs for type 2 diabetes and obesity. These drugs are in enormous demand globally. Lilly is growing revenue at 40–50% per year as the world wakes up to the GLP-1 revolution. The company is betting its next phase on an oral GLP-1 pill and a three-receptor drug even more potent than the current one.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Tirzepatide addresses one of the largest unmet medical needs in history; with orforglipron and retatrutide, Lilly extends the GLP-1 runway well past current product cycles.
- **Bear case:** Revenue and valuation are heavily concentrated in a single molecule class; any safety, pricing, or competitive shock to GLP-1 would disproportionately impact Lilly.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is simple to understand; the primary uncertainty is magnitude of success, not business model comprehension.

## Red Flags

- ~56% revenue concentration in two drugs sharing the same active ingredient (tirzepatide) creates single-point-of-failure risk
- PBM and payer rebate pressure on GLP-1 pricing is growing, net realized prices declining modestly even as volumes surge
- Pipeline binary risk: orforglipron and retatrutide are large embedded assumptions in the forward valuation

## Score: 8 / 10

Eli Lilly's business model is clean and understandable — a premium pharma company with extraordinary near-term visibility from GLP-1 dominance — but the patent-cliff dynamic and pipeline binary risk introduce complexity that prevents a top score.

### Section 2: Durable Competitive Advantage

# Durable Competitive Advantage — LLY

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-11
**Data Sources:** Web search (AlphaSpread, AInvest, EverytTicker/BeyondSPX, DrugDiscoveryTrends, PharmExec, BioPharma Dive, Lilly investor relations Q4 2025 press release, Chemical & Engineering News on patent expirations); training knowledge on pharmaceutical competitive dynamics.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Tirzepatide's core compound patent runs to 2036 with follow-on patents extending to 2041, providing a hard legal moat of 10–15 years. | 5 |
| 2 | Lilly has committed $50B in manufacturing investment since 2020, growing incretin production capacity 1.8x in 2025 — transforming supply from a constraint into a structural barrier. | 5 |
| 3 | Gross margins of approximately 83% in 2025 and ROE of ~103% are numerical evidence of exceptional pricing power and moat depth. | 4 |
| 4 | Orforglipron and retatrutide are positioned to succeed tirzepatide, suggesting the moat is not tied to a single molecule but to a compounding R&D engine and regulatory know-how. | 4 |
| 5 | Novo Nordisk is the only credible near-term rival; its semaglutide franchise (Ozempic/Wegovy) generated ~$26B in 2024 vs. Lilly's $36.5B in 2025, and clinical data suggests tirzepatide is modestly superior. | 4 |
| 6 | GLP-1 patent expiry concerns are real: a 2025 C&EN analysis flagged looming expirations drawing generic interest, and compounders have already sold tirzepatide during shortage periods. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Type of moat.** Eli Lilly's competitive advantage is multi-layered. The primary pillar is **intangible assets / patents**: tirzepatide is patent-protected as a compound until 2036 and via formulation/method patents to 2041. Drug development in this class requires a decade of clinical trials, billions in investment, and FDA/EMA approval — a regulatory moat that is functionally impossible to shortcut. Secondary pillars are **scale economics in manufacturing** (Lilly's $50B capex program is now yielding 1.8x incretin capacity at a cost per unit that new entrants cannot match) and **brand/clinical trust** (Mounjaro and Zepbound are now the reference compounds for efficacy comparisons in diabetes and obesity, and physicians have years of prescribing experience with them).

**Durability over 10 years.** The moat is credible through the mid-2030s under several conditions. Tirzepatide patent protection holds. Orforglipron (oral GLP-1, FDA decision expected 2026) and retatrutide (GLP-1/GIP/glucagon triple agonist, peak sales estimates of $30B by 2031) serve as successor compounds, extending the GLP-1 franchise beyond a single molecule. Lilly's pipeline depth — 50+ programs in clinical development — and its history of launching Mounjaro and Zepbound in rapid succession suggest a functioning R&D platform, not a one-hit wonder. That said, every pharmaceutical moat faces a hard date: post-2036 without new patent protection, generics will enter.

**Moat evidence in the numbers.** Gross margin of ~83% (2025) is exceptional even for pharma — most large-cap pharmaceutical companies operate at 70–80%. This reflects tirzepatide's premium pricing and Lilly's manufacturing efficiency improvements. Return on equity of ~103% is stratospheric, reflecting the inherent capital-lightness of a drug in its commercial ramp (heavy R&D sunk cost, very high incremental margins). Revenue grew 45% in 2025 and is guided to grow ~25% in 2026 to $80–83B, making Lilly the projected revenue leader in global pharma for 2026. These are moat-consistent results: only a company with genuine pricing power and demand pull can sustain this trajectory.

**Where is the moat weakest?** Three points. First, **GLP-1 class competition**: Novo Nordisk's semaglutide (Wegovy/Ozempic) is a near-equivalent product. Novo is not a weak competitor — it generates $26B/year from the same molecule class. Pfizer, AstraZeneca, and others have GLP-1 programs in development. While Lilly currently leads on weight loss efficacy, the gap is not insurmountable. Second, **payer/PBM pushback**: PBMs are already demanding larger rebates to maintain GLP-1 formulary access, which suppresses realized net prices even as volumes grow. Government payer expansion (Medicare GLP-1 coverage) may require further pricing concessions. Third, **compounders and generics**: during periods of supply shortage in 2023–2024, compounding pharmacies sold tirzepatide legally, and some still do. The FDA has since moved to tighten oversight, but this highlights how the market can route around patent protection under certain conditions.

**Does the advantage reset every product cycle?** This is the critical long-term question for Lilly. The company has demonstrated it can reset: it moved from Humalog/Trulicity (earlier-generation diabetes drugs) to tirzepatide successfully, and is already developing the next generation in orforglipron and retatrutide. The R&D capability — including its manufacturing platform and regulatory relationships — does not reset. However, the specific revenue concentration in tirzepatide means that clinical failure of the next-generation pipeline or a major safety finding would be a severe moat disruption. The advantage resets at the franchise level but is supported by institutional R&D capability that is more durable.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A $50B manufacturing moat, patents to 2041, and a pipeline of successive GLP-1 blockbusters (orforglipron, retatrutide) means Lilly can extend GLP-1 dominance for 15+ years.
- **Bear case:** Moats in pharma are ultimately time-limited by patent expiry, and Novo Nordisk's scale and resources mean the GLP-1 market is a duopoly that could erode Lilly's margins.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — moat is demonstrably wide today; durability past 2036 depends on pipeline execution, which has inherent uncertainty.

## Red Flags

- Tirzepatide compound patent expiry in 2036 creates a known cliff date; Lilly must have successor drugs commercially proven before then
- Novo Nordisk's semaglutide program is a well-capitalized near-equivalent competitor; this is not a monopoly
- PBM rebate pressure is eroding net pricing on GLP-1s even as volumes grow; realized prices declined modestly in 2025 despite 43% revenue growth

## Score: 8 / 10

Lilly has a wide, well-evidenced moat today — patents, manufacturing scale, R&D platform, and clinical credibility — but every pharmaceutical moat is ultimately bounded by time, and the 2036 patent cliff and Novo Nordisk competition prevent a score of 9 or 10.

# Business Economics — LLY

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Eli Lilly's ROIC has inflected sharply upward with the commercial success of the incretin franchise. ROIC was 13.9% in 2021, dipped to 10.2% in 2023 during the commercial ramp of Mounjaro, and has since accelerated to 16.97% in 2024 and 25.18% in 2025 — a level that compares favorably with the best compounders in any sector. ROE has followed a similar trajectory, from 48% in 2023 to 84% in 2024 and 101% in 2025. The ROE figures are somewhat inflated by the relatively modest equity base ($26.5B against $112.5B in total assets), but the asset-level returns (ROA of 22% in 2025) confirm genuine underlying capital productivity. The structural driver is simple: Mounjaro and Zepbound carry manufacturing costs well below their pricing, and as volumes have scaled, incremental margins have been very high.

**Margin Structure**

Gross margin has expanded from 74.2% in 2021 to 83.0% in 2025, a 9-point gain. This expansion is driven by two forces: (1) the product mix shift toward GLP-1 injectables and semaglutide-competitive drugs, which have very high gross margins at scale, and (2) manufacturing efficiencies as the company has moved from capacity-constrained supply to more normalized production. Operating margin has expanded even more dramatically, from 22.5% in 2021 to 40.4% in 2025, reflecting significant operating leverage on the SG&A and R&D base as revenue has scaled. Net margin was 31.7% in FY 2025. These are world-class margins for a pharmaceutical manufacturer, comparable to firms like Novo Nordisk and superior to most large pharma peers.

**Cash Generation**

Operating cash flow reached $16.8B in FY 2025, up 91% year-over-year. Free cash flow was $9.0B in 2025, compared with $3.8B in 2024 and just $0.8B in 2023 (when the capex surge began). The 2023 FCF trough reflects a deliberate, accelerated capital deployment into manufacturing — not a deterioration in business quality. FCF conversion (FCF as a percentage of net income) was 43% in 2025, which appears low, but normalizing for the growth-oriented capex overhang (i.e., assuming maintenance capex of ~$1.5–2B), owner earnings are closer to $15–16B — roughly 75–80% of net income. The company has explicitly guided that this capex cycle will moderate as capacity comes online. Maintenance capex for the core business is estimated at $1.5–2.0B. The heavy growth capex temporarily depresses FCF but is building a manufacturing moat for the incretin era.

**Revenue Quality**

Revenue quality has improved substantially. The two primary growth drivers — Mounjaro (tirzepatide for type 2 diabetes) and Zepbound (tirzepatide for obesity) — are chronic-use therapies taken via weekly auto-injector. Patient retention in GLP-1 medications is high once titrated, and the addressable population is enormous (estimated 100M+ Americans with obesity or type 2 diabetes). By FY 2025, the combined GLP-1 franchise is estimated to represent over 50% of total revenue given the trajectory. As GLP-1 manufacturing scales, variable production costs per unit should continue to decline, further supporting margin expansion.
