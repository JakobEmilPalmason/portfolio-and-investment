## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital**

Eli Lilly's ROIC has inflected sharply upward with the commercial success of the incretin franchise. ROIC was 13.9% in 2021, dipped to 10.2% in 2023 during the commercial ramp of Mounjaro, and has since accelerated to 16.97% in 2024 and 25.18% in 2025 — a level that compares favorably with the best compounders in any sector. ROE has followed a similar trajectory, from 48% in 2023 to 84% in 2024 and 101% in 2025. The ROE figures are somewhat inflated by the relatively modest equity base ($26.5B against $112.5B in total assets), but the asset-level returns (ROA of 22% in 2025) confirm genuine underlying capital productivity. The structural driver is simple: Mounjaro and Zepbound carry manufacturing costs well below their pricing, and as volumes have scaled, incremental margins have been very high.

**Margin Structure**

Gross margin has expanded from 74.2% in 2021 to 83.0% in 2025, a 9-point gain. This expansion is driven by two forces: (1) the product mix shift toward GLP-1 injectables and semaglutide-competitive drugs, which have very high gross margins at scale, and (2) manufacturing efficiencies as the company has moved from capacity-constrained supply to more normalized production. Operating margin has expanded even more dramatically, from 22.5% in 2021 to 40.4% in 2025, reflecting significant operating leverage on the SG&A and R&D base as revenue has scaled. Net margin was 31.7% in FY 2025. These are world-class margins for a pharmaceutical manufacturer, comparable to firms like Novo Nordisk and superior to most large pharma peers.

**Cash Generation**

Operating cash flow reached $16.8B in FY 2025, up 91% year-over-year. Free cash flow was $9.0B in 2025, compared with $3.8B in 2024 and just $0.8B in 2023 (when the capex surge began). The 2023 FCF trough reflects a deliberate, accelerated capital deployment into manufacturing — not a deterioration in business quality. FCF conversion (FCF as a percentage of net income) was 43% in 2025, which appears low, but normalizing for the growth-oriented capex overhang (i.e., assuming maintenance capex of ~$1.5–2B), owner earnings are closer to $15–16B — roughly 75–80% of net income. D&A was $2.0B in 2025, so owner earnings (net income + D&A – estimated maintenance capex) are approximately $20.6B + $2.0B – $1.8B = ~$20.8B.

**Capital Intensity**

Lilly is undergoing a historic shift in capital intensity. For most of its history it was a moderately asset-light pharmaceutical business with capex running $1.3–2.0B annually. In 2023–2025 it accelerated capital expenditure dramatically to $3.4B, $5.1B, and $7.8B respectively — a cumulative $16B+ investment cycle. This is almost entirely growth capex tied to GLP-1 manufacturing: new facilities in North Carolina, Indiana, Germany, and Ireland. The company has explicitly guided that this capex cycle will moderate as capacity comes online. Maintenance capex for the core business is estimated at $1.5–2.0B. The heavy growth capex temporarily depresses FCF but is building a manufacturing moat for the incretin era.

**Revenue Quality**

Revenue quality has improved substantially. The two primary growth drivers — Mounjaro (tirzepatide for type 2 diabetes) and Zepbound (tirzepatide for obesity) — are chronic-use therapies taken via weekly auto-injector. Patient retention in GLP-1 medications is high once titrated, and the addressable population is enormous (estimated 100M+ Americans with obesity or type 2 diabetes). In FY 2024, Mounjaro generated approximately $11.5B in revenue and Zepbound approximately $4.9B, together representing roughly 36% of the $45B total revenue. By FY 2025, the combined GLP-1 franchise is estimated to represent over 50% of total revenue given the trajectory. The remaining revenue comes from Verzenio (breast cancer, ~$4B), Jardiance (heart failure/diabetes, ~$2B), Taltz (psoriasis), Humalog/insulin (declining), and other mature products. The insulin franchise is declining but is being managed as a cash cow.

**Operating Leverage**

Operating leverage has been exceptional. Between FY 2023 and FY 2025, revenue grew 91% ($34.1B to $65.2B) while operating income grew 307% ($6.5B to $26.3B). This means every dollar of incremental revenue dropped through at roughly 51 cents of operating income — confirming very high operating leverage. The underlying mechanism is that SG&A and R&D do not scale proportionally with revenue once the GLP-1 commercial infrastructure is established. R&D as a percentage of revenue has declined from approximately 24% in 2022 to under 20% in 2025, and SG&A has remained relatively stable in dollar terms. As GLP-1 manufacturing scales, variable production costs per unit should continue to decline, further supporting margin expansion.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** ROIC of 25%+ is self-reinforcing — high returns fund the R&D and manufacturing investments that sustain the moat, making Lilly one of the most economically powerful pharmaceutical franchises in the world.
- **Bear case:** FCF conversion is suppressed by $7–8B annual growth capex, and if GLP-1 demand disappoints or competitive dynamics erode pricing, the capex-heavy manufacturing build-out becomes a stranded asset.
- **Confidence:** High — the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow data are well-sourced from FY 2021–2025 actuals; product mix estimates rely on training knowledge and widely-reported analyst data confirmed directionally by aggregate revenue figures.

## Red Flags

- FCF conversion currently low (~43%) due to massive growth capex cycle — normalizes only when manufacturing build-out completes.
- Heavy concentration in tirzepatide (Mounjaro + Zepbound): if a superior GLP-1/GIP competitor gains significant share, or if semaglutide oral formulations gain traction ahead of expectations, near-term revenue trajectory could disappoint.
- Capex of $7.8B in FY 2025 is now material enough that any manufacturing delay or quality issue at new facilities could have a financial impact.

## Score: 8 / 10

Eli Lilly has achieved world-class margins (83% gross, 40% operating) and ROIC of 25%+ in FY 2025, with explosive operating leverage, but FCF conversion is temporarily suppressed by the largest manufacturing investment cycle in the company's history, and revenue is heavily concentrated in a single molecule (tirzepatide) at a critical juncture of competitive pressure from Novo Nordisk.
