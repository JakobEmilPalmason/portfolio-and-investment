## Detailed Analysis

**Debt levels and leverage.** Analog Devices carries approximately $7.6–8.6B in total gross debt as of late 2025, up from pre-Maxim levels (~$3–4B) due to the $21 billion all-stock acquisition of Maxim Integrated in August 2021 (which also brought Maxim's existing debt onto ADI's balance sheet). Cash on hand has ranged from $1.8B to $2.4B in recent quarters, putting net debt in the range of approximately $5.3–6.8B. With EBITDA running at approximately $5B+ annually at FY2025 revenue levels (operating income of ~$2.6B EBIT plus D&A of approximately $2.1B per year), the net debt/EBITDA ratio is approximately 1.1–1.4x — well within conservative territory. For context, ratings agencies typically view 2x as comfortable and 4x+ as concerning for industrial-technology companies. ADI is firmly in the comfortable zone.

**Interest coverage.** Based on available data, ADI's EBIT of approximately $2.6B covers interest expense by approximately 8.5–12x depending on the time period and metric used. One data point shows interest coverage at 12.4x; another source cites 8.5x. Either figure is well above the 5x threshold that defines comfort and materially above the 3x warning level. Interest expense on ~$8B of debt at blended rates of approximately 3–4% runs roughly $280–320M annually, a negligible burden given ADI's FCF generation. There is no meaningful risk of interest expense impairing the business under realistic scenarios.

**Liquidity position.** ADI maintains approximately $1.8–2.4B of cash and short-term investments. In addition, the company operates a commercial paper program supporting up to $3.0 billion in short-term unsecured notes, giving it access to a substantial additional liquidity buffer. Current liabilities stood at approximately $2.7B as of May 2025. The current ratio (current assets / current liabilities) is not explicitly stated in available data but, given cash balances and the nature of ADI's assets, there is no short-term liquidity concern. The company generated $5.1B in operating cash flow over the trailing twelve months ending Q1 FY2026, providing continuous replenishment of liquidity.

**Refinancing risk and debt maturity profile.** ADI has issued staggered fixed-rate notes, including tranches maturing June 2028 (~$850M net proceeds) and June 2030 (~$650M net proceeds). The staggered maturity schedule and access to capital markets for a blue-chip investment-grade semiconductor company suggest minimal refinancing risk. ADI carries an investment-grade credit rating (it was rated Baa1/BBB+ at acquisition close and has been on stable/improving outlook). The company does not depend on capital markets being open to meet near-term obligations — its annual FCF of $4.3–4.6B far exceeds any single-year maturity.

**Off-balance-sheet obligations.** ADI has operating lease obligations (fab equipment, office leases) which are standard for the industry and not unusual in size. Pension obligations are present but not material relative to the overall balance sheet. No significant contingent liabilities are flagged in recent filings. The $3.0B commercial paper program is a liquidity tool, not a structural debt reliance.

**Stress test: revenue down 30% for two years.** ADI's prior cycle trough (FY2023–FY2024) provides a real-world stress test: revenue fell from approximately $12B (FY2022 peak) to $9.4B (FY2024 trough), a decline of ~22%. During this period, ADI continued to generate positive FCF and continued paying and growing its dividend. A hypothetical 30% revenue decline from FY2025's $11B would bring revenue to approximately $7.7B. At ADI's structural cost base and fab-lite model, operating margins would compress but remain positive — peer history and management commentary suggest adjusted operating margins of 30%+ are defensible even in deep troughs. FCF at that revenue level would fall to approximately $2–2.5B, still well above annual interest expense of ~$300M and annual dividend of ~$1.4B. ADI could absorb a 30% revenue drop for two years without needing to issue equity. Deleveraging would slow but not reverse.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Net debt/EBITDA of ~1.1–1.4x, interest coverage of 8.5–12x, and $4.3–4.6B annual FCF give ADI a fortress-adjacent balance sheet; even in a severe cycle downturn it is self-funding.
- **Bear case:** Gross debt of ~$8.6B is large in absolute terms and reflects Maxim acquisition leverage; in a prolonged deep downturn, the dividend (~$1.4B/year) plus interest could absorb the majority of trough FCF.
- **Confidence:** High — debt levels, cash balances, and interest coverage data are sourced from recent earnings releases and SEC filings; stress test uses actual prior-cycle margin data.

## Red Flags

- Gross debt of ~$8.6B is the direct legacy of the Maxim acquisition and remains elevated relative to pre-2021 levels; deleveraging is in progress but not complete.
- A $3.0B commercial paper program, while a normal liquidity tool, adds a layer of short-term refinancing exposure that would be tested in a severe credit market dislocation.
- Dividend commitment of ~$1.4B/year limits flexibility at trough FCF; management has prioritized dividend growth, which could be problematic in an extreme scenario.

## Score: 8 / 10

ADI earns an 8 for conservative net leverage (~1.1–1.4x net debt/EBITDA), strong interest coverage (8.5–12x), ample liquidity, and FCF generation that comfortably covers all obligations — with a one-point deduction for the elevated gross debt absolute level inherited from Maxim and the dividend commitment that reduces financial flexibility at cycle troughs.
