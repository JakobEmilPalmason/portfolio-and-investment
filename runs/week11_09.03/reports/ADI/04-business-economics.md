## Detailed Analysis

**Revenue and growth quality.** ADI generates revenue across four end markets: Industrial (~47–53% of revenue), Automotive (~20–30%), Communications (~13–15%), and Consumer (remainder). In FY2025, total revenue reached approximately $11.0 billion, up ~17% from FY2024's ~$9.4 billion, itself a down year due to inventory digestion across the analog semiconductor cycle. The industrial and communications verticals led FY2025 recovery. By Q1 FY2026 (ended January 31, 2026), quarterly revenue had reached $3.16 billion (+30% YoY), with industrial up 38% YoY and communications up 63% YoY driven by data center and wireless demand. The revenue base is genuinely recurring: approximately half comes from products launched more than 10 years ago. Customers design ADI chips into their systems for multi-year or decade-long production runs, making displacement extremely rare. This is sticky, repeating revenue — not transactional.

**Margin structure.** ADI's gross margin on an adjusted basis consistently runs in the 68–70% range; on a GAAP basis, gross margins have hovered near 57–62% due to Maxim-related amortization. In Q3 FY2025, GAAP gross margin was 69.2%, suggesting amortization drag is diminishing as Maxim intangibles wind down. Adjusted operating margin reached ~42% in Q3 FY2025 and was guided toward 43.5% in Q4. For FY2024, the company maintained operating margins above 40% on an adjusted basis even in a down-cycle year (FY2024 revenue was ~9.4B vs. the prior peak of ~$12B in FY2022). This is a meaningful demonstration of operating leverage: ADI's cost base is largely fixed (R&D, SG&A), so margin expansion on incremental revenue is significant. As FY2025 revenue recovers toward prior peaks, operating leverage is clearly flowing through.

**FCF generation and conversion.** In FY2025, ADI generated approximately $4.3 billion in free cash flow (~39% of revenue), with operating cash flow of approximately $4.8 billion and capex of approximately $500 million. By the TTM period ending Q1 FY2026, FCF had risen to $4.6 billion (39% of ~$11.8B annualized revenue). FCF conversion from net income is inflated by the large non-cash amortization charge from Maxim (which runs through net income but not FCF), but even normalized, cash conversion is robust. Owner earnings (net income + D&A minus maintenance capex) are high given the asset-light fab-lite model. In FY2024, D&A was approximately $2.1B annually (Q4 alone was $520M), and maintenance capex is estimated at roughly $200–300M of the total $730M capex spend; the remainder is growth/capacity investment.

**Capital intensity and the fab-lite model.** ADI operates a fab-lite hybrid: it owns some manufacturing capacity (bipolar, MEMS, high-performance processes that are difficult to outsource) but contracts out a significant portion of CMOS production to foundries like TSMC. Long-term capex guidance of 4–6% of revenue reflects a moderately capital-intensive model for the industry, but meaningfully below pure foundry or IDM peers. In FY2024, total capex was ~$730M (~7.8% of FY2024 revenue), above the long-term guide, as ADI invested in manufacturing resilience post-COVID. This is now normalizing back toward the 4–6% range, improving FCF trajectory. The business does not require continuous heavy reinvestment to maintain its competitive position — the moat comes from IP, customer design-ins, and process expertise, not from ever-larger fabs.

**ROIC — the Maxim distortion.** Reported GAAP ROIC is approximately 3–7% over FY2021–FY2025, which looks unimpressive. However, this is almost entirely an artifact of the $21B Maxim acquisition creating approximately $20B+ of goodwill and intangibles on the balance sheet, inflating the invested capital denominator. The underlying operating business, stripped of acquisition premium, earns far higher returns. Adjusted ROIC (using tangible invested capital or excluding acquisition goodwill) is meaningfully higher — consistent with ADI's pricing power, high gross margins, and the analog semiconductor economics of long product lifecycles and sticky customer relationships. Investors should view the GAAP ROIC as an accounting artifact rather than a signal of capital destruction.

**Operating leverage.** The analog semiconductor business model exhibits strong operating leverage. ADI's variable costs (wafer costs, some direct labor) are a fraction of revenue; R&D and SG&A are largely fixed. As revenue recovers from FY2024's trough toward FY2022 peak levels ($12B+), margins expand rapidly. The Q1 FY2026 trajectory — revenue +30% YoY, FCF TTM up to $4.6B — validates this dynamic. Incremental margins on cyclical recovery are high, compounding the already-exceptional FCF yield at scale.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Recurring design-in revenue, 40%+ adjusted operating margins, and $4.3–4.6B annual FCF production represent world-class business economics that compound over full cycles.
- **Bear case:** GAAP ROIC is structurally dampened by goodwill; the business is moderately cyclical (FY2023 saw revenue fall from $12B to $9.4B), and peak FCF is only achievable at cycle tops.
- **Confidence:** High — margin, FCF, and revenue data are from published earnings releases; ROIC interpretation is well-supported by the known Maxim acquisition accounting.

## Red Flags

- GAAP ROIC of 3–7% is weak in absolute terms; investors must understand the Maxim goodwill distortion to interpret correctly.
- FY2022–FY2024 cyclical decline (from $12B to $9.4B revenue) demonstrates meaningful semiconductor cycle exposure — this is not fully recession-resistant.
- Capex in FY2024 ran above long-term guidance (7–8% vs. 4–6%), though this appears to be normalizing.

## Score: 8 / 10

ADI earns an 8 due to exceptional FCF generation, world-class adjusted operating margins (~40–43%), genuinely recurring design-in revenue, and moderate capital intensity — offset only by the cyclicality inherent in the analog semiconductor industry and the GAAP ROIC distortion from the Maxim acquisition goodwill.
