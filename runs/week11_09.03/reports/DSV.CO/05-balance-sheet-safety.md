## Detailed Analysis

**Post-Acquisition Debt Structure.** The Schenker acquisition transformed DSV's balance sheet fundamentally. The total enterprise value paid was approximately DKK 106.7bn (~EUR 14.3bn), of which net cash outflow was DKK 75.8bn (after DKK 11bn of Schenker's own cash was acquired). Financing was split roughly equally: approximately DKK 37.5bn raised through a new equity issuance (completed October 2024, before closing) and approximately DKK 37.5bn raised via a multi-tranche bond issuance denominated in euros. Net interest-bearing debt (including lease liabilities) at year-end 2025 was DKK 86,624m (~EUR 11.6bn), compared to approximately DKK 20–22bn prior to the transaction (pre-acquisition standalone debt). This is a fourfold increase in gross financial debt, concentrated in the 2024–2025 financing window.

**Leverage Ratio.** The gearing ratio at year-end 2025 was 2.8x EBITDA (as defined by DSV: net interest-bearing debt including lease liabilities / EBITDA before special items). At the completion of the acquisition in April 2025, the pro-forma gearing was approximately 3.0x. The improvement from 3.0x to 2.8x within eight months of ownership reflects the strength of combined free cash flow — DKK 16,335m of adjusted FCF was generated in 2025, and ~DKK 7bn was applied to debt reduction. DSV's internal target is below 2.0x, which it aims to reach by H1 2027. To close the gap from 2.8x to 2.0x requires approximately DKK 25–30bn of further net debt reduction (depending on EBITDA growth), implying sustained FCF deployment at current or higher rates. This is achievable but assumes no meaningful earnings disappointment.

**Interest Coverage.** Specific interest expense figures for 2025 were not disclosed in the publicly available summaries reviewed, but the following estimate can be constructed: at DKK 86,624m of net debt and an assumed blended interest rate of approximately 3.0–3.5% (consistent with the EUR bond tranches issued in 2024 at coupons ranging from 3.375% to implied market rates for 6-8 year maturities), annual interest expense is approximately DKK 2,600–3,000m. DSV's EBIT before special items for 2025 was DKK 19,611m. This implies an interest coverage ratio (EBIT / interest) of approximately 6.5–7.5x — comfortably above the 5x threshold considered comfortable for investment-grade corporates, and above the 3x warning level. The 2026 EBIT guidance mid-point of DKK 24,250m would push coverage to approximately 8–9x even if interest expense rises modestly with refinancing. Coverage is not a near-term concern.

**Debt Maturity Schedule.** The bond tranches issued in October 2024 are euro-denominated and laddered across maturities: EUR 650m due 2026, EUR 600m due 2026, EUR 1,000m due 2028, EUR 1,250m due 2030, EUR 750m due 2032, and EUR 750m due 2034. The two 2026 tranches total approximately EUR 1.25bn (~DKK 9–10bn at current EUR/DKK rates), representing a meaningful near-term refinancing obligation. At DSV's current FCF run-rate of DKK 16bn+, internal cash generation alone could repay these tranches without accessing capital markets, reducing refinancing risk significantly. Longer-dated maturities (2028–2034) are well-distributed and provide structural stability.

**Liquidity Position.** DSV maintains a committed revolving credit facility alongside its bond programme, though the precise size of undrawn facilities post-Schenker was not publicly itemised in available summaries. The company generated DKK 16,335m in adjusted FCF in 2025 and has communicated that share buybacks are suspended until the gearing ratio returns below 2.0x — this capital allocation discipline effectively directs all surplus cash to debt reduction, strengthening the liquidity runway. Cash on hand (including Schenker's acquired DKK 11bn at closing) provides an additional buffer.

**Credit Ratings.** S&P Global maintains DSV at A- with a negative outlook, having revised the outlook from stable upon announcement of the Schenker deal. The negative outlook reflects the risk that leverage does not deleverage to A- commensurate levels within 18 months of deal close (i.e., by approximately October 2026). Moody's has affirmed DSV Finance at A3 (equivalent to A-) with a stable outlook, and expects Moody's-adjusted debt/EBITDA to fall to approximately 2.5x over 12–18 months. The divergence in outlook (S&P negative, Moody's stable) reflects differing methodological treatments of lease obligations and integration execution confidence. A one-notch downgrade to BBB+ by S&P would be meaningful but not disruptive — DSV would retain investment-grade access to bond markets.

**Stress Test: -30% Revenue for 2 Years.** A 30% revenue decline for DSV would mechanically compress gross profit and EBIT. Using 2025 as the base: a 30% revenue fall implies approximately DKK 173bn of revenue. DSV's cost structure is largely variable (it pays carriers on a per-shipment basis), so gross profit percentage would likely be preserved at roughly 20–21%, yielding gross profit of approximately DKK 35–36bn. However, EBIT margins would compress substantially as fixed overheads (headcount, leases, IT, amortisation) cannot be cut quickly. A reasonable stressed EBIT estimate in a -30% scenario might be DKK 8–10bn — compared to interest expense of ~DKK 3bn, the company would remain cash-generative and EBIT-positive, though FCF available for debt service would drop sharply. With DKK 86bn of net debt, two years of minimal debt reduction would leave the company at roughly 3.5–4x EBITDA, which is uncomfortable but not existential for an A-rated borrower with laddered maturities. Equity issuance would likely not be required in this scenario, though dividend cuts and deeper cost actions would be necessary. Survival without issuing equity: likely yes, but with credit rating pressure.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** DSV reduces net debt by DKK 15–20bn over 2026–2027 via strong FCF, reaches below 2.0x gearing by H1 2027 as guided, S&P returns to stable outlook, and the balance sheet risk is fully resolved within 18 months.
- **Bear case:** A global freight recession (similar in magnitude to 2023) hits in 2026, EBIT disappoints relative to the DKK 23–25.5bn guidance, deleveraging stalls above 2.5x, and S&P downgrades to BBB+ — widening DSV's funding costs and increasing financial risk precisely when integration spending is at its peak.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The deleveraging trajectory is credible given FCF generation, but the 2.8x starting point and negative S&P outlook leave limited room for error; this is a balance sheet under repair, not a fortress.

---

## Red Flags

- Net debt including leases of DKK 86,624m is approximately 4x the pre-Schenker debt level — a step-change in financial risk that takes years to fully normalise
- S&P's negative outlook on the A- rating implies a real probability of a one-notch downgrade if FCF disappoints or integration falters in 2026
- Two EUR bond tranches totalling ~EUR 1.25bn mature in 2026; while likely manageable with existing FCF, refinancing in a higher-rate environment is not cost-free
- Schenker's Road and Contract Logistics assets include significant operating lease obligations (warehouses, terminals, trucks) not fully quantified in public summaries — total lease liabilities on the combined balance sheet are materially larger than DSV's legacy DKK 14.1bn figure
- Share buybacks are suspended and dividends held at DKK 7.00/share — the balance sheet is constraining capital returns to shareholders for the foreseeable future
- Pension liabilities (excluded from gearing calculation) likely grew substantially with the Schenker addition — Schenker operates in Germany and other European markets with defined-benefit plan obligations

---

## Score: 5 / 10

DSV's balance sheet carries elevated but manageable leverage at 2.8x EBITDA post-Schenker, with a credible deleveraging path and investment-grade ratings intact; however, the near-term debt burden, suspended buybacks, negative S&P outlook, and limited stress-test buffer collectively place this squarely in the "moderate leverage, not conservative" bracket until at least 2027.
