## Detailed Analysis

**Owner Earnings Today: The SBC Problem**

Snowflake's owner earnings calculation is the single most important thing to understand about this stock's valuation. Start with GAAP net income: negative $1.33 billion for FY2026. Add back depreciation and amortization (relatively small for a cloud-native company — capital expenditures were only about $46M-$104M in recent fiscal years, and D&A is similarly modest). Subtract maintenance capex (minimal for a software business). The result: owner earnings are deeply negative, somewhere around negative $1.2 billion. The company's "adjusted free cash flow" of roughly $1.17B (25% of $4.68B revenue) looks attractive, but it is a non-GAAP metric that strips out stock-based compensation of $1.4 billion. SBC is a real cost — it dilutes existing shareholders. When you add SBC back, there are no owner earnings to speak of. This is the fundamental tension in Snowflake's valuation story: cash generation looks solid on an adjusted basis, but GAAP profitability remains elusive because the company is paying a huge portion of its workforce in equity. SBC has improved from 43% of revenue in FY2025 to 36% in FY2026, and if that trend continues toward 20-25% over the next 3-5 years as the company scales, genuine GAAP profitability becomes plausible. But it is not here yet.

**Scenario Analysis**

For all scenarios, I use a 10% discount rate and a 5-year horizon (FY2031), then apply a terminal multiple to estimated owner earnings or free cash flow.

*Bear Case (things go wrong):* Revenue growth decelerates to 15-18% annually as AI workloads shift to competitors like Databricks, consumption-based pricing proves volatile in a downturn, and SBC remains elevated at 30%+ of revenue. By FY2031, revenue reaches ~$9B, adjusted FCF margin is 20% ($1.8B), but after SBC of ~$2.7B (30%), true owner earnings are still negative. Applying a 25x multiple to the $1.8B adjusted FCF and discounting back at 10% gives an enterprise value of roughly $28B, or about $84/share. Alternatively, at 5x revenue, you get $45B enterprise value, or about $134/share. Bear case range: $85-$135/share, implying 20-50% downside from current levels.

*Base Case (reasonable continuation):* Revenue compounds at 22-25% annually, reaching ~$11.5B by FY2031. Non-GAAP operating margins expand to 18-20%. SBC declines to 22-25% of revenue (~$2.6B). Adjusted FCF margin reaches 28% (~$3.2B). True owner earnings (FCF minus SBC excess over cash comp equivalent) are roughly $1.5-2.0B. At a 30x multiple on $1.8B true FCF, discounted at 10%, enterprise value is approximately $55-60B, or $165-$180/share. This is essentially where the stock trades today, suggesting the market is pricing in the base case with reasonable accuracy.

*Bull Case (things go right):* AI-driven data consumption accelerates growth to 28-30% annually, Snowflake captures dominant share of the AI data cloud, and operating leverage kicks in aggressively. Revenue reaches $14B by FY2031. SBC drops to 18% of revenue. Non-GAAP operating margin hits 25%. Adjusted FCF margin reaches 32% ($4.5B). True owner earnings reach $3.0B+. At 35x, discounted back, enterprise value is approximately $100B, or ~$300/share, roughly 80% upside. This aligns with the high end of analyst price targets ($280-$500).

**Multiples in Context**

At ~12x trailing revenue (P/S), Snowflake is cheaper than at almost any point in its public history — the stock has traded at 30-100x revenue in the past. However, the growth rate has also decelerated from 100%+ to ~29%. On a price-to-next-twelve-months revenue basis (~10x), it is comparable to other high-growth cloud peers but still at a premium to the broader SaaS universe (5-8x for 20-25% growers). Versus Databricks (private, ~16x NTM revenue, ~55% growth), Snowflake actually looks cheaper on a growth-adjusted basis. Versus Datadog (~12x NTM revenue, similar growth), Snowflake trades roughly in line. The EV/EBITDA multiple is not meaningful because GAAP EBITDA remains negative. The stock has no traditional P/E ratio.

**What Must Be True for Today's Price to Be Justified**

At $168/share and ~$57B market cap (approximately $53B enterprise value after netting cash), the market is pricing in: (1) continued 22-27% revenue growth for the next 3-5 years, (2) meaningful margin expansion with SBC declining as a percentage of revenue, (3) eventual true GAAP profitability by FY2029-2030, and (4) a terminal valuation multiple of 25-30x true free cash flow. These assumptions are not heroic — they are roughly in line with management guidance and current trajectory. But they do require execution: the company must successfully monetize AI workloads, defend against Databricks competition, and demonstrate operating leverage. There is little room for negative surprises.

**Implied Expectations vs. Likely Reality**

The market is not pricing in perfection — that was the case when SNOW traded at $300-$400 in 2021-2022. At $168, the market has significantly reset expectations. However, it is also not pricing in disaster. The stock is roughly fairly valued based on base-case assumptions. The key swing factor is whether AI-driven data consumption becomes a durable tailwind (bull case) or whether Databricks and other competitors erode Snowflake's competitive position (bear case). The $9.77B in remaining performance obligations (+42% YoY) and stable 125% net revenue retention provide some comfort that near-term growth is secure. The longer-term trajectory beyond FY2028 is where uncertainty increases substantially.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** AI data consumption accelerates growth above 25% for 5+ years, SBC normalizes below 20% of revenue, and Snowflake generates $3B+ in true owner earnings by FY2031 — stock worth $280-$300.
- **Bear case:** Growth decelerates to mid-teens as Databricks wins the AI workload war, SBC stays elevated, and the stock re-rates to 5-7x revenue — implying $85-$135/share, or 20-50% downside.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The base case analysis is well-supported by current financial data and guidance, but the 5-year trajectory depends heavily on competitive dynamics in AI/data platforms, which are highly uncertain.

## Red Flags

- Stock-based compensation of $1.4B (36% of revenue) means GAAP profitability is years away and existing shareholders face ongoing dilution, even as share count has been relatively stable near 335M shares.
- Consumption-based revenue model creates inherent volatility — customers can dial usage up or down, making revenue less predictable than subscription SaaS.
- Databricks is growing nearly 2x faster at similar scale and has raised $100B in private valuation, signaling intense competitive pressure in the data platform market.
- The gap between adjusted free cash flow ($1.17B) and GAAP net income (-$1.33B) is roughly $2.5B — this level of non-GAAP adjustment should make any investor uncomfortable about the "real" economics.
- Reverse DCF suggests the stock is priced for near-perfect execution on growth AND margin expansion simultaneously — limited margin of safety.

## Score: 5 / 10

Snowflake appears roughly fairly valued at $168/share. The base case analysis yields an intrinsic value range of $165-$180, which is essentially the current price. There is no clear margin of safety for a Buffett-style investor — the stock requires optimistic (but not unreasonable) assumptions about AI-driven growth and margin expansion to justify meaningful upside, and the massive stock-based compensation makes true owner earnings elusive. This is a "watch, don't own" situation until either the price drops meaningfully or the company demonstrates sustained GAAP profitability.
