## Detailed Analysis

### Owner Earnings Today

Owner earnings = net income + depreciation & amortization - maintenance capex. For TSMC, this calculation requires separating maintenance capex (keeping existing capacity running) from growth capex (building new fabs and capacity). TSMC does not explicitly break out maintenance vs. growth capex, but industry analysts typically estimate maintenance capex for a leading-edge fab company at roughly 30-40% of total capex, with the remainder being growth investment.

For FY2025: Net income was approximately $55B (NT$1.72 trillion). Depreciation and amortization was approximately $53B (annualizing the trailing twelve-month figure of $52.6B through mid-2025, with D&A rising through the year). Total capex was $40.9B. If we estimate maintenance capex at roughly 35% of total capex, that gives us ~$14B in maintenance capex. Owner earnings = $55B + $53B - $14B = ~$94B. However, this number is misleadingly high because depreciation currently exceeds total capex -- the legacy asset base is depreciating faster than new assets are being added to service (since new fab spending ramps depreciation with a lag). A more conservative and honest approach: net income minus the portion of capex not covered by depreciation gives us approximately $55B + ($53B - $40.9B) = $67B in rough cash earnings. But the truest owner earnings figure is simply free cash flow adjusted for growth spending. FY2025 free cash flow was ~$32B (NT$1,003B). If we add back growth capex (~$27B), owner earnings are roughly $59B. I will use a range of $55-60B for current normalized owner earnings, acknowledging this is somewhat generous given the capital intensity cycle TSMC is in.

On a per-ADR basis (5.19B ADR-equivalent shares): owner earnings = roughly $10.60-$11.60 per ADR share.

### Scenario Analysis (bear/base/bull with 5-year projections)

All scenarios use a 10% discount rate and project to Year 5 (FY2030). TSMC has ~5.19B ADR-equivalent shares outstanding.

**Bear Case: Growth Disappoints, Margins Compress**

Assumptions: Revenue grows at 15% CAGR (vs. guided 25%) due to AI spending slowdown, competition from Intel 18A/Samsung, or macro headwinds. Net margins compress from 45% to 38% as overseas fab costs weigh on profitability. Owner earnings in Year 5: Revenue ~$245B x 38% margin = $93B net income. With higher D&A from massive capex cycle, maintenance capex rises. Owner earnings ~$75B. Terminal multiple: 18x (reflecting slower growth, in line with historical average). Enterprise value = $75B x 18 = $1,350B. Discounted back 5 years at 10% = $1,350B / 1.61 = $838B. Per ADR share: $838B / 5.19B = ~$161.

**Base Case: Guided Growth Materializes**

Assumptions: Revenue grows at 22% CAGR (slightly below the guided 25%, being conservative). Net margins hold at 42% as overseas fabs mature and pricing power offsets higher costs. Owner earnings in Year 5: Revenue ~$330B x 42% = $139B net income. Depreciation catches up with capex as the investment cycle matures. Owner earnings ~$110B. Terminal multiple: 22x (reasonable for a high-quality, moderate-growth company). Enterprise value = $110B x 22 = $2,420B. Discounted back 5 years at 10% = $2,420B / 1.61 = $1,503B. Per ADR share: $1,503B / 5.19B = ~$290.

**Bull Case: AI Supercycle Exceeds Expectations**

Assumptions: Revenue grows at 28% CAGR as AI spending accelerates beyond current projections and TSMC captures even more share with 2nm/A16 dominance. Net margins expand to 47% as advanced nodes command premium pricing and scale efficiencies kick in. Owner earnings in Year 5: Revenue ~$420B x 47% = $197B net income. Capex intensity normalizes. Owner earnings ~$165B. Terminal multiple: 25x (reflecting continued growth and monopoly-like market position). Enterprise value = $165B x 25 = $4,125B. Discounted back 5 years at 10% = $4,125B / 1.61 = $2,562B. Per ADR share: $2,562B / 5.19B = ~$494.

**Summary:**
- Bear: ~$161/ADR (55% downside)
- Base: ~$290/ADR (18% downside)
- Bull: ~$494/ADR (40% upside)

The probability-weighted value depends on how you weight these scenarios. At equal weighting: ($161 + $290 + $494) / 3 = ~$315, roughly 11% below today's price. Weighting the base case more heavily (50% base, 25% each bear/bull): $315 as well. This suggests the stock is roughly fairly valued to slightly overvalued at current levels.

### Multiples in Context

TSMC currently trades at:
- **Trailing P/E:** ~33x (vs. 5-year average of ~24x, 10-year average of ~22x)
- **Forward P/E:** ~26x (on 2026E EPS of ~$13.60)
- **EV/EBITDA:** ~19x
- **P/FCF:** ~50x (elevated because FCF is depressed by massive capex)

The trailing P/E of 33x is 40-50% above historical norms. However, context matters: TSMC's growth rate has also accelerated well beyond historical norms (35.9% revenue growth in 2025 vs. low-teens in prior years). A 33x multiple on rapidly growing, high-margin earnings is not unreasonable -- but it does assume the growth continues.

Peers offer limited comparison. GlobalFoundries (~30x P/E) operates at much lower margins and in trailing-edge nodes. Samsung's semiconductor division (~30-38x P/E) is struggling with yields and losing foundry share. Intel Foundry Services is not separately valued and is burning cash. TSMC deserves a premium over all of them, but the size of that premium at current prices is the question.

A 26x forward P/E on 2026E earnings implies about 20%+ earnings growth is expected going forward. Given TSMC's guided 25% revenue CAGR through 2029 and potential for margin expansion, this is not heroic -- but it is not conservative either. The market is pricing in a continuation of strong execution.

### What Must Be True for Today's Price

At $354/ADR and ~$1.84T market cap, and assuming a 10% required return, the stock needs to compound intrinsic value at 10% annually. That means in 5 years, intrinsic value needs to be ~$1.84T x 1.61 = ~$2.96T.

If we assign a terminal P/E of 22x (normalizing toward long-run averages as growth decelerates), that implies net income of $2.96T / 22 = $135B in Year 5. That would require net income to grow from $55B to $135B, a ~20% CAGR. This is achievable if revenue hits ~$330B (22% CAGR) and margins hold at ~41%. These are not outlandish assumptions, but they are firmly in the "things go according to plan" category.

What would break the thesis: (1) AI spending slows or shifts away from leading-edge silicon, (2) competitors close the technology gap, (3) overseas fab costs permanently compress margins, (4) geopolitical disruption, or (5) the market re-rates the multiple downward to historical norms.

### Implied Expectations vs. Reality

The market is pricing in strong but not extreme growth. A 26x forward P/E for a company growing earnings 20-30% annually with 45%+ net margins and near-monopoly market position is not "pricing in perfection." However, it is pricing in consistently excellent execution.

The risk is asymmetric in a subtle way: if TSMC delivers on its guidance, the stock may return a reasonable 10-15% annually. If something goes meaningfully wrong (demand miss, margin compression, geopolitical shock), the downside is 30-50%. The stock offers moderate upside with meaningful downside risk. That is not a Buffett-style setup, which typically involves buying when the market is pricing in disaster on a great business. Here, the market is pricing in success.

One important nuance: the P/FCF of ~50x looks alarming, but free cash flow is artificially depressed by the massive growth capex cycle. As TSMC's capex intensity normalizes in 2028-2030 and depreciation catches up, FCF should inflect sharply higher. The market appears to be looking through the current FCF trough, which is probably correct.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** If the AI supercycle sustains a 25%+ revenue CAGR through 2029 and TSMC's pricing power keeps margins above 45%, the stock is modestly undervalued at current prices and could reach $450-500.
- **Bear case:** If AI spending decelerates, overseas fab costs pressure margins, or geopolitical tensions escalate, the stock could trade down to $160-200, representing 40-55% downside from current levels.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The business quality is very high, but the current valuation leaves limited margin of safety; the range of reasonable outcomes is wide, and geopolitical tail risk is unquantifiable.

## Red Flags

- Stock trades 40-50% above its 5-year average P/E, requiring sustained above-historical growth to justify the premium.
- Capex is rising sharply ($52-56B in 2026, up 30% YoY), and if demand disappoints, these become sunk costs that drag on returns.
- Concentration risk: ~67-71% of global foundry revenue depends on a single company in a geopolitically sensitive location.
- Free cash flow ($32B) is well below net income ($55B), and this gap will widen before it narrows as 2026 capex ramps.
- Overseas fabs (Arizona, Japan, Germany) cost 30-50% more to build and operate than Taiwan fabs, creating structural margin pressure.
- No meaningful share buyback program; capital return is primarily through modest dividends (~0.65% yield).
- The 2nm node is expected to surpass 3nm and 5nm in revenue by Q3 2026 -- any yield problems or delays would be material.

## Score: 5 / 10

TSMC is a world-class business trading at a fair-to-slightly-rich price. The base case suggests modest downside, the bear case is severe, and the bull case offers moderate upside. There is no clear margin of safety at current levels. For a Buffett-style investor, the right move is to admire the business and wait for a better price -- or accept fair-value returns with meaningful risk.
