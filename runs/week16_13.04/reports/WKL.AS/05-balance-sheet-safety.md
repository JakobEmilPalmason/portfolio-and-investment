## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure: Rising but Manageable.** Total debt has grown from EUR 3.6B in FY22 to EUR 4.9B in FY25, with long-term debt rising from EUR 2.6B to EUR 4.0B. Net debt (debt minus cash) increased from EUR 2.0B to EUR 3.8B. The Debt/EBITDA ratio has ticked up modestly from 2.0x to 2.2x, and Net Debt/EBITDA from 1.1x to 1.7x. Both remain within the company's stated target range of 1.5x to 2.5x. However, the direction matters: three years ago this was a barely-leveraged business; today it carries meaningfully more debt. The reason is straightforward — Wolters Kluwer has been returning capital to shareholders through approximately EUR 1.0-1.1B in buybacks annually for four consecutive years, plus a growing dividend (EUR 2.52/share for FY25, up 8%). Total shareholder returns have been running at roughly EUR 1.6B per year against free cash flow of EUR 1.3-1.4B. The gap has been funded by incremental debt. This is a deliberate capital allocation choice, not operational deterioration, but it has a limit.

**Interest Coverage: Comfortable but Deteriorating.** EBIT/Interest coverage was 20.3x in FY22 and has dropped to 15.1x in FY25. This is still extremely comfortable — any coverage above 5x is safe by conventional standards, and 15x provides enormous cushion. But the trajectory tells a story: interest expense has risen from EUR 66M to EUR 117M (77% increase), reflecting both higher debt balances and higher coupons on newly issued bonds. In March 2025, Wolters Kluwer issued a EUR 500M seven-year Eurobond at 3.375% coupon, and in June 2025 a EUR 500M five-year Eurobond — both at rates well above the sub-1% rates available during 2020-2021. Management guided adjusted net financing costs rising to approximately EUR 110M for FY26 (from EUR 86M adjusted in FY25), confirming the trend will continue. Even so, on EUR 1.7B of EBIT, an interest bill of EUR 110-120M is manageable.

**Debt Maturity Profile: Well-Laddered, No Refinancing Cliff.** The 2025 bond issuances extended the average debt maturity from roughly 4.5 years to approximately 6 years. The two new EUR 500M Eurobonds mature in 2030 and 2032, spreading the refinancing burden across the late 2020s and early 2030s. This means Wolters Kluwer does not face a near-term maturity wall. The bonds are senior unsecured, which is appropriate for an A- rated issuer. The company would face no difficulty accessing capital markets even in a stressed environment, given its credit profile and predictable cash flows.

**Liquidity: Adequate Despite Low Current Ratio.** The current ratio of 0.6x looks alarming in isolation, but this is a structural feature of subscription-based businesses, not a sign of distress. Current liabilities of EUR 4.1B include a large portion of deferred revenue — money already collected from customers for services not yet delivered. This is an economic asset masquerading as a liability. Cash on hand was EUR 932M at year-end FY25, and the company has access to undrawn revolving credit facilities (standard for companies of this size and rating, though the exact size is not disclosed in the available data). Free cash flow generation of EUR 1.35B per year provides ample liquidity to service debt, fund operations, and cover near-term maturities. Could Wolters Kluwer survive 12-24 months with zero revenue? No — but no information services company could. The relevant test is: can it survive a prolonged downturn with, say, a 15-20% revenue decline? The answer is yes, comfortably. In the severe COVID disruption of 2020, Wolters Kluwer barely noticed — recurring revenue businesses with mandatory-use products in compliance, tax, legal, and healthcare are about as recession-resistant as they come.

**Equity Erosion: By Design, But Worth Watching.** Stockholders' equity dropped from EUR 2.3B to EUR 798M over four years, while Debt/Equity exploded from 156% to 618%. This looks terrible on paper but is entirely driven by share buybacks (EUR 1.0-1.1B annually) that reduce equity. The buyback program for FY25 was EUR 1B, accelerated for completion by November 2025, and a new EUR 500M program was announced for 2026. The question is not whether buybacks are appropriate (they are, for a cash-generative business with limited reinvestment needs) but whether the price paid was sensible. With shares purchased at prices ranging from EUR 90-160 over 2023-2025 and the stock now at EUR 67, a meaningful portion of past buybacks are underwater. This does not impair the business operationally, but it does mean shareholders' capital was deployed at prices that destroyed short-term value. If the stock price recovers toward intrinsic value (quant model base case EUR 155), these buybacks will look reasonable in hindsight. If the business deteriorates, they will look imprudent.

**Goodwill and Intangible Assets: The Elephant on the Balance Sheet.** Wolters Kluwer is an acquisition-built business. Total assets are EUR 9.6B, and the company carries significant goodwill and acquired intangible assets from decades of buying publishers, software companies, and data providers. Amortization of acquired intangible assets and goodwill was EUR 157M in FY25 (up from EUR 149M in FY24), reflecting recent acquisitions including Brightflag, Inisoft, Libra, and the EUR 386M RASi acquisition completed in March 2025. While the exact goodwill figure is not broken out in the available summary data, goodwill and intangibles typically constitute 60-70% of total assets for roll-up information services companies. For Wolters Kluwer, this is less concerning than for a typical roll-up because: (a) the acquired businesses have genuine recurring revenue streams with high retention, (b) the company has never taken a material goodwill impairment, and (c) organic growth of 6% demonstrates the acquired assets are productive, not wasting. Still, a material impairment in a downturn would further reduce already-thin equity.

**Stress Test: Revenue -30% for Two Years.** If revenue dropped 30% (from EUR 6.1B to EUR 4.3B), operating income at a compressed 20% margin would be approximately EUR 860M. Interest expense of EUR 120M would be covered 7.2x — still comfortable. Free cash flow would drop to perhaps EUR 500-600M, enough to service debt but forcing a suspension of buybacks and possibly the dividend. Given that 83% of revenue is recurring and the products are embedded in regulatory compliance workflows, a 30% revenue decline is an extreme tail scenario — far worse than anything experienced in 2008-2009 or 2020. A more realistic stress of 10-15% revenue decline would barely dent the company's financial safety.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A- credit rating, well-laddered maturities extending to 2032, and EUR 1.35B in annual free cash flow mean Wolters Kluwer can comfortably service its debt, maintain the dividend, and continue modest buybacks even in a recession — the balance sheet supports rather than threatens the business.
- **Bear case:** If organic growth stalls and the EUR 3.8B net debt burden persists while management continues prioritizing buybacks over deleveraging, leverage could creep toward the top of the 2.5x target range, particularly if a large acquisition is attempted — the margin of safety on the balance sheet is narrower than it was three years ago.
- **Confidence:** High — The debt structure is transparent, the maturity profile is well-managed, the credit rating was just upgraded, and the cash flow generation provides a thick cushion. The rising leverage trend is the only genuine concern, and it is manageable.

## Red Flags

- Net debt nearly doubled in three years (EUR 2.0B to EUR 3.8B) while equity was halved — the company is systematically replacing equity with debt to fund buybacks. This is fine at current EBITDA levels but leaves less buffer if fundamentals deteriorate.
- A meaningful share of the EUR 4.1B in buybacks over FY22-FY25 was executed at prices well above the current EUR 67 stock price — capital destruction in hindsight, though this is a sunk cost and does not affect future safety.
- Interest expense guidance of EUR 110M for FY26 represents a further step up — the cost of maintaining this capital structure is rising.
- Goodwill and intangibles likely dominate the balance sheet (estimated 60-70% of EUR 9.6B total assets); a material impairment would push equity negative, though this would be an accounting event, not a solvency event.

## Score: 7 / 10

Wolters Kluwer's balance sheet is strong but no longer conservative. An A- credit rating, 15x interest coverage, well-laddered maturities to 2032, and EUR 1.35B annual free cash flow provide genuine safety. The deduction from 8 or 9 reflects the deliberate leveraging trend — net debt nearly doubled in three years, equity has eroded to EUR 798M, and management shows no sign of slowing buybacks. The balance sheet can handle a recession, but it has less cushion than it did three years ago.
