## Detailed Analysis

**Skin in the game.** Maria Black holds about 76,161 shares worth ~$19M at today's price. That's meaningful on a personal basis — more than most hired CEOs hold — and it exceeds the 6x base-salary ownership requirement. But in a $81B market-cap company it's a rounding error; she is a professional manager, not an owner-operator. There is no founding family and no insider holder of scale. Institutional ownership sits around 80%, which is typical for an S&P component. A more informative signal would be directors and named officers buying on the open market during the 37% drawdown from the 52-week high. I did not find an insider cluster buying pattern in the public records searched, which is a mildly negative signal — when management are convinced, they buy; when they're cautious, they hold.

**Compensation design.** Total CEO comp was ~$19.1M in FY2025, up ~20.6% from FY2024. 5.9% base, 94.1% variable, 82.3% long-term incentive — this structure scores well. Long-term awards are split between performance shares tied to EPS and TSR and restricted stock. The direction of the structure (heavy LTI, per-share EPS target, relative TSR vs peer index) is close to best practice. The one concern is absolute quantum — $19M is rich for a mid-growth compounder, and the 20% y/y jump in comp came in a year when the stock finished well off its highs. Not egregious, but not humble either.

**Buybacks — the real test.** This is where ADP tells on itself. FY22 buybacks were $1.97B, FY23 $1.12B, FY24 $1.23B, FY25 $1.28B. The FY22 number was at prices $200-250 — roughly today's price — and can be defended. The FY23-FY25 buybacks averaged $1.2B/yr at average prices $220-280, i.e. at or above today's quant-model base IV of $228. That is not a disaster but it is not opportunistic either. The newly announced $6B authorization was released in Aug 2025 when the stock was $280-300 — above base IV. Management have been steady dollar-amount buyers, which is a middling policy: not value-destroying, not value-creating. A great allocator would accelerate buybacks now (at $200, trading near IV-bear and below IV-base, with MC 87% probability of undervaluation) — watching whether Q3/Q4 FY26 buyback pace actually steps up is the real stewardship test.

**Dividend discipline.** 51 consecutive years of dividend increases, raised 10% in Nov 2025 to an annualized $6.80 (3.4% yield). Payout ratio is ~$2.74B / $4.1B net income = ~67%, generous but supportable. This is the most credible signal in the file: a company doesn't run a 51-year dividend streak by accident. Management treat the dividend as a commitment, not a decoration. For a business that should grow at GDP+5% indefinitely, this is exactly the right capital-return instrument.

**M&A and reinvestment.** ADP's M&A has been disciplined and small-bolt-on in character: WorkForce Software acquisition (workforce management, $1.2B range, closed 2024) and earlier tuck-ins in EMEA. No transformative acquisitions, no empire-building. The last major corporate action was the spinoff of CDK Global (auto-dealer software) in 2014, which the market later rewarded. Management have been consistent in saying they prefer buybacks and small tech tuck-ins over large M&A. That is the right frame for a moat compounder.

**Debt — the one thing worth asking about.** Total debt stepped up from $3.3B to $9.1B in FY25. Net debt went from ~$0B to $5.4B. The company held $7.8B cash at year-end, up from $3.3B. The likely story: ADP issued debt at attractive rates to pre-fund buybacks and/or to refinance maturities. Debt/EBITDA still 1.4x, interest coverage 12.6x — no financial strain. But the leverage decision deserves a direct question on a future call: are they arbitraging a cheap debt window, or is this the start of a more levered capital structure? A Buffett-style allocator would prefer the first explanation.

**Candor and consistency.** The Q1 and Q2 FY26 earnings call readouts describe honest disclosures — PEO margin contraction (down 70 bps to 14.4% in Q2) was flagged directly; client revenue retention guide-down 10-30 bps was communicated; AI-related investor concerns were addressed head-on. Management are not defensive. The "sleepy giant" framing from financial-press coverage fits: they under-promise on short-term sizzle, over-deliver on long-term cash return. Carlos Rodriguez (predecessor CEO, now off the Board) ran the business for 12 years with the same calm tone; Maria Black has extended it. That cultural continuity is valuable.

**Governance.** Single share class. Independent non-executive Chair (reinstated Jan 2024). Board majority independent; no related-party issues in the public record. No dual-class, no supermajority provisions. CEO and Chair separated. This is textbook corporate governance for a mature compounder.

**The simple tell.** Read the Q2 FY26 call prepared remarks: Maria Black talks about bookings by segment, retention by cohort, PEO pass-throughs, and float yield — all in plain language. She does not puff the AI narrative, does not oversell international, does not hide PEO margin pressure. That is the voice of an owner, not a politician. The one thing missing is a more forceful stance on opportunistic buybacks during the current drawdown.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** 51-year dividend streak plus a disciplined, returns-focused capital structure under a long-tenured CEO who rose through the ranks — management will quietly keep compounding per-share value for decades.
- **Bear case:** Competent caretakers rather than bold allocators — steady buybacks near IV rather than opportunistic buybacks near bear IV, modest insider buying in the drawdown, and CEO comp creeping up faster than shareholder returns suggest the mindset is corporate rather than owner.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the 50-year dividend record, modest SBC, clean governance, and moderate CEO ownership all support trust; the FY25 leverage step-up and the lack of insider buying on the drawdown are the open questions.

## Red Flags

- CEO total comp up 20.6% y/y to $19.1M in a year when the stock underperformed — quantum is high for a compounder of this growth rate, though structure (82% LTI) is defensible.
- Step-change in total debt ($3.3B → $9.1B in one year) without a clear public rationale tied to a strategic deal — likely benign (cheap debt + cash build) but deserves a direct question.
- Buybacks have been steady-dollar rather than opportunistic — no visible dial-up at prices below base IV, which is what a great capital allocator would do; watch FY26 Q3/Q4 pace.
- No recorded cluster of insider open-market buying during the 37% drawdown — small negative signal on internal conviction.

## Score: 7 / 10

A competent, disciplined, long-tenured team with a 51-year dividend record and clean governance — held back from an 8 by rising CEO comp quantum, merely-steady (not opportunistic) buyback behavior, and absence of visible insider buying during the drawdown.
