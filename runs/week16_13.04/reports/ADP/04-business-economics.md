## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital.** Reported ROIC looks spectacular (37% in FY2025, 54% in FY2024) and reported ROE even more so (76%–101% range over four years). Both figures need careful reading. ADP's balance sheet is dominated by "funds held for clients" — roughly $43B of current assets sit against $41B of client-funds obligations — which massively inflates the denominator of ROA (7.6% in FY2025) and indirectly suppresses it relative to the operating business's true asset intensity. If you strip the client-funds trust from both sides and look at invested capital used by the operating business (roughly equity plus corporate debt minus corporate cash), you get an operating business generating $4.1B of net income on a small operating invested-capital base — returns are genuinely best-in-class, even if the precise printed 37–54% ROIC is not apples-to-apples with an asset-heavy peer. The direction is what matters: returns have been consistently high and have not degraded as revenue grew ~25% over three years.

**Margin structure.** Gross margin rose from 42.6% (FY2022) to 46.0% (FY2025) — a 340 bps expansion driven by mix shift toward higher-margin Employer Services revenue and operating scale. Operating margin went from 23.1% to 26.3% over the same window. Q2 FY2026 adjusted EBIT margin expanded another 80 bps y/y to 26%, with Employer Services segment margin slightly down 50 bps and PEO down 70 bps — meaning total margin expansion came from mix and from float/other operating efficiency, not from segment-level pricing. This is a real flag to watch: if segment margins continue to compress, total margin expansion relies on float income holding up.

**Cash generation and owner earnings.** FCF grew from $2.5B (FY2022) to $4.4B (FY2025), a 20% CAGR, with FY2025 FCF margin at 21.4%. FCF conversion averaged 101% across four years. The quant model's maintenance-capex breakdown shows virtually 100% of the $547M FY2025 capex is maintenance — meaning this is not an asset-light software business in disguise of a capex-heavy one; the true capital intensity is low, but it's also not zero. Stock-based compensation is modest at $266M (1.3% of revenue), so cash and GAAP earnings largely agree. Owner earnings of $4.1B in FY2025 are essentially identical to reported net income, which is the cleanest possible signal that the reported numbers reflect economic reality.

**Capital intensity and reinvestment.** Capex has been a remarkably flat $547–572M per year for four years while revenue grew from $16.5B to $20.6B. That's the textbook economic signature of an asset-light services business with operating leverage — incremental revenue falls to the bottom line at high margins because the infrastructure (data centers, platform, sales force) already exists. R&D is not broken out in the standardized income statement, but ADP does invest in platform modernization (DocumentCloud, AI-assisted products) and those costs flow through opex rather than as capitalized software.

**Revenue quality — the float income question.** Roughly 100% of ADP's revenue is recurring (payroll processing, HR outsourcing, PEO services are all subscription-style), which is a massive quality advantage. However, a material chunk of operating income — in the range of $1.0–1.3B — comes from interest earned on client payroll funds held in trust before disbursement. Per management's FY2026 guide, float income is projected at $1.31–1.33B with a blended 3.8% new-purchase yield, 4–5% client-funds balance growth, and a laddered fixed-income portfolio. This is economically wonderful (high-margin, near-zero incremental cost) but it is rate-sensitive: a sharp Fed pivot would compress this stream over a 2–3 year reinvestment cycle as older bonds mature and new purchases happen at lower yields. The ladder cushions a sudden drop but doesn't eliminate the exposure. Longer-duration positioning is what lets ADP capture multi-year rate regimes — but it's also why low-rate years like 2020–2021 hurt this line item badly.

**Operating leverage and growth quality.** Q2 FY2026 reported 6% revenue growth and 11% adjusted EPS growth — incremental profit growing at roughly 1.8x revenue growth is classic operating-leverage math. ADP also raised FY2026 guidance to >6% revenue growth and 9–10% EPS growth, signaling management confidence in the trajectory. Worker counts on ADP's platform and pays-per-control metrics remained healthy per Pay Insights commentary. The business is not cyclically exposed in the extreme sense — payroll is non-discretionary — but it does feel a wage-bill drag during employment downturns, and PEO in particular is exposed to small/mid-business churn.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Continued mid-single-digit organic revenue growth, sustained float income at 3.5%+ yields, and steady margin expansion drive EPS growth of ~10%/yr with the quant-model base IV of ~$228/share becoming achievable within 12–18 months.
- **Bear case:** A fast Fed easing cycle compresses float income $300–500M over two years while segment-margin erosion (already visible in Q2 FY2026) continues, turning reported 11% EPS growth into low-single-digit.
- **Confidence:** High — ADP's financials are exceptionally clean, the business model has been stable for decades, and both Q2 FY2026 results and 2026 guidance confirm the trajectory visible in the historical data.

## Red Flags

- Segment-level margin compression in Q2 FY2026 (Employer Services −50 bps, PEO −70 bps) was masked by a favorable mix and float — if this persists for several more quarters without rate support, the true operating margin trend is weaker than the headline.
- Float income dependency: ~$1.3B of annualized interest income is not permanent economics — it's a function of the rate environment and the client-funds balance. A valuation that capitalizes current float income at the same multiple as recurring subscription revenue is implicitly betting on rates staying higher for longer.
- Reported ROIC/ROE are not a clean apples-to-apples metric with asset-light software peers because the client-funds trust distorts both numerator (via float income) and denominator (via invested capital definitions).
- R&D spend is not disclosed in the financials pull — makes it harder to assess platform reinvestment intensity against peers like Paychex or Workday.

## Score: 8 / 10

A high-quality economic engine with durable recurring revenue, expanding margins, best-in-class FCF conversion, and disciplined capital return — docked from 9 because float income is a real but rate-sensitive earnings layer and segment margins are showing early compression.
