## Detailed Analysis

**Which moats does ADP actually have?** Three: (1) **Switching costs**, (2) **Scale in regulatory/compliance infrastructure**, and (3) a weaker **Brand/trust** moat in the enterprise book. None of the big three — network effects, a pure cost moat, or a patent moat — applies cleanly. Each claimed moat needs to be tested against a cheaper, near-identical substitute, and on that test ADP does hold up in the middle-market and enterprise segments but has clearly lost ground at the bottom.

**Switching costs — the core of the moat.** Payroll is one of the few SaaS workloads where a migration genuinely goes wrong. Historical compensation data, tax withholdings, benefit elections, garnishments, 401(k) deferrals, state unemployment accounts and W-2 history all have to move cleanly or the CFO gets a nasty call from the IRS. In practice mid-market payroll migrations run 6-12 months and require parallel runs for 1-2 quarters. CFOs of established businesses tend to describe switching as "nobody got fired for keeping ADP." The financial fingerprint is client revenue retention of ~91-92% for an extended period — exceptionally high for a multi-million-client book where any one customer can leave at any time. The FY26 guidance to drop 10-30 bps on retention is the first negative signal in several years; small but worth tracking because it tests the moat.

**Scale in compliance.** Nobody in the world processes payroll at ADP's US scale. That scale shows up in three places: (1) legal and tax engineering — ADP maintains live wage-tax tables for every US state, county and municipality, and the same for each country where it operates; rewriting that library is a multi-year expense for any new entrant; (2) bank and tax-agency plumbing — relationships with every major payroll-depository bank and state tax authority, including ACH error resolution workflows; (3) the float itself — $30B+ of client funds in transit, which is both a profit center and a counter-party signal that the money is safe. This is shared scale — costs amortize over a bigger base, which is why ADP's operating margin has grown 320 bps in three years while Paychex's sits in the high-30s on a smaller, more SMB-concentrated book. Workday and Rippling are formidable, but they have to rent much of this plumbing from partners or build it at a disadvantage.

**Brand and trust.** In enterprise, ADP has the quiet reputation of "never made the news for a payroll disaster." That's worth something; payroll failure is a career-ending event for a CHRO. In SMB, though, the brand works against them — the independent review sites describe ADP as "outdated interface, poor support, hidden fees, fragmented modules." If a cheaper, identical product appeared in SMB, customers would switch; Gusto and Rippling have been that near-identical product for segments of the market. So the brand moat is real at the top, weak at the bottom.

**Moat durability — the next 10 years.** Two realistic threats: (1) **AI disruption** of the computation layer. Generative AI can write the tax-calculation logic — but filing payroll taxes correctly across 50 states is a liability problem, not a software problem. An AI that's 99.5% accurate still exposes a CFO to 0.5% of 10,000 tax filings going wrong. ADP's moat isn't that it does math; it's that it takes the liability. That moat is more robust than the market seems to credit. (2) **Modern-UX competitors** (Rippling, Gusto) continuing to nibble at SMB and lower-mid-market. This is a real and ongoing erosion. ADP has responded by opening the ADP Marketplace to third-party apps, modernizing its UX, and pushing international where it has less SMB competition. The FY26 results showed enterprise and international as the fastest-growing books — evidence the mix is rotating toward the defensible top.

**Evidence in the numbers.** Pricing power test: operating margin rose 320 bps over three years (FY22-FY25) while revenue growth averaged ~7%; you can only expand margins like that if you're either cutting costs hard or raising price faster than cost inflation — ADP is doing both. ROIC 37-54% (FY25 ROIC reset lower to 37% partly because invested capital doubled from a recent debt issuance, a denominator effect). FCF margin climbed from 15.4% (FY22) to 21.4% (FY25). These are the financial fingerprints of a moat, not a commodity.

**Where is the moat weakest?** (1) The SMB flank against Rippling/Gusto. (2) Any future softening in short rates, which would reveal how much of recent margin expansion came from float rather than pricing — float is a macro tailwind, not a company moat. (3) The "AI-native" HR platforms (Paycom's Self-Service Insights, Workday's AI reconciliation) are slowly raising the product bar — ADP can match on spend, but can't match on cloud-native code base without a long cycle of rewrites.

**Does the advantage reset every cycle?** No. Unlike hardware or gaming, payroll doesn't reset — the installed base keeps paying every two weeks. This is structurally different from, say, a smartphone OEM. The compounding comes from the installed base throwing off cash that can be reinvested.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Switching costs plus regulatory-scale moat protect 70%+ of the revenue base (enterprise, mid-market, international, tax filing) essentially indefinitely, while AI investment lets ADP defend and extend the premium tier.
- **Bear case:** SMB share loss to Rippling/Gusto accelerates, AI commoditizes the product ADP sells, and float-income tailwind reverses — leaving a lower-growth, lower-margin business that doesn't justify a premium multiple.
- **Confidence:** High — ten years of margin and retention data plus clear market-share ranking give a readable moat; the only uncertainty is rate of AI-driven erosion.

## Red Flags

- FY26 guided 10-30 bps drop in client revenue retention — small but the first negative move on the single most important moat metric.
- Operating margin expansion partly flattered by rising float yields FY23-FY25; hard to separate moat-driven pricing from rate-cycle windfall without quarterly detail on float contribution.
- SMB segment consistently loses reviews to modern competitors — if Rippling or Gusto ever move up-market successfully, ADP's enterprise moat will face a more capable attacker.
- PEO revenue growth is partly cosmetic (zero-margin pass-throughs); underlying PEO ex-pass-through growth guided 3-5% for FY26, which is closer to GDP than what a moat business should produce.

## Score: 8 / 10

Genuine switching-cost and regulatory-scale moat in the profitable core (enterprise, mid-market, tax-filing infrastructure), eroding at the SMB margin — a strong 8 rather than a 9 because the SMB flank is visibly losing share and the float-income tailwind muddies the moat evidence.
