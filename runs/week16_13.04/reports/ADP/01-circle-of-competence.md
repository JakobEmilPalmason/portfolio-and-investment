## Detailed Analysis

**What ADP sells and to whom.** ADP runs the payroll for about one in six American workers. If you work in the US and glance at the tiny logo in the bottom-left of your paycheck stub, there's a decent chance it says ADP. The product is simple to describe: an employer hands ADP its list of employees, hours and pay rates; ADP calculates gross-to-net pay, withholds the right federal/state/local taxes, pushes direct deposits to employees, files the tax forms, and handles the year-end W-2s. On top of that core payroll backbone they sell HR software (benefits admin, time & attendance, onboarding, recruiting, analytics), retirement and insurance services, and a bundled Professional Employer Organization (PEO) offering — ADP TotalSource — where ADP becomes a co-employer of record and takes on HR/benefits/compliance for small businesses that don't want the headache. Customers range from 1-employee shops to multinational enterprises, ~1.1 million total, across ~140 countries.

**How it actually makes money.** Three revenue streams, all recurring: (1) per-employee-per-month (PEPM) payroll/HR processing fees, invoiced on each pay cycle; (2) PEO revenue, which blends service fees with pass-through benefit/workers'-comp premiums (ADP books the gross and flags the pass-through portion as zero-margin); (3) interest income on the float — when a client runs payroll on Monday for a Friday pay date, ADP parks the tax-withholding and net-pay balances in short-term Treasuries and commercial paper for a few days and keeps the interest. Float is a real profit center, not an accounting quirk: at ~5% short rates on $30B+ of average client funds the pre-tax contribution runs several hundred million. Rising rates helped FY23-FY25; falling rates would be a headwind.

**Key drivers.** Four variables carry most of the explanatory power. (1) **Pays per control** — the number of paychecks processed per existing client — which tracks US employment; guided flat for FY26, a mild headwind reflecting softening labor markets. (2) **New bookings growth** (guided +4-7% for FY26 ES) — sales productivity and competitive win rate. (3) **Client revenue retention** (guided down 10-30 bps in FY26 — worth watching). (4) **Float yields** on client money. PEPM pricing is a fifth driver but realized price is modest (low-single-digits) and is more a margin lever than a growth lever.

**Predictability.** Extremely high by large-cap standards. Revenue is contract-based, billed per pay cycle, and retention sits in the low-90s%. The business is not very seasonal at the revenue line (payroll runs every two weeks in any economy) but Q2 (Oct-Dec) and Q4 (Apr-Jun) carry slightly heavier benefit-selling and year-end tax cycles. Downturns bite through two channels — fewer paychecks (pays-per-control) and lower float yields — rather than through cancellations, which is why ADP's revenue dip in 2020 was only ~1% while many SaaS peers saw double-digit drops.

**Customer concentration.** Essentially none. No customer material. The mix is heavy in US small- and mid-market, with enterprise and international the two fastest-growing books in the latest quarter. Switching behavior is sticky in the enterprise ("we'd rather die than migrate payroll systems") and less sticky in SMB where newer entrants like Gusto and Rippling have been chipping away. This diversity is why ADP has been able to compound revenue through every recession since 1949.

**Can you explain it in 2 minutes?** Yes. "ADP runs payroll. Employers pay them a small fee per employee per pay cycle to calculate paychecks, send the money, and file the tax forms. They also earn float interest on the payroll dollars in transit. The business repeats every two weeks, forever, for over a million employers." That's as clean as a business model gets. Complexity enters only when you dig into the PEO pass-throughs and the float-yield sensitivity — neither of which changes the basic picture.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A regulated-complexity, float-levered, mission-critical business that has compounded revenue and dividends for 50+ years and remains the default payroll choice for US enterprises — easy to understand, hard to disrupt at the top of the market.
- **Bear case:** A mature, GDP-plus grower whose SMB flank is being eroded by more modern UIs (Gusto, Rippling, Paycom) and whose float income fades if short rates keep falling — investors may be correctly pricing a permanently slower compounder.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is transparent, financials are clean, and segment disclosure is reasonable.

## Red Flags

- Headline PEO revenue growth overstates true economic growth because of large zero-margin pass-throughs — must be backed out to read the underlying rate (FY26 PEO ex-pass-through growth guided at only 3-5%).
- Float income is exposed to short-rate direction — not a company-specific skill, more of a macro tailwind/headwind that investors sometimes mis-attribute to operating strength.
- Guided 10-30 bps client revenue retention decline in FY26 is the first crack in the famous stickiness narrative — small, but worth monitoring.

## Score: 9 / 10

Among the clearest business models in the S&P 500 — recurring, essential, diversified, with honest segment disclosure; the only reason it's not a 10 is the float-income and PEO pass-through nuance that casual investors often misread.
