## Detailed Analysis

**The engine has broken, and management has not yet fixed it.** For the period FY2021–FY2023, UnitedHealth earned 16–18% ROIC on a steadily-compounding $135–151B invested-capital base, with gross margins around 24.5%, operating margins near 8.7%, and FCF conversion reliably above 110% of net income. That is the profile of a high-quality compounder. By FY2025 that picture has inverted: ROIC is 9.7%, operating margin is 4.2%, gross margin is 18.5%, and absolute operating income has been halved. Revenue in FY25 was $447.6B — $123B higher than FY22 — yet operating income ($19.0B) is below the FY22 level ($28.4B). This is the textbook definition of a business that is getting bigger and worse.

**Segment economics show Medicare Advantage is the epicenter.** UnitedHealthcare grew revenue 16% to $344.9B in 2025 but saw its earnings fall roughly 40% year-over-year. The driver is structural, not transient: elevated MA utilization (Q1 2025 care activity grew at twice the rate of 2024), CMS funding cuts, the V28 risk-coding rework that management says cost insurers ~$130B in aggregate, and IRA-driven Part D redesign. Full-year 2025 adjusted MCR of 88.9% versus FY24's 85.5% is not a rounding error — each 100 bps of MCR on a $345B revenue base is roughly $3.5B of gross profit. Management's FY26 MCR guide of 88.8% implies no reversion, just stabilization. The company also plans to exit 2.3–2.8M unprofitable MA members in 2026, total UHC enrollment guided down to ~47M (from 49.8M in 2025) — a visible, discretionary shrinkage, which is the right move but admits past mispricing.

**Optum Health's collapse is the most damaging finding.** Optum was the growth story that justified UNH trading at a premium multiple for a decade: vertical integration with value-based care, asset-light analytics (Insight), and the pharmacy franchise (Rx). In FY25 Optum Health revenue was $102.0B (down 3% YoY) and GAAP operating earnings were negative $278M — a swing from $7.8B of profit in FY24. Adjusted earnings of $2.3B versus $7.9B also reflect a 70% decline. Optum Rx grew revenue 16% to $154.7B, but margin compressed to 3.9% from 4.5% (mix effect from higher-cost drugs with lower pharmacy-services margin). Only Optum Insight held up reasonably — revenue up 4% to $19.4B — but earnings still fell to $2.6B from $3.1B. The "Optum flywheel" that was supposed to de-risk the insurance business is itself struggling.

**Cash generation is surprisingly resilient, which is the one bright spot.** FCF in FY25 was $16.1B (3.6% of revenue) — lower than FY24's $20.7B but still real cash. FCF-to-net-income ran at 133% in FY25 because insurance balance sheets benefit from float (premiums collected ahead of claims paid), and that dynamic doesn't disappear when underwriting weakens. Capex is low — $3.6B annually on $447B of revenue (~0.8%) — so owner earnings (NI + D&A − maintenance capex) were $12.8B in FY25. The business is asset-light in the Buffett sense: it does not need to pour capital into plant and equipment. But owner earnings fell from $23.0B (FY23) → $15.0B (FY24) → $12.8B (FY25) in tandem with operating income. Healthy conversion of a shrinking profit pool is still a shrinking profit pool.

**Revenue quality is genuinely recurring but highly regulated.** Subscription-like premium income and repeat pharmacy volumes are sticky, but pricing is negotiated annually (commercial) or set by CMS (Medicare, Medicaid), which caps the company's ability to reprice quickly when costs run hot. The FY25 operating-income collapse is exactly this dynamic: costs moved fast, pricing did not. UNH has demonstrated in prior cycles (2008–2010, 2014–2016) that it can re-bid and re-price MA membership over 12–24 months, and the planned 2026 member exits plus 2026 MCR guidance suggest the pricing rebuild is underway. Historically UNH has rebuilt MA margin within two bid cycles. The question for the business-economics umbrella is whether the trough ROIC is 9–10% or closer to the 8% seen in recessionary prior cycles.

**Operating leverage now runs in reverse.** In a normal year, UNH adds revenue faster than costs and margins expand. FY25 demonstrated negative operating leverage at scale: +$47B revenue, −$13B operating income. Gross margin compressed 380 bps in a single year. Until MCR normalizes and Optum Health's clinic/V28 issues wash through, the company's "bigger = better" narrative is suspended.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** MA repricing and member exits restore MCR to the 85–86% range by FY27, Optum Health margin normalizes toward 4–5%, and ROIC rebuilds to 14–15% within two to three years — a familiar UNH playbook from prior cycles.
- **Bear case:** V28 coding changes and IRA Part D redesign are permanent step-downs, Optum Health's clinical-delivery economics prove structurally worse than the 2019–2023 era, and ROIC settles in the 10–11% range — making UNH a high-single-digit-return compounder at best.
- **Confidence:** Medium — historical pattern strongly supports mean reversion within UHC's pricing, but the simultaneous stress across Optum Health and Medicare (regulatory-driven, not market-driven) is unprecedented in the post-2008 record. Management credibility also took a hit with the 2025 guidance suspension.

## Red Flags

- Gross margin erosion of 610 bps over three years is a direct read-through to underwriting mispricing and regulatory absorption — not a recoverable one-time cost.
- Optum Health reporting a GAAP operating loss is a first-in-the-modern-era event for a segment that was the strategic differentiator.
- FY26 guidance (MCR 88.8%) tells you management sees no near-term reversion. That is honest disclosure but also an admission that the 2024–2025 margin base is the new starting point.
- Every "adjustment" in the FY25 Optum Health reconciliation should be scrutinized — a $2.6B gap between GAAP operating loss and adjusted earnings is large, and repeating "non-recurring" items is a classic quality-deterioration tell.
- Member mix shift: exiting 2.3–2.8M MA lives means fewer shared overhead absorbers; the remaining book should theoretically be higher-margin, but it also means UNH is conceding market share to peers in what had been its signature business.

## Score: 5 / 10

UNH was an 8/10 business-economics story through FY2023, and the DNA — insurance float, asset-light capex, negotiated recurring revenue — has not changed. But today's numbers (9.7% ROIC, 4.2% operating margin, 2.7% net margin, Optum Health losing money) describe an average business, not a superior one. I am scoring the current economics, not the 10-year average or the hoped-for recovery. A rebuild to 7+ is plausible within 24–36 months; until then this is a 5.
