## Detailed Analysis

**What type of moat exists.** The moat is **scale economics** — specifically, the procurement and network leverage that comes from being one of three global forwarders that airlines and ocean carriers cannot afford to ignore. When DSV sits down with Maersk or Lufthansa Cargo to negotiate annual capacity allocations, it brings vastly larger volumes to the table than a regional forwarder; this translates into better buy rates that DSV can either pass through (winning more business) or partially keep (higher gross profit yield). This is the same structural advantage Costco has in retail. It is not glamorous but it compounds. The Schenker deal materially widens this advantage: the combined entity is ~1.6x K+N's air/ocean forwarding volumes, giving DSV a measurable buying edge for the first time (Alcott Global Top 10; Air Cargo News Top 25; TI-Insight analysis). Switching costs exist in a secondary way — for customers running integrated contract logistics (dedicated warehousing, TMS integration, customs work), moving a full supply chain is painful and slow. But in pure Air & Sea forwarding, enterprise shippers typically split volume across 3–5 providers and re-tender annually, which limits pricing power.

**How durable is the moat.** Reasonably durable on a 10-year view, but not immortal. The durability comes from three facts: (1) freight forwarding is capital-cheap to run but capital-expensive to reach global scale — building a genuine 90-country network takes 30 years and billions, as DSV itself demonstrates through its own roll-up history (DSV founded 1976, made 60+ acquisitions, culminating in Schenker); (2) customers of scale — Apple, VW, Johnson & Johnson — will not hand logistics to an unproven provider, so the approved-vendor list is short and self-reinforcing; (3) consolidation is continuing — each additional merger shrinks the count of credible global alternatives. The durability risks are real but bounded: digital forwarders (Flexport, Forto) have had some impact at the SME end, not the enterprise end; AI-driven routing/quoting will erode some of the back-office advantage over time; and the Road division is commoditised (truckload/groupage is a fragmented, low-margin business regardless of size) (DSV Wikipedia / History; The Loadstar).

**Evidence of moat in the numbers.** The gross profit line is the moat signal, not the revenue line. From FY2022 to FY2024, DSV's revenue fell from DKK 235.7bn to DKK 167.1bn as freight rates normalised — a 29% drop. Over the same period, gross profit only fell from DKK 52.1bn to DKK 43.0bn — a 17% drop. That gap is the asset-light model doing its work: when spot rates fall, so do buy rates, and DSV keeps a disproportionate share of the profit pool. EBIT margin over FY2021–2025 has been volatile but never collapsed — low of 7.9% in FY2025 (during the Schenker absorption) and high of 11.7% in FY2023. ROIC on pre-Schenker invested capital reached 20.5% in FY2022, consistent with a real moat. Pricing power, narrowly defined: DSV can maintain gross profit per unit better than small forwarders during downturns, but cannot "raise prices" freely during upcycles — customers benchmark aggressively. The fair test is relative, not absolute, and DSV consistently earns gross profit yield above industry median (DSV FY2025 presentation; financials.md).

**Where the moat is weakest.** Two places. First, the **Road division** — European road freight, groupage, domestic distribution — is structurally low-margin and commoditised. Small national carriers with lower cost bases can compete effectively on price. DSV explicitly called out negative organic growth in Road during 2025 and has not managed to differentiate this division in 20+ years of effort. Second, **the middle customer segment** — SMEs and mid-market shippers — where digital forwarders and freight marketplaces (Freightos, Flexport) have chipped away at the traditional forwarder margin. DSV's moat is real at the enterprise end and largely absent at the small-customer end. Solutions (contract logistics) is a moderate-moat business: dedicated warehouses with multi-year contracts have high switching friction, but the industry is competitive and margins are not software-like (FY2025 segment commentary; Freightos market note).

**Do advantages reset every product cycle.** No — this is a capital/network business, not a hits business. Unlike consumer electronics or fashion, DSV does not need to "reinvent" itself every 2–3 years. The moat components (network, carrier relationships, customer approvals, scale buying power) accumulate over decades and do not reset. This is the opposite of, say, a semiconductor designer or a game studio. That structural fact is a point in DSV's favour on durability.

**Net view.** DSV has a real, cost-based moat, widened by Schenker but not transformed by it. In 10 years it will still be a top-3 global forwarder. The question is whether that scale position earns 15%+ ROIC (as it did FY2021–2023) or grinds at ~10% (as it may during integration). The current share price is betting on the former; the current operating reality is closer to the latter.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The Schenker combination compounds DSV's scale advantage into a true duopoly-with-DHL structure, pushing normalised ROIC back to the 15–20% range and letting the business earn asset-light, compoundable returns for another decade.
- **Bear case:** Freight forwarding stays structurally competitive, digital entrants gradually pressure SME and mid-market margins, Road division remains a drag, and DSV earns ~10% normalised ROIC — a fine business but not a wide-moat compounder.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the existence of a real scale advantage is well-documented and visible in the gross profit stability data; the unknown is how much of it shows up in ROIC once Schenker is digested.

## Red Flags

- Reported FY2025 ROIC of 7.6% (vs 20.5% in FY2022) — even accounting for the Schenker goodwill effect, this is the lowest ROIC since 2016 and needs to re-expand to validate the moat thesis.
- Freight forwarding has structurally lower gross margins than K+N or DHL's air freight arms in some quarters — DSV's edge is efficiency (opex), not gross take rate.
- Road division organic decline (2025) suggests the moat does not apply to all three divisions.
- Digital/tech-first forwarders (Flexport, Forto) have successfully attacked the SME tier — same risk vector exists for the mid-market over 5–10 years.
- Customer tender cycles are annual for most enterprise contracts — limited contractual lock-in beyond contract logistics.

## Score: 6 / 10

DSV has a real cost-and-scale moat, reinforced by the Schenker acquisition, but it is a narrow-to-medium moat, not a wide one. Freight forwarding is a competitive, cyclical industry where the top players earn ~10–20% ROIC across cycles — respectable but not exceptional. The 6 reflects: genuine scale advantage confirmed by gross profit resilience during freight rate downturns (+), 10-year durability of the network effect (+), but low absolute operating margins, high cyclicality, and a commoditised Road division (-). Another 1 point could be earned if post-integration ROIC demonstrates a step-up vs. pre-deal K+N/DHL parity.
