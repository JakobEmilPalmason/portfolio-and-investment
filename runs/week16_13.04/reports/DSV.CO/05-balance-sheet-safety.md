## Detailed Analysis

**This is a mid-deleveraging balance sheet, not a fortress.** At FY2025 close, DSV carried DKK 97.9B gross debt and DKK 56.5B net debt, producing Debt/EBITDA of 4.0x and Net Debt/EBITDA of 2.3x. Compare that to FY2022, when the company ran 1.3x and 0.5x respectively with DKK 39B gross debt. The Schenker deal, financed with a DKK 42B rights issue and roughly DKK 60B of new senior unsecured debt (including a multi-tranche bond issuance in late 2024/early 2025), took DSV from "effectively net cash by cash-flow standards" to "materially leveraged." That said, 2.3x net leverage is well within investment-grade norms for a cash-generative business, and DSV is actively paying down. Net interest-bearing debt (DSV's own disclosure metric) was DKK 86.6B at year-end, already more than DKK 7B lower than immediately after the Schenker close. Management has stated it will not resume share buybacks until Net Debt/EBITDA is back below 2.0x — a strong commitment that signals capital discipline through the deleveraging window.

**Interest coverage is the metric most worth watching.** The collapse from 17.8x (FY2022) to 4.3x (FY2025) is dramatic. On the numerator side, EBIT fell as margins compressed from Schenker integration; on the denominator, interest expense grew from DKK 1.4B (FY2022) to DKK 3.7B (FY2025) as the new acquisition debt was priced at 2024–2025 rates materially higher than DSV's legacy bonds. At 4.3x coverage, the company is comfortably servicing debt, but it has moved from "unquestionably safe" to "safe with a margin, if EBIT holds." Management's DKK 23–25.5B EBIT guidance for 2026 would lift coverage to roughly 6–7x (assuming interest expense stays around DKK 3.5B), back into genuinely comfortable territory. The leverage story and the margin story are the same story: if synergies land, this metric heals.

**Liquidity is genuinely strong.** The company reported DKK 12.2B cash plus DKK 8.4B undrawn revolving credit facilities as of Q3 2025 — roughly DKK 20B of total liquidity against DKK 82.3B of current liabilities. Note the current ratio of 1.0x looks thin, but a large portion of current liabilities in freight forwarding is trade payables that turn over with receivables, not true financial claims. Adjusted free cash flow of DKK 16.3B in 2025 means DSV is internally funding both integration capex (expected DKK 6.5B in special items for 2026) and debt paydown. The company does not need the capital markets to be open to survive — this is the Buffett test, and DSV passes it.

**Refinancing risk is manageable, not negligible.** The Schenker financing was executed across multiple bond tranches of varying maturity to avoid a bunched maturity wall. S&P's commentary notes DSV has debt coming due over the next three years and its base case assumes DSV pays this down from operating cash flow rather than rolling aggressively into new debt. In a credit-market shock, DSV's investment-grade status and large RCF would likely provide access — but at worse pricing. This is a meaningful degradation from pre-Schenker DSV, which could shrug off credit conditions entirely because it held net cash.

**Off-balance-sheet and pension exposure is limited.** Freight forwarding is asset-light and lease-light by industry standards; operating leases are on the balance sheet post-IFRS 16. Pension obligations at DSV have historically been immaterial (small relative to EBITDA). The post-Schenker picture includes Schenker's pension liabilities, primarily in Germany, which the DSV annual report indicates have not materially changed the obligation profile. No meaningful off-balance-sheet red flags are disclosed.

**Stress test: can DSV survive a bad two years?** Plug in a 30% revenue drop (freight rates collapse + volume recession, similar to what happened in 2023 when revenue fell from DKK 235.7B to DKK 150.8B). At an 8% operating margin on DKK 173B of revenue, EBIT would be ~DKK 13.8B. Interest expense of ~DKK 3.5B leaves pre-tax income around DKK 10B and operating cash flow of roughly DKK 12–14B. Debt service is covered; capex requirement is trivial (DKK 2.5B); integration special items might be deferred. The company would still produce positive free cash flow and would continue to delever. It would not need to raise equity. This is the "not losing money" criterion, and DSV clears it — but with noticeably less margin than the pre-Schenker balance sheet offered.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** DKK 16B+ of adjusted FCF continues into 2026, Net Debt/EBITDA drops below 2.0x by end-2026, share buybacks resume in 2027, and the balance sheet returns to its historically conservative structure with normalized 5–10x interest coverage.
- **Bear case:** A global trade recession compresses EBIT to DKK 13–15B just as DKK 15–20B of debt needs refinancing at elevated rates; interest coverage dips below 3x; S&P downgrades from A- to BBB+, raising cost of debt and pushing deleveraging out past 2028.
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — the post-Schenker balance sheet is quantitatively well-understood from disclosures, liquidity is strong, and the deleveraging path is explicit in management commentary. The only real uncertainty is cyclical EBIT durability.

## Red Flags

- Debt/EBITDA of 4.0x on a gross basis is the highest leverage DSV has ever carried — a clear departure from the company's historical conservative posture (consistently below 2x gross for the past decade).
- Interest coverage of 4.3x is below the "comfortable" 5x threshold. Not in warning territory but no longer fortress-like.
- S&P rating outlook is Negative. A downgrade from A- to BBB+ is a realistic scenario if integration or freight-market conditions deteriorate, and would raise refinancing costs.
- FY2025 current ratio of 1.0x is the tightest in the visible history. The FY2024 3.2x figure was temporarily inflated by undeployed rights-issue proceeds — do not mistake it for a recurring cushion.
- Pro-forma leverage is sensitive to the "synergized EBITDA" denominator. If the DKK 9B synergy target slips, nominal Debt/EBITDA worsens even if absolute debt is paid down.

## Score: 6 / 10

A 6 reflects the honest picture: this was a 9 balance sheet before Schenker and it will likely be an 8 again by 2027, but right now it is a mid-leveraged industrial with interest coverage at the low end of comfortable, tight current ratio, and a rating outlook pointed the wrong way. Liquidity is strong, cash generation is real, and management is visibly deleveraging — but the margin of safety on the balance sheet is genuinely lower than it was 18 months ago, and a Buffett-style analyst should not pretend otherwise.
