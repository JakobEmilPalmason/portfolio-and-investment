## Detailed Analysis

**What DSV sells, and to whom.** DSV is a third-party logistics (3PL) provider — more specifically, a global freight forwarder. It does not own most of the ships, airplanes, or long-haul trucks that actually move the goods. Instead, it sells a service: it arranges the movement of a customer's cargo from point A to point B, handles customs, books warehousing, and consolidates shipments. Customers are other businesses — car manufacturers, pharma companies, electronics makers, retailers, defense primes. Think of DSV as a travel agent for industrial cargo: it negotiates rates with airlines, ocean carriers, and road hauliers, and resells capacity to shippers who do not want the hassle of managing 200 carrier relationships themselves (DSV industry verticals; Approved Forwarders 3PL primer).

**How it actually makes money.** Three levers. First, **freight forwarding spread** (Air & Sea and the forwarding part of Road): DSV buys capacity wholesale — a 40-foot container on a Shanghai-to-Rotterdam vessel, say — and sells slots on it to customers at a higher unit price. The margin is the "gross profit yield." Second, **contract logistics** (Solutions): warehousing, pick-and-pack, value-added services under multi-year contracts, usually dedicated or shared facilities. Third, **road transport**: mainly European groupage, less-than-truckload, and full-truck services, where DSV owns some terminals but the tractors are typically contracted. This is why the model is called "asset-light" — DSV's largest balance-sheet asset after Schenker is goodwill, not fleet (DSV "Why choose us"; Approved Forwarders).

**The key drivers.** Four variables matter most: (1) **global trade volume** — if container throughput and air cargo tonnage rise, DSV ships more units; (2) **freight rate levels and volatility** — spiky sea/air rates in 2021–2022 drove record gross profit per unit; normalisation in 2023–2024 compressed it, and 2025 saw re-expansion to DKK 66.9bn gross profit from DKK 43.0bn (Yahoo financials); (3) **gross profit yield per shipment** — DSV's ability to price discipline and consolidate volume, where it has shown edge; (4) **integration execution** on Schenker — every 1 percentage point of EBIT margin uplift on ~DKK 150bn of Schenker revenue is ~DKK 1.5bn of run-rate profit (FY2025 results commentary; FY2026 guidance of DKK 23.0–25.5bn EBIT before special items).

**How predictable is the revenue.** Medium. It is **not** a subscription or contract-recurring business in the software sense. Freight forwarding revenue is per-shipment, priced per leg, and heavily influenced by spot and short-tender rates. Contract logistics (Solutions) is the closest thing to recurring: multi-year warehouse contracts with minimum-volume or cost-plus economics. DSV's gross profit stream has been notably more stable than its revenue line — when freight rates spiked in 2021–22, revenue ballooned to DKK 235.7bn (FY2022) and net income to DKK 17.6bn; as rates normalised FY2023–24, revenue dropped to DKK 150.8bn / DKK 167.1bn but gross profit held at ~DKK 43bn. That is the asset-light model doing its job. The cyclical downside: FY2024 ROIC fell to 9.1% from 20.5% in FY2022 — the business cycles with global trade (financials.md; DSV FY2025 presentation).

**Customer concentration.** No evidence of unusual concentration. DSV describes strategic verticals — automotive, consumer, healthcare, industrial, technology — and serves both large multinationals and mid-market. The company does not disclose a top-10-customer percentage in the annual report abstract, but no public filing flags any single customer as a going-concern risk. Automotive was called out as a weak vertical in 2025 ("continued challenging market conditions, especially within domestic groupage in Europe and within the Automotive vertical" — Q1 2025 interim report). Broad industry mix reduces single-sector risk, but exposure to global trade cycle (autos, tech hardware) remains high (DSV industry solutions page).

**Can I explain it in two minutes?** Yes. "DSV is the world's biggest freight middleman. When Volvo needs brake parts moved from Poland to South Carolina, or when Novo Nordisk needs cold-chain insulin moved to Japan, DSV quotes a price, books capacity with airlines or shipping lines, handles customs paperwork, and delivers. It does not own most of the ships or planes. It makes money on the spread between what the carrier charges and what the customer pays, plus separate fees for warehousing. After buying DB Schenker in April 2025 for EUR 14.3bn, it overtook Kuehne+Nagel as the #1 globally. The business is cyclical — it moves with global trade — and the big bet for the next two years is pulling DKK 9bn of annual cost synergies out of Schenker." That is a legible, non-magical business.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The combined DSV+Schenker is a scaled, asset-light cash compounder; Schenker integration plays out ahead of plan (now targeted for end-2026 vs end-2028), synergies flow to the bottom line, and EBIT margin re-expands toward pre-merger levels — all while the model remains simple to understand.
- **Bear case:** Integration drags longer than planned, cultural clashes with a large German unionised workforce slow decision-making, and freight rates stay soft, compressing the asset-light spread; the business stays understandable but earns a lower structural return on capital than the pre-Schenker DSV did.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model itself is unambiguous and widely documented; the only part requiring judgement is integration execution, which is quantifiable.

## Red Flags

- FY2025 reported financials blend 8 months of Schenker with 12 months of legacy DSV — year-over-year metrics are not clean comparisons and will remain distorted through at least H1 2026.
- Freight forwarding economics are procyclical; in a sharp trade slowdown, gross profit per unit compresses quickly and the operating leverage works in reverse.
- Three distinct divisions (Air & Sea, Road, Solutions) mean the business is not monolithic — Road in particular has been a margin laggard ("negative organic growth" called out in Q1 2025).
- Some end-verticals (automotive, European industrial) are themselves under structural pressure (EV transition, German industrial softness), which flows through to DSV volume.

## Score: 7 / 10

The business model is clean and legible — an asset-light global freight middleman with three divisions and one dominant integration project. I can explain it to a smart non-specialist in under two minutes. The deduction from a higher score is honest: revenue is cyclical and spot-priced (not recurring), FY2025 statements are messy due to the mid-year Schenker close, and the near-term P&L is dominated by a single integration that is complex by any standard. This is understandable, not simple.
