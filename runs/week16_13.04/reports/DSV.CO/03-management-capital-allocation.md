## Detailed Analysis

**Track record on M&A — the single most important test.** DSV's history is essentially a string of major acquisitions executed well over 20 years: Frans Maas (2006), ABX LOGISTICS (2008), UTi Worldwide (2016, USD 1.35bn), Panalpina (2019, ~USD 5bn), Agility GIL (2021, DKK 30.2bn), and Schenker (2025, EUR 14.3bn). Each deal was larger than the last, each was followed by an integration program, and — crucially — each was integrated on or near plan. Panalpina is the best-documented case: DSV guided to ~DKK 2.2bn of cost synergies, later raised the target, and ultimately realised ~DKK 3.7bn including COVID-era initiatives by end-2021, roughly one year ahead of original schedule. Agility GIL synergies were similarly absorbed with minimal drama. This is not a one-time success — it is a 20-year pattern of management taking over freight-forwarder peers, eliminating duplicated overhead, and harvesting margin. That is a rare, measurable management edge (DSV Panalpina 2020 Annual Report; Mergersight Schenker analysis; TI-Insight historical coverage).

**The Schenker test and what it says about allocation discipline.** Schenker is ~3x the Panalpina deal size and was partly paid for with equity (~DKK 37.5bn issued October 2024) and partly with bonds — not a pure levered-roll-up. That matters: management chose to dilute existing holders (and itself) rather than stretch the balance sheet through an integration cycle. Moody's affirmed A3 stable on the assumption that Net Debt/EBITDA would drop toward 2.5x within 12–18 months; S&P kept A- but moved to negative outlook — the rating agencies are treating this as executable, not reckless. The early data points support management: 30% integration completion in 2025 and a 2-year pull-forward of the completion date (end-2026 vs original end-2028) is the strongest possible integration signal DSV could give at this stage. FY2026 EBIT guidance of DKK 23–25.5bn implies a step-up from DKK 19.6bn in FY2025 largely from synergies. Net debt / EBITDA of ~2.3x at end-2025 with DKK 56.5bn net debt is manageable on DKK 24.5bn EBITDA and falling as synergies compound (FY2025 Annual Report announcement; Moody's and S&P updates).

**Buybacks and dividends.** Historical buyback behaviour has been disciplined — large in FY2022 (DKK 20.3bn) and FY2023 (DKK 14bn) when the shares traded in the 1,300–1,900 range and the balance sheet had room, then cut to DKK 3.3bn in FY2024 as Schenker financing loomed, then paused entirely in FY2025 (buybacks: DKK 0). This is the right pattern — buybacks are a residual use of cash, not a quarterly obligation, and management correctly redirected capital to Schenker when the larger opportunity appeared. Dividend is modest and growing (7.00 DKK per share for FY2025, ~8.6% 3-year CAGR) with a yield of 0.41% — DSV is a compounder, not an income stock, which is appropriate for a business that can redeploy capital at 10%+ returns. Share count has been falling for years through buybacks: from ~240m pre-Panalpina to ~219m pre-Schenker, then back up to ~236.7m post the 2024 equity raise (financials.md; DSV share information; Digrin dividend history).

**Compensation design.** The 2025 Remuneration Policy moved toward a cleaner split: fixed salary, an STI (short-term incentive) cash bonus tied to EBIT, share price, and sustainability targets, and an LTI (long-term incentive) share option grant with a 3-year vest. Cash bonuses in 2025 totalled DKK 13.3m, of which DKK 7.2m was the new Executive Board performance bonus. SBC at DKK 359m on DKK 247bn revenue is ~0.15% — effectively negligible dilution, unlike US tech companies running 5–10% SBC. The prior variable share-option grant was replaced with a fixed share-option amount, which slightly reduces upside alignment but also reduces gamed optics. Overall, the compensation structure is conservative-Danish — low absolute payouts, moderate stock incentives, no mega-package structures. No dual-class share structure (DSV Remuneration Report 2025; Remuneration Policy March 2025).

**Candor and consistency.** The communication pattern over 10+ years has been plain-spoken and rare-to-overpromise. Under Andersen, guidance was typically conservative at Q1 and raised through the year. The current Schenker roadmap was laid out in detail at announcement (synergy target, timing, funding mix) and actual delivery is running ahead; the decision to pull the completion date forward from 2028 to 2026 at FY2025 results, rather than quietly holding the line and over-delivering, suggests confidence rather than spin. Negative news — automotive vertical weakness, Road division underperformance — is disclosed rather than buried. Capital Markets Day is scheduled for May 12, 2026, where management will presumably present a fresh multi-year plan (DSV CMD 2026 announcement; FY2025 annual report commentary; The Loadstar integration coverage).

**Governance.** One-share-one-vote structure. Board appears independent in composition with no controlling family or founder. Largest external shareholder is Agility Group at 8.03% — a consequence of the 2021 GIL deal being partly stock-funded — which is a passive strategic holding rather than an activist or controlling stake. BlackRock moved from 5.88% to below 5% during 2025 (likely ordinary passive flows, not a signal). Insider aggregate holdings are meaningful in absolute DKK terms (~DKK 32bn reported) but not concentrated in a single individual. No related-party transaction flags have been raised in public filings. One mild concern: the new CEO Jens Lund was CFO for 19 years during the roll-up era — he knows the playbook but was also a key architect of the deals, so he does not bring an outside challenge to the strategy. Continuity is probably a net positive here but the board should be holding him accountable to numeric synergy milestones (major shareholder announcements; DSV Board of Directors page; Simply Wall St ownership).

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Management's 20-year M&A track record proves out again on Schenker — synergies come in on the accelerated timeline, leverage normalises by H1 2027, buybacks resume at attractive prices, and the company re-rates on re-expanded ROIC; Jens Lund turns out to be as disciplined a CEO as Andersen was.
- **Bear case:** Schenker proves harder than prior deals due to scale (3x Panalpina), cultural (Germany/unions), and macro (soft freight markets) factors; synergy timing slips, leverage lingers, buybacks stay paused, and the stock spends 2–3 years as dead money waiting for the integration to play out.
- **Confidence:** High on capital-allocation judgement (20 years of evidence); Medium on Schenker-specific execution (6 months of post-close data is not enough to declare victory).

## Red Flags

- CEO transition (Sept 2024) and largest deal in company history (closed April 2025) occurred within 7 months of each other — concentrated change, elevated execution risk.
- Road division has been a margin laggard for years under prior management; no evidence yet that the new team has a different plan.
- Buybacks paused FY2025 and unlikely to resume materially before 2027 — a capital-return hiatus while integration runs.
- German union sensitivities on Schenker layoffs could slow cost-take, and DSV made a 2-year no-layoff pledge in Germany that limits early synergy realisation.
- Agility Group's 8.03% stake (legacy from GIL sale) is a large passive overhang — if they decided to exit post-Schenker close, it would pressure the stock.
- New STI/LTI mix is reasonable but not exceptional — could be more heavily tilted to per-share metrics (ROIC, EPS, FCF per share) rather than absolute EBIT.

## Score: 8 / 10

Management is one of the best capital allocators in European industrials over the last two decades. The M&A playbook — acquire, integrate, harvest synergies, return excess cash, repeat — has worked 5+ times at progressively larger scale. The Schenker deal was funded more conservatively than it had to be (partial equity issuance), and the early integration pace is running ahead of plan. The deduction from a 9 reflects real, bounded risks: new CEO running the biggest deal ever, paused buybacks during integration, German union constraints on cost-take, and the honest fact that 12 months of post-close data is not enough to say "it's working" with certainty. This is a genuinely owner-oriented management team executing a high-stakes transaction.
