## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: ASML is the sole-source supplier of EUV and High-NA lithography systems (plus a growing €2B+ installed-base service annuity on 6,000+ fielded tools), and TSMC, Samsung, and Intel keep buying because no one else on Earth can print sub-3nm transistors.
2. **Moat**: A 100% EUV share locked in by the Zeiss SMT optics partnership (25% owned), the Cymer light source (acquired), ~800 specialist suppliers, 2,000+ EUV patents, and 40 years of co-developed know-how that widens (not resets) with every node — a 10-year-plus structural monopoly.
3. **Economics**: ROIC has averaged 41.6% over FY2022–FY2025 against a ~12% WACC, gross margin stepped from 50.5% to 52.8%, FY2025 delivered €11B FCF on €32.7B revenue, and the €38.8B backlog gives two years of forward revenue visibility — textbook economic machine.
4. **Management**: Fouquet succeeded Wennink in an orderly internal handover, SBC is only 0.6% of revenue, ~€12B of cumulative buybacks since FY2022 have net-reduced share count, and M&A has been limited to vertical tucks (Cymer, Zeiss SMT stake, HMI) — though dropping quarterly order-intake disclosure in Q1 2026 is a minor transparency regression.
5. **Debt risk**: Net cash of €8.9B (€13.3B cash vs €4.4B debt), 97× interest coverage, 0.35× debt/EBITDA, and a customer-prepaid backlog mean ASML could absorb a 30% revenue decline for two years while still funding €4.7B of R&D and never touching the capital markets.
6. **Price**: To justify $1,459.80, you must believe ASML sustains 22%+ revenue growth beyond 2030, operating margin expands toward 40%, WACC is sub-10%, and the multiple stays at 22–25× EV/EBITDA — three aggressive priors stacked simultaneously, and Monte Carlo puts P(IV > Price) at 0.0%.
7. **Margin of safety**: There is no price MOS — the stock trades 95% above my $750 base IV and 46% above my $1,000 bull IV — so the only cushion is the business itself (monopoly + fortress balance sheet), which protects permanence of capital but not against a repeat of the ~50% drawdowns seen in 2022 and late-2024/early-2025.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if High-NA ramp slips 2+ years and customer yields disappoint, if the MATCH Act (or equivalent) passes and cuts China DUV revenue while TSMC publicly defers High-NA beyond 1.4nm, or if gross margin fails to re-expand above 53% by FY2027 as the High-NA learning curve promised.

## Key Risks

1. **Valuation risk (highest likelihood, highest near-term severity).** At 30.5× forward P/E, 43.8× EV/EBITDA, and 69.5× P/FCF, the market is pricing ASML for a 2030 bull case to become the 2030 base case. Any multiple compression toward historical 22–25× forward translates to 20–35% price impairment even if fundamentals hold — and the entire DCF sensitivity grid (25 cells) sits below the current quote.
2. **US/Dutch export controls — MATCH Act (happening now, medium severity).** Q1 2026 China mix already compressed from 36% to 19% of sales in one quarter. A DUV-to-China ban proposed in the April 2026 MATCH Act would remove an additional ~10% of EPS. This is the one risk no amount of engineering can solve.
3. **Semiconductor capex cycle turning in 2027–2028 (medium likelihood, high severity).** ASML revenue has fallen 20–30% in prior cyclical troughs, and the stock has drawn down 45–50% twice in the last four years (2022 SOX sell-off; Oct 2024–April 2025). Cyclicality is a fact, not a scenario.
4. **Customer concentration — TSMC's High-NA adoption path (medium likelihood, high severity).** TSMC has publicly deferred High-NA to 1.4nm. If Samsung or Intel also slip, the 2027–2028 High-NA ramp rests on fewer orders than the current thesis requires.
5. **Gross-margin learning curve on High-NA (medium likelihood, medium severity).** Early High-NA units are sold at compressed margins. The 2030 guide (56–60% GM) requires the learning curve to bend. If reported GM does not re-expand above 53% by FY2027, one pillar of the long-term thesis softens.

# Circle of Competence — ASML

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-18
**Data Sources:** User-provided `context/ASML/financials.md` and `quant-valuation.md` (yfinance, 2026-04-18); ASML Q1 2026 press release (asml.com); CNBC Q1 2026 earnings coverage (2026-04-15); TrendForce reporting on High-NA EUV adoption; training knowledge of lithography industry. Company filings treated as primary; some market-share and customer-concentration figures are widely reported but not disclosed at precise granularity.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ASML is the sole supplier of extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography systems — there is no second vendor on Earth | 5 |
| 2 | Revenue is split between system sales (chunky, one-time, ~€200M–€400M per machine) and a growing, recurring installed-base services business (~€6–7B/yr) | 4 |
| 3 | Top three customers (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) account for the overwhelming majority of leading-edge revenue — extreme customer concentration | 4 |
| 4 | FY2025 revenue of €32.7B, FY2026 guided to €36–40B; Q1 2026 already €8.8B with 53% gross margin | 3 |
| 5 | China exposure dropped from 36% (Q4 2025) to 19% (Q1 2026) as export controls bite — meaningful demand-mix shift | 4 |
| 6 | Business is understandable at a functional level but technically deep; "how it makes money" is simple, "why only they can do it" requires study | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

ASML sells the machines that print microchips. More specifically, it sells photolithography systems — room-sized pieces of equipment that use light to etch the transistor patterns onto silicon wafers. Every leading-edge logic chip (the processors in phones, AI accelerators, laptops) and increasingly every leading-edge memory chip is made by a tool that ASML built. The customer list is short and famous: TSMC, Samsung Foundry, Intel Foundry, SK Hynix, Micron, and a long tail of Chinese memory/logic fabs buying older DUV (deep ultraviolet) equipment. In one sentence: ASML is the landlord of Moore's Law, and the foundries are the tenants.

The revenue model has two halves. The first half is system sales — "boxes" shipped to customers at prices that range from roughly €50M for an older DUV immersion tool, €180–220M for a current-generation Low-NA EUV (NXE:3800E), up to roughly €380M+ for a High-NA EUV (EXE:5200B). This half is lumpy: you book revenue when a tool is shipped and accepted. The second half is the installed-base business — service contracts, upgrades, and spare parts for the ~6,000+ systems already in the field. This half is recurring, high-margin, and now roughly a fifth of total revenue. In FY2025 total revenue was €32.7B, operating margin 34.6%, gross margin 52.8%. Q1 2026 came in at €8.8B with 53% gross margin, and 2026 guidance was raised to €36–40B (per ASML and CNBC). So the business converts massive, concentrated demand cycles into very high unit economics.

There are really only three or four variables that drive this company, which is exactly the Buffett ideal. First, **leading-edge capex by the top three foundries** — when TSMC and Samsung build new fabs at 3nm, 2nm, 1.4nm, they must order ASML tools. Second, **wafer starts and utilization** on the existing installed base, which drive services revenue. Third, **the pace of High-NA EUV adoption** — each machine is ~2x the price of the previous generation and enables sub-2nm nodes. Fourth, **geopolitics**, which is the wild card: US and Dutch export rules have already pushed Chinese share from ~36% in Q4 2025 to ~19% in Q1 2026, and a proposed US bill would further restrict DUV sales. Outside those four, everything (FX, individual node timing, specific AI end-demand) matters less.

Revenue predictability is a "yes, with an asterisk." The backlog is enormous and customers give multi-year visibility on tool orders, but cycles are real — when foundries pause capex (as happened in memory in 2023), ASML's top line swings hard. Services is the smoother layer underneath. Notably, this was the first quarter ASML stopped disclosing quarterly order intake, which has made the lumpy half of the business a bit less transparent (CNBC, 2026-04-15). For an investor the right mental model is: system revenue moves with the fab build cycle, services compounds quietly, and the sum-of-parts should grow at mid-teens through the High-NA ramp if the current customer roadmaps hold.

Customer concentration is a real feature, not a bug. A handful of foundries buy almost everything. If TSMC decided to skip High-NA at 1.4nm (which it has publicly said it will delay to later nodes, per TrendForce), that's a meaningful near-term dent. Samsung accelerating High-NA for 2nm (two EXE:5200B tools confirmed for 1H26) partly offsets. Intel is the largest current High-NA bettor, with the first commercial EXE:5200B already installed. The implication: three customers effectively vote on what ASML's next three years look like.

Can you explain ASML in two minutes? Yes — "they are the sole maker of the lithography machines needed to produce advanced chips; the top three chipmakers buy them; they earn ~35% operating margins and have a decade-plus technology lead." The deeper "why only them" (Zeiss optics, Cymer light source, 40 years of co-development) takes longer, but the *business* is simple. This sits inside the circle of competence for anyone willing to study semiconductor capital equipment; it is not a black box.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** High-NA EUV ramps cleanly from 2027 onward, AI-driven wafer demand sustains fab capex through the decade, and ASML grows revenue at 10–15%/yr with expanding margins while services compounds as a moat-widener.
- **Bear case:** China export restrictions tighten further (proposed DUV ban), TSMC defers High-NA adoption, and a cyclical foundry capex pause produces a 20–30% revenue air-pocket in 2027–2028 despite the "monopoly."
- **Confidence:** High — the business is narrow, the customer list is short, the financials are clean, and the key drivers can be counted on one hand.

## Red Flags

- ASML stopped disclosing quarterly bookings in Q1 2026 — a modest transparency step backwards on the single most-watched leading indicator.
- Customer concentration at the top three foundries means any one customer's roadmap slip (especially TSMC on High-NA) directly dents the thesis.
- China dropped from 36% → 19% of sales in one quarter; further export-control escalation remains an open-ended risk.
- Revenue is reported in EUR while the ADR trades in USD — FX creates cosmetic noise in cross-statement comparisons.

## Score: 9 / 10

Few businesses are this clean to explain: one product category, a handful of customers, a small set of drivers, and dominant economics — the only reason this isn't a 10 is the geopolitical overlay that no one, including management, can fully underwrite.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — ASML

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-18
**Data Sources:** User-provided `context/ASML/financials.md` and `quant-valuation.md` (yfinance, 2026-04-18); TrendForce articles on High-NA EUV at Intel, Samsung, SK Hynix, TSMC; Tom's Hardware EXE:5200B installation coverage; Strange VC "ASML's 30-Year Monopoly" essay; MMMT Wealth moat deep-dive; training knowledge of lithography industry history and the Zeiss, Cymer, TRUMPF supply-chain structure. Patent counts and supplier counts reported variably across sources — ranges used below.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | ASML is a true monopoly in EUV — 100% share; Nikon and Canon never productized EUV and have effectively abandoned the race | 5 |
| 2 | The Zeiss SMT partnership (ASML owns ~25%) provides EUV mirrors/optics that no rival can source — a 20+ year hard-tech lock-in | 5 |
| 3 | ROIC ran 35–49% across FY2022–FY2025; gross margins stepped up from 50.5% → 52.8% over the same period — textbook evidence of pricing power | 4 |
| 4 | Service/installed-base revenue on 6,000+ fielded systems creates annuity income that would persist even if new-system orders paused for several years | 4 |
| 5 | Moat is cycle-independent, not cycle-dependent — unlike most hardware, each product generation *reinforces* rather than resets the lead (light-source + optics compound) | 4 |
| 6 | Weakest flank: single-customer leverage (TSMC can delay adoption) and geopolitical action (export controls) — both exogenous to the technology moat | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

ASML is one of the purest moats in public markets and the textbook example of what Buffett would call a "toll bridge." The monopoly is not a metaphor — in EUV lithography, market share is 100%. Nikon, once a credible photolithography rival, never shipped a commercial EUV tool and has effectively exited the leading edge. Canon offers "nanoimprint" as a niche alternative but it is not a substitute for EUV in high-volume manufacturing. In DUV immersion (the prior generation), ASML holds roughly 90%+ share with Nikon at the remainder. The competitive landscape is not "who else makes these?" — it's "who else *could*?" — and the honest answer is no one, on a 10-year horizon.

The moat is layered and mutually reinforcing. First is the **Zeiss SMT partnership**: EUV requires mirrors machined to sub-nanometer smoothness, a capability that exists nowhere else in the world. ASML bought 24.9% of Carl Zeiss SMT in 2016 for €1B precisely to lock this in, and the two companies co-develop every optics generation 5–10 years ahead of shipment. Second is the **Cymer light source** (ASML acquired Cymer in 2013) — generating EUV light requires firing 50,000 laser pulses per second at tin droplets to produce plasma; this is the kind of engineering that took 20 years to get to production yield. Third is the **supply-chain orchestration**: reports consistently cite 800+ specialized suppliers (TRUMPF for lasers, IBM for EUV photomasks, dozens of specialty chemical and component firms) that only ASML has the relationships and systems-engineering ability to integrate. Fourth is **intellectual property**: estimates range from 2,000+ EUV-specific patents to 10,000+ across the portfolio, plus the non-codified know-how that sits in a ~40,000-person Veldhoven talent pool.

The numbers validate the moat. FY2022–FY2025 ROIC: 42.3%, 48.9%, 35.6%, 39.5%. Gross margin stepped from 50.5% (FY2022) to 52.8% (FY2025), with Q1 2026 at 53.0% — even as mix shifted toward China DUV (lower ASP) and back away. Operating margin 34.6% in FY2025. These are not semiconductor-cyclical numbers; they are luxury-goods-adjacent economics attached to $400M industrial machines. And critically, the company was able to *raise* prices across tool generations by meaningful multiples — EXE:5200B at ~$380M+ vs. NXE:3800E at ~$200M — without losing a single leading-edge customer. That's pricing power in the purest form.

Switching costs reinforce the technology lead. A fab designs its process nodes around specific lithography tools 5+ years in advance. Recipes, resists, metrology, and yield learning all bake to a particular scanner. Swapping ASML out would mean re-qualifying an entire fab process, which doesn't happen in practice. This is why the installed base matters so much: once you've placed a dozen NXE tools, you are a captive services customer for 10+ years. The services half of the business (installed-base revenue) should continue compounding even if system sales have a cyclical pause.

Where is the moat weakest? Two places. First, **customer power**: the top three customers can individually delay purchasing decisions, and TSMC's public choice to stay on Low-NA EUV through 2nm and adopt High-NA only at 1.4nm is a textbook example of a monopolist's biggest customer saying "we'll wait." This doesn't break the moat but it does flatten near-term growth. Second, **government action**: export controls are the one force that can take revenue away regardless of technology leadership. The Dutch and US governments have already walled off EUV from China entirely and ratcheted DUV restrictions through 2025–2026; a proposed US bill would restrict DUV further. Geopolitics is the single vector ASML cannot engineer its way around.

Does each product cycle reset the advantage? This is the classic "hardware trap" question, and here the answer is genuinely no. The EUV-to-High-NA transition doesn't re-open the market — the same Zeiss optics partnership, the same Cymer light source, the same systems-integration capability scale up. Each new node makes the physics harder, which widens (not narrows) the gap between ASML and any hypothetical entrant. On a 10-year horizon, I see zero credible path for a competitor to reach commercial EUV — the lead-time alone would be a decade plus the ~€50B+ cumulative R&D already spent. That is a durable moat.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** High-NA becomes the mandatory tool for every sub-2nm node, ASML's ASP-per-tool roughly doubles, services revenue compounds mid-teens, and the company enjoys another decade of 35%+ operating margins with no competitive threat.
- **Bear case:** A TSMC-led High-NA adoption delay combined with a punitive DUV export ban compresses 2027–2028 revenue growth into low-single-digits despite the monopoly, proving that even a perfect moat can't outrun customer and political gravity.
- **Confidence:** High — this is among the clearest moats in public equities; the technology lead, supply-chain lock-in, and economic evidence all align.

## Red Flags

- Geopolitics is the one risk management cannot solve — further escalation of US/EU/Dutch export controls is a genuine tail event for the China segment.
- Customer concentration (TSMC, Samsung, Intel collectively) means moat strength exceeds *commercial* bargaining power — one big customer can move near-term results.
- High-NA adoption risk: if tools underperform customer yield expectations during 2026–2027 ramp, the ASP-expansion thesis gets delayed (not killed).
- Price-to-book of 1,271x reflects a business that is essentially all intellectual capital — the balance sheet is light, which is fine but means book-value anchoring is meaningless.

## Score: 10 / 10

A textbook, wide, durable moat with multiple reinforcing structural advantages (sole-source optics partnership, 40 years of co-developed IP, installed-base lock-in, supplier ecosystem); this is one of the cleanest monopolies in the global equity market and deserves the top of the scale.

# Management & Capital Allocation — ASML

**Analyst Role:** Stewardship Analyst
**Date:** 2026-04-18
**Data Sources:** User-provided `context/ASML/financials.md` and `quant-valuation.md` (yfinance, 2026-04-18); ASML Q1 2026 press release; ASML 2025 Corporate Governance section PDF; CNBC 2026-04-15 earnings coverage; Bloomberg coverage of Wennink transition to Heineken; IMD "I by IMD" profile of Fouquet; Bits&Chips interview "the faster you grow, the less efficient you get"; Bulios capital-return summary; training knowledge of Dutch two-tier board structure. Insider ownership is low in absolute terms (common for Dutch-listed large caps) — figures reported broadly rather than precisely.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | CEO succession from Peter Wennink (22 years, CFO then CEO) to Christophe Fouquet (former CBO) completed April 2024 — internal promotion, not outside hire | 4 |
| 2 | New €12B share buyback authorized for 2026–2028; €1.1B executed in Q1 2026; FY2025 buybacks totaled $6.0B — a sharp step up from $500M in FY2024 | 4 |
| 3 | Dividend raised 17% YoY to €7.50/share for 2025 — a multi-year pattern of modest but consistent dividend growth | 3 |
| 4 | Share count trend: ASML has been a net share-reducer — FY2022–FY2025 cumulative buybacks of ~$12B materially more than offset SBC | 4 |
| 5 | SBC at €202M on €32.7B revenue (~0.6%) is strikingly low for a tech company — not a dilution risk | 3 |
| 6 | Governance is Dutch two-tier (Supervisory Board + Management Board); no dual-class shares; Stichting foundation exists as takeover defense | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

The first capital-allocation question for any stock: is management rational with shareholder money? On ASML, the answer across the last five years is a clean "yes, with minor notes." The simple test — share count — shows it clearly. ASML spent $6.0B on buybacks in FY2025, $500M in FY2024, $1.0B in FY2023, and $4.6B in FY2022, for roughly $12B cumulative. Against ~385M diluted shares today, that's ~3.5% of the current market cap retired in four years, and the pie has been shrinking net of stock-based comp. Critically, stock-based comp is only €202M on €32.7B revenue (~0.6%) — for reference, that's roughly one-fifth of what typical US big-cap tech companies run. ASML is not giving the company away to employees.

The timing on buybacks is where the honesty question gets harder. FY2022 ($4.6B) was executed at an average price in the ~€500–600 range (a lucky time to buy in retrospect). FY2025 ($6.0B) was executed with the stock at much higher levels. The newly-announced €12B 2026–2028 program will be spent with the stock at ~$1,460 / ~€1,330, near all-time highs. At 47x trailing P/E and 30x forward P/E, this is not a contrarian buyback — it's a steady-state capital-return program. I'd grade this as "fine but not heroic"; management is not opportunistic the way Buffett is, but neither are they buying at obvious bubble prices. The services-heavy backend and decade-long growth visibility make a ratable buyback defensible, though not optimal on a per-share IV basis.

Dividend policy is sober: €7.50 for 2025 (17% increase YoY), representing a 0.6% yield on the current price. Payout is well below 50% of earnings — plenty of room to grow. ASML has historically preferred buybacks over dividends, which fits a high-ROIC compounder's playbook. M&A has been minimalist and strategic rather than empire-building: Cymer (2013, $2.6B) to lock in light sources, a ~25% stake in Carl Zeiss SMT (2016, €1B) to lock in optics, HMI (2016, ~$3.1B) for e-beam metrology. Every major deal was a vertical-integration move inside the core EUV value chain, not a diversification. This is exactly the kind of M&A Buffett and Munger have praised — "we don't do deals outside our circle."

Management quality under Christophe Fouquet is being tested in real time. He took over from Peter Wennink in April 2024 after a long internal apprenticeship (he was CBO, effectively running the customer-facing business before that). Succession was telegraphed well in advance and handled without drama — always a good sign. Fouquet's public communication style is direct; the Bits&Chips interview headline "the faster you grow, the less efficient you get" is the kind of operational humility Wennink was also known for. Q1 2026 guidance raised revenue range from €34–39B to €36–40B and Fouquet was candid about China risk and export-control uncertainty. No obvious spin or hype. That said, the one negative signal is that Q1 2026 was the first quarter where ASML stopped disclosing quarterly order intake, which has been the single most-watched leading indicator for the name. That's a transparency decision I'd push back on if I were a shareholder.

Governance is standard Dutch two-tier: a Supervisory Board (chaired by Nils Andersen, former Maersk CEO) and a Management Board (Fouquet as CEO, Roger Dassen as CFO, Jim Koonmen and Wayne Allan as Chief Strategy/Operations). No dual-class structure. A Stichting (foundation) exists as a takeover-defense mechanism common to Dutch public companies — not a dealbreaker, but worth knowing. Insider ownership in shares is low in absolute terms (typical for Dutch large-caps; Fouquet owns shares but not the multi-percent founder stake that US investors might look for). Institutional ownership sits around 75–80%. Compensation design ties heavily to multi-year EPS and relative TSR, which is better than revenue/EBITDA-based pay and reasonably aligned with per-share value. Roger Dassen has been CFO since 2018 and is a respected communicator — his investor-call narratives tend to be unusually clear for a capital-equipment CFO.

The simple tell — reading the last two earnings transcripts and the annual report — produces a consistent voice: plain-spoken, honest about cyclicality, candid about China, unafraid to guide wide ranges. Management doesn't cherry-pick metrics. They don't hide behind "adjusted" numbers (though they do use them). When the 2023 memory downturn hit they acknowledged it plainly. When the China pull-forward peaked in 2024, they flagged the reversion. That's the behavior of owners, not politicians — even if their actual share ownership is modest.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Fouquet extends the Wennink legacy of disciplined vertical M&A, ratable capital return, and honest communication while stewarding the High-NA transition cleanly — a "quiet compounder" story of steady buybacks shrinking the share count while earnings grow.
- **Bear case:** New management buys back stock at 45–50x P/E throughout 2026–2028, proves unopportunistic, and silently lowers transparency (starting with dropped order disclosure) — not a scandal, just a quiet erosion of the Wennink-era owner-operator culture.
- **Confidence:** Medium–High — the track record under Wennink was strong, Fouquet is an internal promotion, but 18 months is a short test period and the dropped bookings disclosure is a yellow flag worth watching.

## Red Flags

- Discontinuing quarterly order-intake disclosure in Q1 2026 — a transparency regression on the most-watched metric.
- Buybacks executed at elevated multiples (current ~47x trailing P/E) — ratable, not opportunistic.
- Insider ownership in terms of dollar shares is low — the classic "widely-held European large-cap" pattern; Fouquet, Dassen, and the board are professional managers rather than owner-operators.
- Stichting takeover defense in place — not active currently, but a governance feature US-trained investors should be aware of.

## Score: 8 / 10

Strong, owner-operator-adjacent capital allocation under a well-telegraphed CEO succession; the combination of disciplined vertical M&A, low SBC, meaningful net share retirement, and candid communication earns high marks — docked from 9 for the dropped order-book disclosure and ratable (not opportunistic) buybacks at premium multiples.

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on capital.** ASML's returns profile is at the very top end of the public universe. The point is that ASML can absorb ~EUR 10B of extra invested capital in two years and still generate ~40% pre-tax-adjusted returns on the base. A near-peerless economic engine — monopoly EUV pricing power, 40% ROIC on a doubling invested-capital base, 34–35% operating margin, asset-light execution, and EUR 38.8B backlog providing two-year visibility. ## Detailed Analysis

Against cash and short-term investments of EUR 13.3B, net debt is roughly negative EUR 8.9B — ASML has EUR 8.9B more cash than debt. The quant model carries net debt at −EUR 8.9B for valuation purposes, confirming this. Interest expense actually declined YoY from EUR 163M (FY2024) despite similar debt levels, likely reflecting maturities of older higher-coupon notes being refinanced or repaid.

Net cash of EUR 8.9B, interest coverage of 97×, debt/EBITDA of 0.35×, EUR 38.8B customer-prepaid backlog, and FCF that keeps growing despite EUR 6B/yr buybacks.
