## Detailed Analysis

ASML is one of the purest moats in public markets and the textbook example of what Buffett would call a "toll bridge." The monopoly is not a metaphor — in EUV lithography, market share is 100%. Nikon, once a credible photolithography rival, never shipped a commercial EUV tool and has effectively exited the leading edge. Canon offers "nanoimprint" as a niche alternative but it is not a substitute for EUV in high-volume manufacturing. In DUV immersion (the prior generation), ASML holds roughly 90%+ share with Nikon at the remainder. The competitive landscape is not "who else makes these?" — it's "who else *could*?" — and the honest answer is no one, on a 10-year horizon.

The moat is layered and mutually reinforcing. First is the **Zeiss SMT partnership**: EUV requires mirrors machined to sub-nanometer smoothness, a capability that exists nowhere else in the world. ASML bought 24.9% of Carl Zeiss SMT in 2016 for €1B precisely to lock this in, and the two companies co-develop every optics generation 5–10 years ahead of shipment. Second is the **Cymer light source** (ASML acquired Cymer in 2013) — generating EUV light requires firing 50,000 laser pulses per second at tin droplets to produce plasma; this is the kind of engineering that took 20 years to get to production yield. Third is the **supply-chain orchestration**: reports consistently cite 800+ specialized suppliers (TRUMPF for lasers, IBM for EUV photomasks, dozens of specialty chemical and component firms) that only ASML has the relationships and systems-engineering ability to integrate. Fourth is **intellectual property**: estimates range from 2,000+ EUV-specific patents to 10,000+ across the portfolio, plus the non-codified know-how that sits in a ~40,000-person Veldhoven talent pool.

The numbers validate the moat. FY2022–FY2025 ROIC: 42.3%, 48.9%, 35.6%, 39.5%. Gross margin stepped from 50.5% (FY2022) to 52.8% (FY2025), with Q1 2026 at 53.0% — even as mix shifted toward China DUV (lower ASP) and back away. Operating margin 34.6% in FY2025. These are not semiconductor-cyclical numbers; they are luxury-goods-adjacent economics attached to $400M industrial machines. And critically, the company was able to *raise* prices across tool generations by meaningful multiples — EXE:5200B at ~$380M+ vs. NXE:3800E at ~$200M — without losing a single leading-edge customer. That's pricing power in the purest form.

Switching costs reinforce the technology lead. A fab designs its process nodes around specific lithography tools 5+ years in advance. Recipes, resists, metrology, and yield learning all bake to a particular scanner. Swapping ASML out would mean re-qualifying an entire fab process, which doesn't happen in practice. This is why the installed base matters so much: once you've placed a dozen NXE tools, you are a captive services customer for 10+ years. The services half of the business (installed-base revenue) should continue compounding even if system sales have a cyclical pause.

Where is the moat weakest? Two places. First, **customer power**: the top three customers can individually delay purchasing decisions, and TSMC's public choice to stay on Low-NA EUV through 2nm and adopt High-NA only at 1.4nm is a textbook example of a monopolist's biggest customer saying "we'll wait." This doesn't break the moat but it does flatten near-term growth. Second, **government action**: export controls are the one force that can take revenue away regardless of technology leadership. The Dutch and US governments have already walled off EUV from China entirely and ratcheted DUV restrictions through 2025–2026; a proposed US bill would restrict DUV further. Geopolitics is the single vector ASML cannot engineer its way around.

Does each product cycle reset the advantage? This is the classic "hardware trap" question, and here the answer is genuinely no. The EUV-to-High-NA transition doesn't re-open the market — the same Zeiss optics partnership, the same Cymer light source, the same systems-integration capability scale up. Each new node makes the physics harder, which widens (not narrows) the gap between ASML and any hypothetical entrant. On a 10-year horizon, I see zero credible path for a competitor to reach commercial EUV — the lead-time alone would be a decade plus the ~€50B+ cumulative R&D already spent. That is a durable moat.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** High-NA becomes the mandatory tool for every sub-2nm node, ASML's ASP-per-tool roughly doubles, services revenue compounds mid-teens, and the company enjoys another decade of 35%+ operating margins with no competitive threat.
- **Bear case:** A TSMC-led High-NA adoption delay combined with a punitive DUV export ban compresses 2027–2028 revenue growth into low-single-digits despite the monopoly, proving that even a perfect moat can't outrun customer and political gravity.
- **Confidence:** High — this is among the clearest moats in public equities; the technology lead, supply-chain lock-in, and economic evidence all align.

## Red Flags

- Geopolitics is the one risk management cannot solve — further escalation of US/EU/Dutch export controls is a genuine tail event for the China segment.
- Customer concentration (TSMC, Samsung, Intel collectively) means moat strength exceeds *commercial* bargaining power — one big customer can move near-term results.
- High-NA adoption risk: if tools underperform customer yield expectations during 2026–2027 ramp, the ASP-expansion thesis gets delayed (not killed).
- Price-to-book of 1,271x reflects a business that is essentially all intellectual capital — the balance sheet is light, which is fine but means book-value anchoring is meaningless.

## Score: 10 / 10

A textbook, wide, durable moat with multiple reinforcing structural advantages (sole-source optics partnership, 40 years of co-developed IP, installed-base lock-in, supplier ecosystem); this is one of the cleanest monopolies in the global equity market and deserves the top of the scale.
