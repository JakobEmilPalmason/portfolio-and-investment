## Detailed Analysis

The first capital-allocation question for any stock: is management rational with shareholder money? On ASML, the answer across the last five years is a clean "yes, with minor notes." The simple test — share count — shows it clearly. ASML spent $6.0B on buybacks in FY2025, $500M in FY2024, $1.0B in FY2023, and $4.6B in FY2022, for roughly $12B cumulative. Against ~385M diluted shares today, that's ~3.5% of the current market cap retired in four years, and the pie has been shrinking net of stock-based comp. Critically, stock-based comp is only €202M on €32.7B revenue (~0.6%) — for reference, that's roughly one-fifth of what typical US big-cap tech companies run. ASML is not giving the company away to employees.

The timing on buybacks is where the honesty question gets harder. FY2022 ($4.6B) was executed at an average price in the ~€500–600 range (a lucky time to buy in retrospect). FY2025 ($6.0B) was executed with the stock at much higher levels. The newly-announced €12B 2026–2028 program will be spent with the stock at ~$1,460 / ~€1,330, near all-time highs. At 47x trailing P/E and 30x forward P/E, this is not a contrarian buyback — it's a steady-state capital-return program. I'd grade this as "fine but not heroic"; management is not opportunistic the way Buffett is, but neither are they buying at obvious bubble prices. The services-heavy backend and decade-long growth visibility make a ratable buyback defensible, though not optimal on a per-share IV basis.

Dividend policy is sober: €7.50 for 2025 (17% increase YoY), representing a 0.6% yield on the current price. Payout is well below 50% of earnings — plenty of room to grow. ASML has historically preferred buybacks over dividends, which fits a high-ROIC compounder's playbook. M&A has been minimalist and strategic rather than empire-building: Cymer (2013, $2.6B) to lock in light sources, a ~25% stake in Carl Zeiss SMT (2016, €1B) to lock in optics, HMI (2016, ~$3.1B) for e-beam metrology. Every major deal was a vertical-integration move inside the core EUV value chain, not a diversification. This is exactly the kind of M&A Buffett and Munger have praised — "we don't do deals outside our circle."

Management quality under Christophe Fouquet is being tested in real time. He took over from Peter Wennink in April 2024 after a long internal apprenticeship (he was CBO, effectively running the customer-facing business before that). Succession was telegraphed well in advance and handled without drama — always a good sign. Fouquet's public communication style is direct; the Bits&Chips interview headline "the faster you grow, the less efficient you get" is the kind of operational humility Wennink was also known for. Q1 2026 guidance raised revenue range from €34–39B to €36–40B and Fouquet was candid about China risk and export-control uncertainty. No obvious spin or hype. That said, the one negative signal is that Q1 2026 was the first quarter where ASML stopped disclosing quarterly order intake, which has been the single most-watched leading indicator for the name. That's a transparency decision I'd push back on if I were a shareholder.

Governance is standard Dutch two-tier: a Supervisory Board (chaired by Nils Andersen, former Maersk CEO) and a Management Board (Fouquet as CEO, Roger Dassen as CFO, Jim Koonmen and Wayne Allan as Chief Strategy/Operations). No dual-class structure. A Stichting (foundation) exists as a takeover-defense mechanism common to Dutch public companies — not a dealbreaker, but worth knowing. Insider ownership in shares is low in absolute terms (typical for Dutch large-caps; Fouquet owns shares but not the multi-percent founder stake that US investors might look for). Institutional ownership sits around 75–80%. Compensation design ties heavily to multi-year EPS and relative TSR, which is better than revenue/EBITDA-based pay and reasonably aligned with per-share value. Roger Dassen has been CFO since 2018 and is a respected communicator — his investor-call narratives tend to be unusually clear for a capital-equipment CFO.

The simple tell — reading the last two earnings transcripts and the annual report — produces a consistent voice: plain-spoken, honest about cyclicality, candid about China, unafraid to guide wide ranges. Management doesn't cherry-pick metrics. They don't hide behind "adjusted" numbers (though they do use them). When the 2023 memory downturn hit they acknowledged it plainly. When the China pull-forward peaked in 2024, they flagged the reversion. That's the behavior of owners, not politicians — even if their actual share ownership is modest.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Fouquet extends the Wennink legacy of disciplined vertical M&A, ratable capital return, and honest communication while stewarding the High-NA transition cleanly — a "quiet compounder" story of steady buybacks shrinking the share count while earnings grow.
- **Bear case:** New management buys back stock at 45–50x P/E throughout 2026–2028, proves unopportunistic, and silently lowers transparency (starting with dropped order disclosure) — not a scandal, just a quiet erosion of the Wennink-era owner-operator culture.
- **Confidence:** Medium–High — the track record under Wennink was strong, Fouquet is an internal promotion, but 18 months is a short test period and the dropped bookings disclosure is a yellow flag worth watching.

## Red Flags

- Discontinuing quarterly order-intake disclosure in Q1 2026 — a transparency regression on the most-watched metric.
- Buybacks executed at elevated multiples (current ~47x trailing P/E) — ratable, not opportunistic.
- Insider ownership in terms of dollar shares is low — the classic "widely-held European large-cap" pattern; Fouquet, Dassen, and the board are professional managers rather than owner-operators.
- Stichting takeover defense in place — not active currently, but a governance feature US-trained investors should be aware of.

## Score: 8 / 10

Strong, owner-operator-adjacent capital allocation under a well-telegraphed CEO succession; the combination of disciplined vertical M&A, low SBC, meaningful net share retirement, and candid communication earns high marks — docked from 9 for the dropped order-book disclosure and ratable (not opportunistic) buybacks at premium multiples.
