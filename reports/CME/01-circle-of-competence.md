## Detailed Analysis

**What CME sells and to whom.** CME Group operates regulated futures and options-on-futures exchanges — primarily CME, CBOT, NYMEX, and COMEX — covering six product lines: interest rates (SOFR, Treasuries), equity index futures (S&P 500, Nasdaq), energy (crude oil, natural gas), agricultural commodities (corn, soybeans, wheat), metals (gold, silver, copper), and foreign exchange. Its customers are banks, broker-dealers, hedge funds, asset managers, corporations hedging commodity exposure, and increasingly retail-adjacent participants accessing markets through intermediaries. CME Group also licenses real-time and historical market data and provides clearing and settlement services through CME Clearing.

**How it makes money.** Revenue falls into two main buckets. Clearing and transaction fees — the toll-booth revenue collected per contract traded and cleared — account for roughly 87% of total revenue. In 2025 quarterly run-rates, this was approximately $1.3–1.4B per quarter, totaling roughly $5.6–5.7B for the year. Market data fees — licensing of real-time prices, historical data, and indices to financial institutions, vendors, and data consumers — produced a record $803M in 2025 (~12% of total). A small remainder comes from other services. The business is essentially a toll road on global risk transfer: every time a financial institution hedges an interest rate exposure or a commodity producer locks in a price, CME collects a fee.

**Key revenue drivers.** There are four variables that determine CME's revenue trajectory. First, average daily volume (ADV) — 28.1M contracts/day in 2025, up 6% year-over-year — is the single most important driver; rate volatility, geopolitical uncertainty, and market stress all drive volume up. Second, rate per contract (RPC) determines how much revenue each contract generates; product mix matters here, as equity index contracts generally carry higher RPCs than rates. Third, market data subscriber growth and pricing power drive the data segment, which is somewhat insulated from volume cycles. Fourth, international expansion is a structural growth lever: Asia ADV grew 18% and EMEA ADV grew 6% in 2025.

**Revenue predictability.** This is a semi-recurring, volume-leveraged business. Market data revenue (~$803M) is highly recurring — annual contracts, subscription-based, with sticky enterprise customers. Transaction fee revenue is not recurring in the conventional sense but is extremely consistent: as long as global financial markets function, participants need to hedge, and CME is the dominant venue. Volume does fluctuate — low-volatility, complacent markets reduce trading activity — but this is a risk that tends to correct with time. The business has now delivered four consecutive years of record revenue, suggesting the underlying structural demand for derivatives is secular, not cyclical.

**Customer concentration.** CME's customers are the global financial system itself. No single customer approaches a material share of volume or revenue. The largest users are major banks and broker-dealers (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, etc.), who collectively facilitate enormous volumes on behalf of their own clients. This diffuse customer base is a significant strength: no single counterparty can threaten CME's economics by exiting.

**Two-minute explanation.** CME Group runs the world's largest futures and options exchange. Every day, banks, hedge funds, and corporations trade contracts to lock in prices for things like interest rates, oil, gold, and stock indexes — essentially buying insurance against price moves. CME charges a small fee for every contract traded and cleared on its platform. The more volatility in markets, the more people hedge, the more CME earns. On top of that, it sells the market data generated by all that trading — prices, volumes, indices — to financial firms who need it to run their businesses. It's a high-margin, asset-light toll road sitting at the center of global risk management.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** CME is a structural monopoly in US interest rate and commodity futures with irreplaceable liquidity, four consecutive years of record revenue, and a secular expansion into international markets and crypto.
- **Bear case:** Revenue is volume-sensitive and can decline in low-volatility environments; the business model is straightforward to describe but the products it supports (SOFR futures, Treasury futures, energy derivatives) require domain knowledge to analyze deeply.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is simple to understand: it is a toll-road on derivatives trading with a complementary data business. The revenue drivers are observable, the customers are diversified, and the track record of record revenues is unambiguous.

## Red Flags

- None identified for circle-of-competence purposes. The business model is transparent and well-documented.

## Score: 9 / 10

CME Group is one of the clearest, most understandable financial infrastructure businesses in existence: a regulated toll-road on global risk transfer with a simple fee-per-contract model, diversified customers, and a high-quality recurring data revenue stream.
