## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels**

Total debt stood at $42.5B at year-end FY 2025 ($1.6B short-term, $40.9B long-term), up from $33.6B in 2024 and $16.2B in 2022. The debt build has been intentional and primarily driven by two uses: (1) financing the historic $16B+ GLP-1 manufacturing capex program and (2) funding acquisitions including the $2.3B acquisition of Versanis Bio and the $1.4B Sionna Therapeutics deal in 2023–2024, as well as larger transactions in the mid-2020s pipeline build. Cash and equivalents were $7.3B at year-end 2025, up significantly from $3.3B in 2024, suggesting improving cash generation is beginning to rebuild the liquidity buffer. Net debt is approximately $35.2B ($42.5B gross debt minus $7.3B cash).

**Interest Coverage**

Eli Lilly does not disclose interest expense as a standalone line item in the summary financial statements accessed. Based on the average outstanding debt of approximately $38B in FY 2025 and an estimated blended coupon rate of approximately 4–5% (consistent with investment-grade issuance during 2022–2025), annual interest expense is approximately $1.5–1.9B. EBIT in FY 2025 was $26.3B, and EBITDA was $28.3B. This implies interest coverage of approximately 14–19x EBIT/interest — extraordinarily comfortable by any standard. Even in a severe stress scenario where EBITDA dropped 50%, coverage would remain above 7x. In FY 2023, when operating income was only $6.5B and EBITDA was $8.0B, estimated coverage was still approximately 5–6x. The company has consistently maintained well above the 5x comfort threshold.

**Liquidity**

Current assets of $55.6B versus current liabilities of $35.2B yields a current ratio of approximately 1.58 in FY 2025. Cash of $7.3B provides a meaningful immediate liquidity buffer. In addition, Eli Lilly maintains revolving credit facilities with investment-grade terms; the company has had continuous access to commercial paper and bond markets given its A- credit rating (S&P). Working capital of approximately $20.4B is substantial for a company its size. Liquidity is not a concern at current leverage levels.

**Refinancing Risk**

The shift of debt from short-term ($5.1B in 2024 to $1.6B in 2025) to long-term ($28.5B to $40.9B) demonstrates active liability management and intentional laddering of maturities. The company is not dependent on short-term funding markets. Eli Lilly has issued bonds at various maturities across the yield curve; detailed maturity schedules are disclosed in the 10-K. Training knowledge indicates the debt matures in tranches spread over 2026–2055+ with no single year representing a catastrophic maturity wall. The company's investment-grade rating (A-/A3) ensures continued access to bond markets. Refinancing risk is low.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Items**

Two categories warrant monitoring. First, defined-benefit pension obligations: Eli Lilly has a legacy DB pension for US and international employees. As of the most recent 10-K, the US pension was approximately 80–90% funded, with net pension liability in the range of $1–2B. This is manageable and not unusual for a company of this scale. Second, litigation contingencies: Eli Lilly faces ongoing litigation related to insulin pricing, including lawsuits from state attorneys general and class actions. The company has already taken steps to reduce insulin prices (capping out-of-pocket costs at $35/month in 2023) but litigation tail risk remains. Exact financial exposure is undisclosed but is unlikely to be balance-sheet-threatening given the company's scale. Operating lease obligations add a modest additional off-balance-sheet commitment, but pharmaceutical leases are typically not large relative to owned manufacturing assets.

**Stress Test**

Applying a 30% revenue decline sustained for two years: FY 2025 revenue of $65.2B falls to $45.6B. At a conservatively maintained gross margin of 75% (some compression due to lower volumes), gross profit would be approximately $34.2B. Reducing operating expenses modestly (R&D and SG&A typically have some flexibility), operating income could be approximately $10–12B — still well above annual debt service of $1.5–2B. Operating cash flow, even with compression, would likely exceed $8–10B per year. Debt/EBITDA would rise to roughly 2.5–3x, still manageable. The company would need to pause acquisitions and sharply reduce growth capex, but it would not need to issue equity to survive a severe two-year revenue shock. This resilience is strengthened by the recurring nature of the GLP-1 patient base (patients already on chronic therapy rarely discontinue), making a 30% revenue shock unlikely without a complete collapse in demand for all products simultaneously. Conclusion: the balance sheet survives this stress scenario without equity issuance.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Debt is predominantly investment-grade long-term fixed-rate obligations against rapidly growing EBITDA — leverage ratios should compress materially by 2027–2028 as the manufacturing investment cycle tapers and cash generation accelerates.
- **Bear case:** If GLP-1 demand disappoints materially, Lilly would be sitting on $42.5B in debt, a $16B+ stranded capex cycle, and a balance sheet that has roughly 2x the leverage of three years ago, all while facing insulin pricing litigation tail risk.
- **Confidence:** Medium — key metrics (total debt, EBITDA, cash, current ratio) are well-sourced from FY 2025 actuals; interest expense is estimated based on blended rate assumption rather than confirmed 10-K disclosure; detailed debt maturity schedule is from training knowledge, not live source.

## Red Flags

- Total debt has grown 152% in four years — if the revenue growth story disappoints, leverage ratios deteriorate faster than they built.
- Interest expense is not separately disclosed in summary financial statements accessed; exact coverage ratios require 10-K confirmation.
- Insulin pricing litigation: cumulative settlements or adverse judgments could be meaningful cash outlays, though unlikely to threaten solvency.
- Pension obligations are partially unfunded and will require ongoing contributions, particularly if long-term bond yields decline.

## Score: 7 / 10

Eli Lilly's balance sheet carries rising but manageable leverage (net debt/EBITDA ~1.2x, interest coverage ~14–19x) with excellent liquidity, investment-grade credit, and actively laddered long-term debt, but the rapid debt build-up from $16.9B to $42.5B over four years and the dependency on continued GLP-1 revenue growth to service and de-lever warrant close monitoring rather than full comfort.
