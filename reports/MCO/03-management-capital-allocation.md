## Detailed Analysis

**Leadership and Tenure.** Rob Fauber became CEO of Moody's Corporation in January 2021, having spent nearly 20 years at the company in roles spanning the analytics, corporate development, and ratings businesses. His career-long immersion in Moody's operations — not a parachuted outsider — gives him deep institutional knowledge. Under his leadership, Moody's has articulated a "connected intelligence" strategy: assembling proprietary datasets (ratings history, private company data, climate/catastrophe models, supply chain data) into an integrated platform that customers can access through multiple products and AI interfaces. The 2025 results ($7.72B revenue, +9%; adjusted EPS $14.94, +20%; FCF $2.6B) validate this strategy operationally.

**Compensation Design.** Fauber's total 2025 compensation was approximately $18.1M (up ~6% from $17.0M in 2024). Compensation is structured with a meaningful portion in performance-based equity — consistent with standard practice for S&P 500 CEOs of this caliber. The payout ratio and absolute level are not excessive for a company generating $2.6B in free cash flow. The CHRC (Compensation and Human Resources Committee) Charter (April 2025) confirms the presence of independent directors on the compensation committee, satisfying NYSE governance standards. No red flags in governance structure.

**Capital Allocation Track Record.** The hierarchy Fauber has articulated is: (1) reinvest in organic growth, (2) strategic M&A for unique data sets, (3) return excess capital to shareholders. This is textbook sensible for a business that throws off substantial free cash flow. The M&A record is largely positive: Bureau van Dijk expanded MA's private company data position precisely when private markets were becoming the dominant growth area in global finance. RMS ($2B, 2021) added catastrophe and climate risk modeling, broadening Moody's addressable market into insurance and ESG-linked risk. Both acquisitions have been integrated into the MA platform. The two-year buyback hiatus (2022–2023) after heavy M&A spending was a reasonable prioritization of balance sheet repair; the resumption of $2.0B in buybacks for 2026 signals management's confidence in normalized free cash flow generation. Dividends have grown for 17 consecutive years without interruption, including through the 2022 issuance downturn — a demonstration of the MA recurring revenue base absorbing the MIS cyclicality.

**Balance Sheet Context.** As of end-2025, Moody's carries approximately $7.0B in long-term debt against $2.4B in cash, resulting in net debt of roughly $4.6B. This is approximately 1.8x 2025 FCF of $2.6B — manageable for a company with high predictable cash generation and investment-grade credit quality. EBIT/interest coverage is approximately 17x. The leverage is a deliberate choice to fund M&A without diluting equity; it is not imprudent.

**Berkshire's Role as Anchor Shareholder.** Berkshire Hathaway's 14.54% stake — held continuously for 25+ years — is qualitatively significant beyond the number. Buffett's public praise of Moody's as one of the best businesses in the world, and his refusal to sell despite a 1,000%+ gain, constitutes arguably the most credible long-term endorsement in the investing world. The presence of a large, permanent, value-oriented anchor shareholder with Buffett-era governance expectations tends to keep management discipline high and hostile-short-termism low.

**Candor and Transparency.** Moody's investor communications are clear, consistent, and specific. The company provides explicit full-year guidance with defined ranges for revenue growth, adjusted operating margin, FCF, and share repurchases. Fauber's public statements at investor conferences acknowledge competitive threats (KBRA/DBRS in ABS niches, AI disruption risk) rather than dismissing them, while articulating Moody's response strategy concretely. This is the candor pattern of management that respects shareholders' ability to evaluate risk.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Fauber's connected intelligence strategy is correctly positioned for the next decade — private credit, AI-driven risk analytics, and climate risk modeling are all structural growth vectors that leverage Moody's existing data assets; capital allocation is disciplined and shareholder-friendly.
- **Bear case:** Major acquisitions (Bureau van Dijk, RMS) required heavy leverage and integration effort; if the next strategic acquisition is larger and less synergistic, capital discipline could be tested; insider selling patterns, while routine, reflect executives who prefer cash to equity — not an alignment concern yet, but worth monitoring.
- **Confidence:** High — Fauber's track record over five years is strong, Berkshire's permanent anchor presence is a governance backstop, and the capital allocation framework is clearly stated and consistently executed.

## Red Flags

- Insider trading activity shows 0 open-market purchases vs. 13 sales over the past 6 months among named executives — this is common in large-cap companies (mostly tax withholding and 10b5-1 plans) but notable that no executives are buying at current prices
- Debt load of $7.0B is elevated in absolute terms; a major credit market freeze combined with a large acquisition could strain the balance sheet
- CEO compensation ($18M) is appropriate in context but is worth monitoring as a fraction of FCF; no current concern

## Score: 8 / 10

Fauber's strategic vision is sound, capital allocation is sensible and consistently executed, Berkshire's anchor ownership provides governance ballast, and 17 years of uninterrupted dividend growth reflects management confidence in cash generation — the only notable reservation is modest direct insider equity ownership outside of grant-and-sell patterns.
