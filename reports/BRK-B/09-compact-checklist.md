# Compact Checklist — BRK-B

**Date:** 2026-03-12

1. **Business**: Berkshire Hathaway owns 80+ operating subsidiaries (BNSF, GEICO, BHE, Precision Castparts, Pilot, and dozens more) plus a $264B equity portfolio, and generates wealth primarily by investing a $176B insurance float — premiums collected today and claims paid later — at near-zero cost.

2. **Moat**: Berkshire's $176B insurance float (the world's largest, grown for 30+ consecutive years) functions as permanent, near-zero-cost leverage that no competitor can replicate without decades of sustained underwriting discipline, reinforced by a $373B fortress balance sheet that makes Berkshire the only credible lender-of-last-resort in any major financial crisis.

3. **Economics**: Berkshire generated $44.49B in operating earnings and ~$25.1B in free cash flow in FY2025, with BNSF margins expanding to 34.5%, GEICO posting an 84.3% combined ratio, and insurance investment income of $12.5B — though headline ROIC (~7%) is structurally depressed by $373B in idle T-bills that are strategic dry powder, not inefficiency.

4. **Management**: Greg Abel bought $14.6M of BRK-B with his own money in March 2026, pledged to invest his entire $15M after-tax salary in BRK stock every year he leads the company, restarted buybacks at ~$492 after a 21-month hiatus, and acknowledged BNSF's operating gap and Kraft Heinz as a prior mistake — all within his first 10 weeks as CEO.

5. **Debt risk**: With $373B in cash and T-bills covering total debt of $129.1B nearly 3x over, 19.2x EBIT interest coverage, and $46B in annual operating cash flow, Berkshire could sustain a 30% revenue decline for a decade without accessing credit markets — and would deploy capital aggressively into the downturn rather than fight for survival.

6. **Price**: At $492/share (1.48x book), sum-of-parts analysis ($369B cash + $264B equity portfolio + operating businesses at 13-15x) supports a narrow intrinsic value range of $456-$501/share, confirming the stock is near the high end of fair value and requires Abel deploying the cash at 9%+ returns to justify meaningful upside from here.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business-level margin of safety is exceptional and nearly unmatched — zero holding-company recourse debt, $369B in liquid T-bills representing $171/share of near-liquid value, and 60+ diversified subsidiaries — but the price-level margin of safety is thin at only 8-15% cushion to bear-case intrinsic value of $420-450.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if Abel demonstrates value-destructive capital allocation — specifically, a large acquisition priced above 12-13x earnings with no clear moat, or the introduction of stock-based compensation and options that signal cultural drift away from the owner-operator model Berkshire has sustained for 60 years.
