## Detailed Analysis

**What does Applied Materials sell, and to whom?**

Applied Materials makes the equipment that semiconductor manufacturers use to build chips. Think of it this way: TSMC, Samsung, and Intel design and operate giant factories called fabs; Applied Materials sells them the machines that go inside those factories — the tools that deposit thin layers of material, etch away unwanted material, and inspect chips at atomic scale. Without Applied's equipment (and competitors like ASML, Lam Research, and KLA), chipmakers could not manufacture semiconductors at all. The customers are the world's leading chipmakers, along with display panel makers (a smaller and declining part of the business).

**How does it actually make money?**

Revenue comes from three sources. The largest is Semiconductor Systems — the upfront sale of large, complex manufacturing tools costing anywhere from hundreds of thousands to tens of millions of dollars each. These tools are not consumable; a fab might use a given machine for ten or more years. The second revenue stream is Applied Global Services (AGS), which covers ongoing service contracts, spare parts, and process optimization consulting. AGS generated $1.56B in Q4 FY2025 alone, growing 15% year-over-year, with more than two-thirds now contractually recurring. The third stream is Display and Adjacent Markets, covering flat-panel display equipment, which is small and not a growth driver.

**What are the key revenue drivers?**

The primary driver is global semiconductor capital expenditure (capex), especially at leading-edge logic (sub-3nm chips) and advanced DRAM. As chipmakers push to smaller nodes, they need more deposition and etch steps — which directly favors Applied Materials. The second driver is AI infrastructure buildout: the push for high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and advanced packaging for AI chips is a structural tailwind. The third driver is the AGS installed base; as more tools are deployed globally, the recurring service revenue base grows even in equipment downturns. The fourth driver, currently a headwind, is China policy — US export restrictions have substantially reduced the accessible market in China.

**How predictable is the revenue?**

Semiconductor equipment is inherently cyclical. When chipmakers pull back on capex (as happened in 2023), equipment orders fall sharply. However, Applied Materials has two moderating factors. First, leading-edge spending is less cyclical than mature-node spending — the world's top foundries rarely pause spending on 3nm or 2nm capacity. Second, the AGS subscription base provides genuine floor revenue: even in a capex downturn, fabs still need spare parts and service to keep existing tools running. Q1 FY2026 revenue of $7.0B was roughly flat year-over-year despite China headwinds, illustrating this partial insulation. Seasonality within a fiscal year is modest; the bigger swings are multi-year capex cycles.

**Customer concentration**

This is a genuine concentration risk. TSMC is the single largest customer and is the cleanest story — they are consistently investing in leading-edge capacity. Samsung and Intel are both investing more cautiously right now as they struggle to win foundry customers. Country concentration is also meaningful: Taiwan (TSMC), South Korea (Samsung/SK Hynix), and China together account for roughly 75%+ of total revenue. The China exposure specifically has become a structural risk given US export controls, which have already removed Applied's access to China's memory and mature-node segments.

**Can you explain this business in 2 minutes?**

Applied Materials makes the machines that make chips. Every time TSMC builds a new fab or Samsung upgrades its memory plant, Applied Materials gets an order. The company has the broadest product portfolio of any equipment vendor — it competes meaningfully in all three major tool categories (deposition, etch, and process control), unlike rivals that specialize in one area. Once a chipmaker installs Applied's tools and qualifies them in their process, switching is painful and expensive — creating strong customer stickiness. A growing service business provides some recurring revenue even when chip capex slows. The main risks are US-China trade policy (applied has already lost most of its China business) and the capital spending cycles of its small number of huge customers.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** AI-driven semiconductor investment is structurally accelerating, and Applied Materials is the broadest-portfolio equipment vendor positioned to capture leading-edge logic, HBM, and advanced packaging demand.
- **Bear case:** China revenue implosion ($600M–$710M headwind in FY2026), concentrated customer base, and cyclical capex exposure could compress earnings meaningfully if a semiconductor downturn coincides with further geopolitical restrictions.
- **Confidence:** High — this is a straightforward industrial technology business with transparent financials, clear customers, and well-documented competitive dynamics. The business model itself is easy to understand; the risks are well-known and quantifiable.

## Red Flags

- China revenue decline from ~40% to ~25% of systems revenue in two years is a structural loss, not a temporary one — export restrictions appear permanent
- Samsung and Intel (two of the three largest customers) are both in strategic difficulty, reducing near-term equipment spending
- Significant customer concentration: three customers drive the vast majority of Semiconductor Systems revenue

## Score: 8 / 10

Applied Materials is a clearly understandable industrial technology company — the business model, revenue drivers, and risks are all transparent — losing two points for meaningful customer concentration and the geopolitical complexity layered onto China exposure.
