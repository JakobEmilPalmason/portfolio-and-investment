## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Broadcom carries approximately $66 billion in total debt as of February 1, 2026 (Q1 FY2026), consisting of $3.15 billion in current maturities and $62.8 billion in long-term debt. Against cash of roughly $10-16 billion (varying by quarter), net debt sits around $50-54 billion. This is a large absolute number, but context matters: Broadcom generates adjusted EBITDA of $43 billion annually (FY2025), putting the debt/adjusted EBITDA ratio at roughly 1.5x. Even using the more conservative GAAP EBITDA of $34 billion, the ratio is approximately 1.9x. Both figures are comfortably below the 2x level that would be considered conservative for a company of this quality. The debt originated primarily from the $61 billion VMware acquisition in November 2023, which initially loaded Broadcom with $74 billion in obligations. The company has since repaid approximately $15 billion, a remarkable pace of deleveraging. Critically, in July 2025, Broadcom fully repaid and terminated the $30.4 billion term loan facility (of which $6 billion remained outstanding) by issuing $6 billion in fixed-rate senior notes. This eliminated floating-rate exposure from the term loans, locking in predictable interest costs. The debt now consists almost entirely of fixed-rate senior notes with maturities spread across 2027, 2029, 2030, 2031, 2033, 2035, 2036, 2038, and 2056. Broadcom also redeemed $3.6 billion of notes due 2027 in late 2025, actively managing near-term maturities. The maturity ladder appears well-laddered, with no single year presenting an outsized refinancing cliff based on the data available.

**Interest Coverage and Debt Service.** Broadcom's EBIT (operating income) for FY2025 was approximately $25.5-26.4 billion, against interest expense of roughly $3.2 billion. That produces an interest coverage ratio of 7.9-9.2x, depending on the exact EBIT figure used. This is well above the 5x comfort zone and suggests Broadcom can service its debt even if earnings fell by half. The shift from floating-rate term loans to fixed-rate notes further de-risks the interest expense line — in a rising rate environment, Broadcom's interest costs are now largely locked in. With Q1 FY2026 showing continued earnings growth (revenue up 29% year-over-year to $19.3B), the coverage ratio is likely improving further.

**Liquidity.** Broadcom's liquidity position is strong. The company holds a $7.5 billion undrawn revolving credit facility (established January 2025, maturing January 2030), with zero borrowings outstanding as of the most recent reports. Cash on hand has fluctuated between $10-16 billion over recent quarters. The current ratio was approximately 1.5-1.9x in FY2025, indicating current assets comfortably exceed current liabilities. Free cash flow of $8 billion per quarter ($26.9B annualized in FY2025) means Broadcom generates enough cash every two months to cover a full year of interest expense. Could Broadcom survive 12-24 months with zero revenue? With $10-16B in cash, a $7.5B revolver, and roughly $15-20B in annual operating expenses, the company could likely sustain operations for approximately 12-18 months on existing resources alone — though this extreme scenario is highly unlikely for a company with Broadcom's entrenched customer relationships and recurring software revenue (VMware).

**Refinancing Risk.** This was the key concern post-VMware acquisition, but Broadcom has methodically addressed it. By replacing floating-rate term loans with fixed-rate notes and laddering maturities out to 2056, the company has dramatically reduced its dependence on capital markets being "open" in any given year. The $3.6B redemption of 2027 notes further reduces near-term refinancing needs. With investment-grade ratings from all three agencies (and recent upgrades), Broadcom would have access to debt markets in all but the most extreme credit crises. The undrawn $7.5B revolver provides additional cushion.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Items.** Specific data on pension obligations was not readily available from web searches. Broadcom, as a semiconductor and software company, does not typically carry large defined-benefit pension plans — its workforce obligations are primarily through defined-contribution (401k-style) plans. Operating leases are now on the balance sheet under ASC 842. No material off-balance-sheet guarantees or contingent liabilities were flagged in the sources reviewed, though a thorough review of the 10-K footnotes would be prudent.

**Stress Test.** If Broadcom's revenue dropped 30% for two years (from ~$64B to ~$45B annually), operating margins would compress but likely remain positive given the high proportion of software revenue (VMware contributes recurring, high-margin revenue). EBITDA might fall to $20-25B, still comfortably covering $3.2B in annual interest expense (6-8x coverage). The company would generate enough free cash flow to continue servicing debt without issuing equity, though buybacks and aggressive debt paydown would pause. Broadcom would survive this scenario without existential risk, which is the test that matters.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Broadcom continues deleveraging at the current pace, reaching net debt/EBITDA below 1.0x within 2-3 years, while FCF growth from AI accelerates — the balance sheet becomes a fortress and credit ratings approach single-A territory across all agencies.
- **Bear case:** A severe semiconductor downturn coincides with a credit market freeze, making it difficult to refinance the ~$3B in near-term maturities, though $27B in annual FCF makes this unlikely to become a survival issue.
- **Confidence:** High — Broadcom just reported Q1 FY2026 earnings three days ago (March 4, 2026), so balance sheet data is very fresh, and the deleveraging trajectory is clearly established with multiple data points.

## Red Flags

- Total absolute debt of $66B remains large — if AI spending declines sharply or VMware integration stumbles, the debt load limits financial flexibility.
- The new $10B buyback authorization, while a sign of confidence, diverts cash from debt reduction — management is choosing returns to shareholders over faster deleveraging.
- Current portion of debt is $3.15B, requiring near-term refinancing or repayment — manageable but worth monitoring.
- Pension and off-balance-sheet liability details were not fully verified from public sources; a review of 10-K footnotes is recommended.

## Score: 7 / 10

Broadcom carries a large absolute debt load from the VMware acquisition, but its aggressive deleveraging ($15B repaid in under two years), strong interest coverage (8-9x), massive free cash flow ($27B annually), well-laddered fixed-rate maturities, undrawn $7.5B revolver, and investment-grade ratings from all three agencies place it firmly in the "conservative leverage, comfortable in a recession" category — though the sheer size of the debt prevents a higher score.
