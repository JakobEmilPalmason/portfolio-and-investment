# Compact Checklist — V

**Date:** 2026-03-11

1. **Business**: Visa sells access to a global payment network — routing authorization messages between issuing and acquiring banks on every card transaction — and earns a toll of roughly 0.11–0.13% on domestic transactions and ~1% on cross-border transactions, producing $40.0B in net revenue and $21.6B in free cash flow in FY2025 with zero credit risk.
2. **Moat**: A self-reinforcing two-sided network effect — 44 million merchant acceptance points in 200+ countries and billions of cardholders — makes it impossible for a new entrant to attract merchants without cardholders or cardholders without merchants, and the switching costs for issuing banks (re-issuing cards, rebuilding integrations, renegotiating contracts) are measured in years and hundreds of millions of dollars, as confirmed by Visa's ~70% global card volume share holding stable for a decade despite billions invested in fintech alternatives.
3. **Economics**: Visa sustains a ~28% ROIC (far above its ~8% WACC), a ~54% FCF margin on $40B net revenue, ~60% non-GAAP operating margin, and near-zero capital intensity with only $1.5B in capex against $21.6B in free cash flow — textbook evidence of an extreme earnings machine with no structural deterioration over the FY2021–FY2025 period.
4. **Management**: Management allocates capital with consistent discipline — $18.3B in buybacks in FY2025 alone reducing diluted share count from ~2.2B to ~1.94B over five years, a growing dividend at a 23% payout ratio well-covered by FCF, and targeted M&A (Currencycloud, Pismo) rather than empire-building — though at 0.09% insider ownership the C-suite are stewards of a great business, not owner-operators.
5. **Debt risk**: With ~$5.2B in net debt against $27B in EBITDA (0.2x net leverage), 45x interest coverage, $20B in liquid assets, and $21.6B annual FCF that dwarfs the $5.3B in debt due in FY2026, Visa could survive even an unprecedented 30% multi-year revenue collapse with no solvency risk.
6. **Price**: At ~$314/share (~24x forward consensus EPS of ~$13.10), the stock requires approximately 8–10% annual EPS growth with a terminal multiple of 20–22x to deliver a 9–10% IRR — a bar meaningfully below Visa's historical 13–15% EPS CAGR, meaning today's price does not demand perfection but does demand that the DOJ antitrust case not produce a structural remedy on debit routing.
7. **Margin of safety**: The price-based margin of safety is thin — $314 sits near the midpoint of the $300–$340 base-case intrinsic value range with only ~15–20% downside to the bear case — but the business itself provides the safety: no credit risk, no inventory, 54% FCF margins, a network moat that took 60 years to build, and a history of recovering from 30–40% drawdowns within 12–24 months.
8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if the DOJ antitrust trial (expected 2027–2028) produces a structural remedy forcing open debit routing access that management explicitly guides will reduce annual revenue by more than $3B, or if net revenue growth falls below 6% for two consecutive quarters alongside FCF margin compression below 45%.
