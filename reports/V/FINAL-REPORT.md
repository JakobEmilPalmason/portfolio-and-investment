# Investment Analysis: V — Visa Inc.

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Verdict:** Own

> Visa is the world's dominant card payment network — a pure toll-road on global commerce that earns a small fraction of a percent on every transaction, takes no credit risk, and generates $21.6 billion in free cash flow on $40 billion in net revenue. The business is among the most economically durable ever built, with a two-sided network moat that has held ~70% global card volume share for a decade despite billions of dollars thrown at fintech alternatives. At $310/share, the stock is fairly valued rather than cheap — three independent DCF models place intrinsic value 10–27% lower — and a live DOJ antitrust case targeting debit economics introduces genuine tail risk; nonetheless, the exceptional business quality, below-historical-average valuation multiples, and decade-long earnings compounding profile justify a core ownership position for a patient long-term investor.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Visa sells access to a two-sided payment network — routing authorization messages between issuing and acquiring banks on every card transaction — earning a small fraction of a percent on $14 trillion in annual payment volume with no credit risk, no inventory, and near-zero marginal cost per additional transaction.

2. **Moat**: A bilateral network effect spanning 44 million merchant locations and billions of cardholders across 200+ countries makes the acceptance footprint impossible to replicate, reinforced by switching costs so high that re-issuing cards and rebuilding merchant contracts would take issuing banks years and hundreds of millions of dollars, as confirmed by Visa's ~70% global card volume share holding stable for a decade despite billions invested in fintech alternatives.

3. **Economics**: Visa generates $21.6B in free cash flow on $40B in net revenue (54% FCF margin), sustains ~28% ROIC against an ~8% WACC, and converts over 100% of GAAP net income to free cash — on a capital base requiring only $1.5B in annual capex.

4. **Management**: Management returns capital with discipline — $18.3B in buybacks in FY2025 alone has reduced diluted share count ~12% since FY2020, governance is standard single-class with no founder entrenchment, and communication is metrics-driven and consistent — but with only 0.09% insider ownership, these are professional stewards of a great business, not owner-operators.

5. **Debt risk**: Net debt is only ~$5.2B against $21.6B in annual free cash flow (net debt/EBITDA ~0.2x), interest coverage is approximately 45x, and $20B in liquid assets means Visa could retire all near-term debt maturities from cash on hand without touching capital markets.

6. **Price**: At $310/share (~24x forward FY2026 consensus EPS of ~$13.10), the current price requires approximately 8–10% annual EPS growth over 10 years — below Visa's historical 13–15% delivery rate — meaning the market is pricing in a meaningful slowdown but not pricing in perfection, and this math only holds if the DOJ antitrust case does not produce a structural debit remedy.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business itself provides the primary protection — no credit risk, a moat that survived COVID and the 2008 crisis intact, and the ability to sustain profitability through a 30% revenue decline — because at $310 the price-based margin of safety is thin, with three independent DCF models placing fair value 10–27% below the current price.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if the DOJ antitrust trial (expected 2027–2028) produces a structural remedy forcing open debit routing access and management revises net revenue growth guidance materially downward as a result, or if net revenue growth falls below 6% for two consecutive quarters alongside FCF margin compression below 45%.

---

## Key Risks

1. **DOJ antitrust case (debit)** — Motion to dismiss denied June 2025; trial expected 2027–2028. A structural remedy forcing open debit routing could impair $7B+ in annual debit revenue, reducing fair value by $50–80/share. This is the single largest unresolved risk and the primary reason the stock trades below historical multiples. Likelihood: 25–30%; Severity: High.

2. **Client incentive escalation** — Incentives grew 14% YoY in FY2025 vs. net revenue growth of 11%; this is a multi-year trend. If incentives continue outpacing gross revenue, effective take rates compress steadily, undermining the margin story. Likelihood: 40%; Severity: Medium (gradual).

3. **Real-time / account-to-account payments adoption** — FedNow, Pix, UPI, and stablecoin rails bypass card networks entirely. Currently primarily P2P, but small-merchant A2A acceptance is growing in emerging markets. Structural displacement of debit card economics in younger demographics is a 10–15 year risk. Likelihood: 20% (accelerated); Severity: Medium-High over long term.

4. **Cross-border volume softening** — Cross-border is Visa's highest-margin segment (~29% of FY2025 revenue). Geopolitical fragmentation, de-dollarization trends, or a global travel slowdown could compress this revenue stream materially. Likelihood: 30%; Severity: Medium.

5. **Credit Card Competition Act (CCCA)** — If passed by Congress, would extend routing competition to credit cards — a more severe scenario than the debit DOJ case. Status as of early 2026: not passed, but periodically resurfaces with political support. Likelihood: 15%; Severity: Very High if enacted.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - Price declines to $275–$290, bringing forward P/E to ~21–22x and approaching the base-case DCF cluster; margin of safety becomes genuine rather than thin
  - DOJ case settles on favorable terms (minor behavioral remedy, no structural routing requirements), removing the primary multiple overhang and signaling path to historical 27–30x forward multiples
  - Value-Added Services growth accelerates further (already +28% YoY in Q1 FY2026) and is re-rated by the market as a distinct high-margin software layer, driving upward earnings estimate revisions

- **Sell triggers**:
  - DOJ trial produces a structural remedy (forced debit routing access) and management revises net revenue growth guidance materially downward
  - Net revenue growth falls below 6% for two consecutive quarters alongside FCF margin compression below 45% — indicating structural, not cyclical, deterioration
  - Valuation reaches 35x+ forward P/E on a full earnings basis (trim point if the position has grown large from price appreciation, as upside becomes asymmetrically limited)

### 1. Circle of Competence

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12

#### Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Visa operates a pure toll-road model: it never lends money and never takes credit risk, it simply moves information between banks and earns a fee each time | 5 |
| 2 | FY2025 net revenue reached $40.0 billion (+11% YoY) with 258 billion processed transactions and $14 trillion in total payments volume | 5 |
| 3 | Revenue is diversified across four streams — data processing ($20.0B), service fees ($17.5B), international transactions ($14.2B), other ($4.1B) — but all four are driven by the same underlying volume and pricing levers | 4 |

#### Detailed Analysis

**What Visa sells and to whom.** Visa sells access to a payment network — the pipes and protocols that let a buyer's bank communicate instantly with a seller's bank to authorize, clear, and settle a transaction. Its direct customers are financial institutions (issuing banks that put Visa-branded cards in consumers' wallets and acquiring banks that sign up merchants). Consumers and merchants are end users of the network but are not Visa's direct paying customers. Visa also sells value-added services such as fraud scoring, currency conversion, and data analytics to financial institutions and enterprise clients.

**How Visa actually makes money.** Visa earns revenue across four reported lines. Service revenues ($17.5B in FY2025) are fees charged to card issuers based on payments volume on Visa cards in a prior period — essentially a licensing fee for using the Visa brand and network. Data processing revenues ($20.0B) are per-transaction fees collected for authorizing, clearing, and settling each payment; these are roughly 0.11–0.13% of transaction value on domestic cards. International transaction revenues ($14.2B) are earned on cross-border transactions where the merchant's and cardholder's banks are in different countries; the yield is approximately 1% of transaction value, making cross-border Visa's most profitable revenue stream. Other revenues ($4.1B, +27% YoY) cover licensing, consulting, and Visa Direct (push payments). Crucially, Visa does not extend credit, take deposits, or absorb losses from card defaults — all credit risk sits with the issuing banks.

**Key revenue drivers.** The three primary volume levers are: (1) payments volume — total dollar value of goods and services purchased with Visa cards, which reached $14 trillion in FY2025; (2) processed transactions — the count of individual swipes, taps, and online transactions, which hit 258 billion; and (3) cross-border volume, up 13% in FY2025 driven by international travel and e-commerce. A secondary lever is pricing: Visa adjusts its fee schedules (client incentives and discount rates) periodically. There is also secular tailwind from the global shift from cash to electronic payments, which remains incomplete — cash still accounts for roughly 20% of global consumer spending.

**Revenue predictability.** Visa's revenue is among the most predictable in large-cap equities. The business has no inventory, no commodity input costs, and no accounts receivable risk. Revenue scales directly and mechanically with payments volume and transaction count, both of which grow with nominal GDP plus the cash-to-card conversion tailwind. The only meaningful near-term volatility comes from cross-border volumes (sensitive to global travel and geopolitical disruption) and currency movements on international revenue. Guidance accuracy is consistently high; Visa has met or beaten consensus EPS estimates in every quarter of the past five fiscal years.

**Customer concentration.** No single bank, merchant, or geography accounts for a material share of Visa's revenue. The top issuers (e.g., JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America) are significant partners but the relationship is deeply symbiotic — switching to a competing network would require re-issuing tens of millions of cards and renegotiating merchant acceptance contracts. Visa operates in over 200 countries. While the US is the largest single market, international markets represent a growing share of volume.

**Two-minute explanation.** Every time a consumer taps a Visa card at a merchant, a message flies from the merchant's terminal through Visa's network to the consumer's bank asking "Is this transaction approved?" Visa routes that message, runs fraud checks, and sends back a yes or no — in under two seconds. For providing that plumbing, Visa collects a small fraction of a percent from the two banks on every transaction, every day, forever. It never touches the money itself. Because every merchant wants to accept Visa (consumers carry it) and every consumer wants to carry Visa (merchants accept it), the network grows stronger with every new participant. Visa FY2025: $40B revenue, $22.5B non-GAAP net income, 258 billion transactions, $14 trillion in payment flows.

#### Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The secular shift from cash to digital payments is a decade-long tailwind; Visa earns a toll on every transaction with no credit risk, no inventory, and near-zero marginal cost at scale.
- **Bear case:** Regulatory intervention on interchange fees or a successful real-time payments alternative (e.g., FedNow, account-to-account schemes) could structurally reduce Visa's volume or pricing power.
- **Confidence:** High — Visa's business model is exceptionally transparent; all revenue drivers are publicly reported quarterly and the underlying mechanics are simple.

#### Red Flags

- Cross-border revenue is the highest-yield segment and is sensitive to global travel disruption (pandemic-era demonstrated this clearly)
- Reported GAAP net income ($20.1B) was lower than non-GAAP ($22.5B) in FY2025 due to a litigation provision increase — ongoing legal exposure to interchange fee lawsuits is a persistent background risk
- GAAP operating expenses rose 30% in FY2025 (vs. 11% non-GAAP) due to litigation charges; this should be monitored for escalation

**Score: 10 / 10**

### 2. Durable Competitive Advantage

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** Web search results (ainvest.com moat analysis, alphaspread.com, seekingalpha.com network effects article, gurufocus.com moat score, macrotrends.net financials, medianama.com FY2025 earnings, official Visa FY2025 earnings release via investor.visa.com). Market share figures sourced from ainvest.com citing card network data; individual analyst estimates — confirmed directionally across multiple sources. Training knowledge used for competitive dynamics context.

#### Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Visa commands ~70% of global credit/debit/prepaid purchase volume, with Mastercard at ~30% — a near-duopoly created and sustained by network effects | 5 |
| 2 | Network effects are self-reinforcing and bilateral: more merchants accept Visa because more consumers carry it; more consumers carry it because more merchants accept it | 5 |
| 3 | FY2025 non-GAAP operating margin was approximately 60%, sustained for multiple years — the clearest numerical evidence of pricing power and moat | 5 |
| 4 | Switching costs for issuing banks are extremely high: re-issuing cards, renegotiating merchant contracts, and rebuilding consumer habit requires years and hundreds of millions in cost | 4 |
| 5 | Visa's brand is globally trusted as a proxy for payment acceptance; it is accepted at ~44 million merchant locations in 200+ countries | 3 |
| 6 | The moat's weakest point is account-to-account (A2A) real-time payments (FedNow, UPI, Pix) which bypass card networks entirely — growing in emerging markets and gaining traction in developed ones | 4 |

#### Detailed Analysis

**Type of moat.** Visa possesses multiple reinforcing moat types, each of which would individually be meaningful; together they are exceptional. The primary moat is a two-sided network effect: the network's value scales with both the number of cardholders and the number of accepting merchants simultaneously. Because Visa has ~44 million merchant acceptance points and billions of cardholders across 200+ countries, the network is essentially impossible to replicate from scratch. A competitor cannot sign up merchants without cardholders and cannot attract cardholders without merchant acceptance. The 2020s fintech wave has confirmed this — despite billions of dollars invested, no new card network has emerged to challenge Visa's acceptance footprint.

Secondary moat dimensions include: (1) switching costs — issuing banks have deep integrations with Visa's systems for authorization, settlement, fraud scoring, and dispute management; migrating away requires rebuilding those integrations, re-issuing physical cards to existing customers, and managing a multi-year transition; (2) intangible assets — Visa's brand represents a globally recognized guarantee of payment acceptance, which commands trust from both cardholders and merchants independently of the underlying financial institution; (3) cost advantage at scale — with 258 billion transactions processed annually over fixed infrastructure, Visa's marginal cost per transaction is fractions of a cent, giving it an unassailable cost position versus any smaller network attempting to compete on price.

**Durability over 10 years.** The card network moat is likely to remain intact over a 10-year horizon, with one meaningful caveat. The secular tailwinds — cash-to-digital conversion, growth in e-commerce, cross-border commerce expansion — all favor Visa. The structural moat (bilateral network, switching costs, global acceptance) is not threatened by any existing or plausible near-term competitor in the card rails. Visa's own response — Visa Direct, its push-payments platform, and its stablecoin settlement infrastructure — suggests management is aware of the A2A risk and is positioning the network to be the rails for new payment forms, not just traditional card transactions.

**Moat in numbers.** Non-GAAP operating margin of approximately 60% sustained over multiple years is the clearest signal of an enduring moat. Free cash flow grew 15% in FY2025 to $21.6 billion — a level that represents ~54% of net revenue being converted to free cash. Market share has been essentially stable for a decade: Visa's ~70% share of global card volume has not eroded despite heavy fintech investment, BNPL growth, and real-time payment infrastructure build-outs in multiple countries.

**Where the moat is weakest.** The primary structural threat is account-to-account (A2A) payment systems that bypass card networks entirely. These include India's UPI, Brazil's Pix, the EU's SEPA Instant, and the US Federal Reserve's FedNow. Stablecoin transaction volume reportedly exceeded Visa's in the first half of 2025 (though the comparison is methodologically contested). In markets where A2A is deeply embedded (India, Brazil), Visa's card share is lower and more contested. In developed markets (US, Europe), A2A has grown but card habits remain dominant.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** No. Visa's acceptance network, issuer relationships, and consumer habits were built over 60+ years. They do not reset with a product launch or a software update. The payment infrastructure Visa operates is deeply embedded in global commerce infrastructure — POS terminals, bank systems, e-commerce checkout flows — in ways that are costly and slow to change.

#### Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A 60% operating margin sustained over many years, ~70% global market share, and 44 million merchant acceptance points reflect a moat that is extremely difficult to replicate and benefits from every dollar of global commerce growth.
- **Bear case:** Real-time A2A payment infrastructure (FedNow, UPI, Pix, stablecoins) is growing globally and could structurally displace card payments in segments Visa currently dominates, compressing both volume and pricing over a 10-15 year horizon.
- **Confidence:** High — the moat evidence is numerical, historical, and structural; the risk is real but is a decade-scale challenge, not an imminent threat.

#### Red Flags

- A2A real-time payments gaining merchant acceptance globally: 37% of merchants accept RTP as of 2025, up sharply YoY
- Stablecoin transaction volumes exceeding traditional card network volumes (methodological comparability is debated, but trajectory is notable)
- Regulatory risk: US and EU regulators continue to scrutinize interchange fee levels; any legislative cap would directly reduce Visa's revenue yield
- Antitrust attention: Visa's attempted acquisition of Plaid (blocked 2021) highlighted regulatory sensitivity; future acquisitions face elevated scrutiny

**Score: 9 / 10**

#### Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership.** Collective insider ownership at Visa is approximately 0.09% of shares outstanding — a figure that reflects Visa's status as a very large, widely held public company that has been institutionally owned for well over a decade. The CEO, CFO, and board members receive substantial equity compensation but tend to sell systematically as RSUs vest. There are no founders with concentrated equity stakes — Visa went public in 2008 with its current institutional ownership structure.

**Capital allocation track record.** The primary use of free cash flow is share buybacks: the company repurchased $18.3B in FY2025, $16.7B in FY2024, and $12.1B in FY2023 — a clear upward trend. The April 2025 $30B new authorization signals continued commitment to buybacks as the primary capital return vehicle. Diluted share count has declined from approximately 2.2B shares in FY2020 to approximately 1.94B in FY2025 — roughly a 12% reduction in five years. Dividends are a secondary return vehicle: current dividend is $2.39/share with a ~23% payout ratio, well covered by free cash flow and growing consistently each year.

**M&A track record.** Visa has been relatively restrained on M&A. Notable transactions include: the Plaid acquisition attempt ($5.3B, 2019, blocked by DOJ in 2021), Currencycloud ($962M, 2021), and Pismo ($1B, 2023). These acquisitions are targeted at extending Visa's infrastructure into B2B payments and new payment rails rather than buying competitors or unrelated businesses. Post-Plaid, the company has been more cautious about large acquisitions that could attract antitrust scrutiny.

**Compensation design.** CEO McInerney's $31.6M FY2025 package is substantial, with approximately 60% in stock awards and options subject to multi-year vesting with performance conditions tied to net revenue growth and EPS. SBC is roughly 4–5% of net revenue — a modest ratio not meaningfully dilutive given the buyback scale.

**Candor and consistency.** Visa's management communications are clear, consistent, and quantitatively grounded. The company has historically met or narrowly beaten its own guidance ranges. Management does not obscure bad news in complex adjustments — the FY2025 GAAP vs. non-GAAP divergence due to litigation provision was disclosed and explained clearly.

**Governance structure.** Visa has a standard single-class voting structure for Class A common stock — no dual-class shares. The board is majority independent with no poison pill provisions. The governance structure is unremarkable in a positive sense.

#### Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Disciplined, consistent capital allocation — $18B+ in annual buybacks reducing share count steadily, growing FCF, simple governance, no empire-building — makes Visa's management one of the most reliable stewards of a great business in large-cap US equities.
- **Bear case:** Very low insider ownership means management's alignment is primarily through stock compensation vesting cycles rather than personal wealth tied to long-term performance.
- **Confidence:** High — capital allocation track record is publicly documented, consistent, and easily verified.

#### Red Flags

- Insider ownership at 0.09% is among the lowest of any large-cap business — executives are employees of a great business rather than owner-operators
- CEO compensation ($31.6M, +21% YoY) is high relative to the incremental management complexity of Visa's simple, self-reinforcing business
- Ongoing litigation provisions (inflating GAAP expenses) represent a persistent legal liability
- Systematic insider selling as equity awards vest — worth monitoring for any unusual volume or concentration

### 4. Business Economics

**Analyst Role:** Business Economics Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-11
**Data Sources:** Visa FY2025 Q4 Earnings Release (investor.visa.com), MacroTrends historical margin/FCF data, GuruFocus ROIC data, stockanalysis.com statistics, web search synthesis of FY2021–FY2025 financials

#### Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |

| 1 | Visa's ROIC averaged ~28–33% over the past 3–5 years (FY2025: ~28%), far above its ~8% WACC — sustained, compounding spread | 5 |

| 3 | FY2025 free cash flow was $21.6B on $40.0B revenue (54% FCF margin), with operating cash flow of $23.1B | 5 |
| 4 | Gross margins consistently ~97.7–97.8%; operating margin ~56%; net margin ~50% — among the highest of any large-cap globally | 5 |

| 6 | Client incentives of $15.8B (FY2025, +14% YoY) are the key cost offset to gross revenue — must be tracked as a margin headwind | 3 |

#### Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** Visa's ROIC has been in the 27–34% range across FY2021–FY2025. ROE climbed from the low 40s in FY2022–2023 to approximately 54–61% by late FY2025, driven partly by continued share buybacks reducing equity. Both metrics have been stable-to-rising, with no year of deterioration.

**Margin Structure.** Visa's gross margin sits at ~97.7–97.8% because its primary "cost of revenue" is minimal. In FY2025, gross revenue was roughly $55.8B and client incentives were $15.8B, netting to $40.0B. Operating margin on net revenue was approximately 56% in FY2025; excluding litigation and restructuring charges, adjusted operating margin is closer to 60–67%. Net income margin was ~50% in FY2025.

**Cash Generation.** FY2025 operating cash flow was $23.1B; free cash flow was $21.6B (after $1.5B capex). FCF conversion relative to net income (~$20.1B) was approximately 107% — above 100% because D&A and non-cash charges supplement net income. This is among the highest FCF conversion ratios of any large-cap business.

**Capital Intensity.** Capex of $1.5B against $40.0B revenue (3.75%) reflects one of the most capital-light large businesses globally. Incremental transaction volume flows through with near-zero marginal capital cost. "Owner earnings" are likely very close to reported FCF.

**Revenue Quality.** The model is quasi-volume-recurring — driven by deeply embedded consumer and merchant behavior that changes very slowly. FY2025 growth of 11.3% was balanced between volume growth and cross-border recovery; value-added services grew faster still. There are no meaningful one-time revenue items.

**Operating Leverage.** Visa's cost base is largely fixed. The primary risk to operating leverage is client incentive growth outpacing gross revenue — in FY2025 incentives grew 14% versus net revenue growth of 11%, a mild headwind. GAAP operating expenses rose 30% in FY2025 due primarily to a ~$2.7B litigation provision, which is non-recurring in nature.

#### Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Visa is among the most economically powerful businesses ever built — asset-light, ~28% ROIC, ~50% net margin, ~54% FCF yield on revenue, with global volume growth compounding for decades at minimal incremental capital cost.
- **Bear case:** Client incentives growing faster than net revenue could compress margins over time, and regulatory/antitrust pressure on interchange fees could structurally reduce the revenue take rate.
- **Confidence:** High — Visa discloses detailed financials; FY2025 is fully reported; margin and return metrics are well-sourced and consistent across multiple data providers.

#### Red Flags

- Client incentives ($15.8B in FY2025, +14% YoY) growing faster than net revenue — if this trend persists, it compresses the effective revenue per transaction
- GAAP operating expenses jumped 30% in FY2025 due to a large litigation provision (~$2.7B+); while non-recurring, regulatory litigation is a persistent overhang
- ROE is elevated partly due to buyback-driven equity reduction; ROIC is the more relevant metric and remains strong

**Score: 10 / 10**

### 5. Balance Sheet Safety

**Analyst Role:** Balance Sheet Safety Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-11
**Data Sources:** Visa FY2025 Q4 Earnings Release (investor.visa.com / s1.q4cdn.com), GuruFocus interest coverage data, BusinessQuant total debt history, web search synthesis of FY2025 10-K filings and SEC filings

#### Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Cash and investment securities totaled $20.0B at September 30, 2025, versus total debt of $25.2B — net debt of only ~$5.2B against $26.6B operating income | 5 |
| 2 | Interest coverage ratio (EBIT/interest) was approximately 33–45x in FY2025 — extreme safety margin on debt service | 5 |
| 3 | Debt maturity schedule is well-laddered: $5.3B due 2026, $2.75B in 2027, ~$12.6B thereafter — near-term maturities are modest relative to annual FCF of $21.6B | 4 |
| 4 | Visa earns net interest income — it collects more interest on its $20B cash/securities than it pays on debt, inverting the typical leverage risk | 4 |
| 5 | The $30B+ U.S. litigation escrow and ongoing MDL settlement create off-balance-sheet contingent liability exposure, though the November 2025 proposed settlement addresses the largest open MDL claims | 3 |
| 6 | Total debt increased 20.8% YoY to $25.2B in FY2025, driven by new senior note issuances — leverage is rising but remains conservative relative to earnings power | 3 |

#### Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** As of September 30, 2025, Visa carried $25.2B in total debt (all senior unsecured notes — fixed-rate, long-duration, no floating-rate exposure). Against FY2025 EBITDA of approximately $27–28B, the net debt-to-EBITDA ratio is approximately 0.2x. Even on gross debt, the ratio is under 1x. This is one of the most conservative leverage profiles among large-cap US companies.
