## Detailed Analysis

**5–10+ Year Suitability.** Over a genuine decade-long horizon, FICO has the characteristics of a compounder: near-monopoly on credit scoring in the world's largest consumer lending market, low capital intensity, strong FCF conversion, and management that has consistently returned capital via buybacks. From 2012 to 2025 the stock compounded at roughly 25%+ annually. The core thesis — that creditworthiness measurement is a critical utility and FICO is the incumbent standard — remains valid. What has changed is that 2025–2026 introduced the first credible structural threat to pricing power in the Scores segment. A 10-year holder should expect FICO to be worth substantially more than today, but must accept that the path could include a genuine multi-year impairment if the competitive transition proves severe. This is not a "set and forget" position — it requires annual thesis review.

**Volatility Tolerance.** FICO is a volatile stock for its market cap. The historical pattern includes a 34% drawdown from Sep 2024 to Mar 2026 with no change in underlying business profitability — purely multiple compression. The bear case from current prices implies a further 42–49% decline. The 2022 trough ($412) represented an 80%+ decline from the prior peak. Investors in FICO must be psychologically and financially prepared for a position to be cut in half or more at some point during a holding period. This eliminates FICO as a suitable holding for risk-averse investors, those with short time horizons, or those who would be forced to sell on drawdowns. Only investors with genuine conviction in the franchise and no forced-selling pressure should hold or buy.

**Catalysts.** In the near term (0–12 months), positive catalysts are limited: Q2 FY2026 earnings beat (April 2026), evidence of resilient mortgage Scores pricing, international Scores acceleration, and Software ARR re-acceleration above 10%. Negative catalysts are more numerous and more likely: additional analyst downgrades following UBS, VantageScore market share data from FHFA reporting, further mortgage volume weakness in a high-rate environment, CFPB or congressional attention to score fee pricing, and any Fannie/Freddie formal guidance expanding score-agnostic underwriting. Longer-term (3–5 years), the primary positive catalyst would be FICO 10T and VantageScore 4.0 being truly additive (both required) rather than substitutive in mortgage underwriting, alongside international Scores becoming a material revenue contributor.

**Position Sizing.** At $1,285/share, FICO is an expensive stock in absolute terms. For a $1M portfolio, a 3% position is ~$30,000 or ~23 shares. A standard 5% position is ~$64,000 or ~50 shares. Given the current uncertainty around the Scores segment's pricing model, sizing above 3–4% of a portfolio is difficult to justify unless: (a) the investor has a high-conviction view that VantageScore fears are overblown, (b) the position was established at lower prices with a lower cost basis, or (c) the investor's risk tolerance and time horizon explicitly accommodate a 40–50% drawdown on this position. New buyers at $1,285 should limit initial sizing to 1–2% of portfolio, with a defined add-down plan to 3% if the stock reaches $950–1,000 (providing genuine margin of safety).

**Portfolio Overlap.** FICO should be evaluated alongside existing holdings in financial data and analytics. It shares thematic overlap with Moody's (MCO), S&P Global (SPGI), Verisk (VRSK), and MSCI — all of which are licensing-model businesses with regulatory moats and similar growth profiles. A portfolio that already holds one or more of these names at meaningful weight has significant concentration in the "information monopoly" thesis. If that thesis faces structural pressure (as FICO's now does with Scores pricing), co-movement risk is high. FICO's specific risk — government-mandated use of its product being relaxed — is unique and not fully shared by MCO/SPGI/MSCI, making it somewhat complementary rather than fully duplicative.

**Entry, Exit, and Review Plan.** Entry: At current prices ($1,285), initiate no position or a monitoring-only starter position (0.5–1%) with a defined add-down plan at $950 (base case discount) and $750 (bear case approaching). Hold condition: the thesis requires intact pricing power in the Scores segment AND Software ARR accelerating above 8% annually. Exit triggers: (a) Scores revenue growth turns negative for two consecutive quarters, (b) FHFA mandates score-agnostic underwriting across all agency lending, (c) VantageScore wins more than 30% verifiable market share in mortgage originations, or (d) price rises significantly above $2,000 (reducing margin of safety from the bull case). Review cadence: quarterly on earnings, mandatory thesis review at each FHFA policy announcement.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** FICO's franchise proves durable, VantageScore fears are absorbed without material pricing rollback, and the 10-year compounder thesis reasserts itself from current depressed prices.
- **Bear case:** Competitive pricing war forces earnings reset, stock tests 2022 lows ($400–500 range), and the thesis is structurally impaired for 3–5 years.
- **Confidence:** Medium — high conviction in the franchise quality, low conviction in the near-term pricing model given the coordinated competitive/regulatory challenge.

## Red Flags

- Stock fell 34% from Sep 2024 peak to March 12, 2026 — and the fundamental challenge (VantageScore, FHFA) is intensifying, not resolving
- Negative shareholder equity and $2.84B debt reduce the buffer if revenue is impaired — buyback program (new $1.5B authorization) seems poorly timed given competitive uncertainty
- No dividend; all capital return via buybacks — at $1,285/share buybacks are only accretive if the stock is below intrinsic value, which is questionable
- Management has not publicly commented on the competitive threat with sufficient specificity; Q2 FY2026 earnings call (April 2026) will be critical
- Portfolio overlap risk if investor holds MCO, SPGI, VRSK, or MSCI simultaneously — correlated downside in a "data licensing moat" re-rating
- High absolute share price ($1,285) makes position sizing awkward for smaller portfolios and reduces optionality for dollar-cost averaging into weakness

## Score: 5 / 10

FICO is a world-class franchise that suits patient, conviction-based investors with a 7–10 year horizon and strong volatility tolerance, but at $1,285 the entry price requires accepting thin safety margins during a genuine thesis stress test — current temperament and sizing discipline demands a watchlist posture rather than a buy.
