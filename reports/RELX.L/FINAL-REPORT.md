**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Verdict:** Watch

The right move is a starter position with active monitoring, sized to allow averaging down if the H1 2026 results confirm the moat is intact.

## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: RELX sells subscription-based access to proprietary data platforms — Elsevier journals for scientists, LexisNexis for lawyers, LexisNexis Risk for banks and insurers, and trade show access via RX — generating 84% electronic and 70%+ recurring revenue that customers renew because the platforms are embedded in their professional workflows.

2. **Moat**: The Risk segment's proprietary records database (billions of court, insurance, and identity records assembled over 20+ years through exclusive data-licensing deals) cannot be replicated at any price, while Legal and STM hold customers through deep workflow entrenchment — retraining staff and migrating decades of workflow integrations would cost more in time and reputational risk than any competitor's price discount can offset.

3. **Economics**: RELX converts 99% of its £3.3bn adjusted operating profit to free cash flow (£2,313m in 2025), operates at a 34.8% adjusted operating margin that has expanded every year for a decade, and earns a ROIC consistently above 15% on a fully-loaded basis — the textbook fingerprint of an asset-light compounder.

4. **Management**: CEO Erik Engstrom has compounded adjusted EPS at high single digits every year of his 16-year tenure through disciplined bolt-on M&A (averaging £200–460m per year, exclusively capability-filling acquisitions), progressive dividend growth, and accelerating buybacks (£1bn in 2024, £1.5bn in 2025, £2.25bn planned for 2026) — a rational, consistent capital allocator with no history of empire-building.

5. **Debt risk**: At 2.0x net debt/EBITDA, A- rated (S&P, Fitch) and A3 (Moody's), with 11.8x interest coverage, a fully-funded pension, and a $3.5bn undrawn revolving credit facility maturing in 2030, RELX's balance sheet can absorb any plausible recession without threatening solvency — a 30% revenue drop (implausible given 84% subscription/electronic revenue) would still leave the business cash-flow positive.

6. **Price**: At 2,594p (20x adjusted 2025 EPS of 128.5p and 14x EV/EBITDA, both at decade lows), the market is pricing in approximately 3% perpetual owner earnings growth — a scenario where AI causes sustained stagnation across Legal, STM, and Risk simultaneously — despite 2025 results delivering 7% underlying revenue growth and record adjusted cash flow with no reported customer churn.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business margin of safety is substantial (99% recurring revenue, no customer concentration, 2.0x leverage, proprietary data moats decades in the making), but the price margin of safety is thin — today's 2,594p sits only ~6% above the bear-case intrinsic value of approximately 2,450p, meaning an investor is relying on the AI disruption thesis being wrong rather than buying at a demonstrable discount to conservative value.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if two consecutive half-year earnings reports show underlying revenue growth decelerating below 4% alongside evidence of rising customer churn or declining Lexis+ AI paying user counts — that combination would confirm AI is substituting rather than supplementing RELX's workflow tools, validating the structural disruption scenario that current pricing has so far assumed without reported evidence.

## Key Risks

1. **AI disruption of Legal and Academic workflows (High impact, Medium probability):** The February 2026 launch of Anthropic's Claude legal plug-in has injected genuine uncertainty about whether AI can commoditise legal research and academic literature discovery. If enterprise buyers — law firms, universities, hospitals — shift meaningful activity to general-purpose AI tools, RELX's subscription renewal rates could deteriorate with a 12–24 month lag. This is the single most important risk and the primary reason the stock has de-rated 38% from its peak despite strong reported results.

2. **Open-access mandates eroding Elsevier's STM journal economics (Medium impact, Medium probability):** The open-access movement has already forced major university consortia in Germany and the US to renegotiate journal agreements. If government mandates accelerate the shift to author-pays open-access publishing, the per-article revenue model for Elsevier could face a structural headwind that analytics tools only partially offset. This is a slow-moving but persistent erosion risk that has been underway for a decade.

3. **Thin price margin of safety if bear case materialises (High impact if wrong):** The current price of 2,594p is only ~6% above the conservatively constructed bear-case intrinsic value of ~2,450p. If the AI disruption thesis proves more valid than current results suggest — or if multiple contraction continues toward 15x earnings as a new sector ceiling — the stock could decline to 1,800–2,000p without any catastrophic business deterioration. The risk is not company insolvency but permanent multiple impairment.

4. **Buybacks at premium prices (Medium impact, Medium probability):** RELX repurchased £1.5bn of shares in 2025 at an average price of 3,797p — 46% above today's level. If the AI disruption thesis is right and the stock does not recover to those levels, the company will have destroyed £700m+ of capital on a single year's buyback programme. The planned £2.25bn buyback in 2026 at current prices (~2,594p) is more defensible, but the pattern of buying at elevated multiples is a recurring capital allocation concern.

5. **Currency risk and FX translation noise (Lower impact, persistent):** Approximately 40–45% of RELX's revenue is USD-denominated, reported in GBP. A sustained GBP appreciation against USD would suppress reported earnings and EPS in a way that masks underlying constant-currency performance, potentially amplifying negative sentiment during an already uncertain period for sector multiples.

## What Would Make This a Buy / What Would Make This a Sell

- **Buy triggers**:
  - H1 2026 results (expected July 2026) confirm underlying revenue growth at 7%+ with no evidence of churn or customer count reduction — the AI disruption thesis loses credibility and current 20x multiple is demonstrably cheap.
  - Stock declines to 2,200–2,400p, placing it at or below the bear-case intrinsic value and providing a genuine price margin of safety for the first time — a better entry point for a full position.
  - A major enterprise win for Lexis+ AI or ScienceDirect AI (a large-scale paid contract) signals that AI is additive to RELX's platform value rather than a substitute, triggering re-rating.

- **Sell triggers**:
  - Two consecutive half-year earnings reports show underlying revenue growth decelerating below 4% alongside rising customer churn or declining Lexis+ AI paying user counts — confirmation that AI is substituting, not supplementing.
  - Stock re-rates above 28x forward adjusted earnings without a step-change in growth — the market has re-priced in too much optimism and the risk/reward again skews negative.
  - A transformational acquisition at a very high price that drives net debt/EBITDA sustainably above 3x, impairing the balance sheet's current resilience and weakening the primary margin of safety.

## Detailed Analysis

RELX sells information, analytics, and decision tools to professionals and institutions. The business is best understood as three distinct but structurally similar franchises bundled into one company. First, **Risk** (the largest segment): LexisNexis Risk Solutions provides identity verification, fraud detection, insurance risk scoring, and financial crime compliance tools to banks, insurers, law enforcement, and government agencies. Second, **Scientific, Technical & Medical (STM)**: Elsevier publishes the world's largest portfolio of peer-reviewed scientific journals (including The Lancet and Cell) and sells access via platforms like ScienceDirect and Scopus to universities, research institutions, hospitals, and corporations. Third, **Legal**: LexisNexis Legal provides case law databases, regulatory research, and AI-powered contract and compliance tools to law firms and in-house counsel. Fourth, **Exhibitions**: RX organises major trade shows (Reed Exhibitions) worldwide — this is the smallest, most cyclical, and least strategically critical segment at roughly 6% of group revenue.

The revenue model is primarily subscriptions and recurring contracts. Institutions sign multi-year enterprise agreements for access to data platforms. In Risk, transactions are billed per query or per decisioning event, creating volume-linked recurring revenue rather than pure subscriptions. In STM, universities buy "Big Deal" journal packages via annual or multi-year contracts. In Legal, law firms pay per-seat or enterprise-wide access fees. This creates a high-visibility revenue base: at the start of any year, the vast majority of that year's revenue is already contracted.

Key revenue drivers are: (1) pricing power — RELX raises prices 3–5% annually on most products and customers renew because switching would mean losing access to unique datasets; (2) volume growth — more data queries in Risk as financial crime compliance mandates expand; (3) analytics upsell — customers migrate from raw data access to higher-value analytical tools (e.g., Lexis+ AI in Legal, predictive scoring models in Risk); (4) subscriber count in STM as global research output grows. Exhibitions revenue is driven by event calendar and economic cycle.

Revenue predictability is high for three of the four segments. Risk, STM, and Legal collectively represent roughly 94% of group revenue and have renewal rates estimated in the high 90s (RELX does not publicly disclose exact churn figures, but contract structure and multi-year agreements are well-documented). The Exhibitions segment is inherently event-driven and saw severe disruption during COVID-19 (2020–2021), which demonstrated its relative fragility. Group-level predictability is strong, with the caveat that FX translation (USD/EUR to GBP) adds quarterly noise.

Customer concentration is low. RELX serves customers in over 180 countries including governments, the world's largest banks, pharmaceutical companies, universities, law firms, and hospitals. No single customer is disclosed as material to group revenues. In STM, individual university library consortia sign large deals, but even the largest (e.g., a major US university system consortium) represents a small fraction of the £3.5bn+ STM revenue base. This diversification means customer-specific risk is minimal.

The business can be explained clearly: RELX owns databases that professionals need to do their jobs — scientists need Elsevier's journals, lawyers need LexisNexis, insurers and banks need LexisNexis Risk's fraud scores — and it charges for access via annual subscriptions. The data assets were built over decades, cannot be easily replicated, and the switching costs for institutional customers are high. The company then layers AI-powered analytical tools on top of these databases to increase value and pricing. It is genuinely one of the clearest and most straightforward information-services businesses in the world.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A dominant, deeply entrenched information platform with 70%+ recurring revenue and pricing power compounds earnings at high single digits indefinitely as AI tools drive further wallet share.
- **Bear case:** Open access mandates erode STM's journal economics, and the Risk segment faces commoditisation as fraud data aggregators proliferate.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is transparent, well-documented, and has been consistent for over a decade with audited financials.

## Red Flags

- Exhibitions segment (~6% of revenue) is cyclical and structurally challenged by digital event alternatives — not material but worth monitoring.
- RELX does not disclose precise customer renewal rates or churn statistics, making exact retention metrics hard to independently verify.
- FX translation risk is meaningful: USD and EUR earnings reported in GBP create headline volatility that doesn't reflect underlying business performance.

## Score: 9 / 10

RELX is one of the most understandable information-services businesses available — four segments with subscription-dominant recurring revenues, a clear pricing mechanism, no customer concentration, and a long public track record that makes the model easy to evaluate.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — RELX.L

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Data Sources:** Web search results (RELX 2025 full-year results; ainvest.com; dcfmodeling.com; beyondspx.com; klover.ai; finimize.com; Seeking Alpha open access article; ad-hoc-news.de); training knowledge on information services sector competitive dynamics.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | RELX's adjusted operating margin reached 34.8% in 2025, up nearly one percentage point year-over-year, demonstrating sustained and expanding pricing power | 5 |
| 2 | The Risk segment's datasets (billions of public records, identity records, insurance claims) took decades to accumulate and cannot be replicated from scratch — a genuine data moat | 5 |
| 3 | Workflow entrenchment is deep: legal professionals, risk analysts, and researchers are trained on RELX platforms, making institutional switching costs extremely high even when competitors offer comparable content | 5 |
| 4 | RELX is integrating AI (Lexis+ AI, LexisNexis+ AI in Risk) directly into workflows, which deepens entrenchment rather than enabling disruption | 4 |
| 5 | The open access movement poses a credible long-term structural risk to Elsevier's STM journal business specifically, though this segment (~37% of revenue) is migrating toward higher-margin analytics tools | 4 |
| 6 | Free cash flow conversion is 99% with FCF of £2,313m in 2025, reflecting an asset-light business where returns on incremental investment are high | 4 |

## Detailed Analysis

RELX possesses multiple overlapping moat types that reinforce each other across its segments. The moat is not uniform — it is strongest in Risk, very strong in Legal, solid in STM's analytics/tools layer, and weakest in Exhibitions. Understanding this segment-by-segment variation is important.

**Data moat (Risk segment — clearest moat in the group).** LexisNexis Risk Solutions owns one of the world's largest repositories of public records: court filings, property records, driver license data, insurance claims histories, criminal records, and identity linkage data. This data was assembled over 20+ years through acquisitions (notably Acxiom assets, ThreatMetrix, ID Analytics, and hundreds of smaller bolt-ons) and direct data licensing relationships with government agencies. A competitor starting today cannot buy access to equivalent data at any price — many of RELX's data streams are exclusive, contractually protected, or require regulatory authorisation. The resulting fraud detection and identity verification products are used in real-time decisioning by banks and insurers; their accuracy depends on data breadth that cannot be matched. This is as close to a true data moat as exists in any public company.

**Switching cost moat (Legal and STM).** LexisNexis has been the primary case law research platform for US and UK lawyers for over 50 years. Law students are trained on it; law firm workflows are built around it; citation formats, case organisers, and brief-writing tools integrate with it. Switching to a competitor (primarily Westlaw/Thomson Reuters) would require retraining staff, migrating workflows, and accepting an inferior citation history during the transition. The cost is not just financial but reputational and operational. Similarly, in STM, ScienceDirect and Scopus are embedded in how researchers discover, cite, and track literature. University library purchasing decisions move slowly and are made by committees. These structural switching costs mean RELX does not need to be meaningfully cheaper or better than alternatives to retain customers — it just needs to be good enough and trusted.

**Moat durability: 10-year assessment.** The Risk data moat should be more durable in 10 years than today — financial crime compliance mandates are expanding globally, digital identity verification is growing as a market, and each additional data stream RELX adds compounds the value of existing data. The Legal moat is durable as long as RELX continues investing in AI tools; the launch of Lexis+ AI and Protégé (AI-enabled legal research tools) has driven double-digit growth in the Legal segment and deepened reliance. The STM moat is the most contested: the open access movement creates structural pressure on Elsevier's subscription journal business, but RELX has been proactively pivoting STM toward higher-value analytics (Scopus, SciVal, research intelligence tools) which are less exposed to open access. The Exhibitions moat is narrow — it rests primarily on established event brands and attendee network effects, which are real but vulnerable to digital competition and economic cycles.

**Moat evidence in numbers.** Operating margins have expanded consistently: from approximately 28% in 2015 to 34.8% in 2025. Free cash flow conversion is near 100%. Revenue growth has been above nominal GDP every year for over a decade. Competitors have not displaced RELX in any core segment despite investing heavily (Thomson Reuters in legal, Clarivate in STM analytics). These are the fingerprints of durable competitive advantage — not luck or temporary product leadership.

**Weakest points.** In STM, the academic community's hostility to Elsevier's pricing is well-documented. Several major universities and research consortia (Projekt DEAL in Germany, UC system in the US) have negotiated or forced transformative open-access agreements that change the revenue-per-article economics. Over the long term, if open access becomes the dominant publishing model, Elsevier's revenue per article could fall from approximately $4,100 to an author-pays model averaging around $2,250–$2,400 — a potentially significant revenue headwind if not offset by growth in analytics tools. This is a real risk, not a theoretical one. In Legal, Westlaw/Thomson Reuters and a new wave of AI-native legal research startups (e.g., Harvey, Casetext before its acquisition by Thomson Reuters) are competing aggressively for the next generation of lawyers who may have weaker LexisNexis habits. RELX's AI investment is a response to this, but the outcome is not predetermined.

**AI as moat amplifier, not disruptor.** RELX's management argues, credibly, that AI tools built on proprietary data make the moat stronger, not weaker. An AI model trained on RELX's exclusive Risk data produces more accurate fraud scores than one trained on public data. Lexis+ AI's legal research capabilities are only as good as the underlying LexisNexis case law database. This means AI investment amplifies existing data advantages rather than resetting the competitive landscape to zero.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Overlapping data, workflow, and switching cost moats continue to compound with AI-enabled tools, driving sustained margin expansion and above-GDP revenue growth for the next decade.
- **Bear case:** Accelerating open access mandates structurally erode STM journal economics, and AI-native legal research tools from startups erode LexisNexis's share of the next generation of legal professionals.
- **Confidence:** High — margin trajectory, cash conversion, and competitor dynamics over 10+ years provide strong evidence of durable moats; the STM open access risk is real but manageable given the analytics pivot.

## Red Flags

- Open access movement is a structural pressure on Elsevier's journal subscription economics, not merely a political risk — it has already forced transformative agreement renegotiations with major university consortia.
- AI-native legal research startups are funded and aggressive; RELX's moat in Legal rests on legacy habits as much as product quality, which is a softer moat than data ownership.
- The Exhibitions business has a shallow moat (event brand recognition) and should not be credited with protecting the group's overall competitive position.

## Score: 9 / 10

RELX has one of the most durable moat stacks in the information services sector — proprietary data assets built over decades in Risk, deep workflow entrenchment in Legal, and AI integration that deepens rather than threatens its competitive position — with the only meaningful caveat being Elsevier's long-term STM journal economics under open access pressure.

# Management & Capital Allocation — RELX.L

## Detailed Analysis

**Management tenure and track record.** Erik Engstrom became CEO in November 2009 and is now one of the longest-tenured executives in the FTSE 100. His track record is exceptional by any quantitative measure: over his tenure, RELX has compounded adjusted EPS at high single digits annually, expanded operating margins from approximately 25% to nearly 35%, and transformed the business from a legacy print information company into a digital analytics platform. CFO Nick Luff joined in 2014, providing continuity in financial strategy. Stability at the top of a compounding business is an asset — RELX's strategic direction (more analytics, less print, AI investment on top of proprietary data) has been consistent and clearly articulated for a decade. The 2025 earnings call (February 12, 2026) showed management presenting straightforward performance data with no spin: 7% underlying revenue growth, 9% operating profit growth, 10% EPS growth, and a 7% dividend increase — all delivered within guidance.

**Capital allocation track record.** RELX's capital allocation framework has been consistently rational. The hierarchy is: (1) reinvest in organic growth (R&D, product development, content acquisition); (2) bolt-on acquisitions to add data or capability; (3) progressive dividend increases; (4) buybacks with residual free cash flow. In practice, acquisitions have averaged roughly £200–460m per year — exclusively small, strategic, capability-filling deals. There have been no transformative, empire-building acquisitions. The five small acquisitions completed in 2024 for £195m total illustrate the pattern: targeted, digestible, focused on data sets or analytics capabilities that slot into existing segments. Divestiture discipline is also evident — RELX sold seven businesses in 2024 for £95m, continuously pruning non-core assets (e.g., RX divested Gamer Network in May 2024). This is the behaviour of capital allocators who think about returns, not executives building empires.

**Buyback quality.** The escalation of buybacks is notable and reflects management's honest assessment that RELX generates more cash than it needs for organic reinvestment at attractive returns. Buybacks rose from £1,000m in 2024 to £1,500m in 2025 to a planned £2,250m in 2026 — well above the analyst consensus expectation of £1.75bn. The critical question is whether these buybacks are at sensible prices. RELX trades at a significant premium to the market (historically 25-35x earnings) which means buybacks are expensive on an absolute basis. However, if the underlying earnings compound at 9-10% annually (as they have), buybacks at premium multiples can still be value-accretive. Management has not explicitly tied buyback aggressiveness to valuation (they do not appear to be value-timing buybacks), which is the main reservation on capital allocation quality. That said, given RELX's consistent growth trajectory, the risk of value destruction from buybacks at high multiples is lower than it would be for a cyclical or slower-growing business.

**Compensation design.** CEO total remuneration of approximately £13.5m is structured with salary representing only ~10.5% of the total. The dominant portion (approximately 89.5%) consists of annual bonuses and long-term incentive plan (LTIP) awards tied to EPS growth and total shareholder return versus peers. This alignment is reasonable — EPS is a per-share metric that rewards growing earnings without inflating the denominator. The LTIP structure means management profits most when shareholders profit. SBC (share-based compensation) funded by the Employee Benefit Trust cost £76m in 2025, representing less than 1% of revenue — not dilutive in any material sense given the buyback programme that runs at 17x that figure.

**Governance.** RELX has a standard UK PLC governance structure. The board is majority independent, chaired by Paul Walker (independent). There is no dual-class share structure and no controlling shareholder. Audit, Remuneration, and Nominations committees are all chaired by independent non-executive directors. This is clean, conventional FTSE 100 governance — no red flags. Insider ownership by the CEO (0.065%, ~£26m) is meaningful at the personal level but represents no effective control over the company. This is characteristic of large-cap professional management businesses rather than founder-led companies — the motivation alignment comes primarily through compensation design rather than ownership concentration.

**Candour and consistency.** RELX's public communication has been consistently straightforward over many years. Guidance is expressed as directional ("continued strong growth") rather than specific point estimates, which insulates management from being held to precise numbers, but the underlying message has been consistent and delivered upon. Engstrom's investor communications do not over-promise. The 2026 buyback increase (£750m above analyst consensus) was presented matter-of-factly, not as a theatrical gesture. There is no history of accounting restatements, guidance reversals, or high-profile strategic errors.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** A disciplined, long-tenured management team with a clear capital allocation framework continues to compound per-share earnings at ~10% annually, accelerating buybacks as cash generation grows.
- **Bear case:** Management's low insider ownership and premium-price buybacks mean the principal-agent alignment is less tight than a founder-led business, and capital could be misallocated in a period of slower organic growth.
- **Confidence:** High — 16+ years of consistent delivery under the same CEO provides substantial evidence of stewardship quality; the primary limitation is the lack of owner-operator alignment at the top.

## Red Flags

- Minimal insider ownership (0.065% for CEO) means alignment is almost entirely through compensation structure rather than personal wealth at stake — a structural limitation common to large-cap managed businesses, but worth noting.
- Buybacks at 25–35x earnings are expensive in absolute terms; if the growth trajectory were to slow, the buyback programme would destroy rather than create value at current prices.
- Dual listing (LSE and NYSE) and ADR structure add complexity without obvious strategic benefit — a minor governance footnote.

## Score: 8 / 10

A highly capable and consistent management team with an excellent 16-year track record of per-share earnings compounding, disciplined bolt-on M&A, and accelerating capital returns — the sole notable limitation being minimal insider ownership, which makes this a professionally managed franchise rather than an owner-operated one.

## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital.** RELX's ROIC has been consistently high over the past decade, averaging close to 20% over a 10-year span and standing at approximately 14.85–15.3% on a reported basis in 2024–2025 (GuruFocus, annualised from TTM data). The slight moderation in reported ROIC versus the historical peak reflects the compounding of goodwill and intangibles from acquisitions; on a tangible capital basis, returns are substantially higher. RELX's own reported post-tax return on average invested capital was 13.6% in 2019 (13.2% in 2018) when calculated under their framework, but management-defined adjusted measures show stronger return profiles. The trend is one of stable-to-improving returns as the business mix shifts further toward high-margin digital analytics and Risk products. ## Detailed Analysis

**Holding Period Suitability**

RELX is structurally a long-duration compounding business. Its subscription and transactional revenue base, deep workflow integration, and consistent 7–9% annual earnings growth make it suitable for a 10+ year holding period under normal circumstances. The new variable is the AI disruption question: the thesis now has an update cadence requirement. An investor must be willing to review results every six months with fresh eyes — if revenue growth decelerates, customer churn data emerges, or AI product adoption metrics plateau, the thesis weakens. This is not a "put it away for 10 years and don't look" situation anymore; it requires active monitoring. For an investor who can do that, the holding period remains appropriate. For one who wants to hold passively, the uncertainty makes this a harder fit.

**Volatility Tolerance**

The historical max drawdown for RELX shares is hard to establish precisely from available data, but the 38% peak-to-trough move in a single year — during a period of fundamental outperformance — is instructive about how the stock behaves when sentiment shifts. An investor who bought at 4,183p in May 2025 is sitting on a £1,600p/share paper loss by March 2026 despite excellent business results. If the stock recovers to the analyst consensus target of 3,733p, that same investor would still be down ~11% from peak. This is the volatility profile of a company that attracts growth multiple compression. If the stock drops another 20% from here (to ~2,075p), an investor should ideally want to buy more — the business is more attractive, the valuation is lower, the dividend yield is higher. Practically, only investors with very high conviction in the moat durability can execute that. The psychological difficulty of buying more during a multi-year re-rating episode (as happened to Bloomberg terminal peers in prior disruptions) should not be underestimated.

**Catalysts**

Within RELX's control: H1 2026 results (expected July 2026) will be the critical data point — specifically, whether revenue growth holds at 7%+ and whether Lexis+ AI adoption metrics show paying customer growth rather than just usage. The £2bn buyback plan at current prices (~3.5–4% yield on market cap) is a mechanical re-rating catalyst if the shares do not deteriorate further. A positive AI product announcement (e.g., a major enterprise contract for AI-powered legal or risk tools) could shift sentiment rapidly. Outside RELX's control: if Anthropic's Claude legal plug-in or OpenAI's legal products gain significant enterprise traction, the disruption narrative gets louder regardless of RELX's own performance. Conversely, if enterprise buyers show continued preference for proprietary workflow-embedded tools over general AI, that is a re-rating catalyst.

**Position Sizing Logic**

At today's price, this is not a "back the truck up" situation — the thin price margin of safety and the genuine binary risk of the AI disruption thesis argue against a maximum position. A starter or mid-sized position of 2–4% of a diversified portfolio captures meaningful upside if the thesis is right without being catastrophic if wrong. The position could be built toward a full core position (4–6%) over time as evidence accumulates either way — specifically, if H1 2026 results confirm continued 7% growth with no signs of churn, increasing the position makes sense. Stopping out: if two consecutive earnings reports show revenue growth decelerating below 4% and customer churn rising, the AI disruption thesis gains credibility and the position should be reduced. Do not size this as if it is a "safe" utility — it has a binary element that makes it more volatile than its historical profile.

**Correlation and Portfolio Overlap**

An investor who owns RELX should audit their portfolio for overlap with Thomson Reuters, Wolters Kluwer, Springer Nature, or other data services compounders. The entire sector has de-rated for the same reason; owning multiple names does not diversify AI disruption risk — it concentrates it. RELX also has meaningful exposure to the US legal market (through LexisNexis), US risk/insurance data, and European scientific publishing — so it overlaps with both US legal tech and European defensive-growth themes. For a UK investor, the USD revenue (roughly 40–45% of total) creates a long-USD position that partially offsets GBP exposure. For a USD investor buying the NYSE-listed RELX ADR, the GBP/USD rate adds a currency layer to the return. This should be explicit rather than incidental in portfolio construction.

**Process Discipline: Entry, Sell, Review**

Entry plan: the current price at ~20x adjusted earnings and ~14x EV/EBITDA is in the range where a first tranche purchase is defensible, not heroic. Waiting for 2,200–2,400p (on additional weakness) would provide a better entry point closer to or below the bear-case intrinsic value. Averaging down below 2,400p increases the margin of safety materially. Do not chase the stock above 3,200p without updated financial confirmation. Sell conditions: (a) stock re-rates above 28x forward adjusted earnings without a step-change in growth — at that point the market is pricing in too much optimism again; (b) two consecutive results showing decelerating growth below 3% and rising churn — the moat is breaking; (c) a transformational acquisition at a very high price that deteriorates the balance sheet significantly (net debt/EBITDA above 3x). Review cadence: H1 results (July 2026) and full-year results (February 2027) are mandatory reviews; any major competitor product launch (particularly from Anthropic, OpenAI, or Google in legal/academic data) triggers an unscheduled review.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** RELX's moat proves durable against AI disruption, the £2bn buyback supports the stock, and H1 2026 results confirm continued 7%+ growth — investor who builds a 3–5% position today earns 50–90% over three to five years.
- **Bear case:** AI disruption accelerates faster than management or analysts anticipate, growth stalls, re-rating continues, and a 20–35% loss from today's level unfolds over two to three years.
- **Confidence:** Medium — RELX is an excellent business at a price that is now reasonable rather than expensive, but the AI disruption question introduces a legitimate binary risk that prevents high-conviction sizing; the temperament required is "patient, monitoring closely, willing to be wrong."

- GBP/USD currency exposure is unhedged for most retail investors and adds a return layer that can move independently of business performance.
