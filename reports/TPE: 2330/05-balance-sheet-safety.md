## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** TSMC carries total debt of approximately NT$993.2B ($31B USD) against shareholder equity of NT$5,460.8B. The debt-to-equity ratio of 18.2% is conservative by any measure. The debt-to-EBITDA ratio stands at just 0.34x — meaning TSMC could theoretically pay off all its debt in about four months using EBITDA alone. This is remarkably low, even by the standards of capital-light technology companies, and especially impressive for a company that operates the most capital-intensive fabs on earth. TSMC's outstanding bonds include USD-denominated senior notes with maturities spread across 2027 (at least $1.15B across multiple tranches at 1% and 4.375% coupons) and 2030 ($1.25B at 1.375%). The mix of very low-coupon legacy bonds and newer issuances at market rates suggests well-laddered maturities. TSMC has no short-term borrowings as of Q3 2025. All debt appears to be long-term and predominantly fixed-rate, which limits exposure to rising interest rates.

**Cash, Liquidity, and Interest Coverage.** TSMC held approximately NT$3,068.6B (~$95B USD) in cash and short-term investments as of late 2025, dramatically exceeding its total debt. This places the company in a net cash position of roughly $64B. The current ratio was 2.4x as of Q2 2025, indicating more than adequate short-term liquidity. Interest expense for 2025 was approximately $303M USD — trivial relative to operating income (EBIT) of approximately NT$1,936B (~$60B USD). This yields an interest coverage ratio well above 100x — essentially, interest expense is rounding error for this company. TSMC earns considerably more in interest income on its cash pile than it pays on its debt, so on a net basis, it is a net interest earner, not a net interest payer.

**The Capex Question — This Is the Real Risk.** The elephant in the room is not TSMC's existing balance sheet but its forward capital commitments. The company guided for $52-56B in capex for 2026, up from $40-42B in 2025 and $28.9B in 2024. The Arizona mega-project alone represents a $165B total commitment across multiple fabs, assembly plants, and an R&D center. Japan and Germany expansions add further commitments, though these appear to be slowing. Full-year 2025 free cash flow was approximately $31B (NT$1.003T), which means that at the midpoint of 2026 capex guidance ($54B), TSMC would need to either draw down cash reserves, take on more debt, or see operating cash flow grow meaningfully to fund the difference. Operating cash flow in Q4 2025 alone was NT$726B (~$23B), suggesting an annualized run rate of ~$90B+ — enough to fund $54B in capex with healthy free cash flow remaining. However, if demand softens, the gap between operating cash flow and capex commitments narrows quickly. This is the central tension for balance sheet safety.

**Stress Test: Revenue Down 30% for Two Years.** TSMC's FY2025 revenue was approximately $122B. A 30% decline would bring revenue to ~$85B. Even assuming operating margins compress from ~51% to ~35% under such a scenario (reasonable given TSMC's high fixed costs), operating income would be ~$30B. If TSMC maintained its planned ~$54B capex, free cash flow would turn sharply negative. However, TSMC has historically shown willingness to cut capex in downturns (capex fell from $17.2B in 2022 to the mid-$20Bs before ramping). In this stress scenario, TSMC could cut capex by 40-50%, reduce it to ~$27-32B, and still generate modestly positive free cash flow. With $95B in cash and short-term investments and only $31B in total debt, the company could also comfortably bridge a two-year downturn by drawing on cash reserves without issuing equity. Even under extreme stress, the balance sheet provides a multi-year cushion. Survival is not in question.

**Credit Ratings and Off-Balance-Sheet Items.** The AA-/Aa3 ratings from S&P and Moody's (both stable) are the highest in the semiconductor industry. These are just two notches below the AAA/Aaa ceiling. For context, these ratings put TSMC on par with companies like Johnson & Johnson and Microsoft. Regarding off-balance-sheet items, TSMC's 20-F filing includes disclosures on defined benefit pension obligations, operating leases (now largely on-balance-sheet under IFRS 16), and purchase commitments for equipment and materials. The specific figures were not fully indexed in web searches as of this analysis date, but based on TSMC's past filings, pension obligations and lease liabilities are modest relative to the overall balance sheet. The larger off-balance-sheet concern is the implicit commitment to continue building fabs — walking away from Arizona or other projects would carry significant reputational and strategic cost, making these commitments quasi-obligatory even if not formally binding.

**Dividends.** TSMC paid TWD 467B (~$14.5B) in dividends in FY2025, up 28.6% year-over-year, with a per-share dividend of TWD 18. The payout ratio relative to net income (approximately TWD 1,718B for FY2025 based on TWD 66.25 EPS x ~25.93B shares) is roughly 27% — quite conservative. In a severe downturn, dividends could be cut to preserve cash, providing an additional safety valve.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** TSMC's net cash position of ~$64B, coupled with annual operating cash flow approaching $90B, allows the company to fund the largest capex program in semiconductor history while maintaining a fortress balance sheet and investment-grade ratings that are the envy of the industry.
- **Bear case:** A severe and prolonged demand downturn (e.g., AI spending pullback or geopolitical disruption) coinciding with peak capex commitments could force TSMC to choose between strategic investment and balance sheet strength, potentially requiring meaningful debt issuance and pressuring its credit profile.
- **Confidence:** High — TSMC's Q4 2025 earnings, balance sheet data, and capex guidance are all publicly reported and recently released; the financial picture is well-documented and transparent.

## Red Flags

- Capex is accelerating dramatically ($29B in 2024 to $41B in 2025 to $54B in 2026), and the $165B Arizona commitment extends years into the future. If AI demand disappoints, TSMC may face a period of over-investment relative to demand.
- Geopolitical risk (Taiwan Strait) is not a balance sheet item but could impair the value of TSMC's Taiwan-based assets, which represent the vast majority of its productive capacity.
- The pace of overseas fab construction (Arizona, Japan, Germany) introduces execution risk and potentially higher costs per wafer, which could pressure margins and cash flow in the medium term.
- Off-balance-sheet commitment details (pension, purchase obligations, equipment commitments) were not fully confirmed from the most recent filing and may be material given the scale of fab construction.

## Score: 8 / 10

TSMC maintains a fortress-level balance sheet with a substantial net cash position (~$64B), debt-to-EBITDA of 0.34x, AA-/Aa3 credit ratings, and interest coverage well above 100x — all of which would normally warrant a 9 or 10. The score is held to an 8 because the unprecedented capex ramp ($52-56B in 2026 alone, with $165B committed to Arizona) introduces a meaningful forward obligation that, in a severe downturn, could pressure the balance sheet even if survival is never in doubt.
