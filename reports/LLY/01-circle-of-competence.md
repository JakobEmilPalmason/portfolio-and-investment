## Detailed Analysis

**What does Eli Lilly sell, and to whom?** Eli Lilly is a global pharmaceutical company that discovers, manufactures, and sells branded prescription drugs. Its primary customers are pharmacies, hospitals, and pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) who purchase drugs on behalf of patients and health insurers. End patients are prescribed Lilly drugs by physicians. Lilly operates in three therapeutic areas: cardiometabolic/obesity (dominant), oncology, and neuroscience/immunology. As of early 2026, the cardiometabolic franchise — specifically the GLP-1/GIP dual agonist tirzepatide — is the overwhelming driver of revenue.

**How does it actually make money?** Lilly earns revenue through product sales of branded drugs. The model is: manufacture a drug under patent protection, price it at a premium (Mounjaro/Zepbound list prices are ~$1,000/month), collect gross revenue from wholesalers and PBMs, then subtract rebates and discounts to arrive at net realized revenue. Gross margins are very high — reported at approximately 83% in the high-growth phase of 2025 — because incremental manufacturing costs for a proven molecule are low relative to its pricing power. R&D and SG&A are the primary cost buckets, alongside the amortization of any acquired intangibles.

**Key revenue drivers:** (1) Tirzepatide volume: demand for GLP-1 therapies for diabetes and obesity is structurally large, with an estimated 170 million potential U.S. patients; (2) Net pricing: Lilly must balance list price with rebates paid to PBMs to maintain formulary access — a real pressure as payer pushback grows; (3) Pipeline launches: orforglipron (oral GLP-1, FDA decision expected 2026), retatrutide (triple agonist GLP-1/GIP/glucagon, Phase III readouts through 2026), and Kisunla (Alzheimer's, EU approval Q3 2025) are the next legs; (4) International: outside-U.S. Mounjaro revenue grew from $899M in Q4 2024 to $3.3B in Q4 2025, showing massive international runway.

**Revenue predictability:** Near-term (1–3 years) revenue is high-visibility given tirzepatide's compounding volume and Lilly's 2026 guidance of $80–83B. However, pharmaceutical revenue has inherent binary risks: a negative Phase III outcome, an FDA rejection, or an unexpected safety signal can abruptly end a product's trajectory. Patent expiry also creates a hard cliff — tirzepatide's core compound patent expires in 2036, with follow-on patents extending to 2041. Beyond 2030, the revenue picture depends heavily on whether retatrutide and orforglipron achieve blockbuster status as predicted ($30B and $16B in peak sales respectively by 2031). The revenue is less predictable over 5–10 year horizons than, say, a consumer staples company.

**Customer concentration and explainability:** Lilly's direct customers are a small number of large wholesale distributors (McKesson, AmerisourceBergen, Cardinal Health) and PBMs. This is industry-standard structure but creates dependency on a few counterparties for access and pricing. Lilly's business can be explained to an intelligent layperson in about two minutes: Lilly makes the most effective injectable drugs for type 2 diabetes and obesity. These drugs are in enormous demand globally. Lilly is growing revenue at 40–50% per year as the world wakes up to the GLP-1 revolution. The company is betting its next phase on an oral GLP-1 pill and a three-receptor drug even more potent than the current one.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Tirzepatide addresses one of the largest unmet medical needs in history; with orforglipron and retatrutide, Lilly extends the GLP-1 runway well past current product cycles.
- **Bear case:** Revenue and valuation are heavily concentrated in a single molecule class; any safety, pricing, or competitive shock to GLP-1 would disproportionately impact Lilly.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is simple to understand; the primary uncertainty is magnitude of success, not business model comprehension.

## Red Flags

- ~56% revenue concentration in two drugs sharing the same active ingredient (tirzepatide) creates single-point-of-failure risk
- PBM and payer rebate pressure on GLP-1 pricing is growing, net realized prices declining modestly even as volumes surge
- Pipeline binary risk: orforglipron and retatrutide are large embedded assumptions in the forward valuation

## Score: 8 / 10

Eli Lilly's business model is clean and understandable — a premium pharma company with extraordinary near-term visibility from GLP-1 dominance — but the patent-cliff dynamic and pipeline binary risk introduce complexity that prevents a top score.
