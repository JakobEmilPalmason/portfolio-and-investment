## Detailed Analysis

**What does IDEXX sell, and to whom?**

IDEXX Laboratories sells diagnostic instruments, consumables, reference laboratory testing services, and practice management software to veterinarians—primarily those treating companion animals (dogs and cats) in private and corporate veterinary clinics. Its customers are professional veterinary practices, not pet owners directly. The company also sells water microbiology testing products to utilities and municipalities, and diagnostic testing products to producers of livestock, poultry, and dairy. The companion animal business is the overwhelming focus, representing roughly 85-88% of total revenue in 2025.

**How does it actually make money?**

IDEXX operates a classic razor-and-blade model. The company places in-clinic diagnostic analyzers (instruments such as the Catalyst chemistry analyzer, ProCyte hematology analyzer, and the newer inVue Dx cellular imaging system) into veterinary clinics, often through multi-year commitment programs that bundle instrument access with consumable purchase requirements. The analyzers themselves are not the profit driver—the recurring revenue from consumable test kits, reagent packs, and reference laboratory send-out tests is where IDEXX earns its margins. On top of this, IDEXX sells and licenses practice management software (Cornerstone, ezyVet) and a client-communication platform (Vello) that deepens the clinic relationship. Revenue is essentially a function of (installed base of instruments) × (tests run per instrument per year) × (price per test) plus reference lab volume.

**What are the key drivers?**

The 2-4 variables that matter most are: (1) **Installed base growth** — new instrument placements, tracked by the company quarter by quarter; in 2025, the premium instrument installed base grew 12% year-over-year; (2) **Diagnostic utilization per visit** — the number and type of tests ordered each time a pet visits the vet; IDEXX has successfully grown this even as total veterinary visit volumes faced headwinds in 2024-2025; (3) **Net pricing** — IDEXX consistently takes price increases on consumables and reference lab fees, contributing meaningfully to organic revenue growth each year; (4) **Veterinary practice growth** — the total number of active vet practices and the humanization of pets (owners treating pets as family members) driving spending on diagnostics they would have previously forgone.

**How predictable is the revenue?**

Revenue is highly predictable. CAG Diagnostics recurring revenue (consumables + reference lab services) represented the majority of CAG revenues and grew 9% as reported and 8% organically in FY2025. This recurring component is locked in by: multi-year commitment programs that contractually obligate practices to use IDEXX consumables once they accept an analyzer placement; deep workflow integration (every test run on an IDEXX instrument routes through IDEXX's VetConnect PLUS software, creating a single diagnostic record system); and the habitual nature of diagnostic ordering in a clinical setting. Seasonality is mild — there are modest peaks in spring/summer when pet activity and vet visits are higher, but no pronounced cliff. Revenue is not spot or project-based.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?**

Customers are veterinary practices — predominantly private small clinics and, increasingly, large corporate consolidators such as VCA (owned by Mars), Banfield (owned by Mars), and BluePearl. IDEXX does not disclose individual customer concentration figures but has stated that no single customer accounts for a material portion of revenue. The large corporate veterinary groups do exercise some collective purchasing leverage, but IDEXX's dominant market position (estimated 60-65% share in North American in-clinic diagnostics) and the stickiness of its installed base limits their negotiating power in practice. IDEXX has greater revenue concentration in North America (~60-65% of revenue) vs. international markets (~35-40%).

**Can you explain the business in 2 minutes?**

Yes, comfortably. IDEXX puts its diagnostic machines into vet clinics, often at subsidized cost, then earns high-margin revenue every time the vet runs a blood test, urinalysis, or cytology sample on that machine. The more machines placed, the more tests run, the more revenue IDEXX earns—automatically, with no incremental sales effort. The vet is locked in by multi-year contracts, integrated software, and staff training. IDEXX also runs its own reference laboratories for send-out tests and sells water testing kits to utilities. This is a well-understood business with clear, countable drivers. There is no meaningful circle-of-competence concern.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The combination of a growing installed instrument base, increasing diagnostics per visit driven by new offerings (Cancer Dx, inVue Dx), and durable pricing power sustains 8-10% organic revenue growth and mid-to-high-teens EPS growth for the next decade.
- **Bear case:** A prolonged decline in veterinary visit volumes (already observed in 2023-2025) limits the denominator of diagnostic utilization, and competing platforms (Mars/Antech, Zoetis) take share at the margin with lower-cost alternatives.
- **Confidence:** High — the business model is transparent, the financials are thoroughly disclosed, and the razor-and-blade structure has operated consistently for over 15 years.

## Red Flags

- Veterinary visit volume has been under pressure in 2023-2025 due to inflation and post-COVID pet adoption normalization; IDEXX has compensated through higher tests-per-visit and pricing, but this dynamic is worth monitoring.
- Revenue is heavily North America-centric; international expansion is ongoing but still a smaller contributor.
- The Water and LPD segments are stable but small, and their growth rates are structurally lower; they add marginal diversification value.

## Score: 9 / 10

IDEXX's business is exceptionally clear — a razor-and-blade model in a recession-resistant, humanization-driven market, with transparent recurring revenue drivers and strong predictability; the only reason it does not score a perfect 10 is modest North American concentration and the ongoing watch on underlying vet visit volumes.
