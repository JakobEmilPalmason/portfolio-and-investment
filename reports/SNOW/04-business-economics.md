## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital:** Snowflake's returns on invested capital are negative on a GAAP basis and have been since the company's founding. ROIC ranges from -16% to -27% depending on the measurement period, and ROE is similarly negative at -24% to -53%. This is driven by cumulative net losses that have eroded book equity. The company has never generated a GAAP profit. On a non-GAAP basis (excluding SBC), the picture is better — non-GAAP operating income was positive in FY2026. But traditional ROIC analysis paints a picture of a company that has not yet earned its cost of capital (WACC ~8%). For a Buffett-style framework, this is a significant concern: Snowflake has consumed billions of investor capital without yet generating accounting profits. The question is whether this reflects a pre-profit growth company building durable competitive advantages, or whether the economics are genuinely poor. The trajectory — narrowing GAAP losses relative to revenue — suggests the former, but the burden of proof remains on the company.

**Margin Structure:** Snowflake's gross margins are strong: non-GAAP product gross margin was 75.8% in FY2026, reflecting the high-margin nature of cloud data platform software. This is consistent with pricing power and low marginal cost of delivery. However, GAAP gross margins are lower (approximately 67%) once SBC allocated to cost of revenue is included. Operating margins tell the critical story: non-GAAP operating margin improved from roughly 5% in FY2025 to 10.5% in FY2026, with guidance for 12.5% in FY2027. This is genuine operating leverage — revenue grew 29% while non-GAAP operating expenses grew more slowly. On a GAAP basis, operating margin was approximately -27% in Q3 FY2026, an improvement from prior years but still deeply negative. Net margin on a GAAP basis was approximately -28% for FY2026 ($1.33B net loss on ~$4.68B revenue). The gap between non-GAAP profitability and GAAP losses is almost entirely stock-based compensation.

**Cash Generation and Owner Earnings:** This is where Snowflake's economics look most interesting. Adjusted free cash flow margin was 25.5% for FY2026, translating to approximately $1.19 billion in FCF. Q4 FCF margin spiked to 61% due to seasonal billing patterns (annual contracts renewing in Q4). However, FCF conversion relative to net income is not meaningful because net income is negative. The more relevant question is whether SBC-adjusted FCF represents real owner earnings. If we treat SBC as a real cost (which it is — it dilutes shareholders), then owner earnings are significantly lower than reported FCF. A rough calculation: $1.19B FCF minus ~$1.6B in SBC gives negative owner earnings of approximately -$400M. This is the fundamental tension in Snowflake's economics: the cash flow statement looks strong, but shareholders are being diluted to fund it. Management is aware of this and is reducing SBC as a percentage of revenue (from 41% to 34% to guided 27%), which would eventually bring real owner earnings positive. Capital expenditure is minimal at ~$46M annually, confirming the asset-light model. Traditional maintenance capex is negligible.

**Revenue Quality:** Snowflake operates a consumption-based model, which is a double-edged sword. On the positive side, it creates natural expansion revenue as customers increase usage — evidenced by the 125% net revenue retention rate. Customers who stay spend 25% more each year. The consumption model also means revenue is earned as services are consumed, not recognized upfront. On the negative side, consumption revenue is inherently less predictable than subscription revenue. If customers reduce workloads, revenue declines without contract protection. However, remaining performance obligations of $9.8 billion (up 42% YoY) provide substantial forward visibility. The customer base grew to 12,621 total customers, with the large-customer cohort ($1M+ spend) growing 27% to 733 accounts. AI-driven workloads are becoming a meaningful new consumption vector, with over 9,100 accounts using AI features by Q4 FY2026.

**Operating Leverage:** The company is beginning to demonstrate operating leverage. Non-GAAP operating margins expanded roughly 550 basis points from FY2025 to FY2026, with FY2027 guidance implying another 200 basis points of expansion. Revenue is growing at 29% while the cost structure is scaling more slowly. However, GAAP operating leverage is harder to identify because SBC continues to grow in absolute terms. If SBC as a percentage of revenue continues declining as guided (to 27% in FY2027), the company should reach GAAP operating profitability within 2-3 years. The fundamental question is whether Snowflake's competitive position will allow it to sustain high gross margins and continue extracting operating leverage as it scales past $5-6 billion in revenue.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Snowflake's 76% gross margins, 25% FCF margins, 125% NRR, and declining SBC ratio suggest a path to 20%+ GAAP operating margins within 3-5 years, at which point ROIC would be exceptional given the asset-light model.
- **Bear case:** SBC-adjusted owner earnings remain negative, GAAP profitability remains years away, and the consumption model exposes revenue to customer spending pullbacks during economic slowdowns, meaning the company may never achieve Buffett-quality economics.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The non-GAAP trajectory is clearly positive, but the massive SBC overhang makes it difficult to determine true economic profitability, and the company has yet to prove it can generate GAAP profits at any scale.

## Red Flags

- Stock-based compensation of ~$1.6B in FY2026 (~34% of revenue) makes GAAP and non-GAAP metrics diverge enormously. SBC is a real economic cost that dilutes shareholders.
- GAAP net loss widened slightly to $1.33B in FY2026 from $1.29B in FY2025 despite 29% revenue growth — the company is spending more in absolute terms even as ratios improve.
- ROIC and ROE are deeply negative on a trailing basis. The company has never earned its cost of capital on a GAAP basis.
- Consumption-based revenue model introduces cyclical risk not present in traditional SaaS subscription models.
- Q4 FCF margin of 61% flatters the annual number due to seasonal billing concentration — investors should focus on the annual 25.5% figure, not the quarterly spike.
- Remaining performance obligations ($9.8B) are large but consumption-based contracts may not convert to revenue at stated rates if customer usage declines.

## Score: 4 / 10

Snowflake has strong gross margins and growing FCF, but deeply negative GAAP returns on capital, persistent net losses, and massive stock-based compensation prevent a higher score under a Buffett-style framework that values actual earnings over adjusted metrics — the company has yet to prove it can generate real owner earnings after accounting for the full cost of employee compensation.
