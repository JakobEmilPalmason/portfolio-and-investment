## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Exceptional for a Capital-Intensive Manufacturer.** TSMC's returns on invested capital tell a remarkable story for a company that spends $30-40B+ per year building semiconductor fabs. ROIC was 28.7% in 2021, surged to 37.3% in 2022 during the post-pandemic chip boom, dipped to 25.2% in 2023 during the inventory correction, recovered to 30.9% in 2024, and then soared to 49.4% in 2025 as AI demand supercharged utilization of advanced nodes. The 20-year average ROIC is 27.5%, and the 5-year average sits at roughly 34%. ROE follows a similar trajectory: 29.7% (2021), 39.2% (2022), 26.7% (2023), 29.9% (2024), and 41.9% (2025). To put this in context, TSMC is generating 25-50% returns on invested capital while operating one of the most asset-heavy businesses in the world. Intel, by contrast, has struggled to reach double-digit ROIC in recent years. The key insight is that TSMC's near-monopoly on leading-edge manufacturing (sub-5nm) allows it to earn premium pricing on an enormous and growing installed base of advanced capacity. The returns are high not because the business is asset-light — it absolutely is not — but because the assets generate so much revenue and profit per dollar invested.

**Margin Structure: Widening and World-Class.** TSMC's margin trajectory over the past five years has been steadily upward. Gross margins were 51.6% in 2021, rose to 59.6% in 2022, dipped to 54.4% in 2023 (inventory correction plus early N3 ramp costs), recovered to 56.1% in 2024, and reached 59.9% for full-year 2025. Q4 2025 set a record at 62.3%, meaningfully above the company's own guidance of 59-61%. Operating margins tell the same story: 41.0% (2021), 49.5% (2022), 42.6% (2023), 45.7% (2024), and 50.8% (2025). Net margins were 37.3% (2021), 43.9% (2022), 38.8% (2023), 40.5% (2024), and 45.1% (2025). These margins are extraordinary for a manufacturer of physical goods — they are more comparable to high-end software companies than to other semiconductor fabs. The improvement is driven by two forces: (1) an accelerating mix shift toward advanced nodes (N3 at 24% and N5 at 35% of 2025 wafer revenue) where TSMC has monopoly-like pricing power, and (2) operating leverage as R&D and SG&A costs (roughly 10% of revenue) grow more slowly than revenue. The risk to margins comes from overseas fab expansion — Arizona, Japan, and Germany fabs carry significantly higher operating costs than Taiwan fabs — but management has committed to passing those costs through to customers via geographic pricing premiums.

**Cash Generation: Strong but Constrained by Capital Appetite.** Here is where TSM's economics diverge sharply from asset-light models like NVIDIA. In 2025, TSMC generated NT$2.275 trillion in operating cash flow on NT$1.718 trillion of net income — a healthy operating cash conversion of 132%. However, capital expenditures consumed NT$1.272 trillion, leaving free cash flow of NT$1.003 trillion (roughly $31B USD). FCF conversion (FCF/net income) was only about 58% in 2025, 75% in 2024, and a dismal 34% in 2023. Looking at the full 2021-2025 period: operating cash flow was NT$1.11T, NT$1.61T, NT$1.24T, NT$1.83T, and NT$2.28T respectively, while capex was NT$839B, NT$1.08T, NT$950B, NT$956B, and NT$1.27T. Free cash flow was NT$273B, NT$528B, NT$292B, NT$870B, and NT$1.003T. The pattern is clear: TSMC generates massive operating cash flow, but the business demands massive reinvestment. For an owner earnings calculation (Buffett's preferred metric): 2025 net income of NT$1.718T plus depreciation of NT$688B minus estimated maintenance capex of roughly NT$400-500B (a rough estimate — TSMC does not disclose maintenance vs. growth capex, but depreciation on existing fabs provides a floor) yields owner earnings of approximately NT$1.9-2.0T. This is substantially higher than reported free cash flow because much of TSMC's capex is growth investment, not maintenance. The business generates real cash for owners even after keeping the lights on — the question is how much of the "growth" capex is truly optional vs. required to maintain competitive position.

**Capital Intensity: The Central Tradeoff.** TSMC's capex intensity is the most important factor in evaluating its business economics. Capex as a percentage of revenue: 2021 was 52.9% (the peak, driven by massive N5/N3 buildout), 2022 was 47.8%, 2023 was 43.9%, 2024 was 33.0%, and 2025 was approximately 33.4% ($40.9B capex on ~$122.9B revenue). The 2026 guidance of $52-56B capex, against consensus revenue estimates of roughly $140-150B, implies capex intensity rising back to 37-40%. Depreciation was NT$688B in 2025 (18% of revenue), up from NT$663B in 2024 and NT$532B in 2023 — and management expects high-teens percentage growth in 2026 depreciation as 2nm fabs come online. This is an unavoidable feature of the foundry business: each new node requires exponentially more expensive EUV lithography tools, and each fab costs $15-20B+. The overseas expansion adds another layer — TSMC's Arizona fabs are reportedly 30-50% more expensive to build and operate than equivalent Taiwan fabs. The company plans up to 12 Arizona fabs, plus facilities in Japan (Kumamoto) and Germany (Dresden). CFO Wendell Huang has stated that capex over the next three years will be "significantly higher" than the roughly $101B spent over the prior three years. The saving grace is that TSMC can price for these costs: with no credible competitor at leading edge (Samsung's yields remain poor, Intel Foundry is years behind), customers have little choice but to absorb the economics.

**Revenue Quality: Deeply Embedded but Concentrated.** TSMC's revenue has an unusual quality profile. It is not "recurring" in the SaaS sense — there are no multi-year contracts with guaranteed minimums. But in practice, foundry relationships are among the stickiest in technology. Designing a chip for a specific process node takes 2-3 years and costs $500M+ for a leading-edge design. Once a customer tapes out on TSMC N3, they are locked in for that chip's entire production life. Apple has manufactured every iPhone chip at TSMC since 2014. NVIDIA has been a TSMC customer for over a decade. The switching costs are astronomical. By platform, HPC (including AI chips) was 58% of 2025 revenue, smartphones 29%, IoT 5%, automotive 5%, and others 3%. By customer, NVIDIA overtook Apple in 2025 as the largest customer at approximately 19% of revenue, with Apple at 17%. AMD accounts for roughly 7%. The top 10 customers represented about 76% of revenue in 2024, and that concentration is likely higher in 2025 given NVIDIA's growth. This concentration is a double risk: dependence on a handful of customers, and dependence on those customers' own capex cycles (particularly the AI infrastructure buildout). Revenue growth has been strong: from NT$1.59T in 2021 to NT$2.26T in 2022 (+42.6%), down to NT$2.16T in 2023 (-4.5%), up to NT$2.89T in 2024 (+33.9%), and up to NT$3.81T in 2025 (+31.6%).

**Operating Leverage: Present but Moderated by Capex Cycles.** TSMC does exhibit meaningful operating leverage. In 2025, revenue grew 31.6% while operating income grew 46.4% and net income grew 48.3% — profits grew materially faster than revenue. In 2024, revenue grew 33.9% while net income grew 36.1% — more modest leverage. The 2023 downturn showed the reverse: revenue declined 4.5% but net income fell 14.2%, demonstrating that the fixed-cost structure (primarily depreciation) cuts both ways. R&D and SG&A together run about 10% of revenue and have been fairly stable, which is where the operating leverage comes from. However, the relentless capex cycle means that depreciation charges grow rapidly during buildout years, which can partially offset the leverage from volume gains. In 2026, management has guided for high-teens depreciation growth — this will pressure operating margins even as revenue is expected to grow around 25%. The net effect is that TSMC has real but lumpy operating leverage, amplified during upcycles and dampened by the ongoing need to build the next generation of capacity.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** TSMC's monopoly on leading-edge foundry manufacturing, combined with the multi-year AI infrastructure buildout, drives sustained 25%+ revenue growth with expanding margins and ROIC above 40%, making it one of the world's great capital-compounding machines despite its heavy asset base.
- **Bear case:** An AI capex slowdown or customer shift to alternative architectures triggers an inventory correction similar to 2023, while simultaneously rising depreciation from $52-56B in annual capex and structurally higher overseas fab costs compress margins back toward mid-40% gross and low-30% ROIC — still decent, but a significant step down from current levels.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The financial data is comprehensive and sourced from official TSMC filings and reputable aggregators. The current economic engine is undeniably strong. The uncertainty lies in the sustainability of AI-driven demand and the margin impact of overseas expansion, both of which will play out over 3-5 years.

## Red Flags

- **FCF conversion well below 80% threshold:** At 58% in 2025 and 34% in 2023, TSMC converts far less of its net income to free cash flow than asset-light businesses. Much of the capex is growth-oriented, but the line between "growth" and "necessary to stay competitive" is blurry in semiconductors.
- **Capex acceleration into 2026-2028:** The jump to $52-56B in 2026 capex (from $40.9B in 2025), with management flagging "significantly higher" spending over three years, means cash will be consumed at an even faster rate. If demand disappoints, these are sunk costs with long depreciation tails.
- **Overseas fab cost premium:** Arizona fabs cost 30-50% more to build and operate than Taiwan equivalents. As overseas capacity grows from a small share to potentially 20%+ of output, blended margins could face structural pressure unless pricing premiums fully offset the cost gap.
- **Customer concentration intensifying:** NVIDIA and Apple together represent roughly 36% of revenue, and the top 10 customers ~76%+. A coordinated pullback in AI capex by hyperscalers would disproportionately impact TSMC.
- **Cyclicality is real:** The 2023 downturn (revenue -4.5%, net income -14.2%) happened just one year after a boom. Semiconductor cycles have not been repealed, even if AI extends the current upcycle.
- **Geopolitical risk as an economic factor:** While not purely an "economics" issue, the concentration of 80%+ of production in Taiwan introduces a risk premium that affects cost of capital and long-term capital allocation decisions.

## Score: 8 / 10

TSMC earns an 8 for business economics — a remarkable achievement for the most capital-intensive major technology company in the world. ROIC of 25-49%, gross margins approaching 60%, and steadily growing free cash flow demonstrate that TSMC's near-monopoly on leading-edge manufacturing converts enormous capital investment into truly exceptional returns. The two-point deduction from a perfect score reflects the unavoidable reality of 33-50% capex-to-revenue ratios, sub-60% FCF conversion, proven cyclicality, and the margin uncertainty introduced by a massive overseas expansion program. This is not an asset-light compounder — it is an asset-heavy compounder, which is rarer and arguably more impressive, but carries inherent risks that lighter models do not.
