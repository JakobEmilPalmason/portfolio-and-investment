## Detailed Analysis

**Insider Ownership and Skin in the Game.** This is the weakest area of TSMC's stewardship story. Insider ownership data shows executives and directors hold a very small percentage of shares, and there has been essentially zero insider buying or selling activity in recent periods. Morris Chang, the founder who retired in 2018, built the company from scratch but his current shareholding is modest relative to the company's enormous market cap. C.C. Wei and Mark Liu (who served as chairman until June 2024 before Wei assumed both roles) do not appear to have made meaningful open-market purchases. This is partly a cultural and structural reality — Taiwanese executive compensation historically tilts toward cash bonuses and profit sharing rather than large equity grants. The National Development Fund of Taiwan's Executive Yuan holds 6.38% as the largest single shareholder, which creates alignment between the government and the company's long-term success but is not the same as management having personal wealth at risk. On the positive side, TSMC's employee restricted stock award (RSA) program, in place since 2021, does tie executive compensation to shareholder outcomes and ESG achievements.

**Capital Allocation Track Record.** TSMC's capital allocation is defined by one dominant activity: reinvestment. The company spent $40.9B in capex in 2025 and has guided $52-56B for 2026 — roughly 42-46% of expected revenue plowed back into the business. This level of reinvestment would be alarming at most companies, but TSMC's returns justify it. ROIC climbed from 19.2% in 2023 to 28.3% in 2025, with the most recent quarter annualizing at ~45%. Gross margins have expanded from 54.4% (2023) to 62.3% (Q4 2025), meaning the capex is not chasing low-return business — it is funding capacity for high-margin leading-edge nodes (N3, N2) where TSMC has essentially no competition. The company allocates 70-80% of capex to leading-edge processes. On dividends, TSMC has been a steady grower: the 2026 dividend of NT$23/share is up 28% from NT$18 in 2025, and NT$14 in 2024, reflecting a sustainable payout ratio of roughly 25-29%. Buybacks are modest and surgical — the company repurchased 3.249M shares in 2024 specifically to offset dilution from employee RSA issuances. The share count has been flat at ~5.186B for years, meaning net dilution is effectively zero. TSMC has essentially no M&A history — it grows organically, which is exactly the right approach for a company with its competitive position.

**The Arizona Question.** The most important capital allocation decision in recent years is the global fab expansion — particularly the Arizona complex. The first Arizona fab entered volume production in Q4 2024 with yields comparable to Taiwan, which is a genuine achievement. However, costs are 4-5x higher than building equivalent capacity in Taiwan, and the Arizona subsidiary lost NT$14.3B ($441M) in 2024, with wafer-level gross margins of only ~8% versus 62% in Taiwan. The total commitment has grown from $12B initially to $165B for a full "gigafab cluster." This investment is partly strategic (customer relationships, geopolitical hedging, CHIPS Act subsidies) and partly compelled by political pressure. It is clearly not an investment TSMC would make on purely economic grounds. The key question is whether TSMC can narrow the cost gap over time as the fabs scale and mature. Management has been transparent about these economics rather than sugarcoating them.

**Compensation and Candor.** TSMC's executive pay, while high by Taiwanese standards (total compensation for the top executives reached NT$1.27B in a recent year, roughly 1,284x the national average), is modest compared to US semiconductor peers. Stock-based compensation is relatively small — RSA issuances of ~3-4M shares annually on a base of 5.186B shares represents less than 0.1% dilution per year, which is then offset by buybacks. This is dramatically lower SBC intensity than US tech companies. On candor, C.C. Wei stands out. His Q4 2025 earnings call is remarkable for its honesty: he openly admitted being "very nervous" about whether AI demand might be a bubble, described spending months personally interviewing hyperscaler customers for evidence, and warned that careless investment "would be a disaster for TSMC for sure." This is the language of an owner, not a politician. He speaks in plain, sometimes blunt terms — TSMC's PR team reportedly has to manage his tendency to say too much rather than too little. The annual report and earnings calls read like operational updates from someone running the business, not marketing materials.

**Governance Structure.** TSMC has a clean governance structure. There are no dual-class shares — one share, one vote. The board has 10 members, with 7 independent directors (over 50% independence). The Audit and Compensation committees consist entirely of independent directors. The company has been ranked in the top 5% of Taiwan's Corporate Governance Evaluations for 11 consecutive years. The National Development Fund has a board seat through its representative, Dr. Chun-Hsien Yeh, which gives the Taiwan government influence but not control. There are no known related-party transaction concerns of significance. The Morris Chang succession to Mark Liu/C.C. Wei was smooth and planned well in advance — Chang announced in 2017 and retired in June 2018, with the company's performance actually accelerating under the new leadership.

**The Simple Tell.** TSMC's communications read like those of a well-run engineering company that happens to be one of the most important businesses on earth. There is no grandiosity, no empire-building language. When Wei discusses the $56B capex budget, he frames it as nervously necessary rather than triumphantly ambitious. The annual reports are factual and metrics-driven. The succession from founder Morris Chang to professional management has been one of the smoothest in global business history — the company has performed better, not worse, under Wei's operational leadership.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** TSMC's management continues to invest massive capex at 25-45% ROIC with near-zero dilution, growing dividends 25%+ annually, while maintaining the disciplined candor that prevents empire-building mistakes.
- **Bear case:** The Arizona/overseas expansion becomes a persistent value-destroying drag, political pressure forces capital allocation decisions that prioritize geopolitics over returns, and the lack of insider ownership means management has insufficient personal downside if things go wrong.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Strong data on capital allocation track record and governance; lower confidence on insider ownership details due to limited public disclosure of personal holdings for Taiwanese executives.

## Red Flags

- Minimal insider ownership by executives — while culturally normal in Taiwan, it means management's personal wealth is not deeply tied to share price performance.
- Arizona fabs are currently operating at 8% gross margins versus 62% in Taiwan, with the total commitment ballooning from $12B to $165B. This is partly a political necessity, but it is the single largest risk to TSMC's capital allocation discipline.
- Government (National Development Fund) is the largest shareholder at 6.38% — generally aligned with long-term value creation, but could introduce political considerations into business decisions (e.g., keeping most advanced tech in Taiwan, or making overseas investments for diplomatic reasons).

## Score: 8 / 10

TSMC earns a strong score for its exceptional capital allocation discipline (near-zero dilution, 25%+ ROIC, sustainable and growing dividends, no wasteful M&A), clean governance, and unusually candid management communication — docked from a 9 primarily for minimal insider ownership and the still-uncertain economics of the massive overseas fab expansion.
