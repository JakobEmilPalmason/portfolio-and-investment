## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Home Depot carried $65.4B in total debt at fiscal year-end FY2025 (ended February 1, 2026), compared with $62.3B in FY2024 and $52.2B in FY2023. Long-term debt stood at $46.3B, with short-term debt (current portion of long-term debt and commercial paper) at $4.5B. The step-change in leverage occurred in FY2024 when the company raised approximately $12.5B in new senior notes and commercial paper to fund the $18.25B SRS Distribution acquisition (completed June 2024), repaying SRS's existing debt of ~$5.5B at closing. Net debt (total debt minus cash) is approximately $64.0B as of February 1, 2026, with cash and equivalents of only $1.4B. The debt/EBITDA ratio, calculated on FY2025 EBITDA of approximately $25.0B (EBIT $20.9B + D&A $4.1B), is 2.6x. This is a material increase from the pre-SRS range of 1.8–2.1x.

**Interest Coverage.** Interest expense for FY2025 was approximately $2.4B (full-year), compared with approximately $1.7B in FY2023. EBIT was $20.9B, implying interest coverage of 8.7x. At the quarterly run-rate ($575M/quarter as of Q2 FY2025), annualized interest of $2.3B against EBIT of $20.9B yields a coverage ratio of approximately 9.1x. This remains healthy by investment-grade standards (typical covenant threshold is 3–4x) and implies interest consumes roughly 11.5% of operating income. Management guided FY2026 net interest expense at approximately $2.2B, reflecting incremental debt repayment and favorable refinancing as short-duration notes mature.

**Liquidity.** Cash on hand is modest at $1.4B (FY2025) versus $1.7B (FY2024) and $3.8B (FY2023, inflated pre-acquisition). However, Home Depot maintains a $4.0B commercial paper program and a large revolving credit facility (approximately $3.5B committed), providing ready access to short-term liquidity. Operating cash flow of $16.3B in FY2025 is the primary liquidity source; the company generated enough OCF to cover capex ($3.7B), dividends ($8.6B estimated, ~$2.30/share quarterly × 993M diluted shares), and partial debt reduction. The current ratio of 1.06x is lean but consistent with a business that operates on negative working capital dynamics — supplier payment terms are longer than inventory turns in the retail business. The tightening from 1.41x in FY2022 to 1.06x in FY2025 reflects the incremental current maturities from the SRS acquisition financing.

**Refinancing Risk.** Home Depot has an investment-grade credit profile (rated A2/A by Moody's/S&P). The debt maturity schedule from the SRS acquisition financing was structured with staggered maturities across 3, 5, 7, 10, and 30-year tranches, limiting near-term cliff refinancing risk. Short-term debt of $4.5B at FY2025 year-end is the primary near-term refinancing obligation, manageable given the $16B+ OCF generation capacity. Management has explicitly guided to reduce leverage to 2.0x Debt/EBITDA by end of FY2026 through a combination of EBITDA growth and debt paydown — implying $4–5B of debt reduction over the next 12 months. Rates on the new notes are higher than the legacy portfolio (issued in a near-zero rate environment), but the blended cost of debt remains approximately 4.5–5.0%.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Under ASC 842, operating lease right-of-use assets and liabilities are recognized on the balance sheet. Home Depot's operating lease liabilities total approximately $8.1B (core HD) plus $538M (SRS), or approximately $8.6B combined. These represent long-term store and distribution center lease obligations and are a genuine fixed-cost commitment. Using the traditional 8x operating rent capitalization methodology applied in the SRS acquisition pro forma, adjusted debt would be approximately $12.1B higher on a combined basis. This does not change the thesis materially but it is the correct frame for evaluating true leverage: on a rent-adjusted basis, total obligations are closer to $75B. The company's store lease structure (average remaining lease term ~10 years with renewal options) is a resilience factor — in extreme stress, lease renegotiations are possible.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** A 30% revenue shock (approximately $49B annual revenue loss from $165B to ~$115B) applied for two consecutive years is a severe but instructive scenario. Assuming: (a) gross margins compress to 30% from 33.3% (customer trade-down, deleveraged buying), (b) operating costs are 75% semi-fixed (limited ability to cut quickly), (c) capex is slashed to $1.5B (pure maintenance), and (d) dividends are cut 50%. Year 1 stressed EBITDA would approximate $10–12B; interest coverage falls to approximately 4.2–5.0x (still above typical covenant floors of 2.5–3.5x). Free cash flow would be approximately $6–7B, sufficient to service the $2.2–2.4B interest burden and maintain operations without equity issuance. Year 2 would be more strained if the revenue shock persisted: FCF could fall to $4–5B, reducing debt paydown capacity but still covering mandatory obligations. The company would almost certainly suspend buybacks and potentially cut dividends, but equity issuance appears unnecessary at this scenario depth. A 40%+ revenue shock sustained for three-plus years would be a different calculation.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Investment-grade balance sheet with $12–16B FCF generation capacity easily services $2.3B annual interest; management on track to restore 2.0x leverage by FY2026 end, which would meaningfully reduce financial risk.
- **Bear case:** $64B net debt is large in absolute terms; if housing weakness persists and OCF underperforms guidance, leverage could remain stubbornly above 2.5x while interest expense consumes a growing share of operating income.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — balance sheet figures are well-documented from public filings; stress test assumptions involve judgment but outcomes are within a reasonable range.

## Red Flags

- Net debt of ~$64B is among the highest in US retail; an unexpected earnings deterioration would quickly re-rate the credit
- Current ratio of 1.06x leaves minimal short-term liquidity buffer; relies heavily on credit facility access
- $4.5B of short-term debt maturities require near-term refinancing in a potentially higher-rate environment
- Operating lease obligations of $8.6B represent genuine fixed-cost commitments that compress downside flexibility
- Management's 2.0x leverage target by FY2026 end depends on EBITDA recovery and housing normalization — both uncertain

## Score: 5 / 10

Home Depot's operating cash flows are ample to service its obligations, and its investment-grade credit profile limits near-term refinancing risk, but the quantum of absolute debt ($65B gross, $64B net) acquired for the SRS acquisition is substantial, leverage ratios are at cycle highs, and liquidity buffers are thin — warranting a below-average safety score until the balance sheet is meaningfully repaired.
