## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game:** HD's institutional ownership dominates; executive insider ownership is not large in absolute share count relative to the company's size. Ted Decker's compensation is heavily equity-based, which aligns his interests with shareholders to a degree, but the stock and option grants are salary-driven rather than the result of open-market purchases with personal capital. This is not unusual for a $400B market cap company, but it falls short of "owner-operator" status.

**Buybacks — timing quality:** Home Depot has historically been a consistent repurchaser of shares, shrinking the share count meaningfully over the prior decade. The $15 billion buyback authorization was announced alongside strong results. However, the SRS acquisition in 2024 forced a halt to repurchases — management took on significant leverage ($56B total debt by end of FY2025) and explicitly prioritized debt paydown over buybacks. The target is to return to 2.0x leverage ratio by end of FY2026 and then resume buybacks. The historical buyback record is solid; the current pause is a consequence of the M&A bet, not mismanagement per se.

**Dividends:** 16 consecutive years of dividend increases is a strong signal of capital discipline. The 1.3% increase in February 2026 (even while highly levered) reflects confidence in cash generation. At $9.32/share annually, the dividend is well-covered by $14.69 adjusted EPS. Payout ratio is around 63% — high but not concerning for a mature, cash-generative business.

**M&A — the big question:** The SRS ($18.25B) and GMS (~$5.5B enterprise value) acquisitions are the defining capital allocation decisions of the current management era. On the bull case: HD paid a fair price to acquire a distribution platform that would take a decade to build organically, accelerating access to the $450B Pro market. On the bear case: HD overpaid at a peak in the acquisition cycle, took on $23B+ in debt to buy businesses whose integration risk is high, and EPS has actually declined (adjusted EPS $14.69 in FY2025 vs. $15.24 in FY2024). The jury is still out. Management has a credible track record before these acquisitions, so this deserves benefit of the doubt — but it's a real risk.

**Share count trend:** Shares outstanding have been reduced meaningfully over the prior decade. The current pause in buybacks is explicitly tied to leverage reduction, and management has communicated a clear timeline (end of 2026) for resumption.

**Compensation design:** Decker's pay is 90.8% variable (stock + options), which is reasonable alignment. The metrics used in comp plans (likely tied to EPS, ROIC, and operating income) are disclosed in proxy statements. No obvious red flags on compensation engineering.

**Candor and consistency:** The December 2025 "strategic update" where HD explicitly provided a "market recovery case" alongside base guidance is a positive transparency signal. Management has been clear that the business is in a housing cycle trough and has avoided pretending otherwise. The dividend increase in a high-leverage year is perhaps slightly aggressive, but the cash flow supports it.

**Governance:** Standard large-cap governance. No dual-class structure. Board composition appears independent. No known related-party or governance red flags.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** SRS/GMS prove to be value-creating acquisitions, leverage comes down as planned by end of 2026, buybacks resume aggressively, and management's Pro market bet pays off with multi-year earnings growth.
- **Bear case:** Integration proves harder than expected, housing market remains frozen through 2027, leverage stays elevated longer than guided, and the capital allocation bet destroys value.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the pre-acquisition management track record is strong, but the $23B M&A bet introduces genuine uncertainty about the quality of this specific capital allocation decision.

## Red Flags

- Total debt of ~$56B at end of FY2025 represents a dramatic change in balance sheet posture driven by M&A — management is betting heavily on the Pro strategy.
- Adjusted EPS declined from $15.24 (FY2024) to $14.69 (FY2025) — shareholders are not yet seeing accretion from the acquisitions.
- Dividend increase in a high-leverage year prioritizes optics over financial conservatism.

## Score: 7 / 10

Management has a long track record of disciplined capital allocation and honest communication, and the Pro distribution strategy has strategic logic — but the scale of the M&A bet, the resulting leverage, and the EPS dilution in the near term warrant a slight downgrade from the historically higher score this team would have earned.
