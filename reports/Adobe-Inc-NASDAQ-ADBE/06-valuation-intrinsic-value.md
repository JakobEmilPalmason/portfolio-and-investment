## Detailed Analysis

### Owner Earnings Estimate

Adobe's FY2025 owner earnings can be estimated as follows: GAAP net income of $7.13B plus depreciation and amortization of approximately $1.7B (annualizing the ~$426M quarterly run rate) minus maintenance capital expenditures. Total capex was only $179M in FY2025 — remarkably low for a company this size, reflecting Adobe's asset-light software model. Maintenance capex is likely the vast majority of that $179M (servers, office buildouts), so call it $150M. That gives us owner earnings of roughly $8.7B, or about $21 per share on ~415M shares outstanding. On a non-GAAP basis (adding back stock-based compensation adjustments), owner earnings are closer to $10B, but I will use the GAAP-based figure as the more conservative and honest anchor.

Looking out 3-5 years, owner earnings depend heavily on revenue growth and margin trajectory. Adobe guided FY2026 revenue of ~$25.8-26B (roughly 9-10% growth). If we assume the company can sustain 8-10% revenue growth through FY2030 (driven by AI monetization, pricing power, and international expansion) while maintaining or slightly expanding its ~37% GAAP operating margin, owner earnings could reach $12-14B by FY2030, or roughly $32-37 per share (assuming continued buybacks reduce the share count to ~380M).

### Scenario Analysis

**Bear Case — AI Disruption Bites (Revenue growth: 4-5%, Margins compress to 32%)**

In this scenario, generative AI tools from competitors (Midjourney, Canva, open-source models) erode Creative Cloud's pricing power. Enterprise customers slow adoption. Revenue grows at low-to-mid single digits, reaching ~$30B by FY2030. GAAP operating margins compress from 37% to 32% as Adobe spends aggressively to defend its position. Owner earnings reach roughly $8.5B, or ~$22/share. Applying a 15x multiple (appropriate for a slow-growth, high-quality software business) yields a 2030 value of $330/share. Discounted back 5 years at 10%, that is roughly $205/share — about 28% below the current price of ~$284.

**Base Case — Steady Compounder (Revenue growth: 8-9%, Margins stable at 37%)**

Adobe continues to grow at a healthy clip, successfully monetizing AI through Firefly credits and premium tiers. Creative Cloud remains the professional standard. Revenue reaches ~$35B by FY2030. Owner earnings grow to ~$12B, or ~$31/share. A 20x multiple (reasonable for a durable, high-margin compounder) gives a 2030 value of $620/share. Discounted back at 10%, that is roughly $385/share — about 35% upside from today.

**Bull Case — AI Acceleration (Revenue growth: 12-14%, Margins expand to 40%)**

Firefly becomes the dominant commercial AI creative platform. Adobe captures new markets (video, 3D, audio generation at scale). Enterprise adoption accelerates. Revenue reaches ~$42B by FY2030. Owner earnings reach ~$16B, or ~$42/share. A 25x multiple yields a 2030 value of $1,050/share. Discounted back at 10%, that is roughly $650/share — more than double from here.

### Multiples in Context

Adobe's current valuation is striking in its cheapness relative to history and peers:

- **Adobe today:** P/E ~15x, EV/EBITDA ~11.5x, P/FCF ~11x
- **Adobe 5-year average:** P/E ~35-40x, P/FCF ~26x
- **Salesforce (CRM):** P/E ~32x (growing ~10%)
- **Microsoft (MSFT):** P/E ~26x (growing ~15%)
- **Intuit (INTU):** P/E ~28x (growing ~12%)
- **ServiceNow (NOW):** P/E ~54x (growing ~18%)
- **Synopsys (SNPS):** P/E ~48-51x

Adobe is growing revenue at 9-11%, generating 41% FCF margins, and trades at roughly half the multiple of Salesforce, which grows at a similar rate with lower margins. The market is pricing Adobe as if it were a declining business, not a record-revenue compounder. The only explanation is that the market has assigned a significant probability to AI disruption permanently impairing Adobe's competitive position.

### Reverse-Engineering the Current Price

At $284/share and ~415M shares outstanding, Adobe's market cap is roughly $118B. With EV around $119B (after netting cash against debt). At FY2025 owner earnings of ~$8.7B, the stock trades at ~13.5x owner earnings.

What does 13.5x owner earnings imply? It implies the market expects Adobe's earnings to be essentially flat or slightly declining over the next 5-10 years, with no credit given for growth. For context, even a zero-growth company with Adobe's quality characteristics (41% FCF margins, dominant market position, 95%+ recurring revenue) would typically command 15-18x earnings. The current price effectively bakes in a scenario where AI disruption causes meaningful erosion of Adobe's business — something between my bear case and a "no growth" case.

To justify the current price with a 10% discount rate, you need to assume Adobe's owner earnings stay flat at ~$8.7B forever and apply a ~13.5x terminal multiple. That is an extremely pessimistic assumption for a business that just grew revenue 11% and has been compounding FCF at 15%+ annually for a decade.

### Implied Expectations vs Reality

The market is pricing in significant pessimism — somewhere between "growth stalls" and "modest decline." This stands in contrast to the actual business reality: record revenue, record free cash flow, 70M+ monthly active users on freemium AI products, Firefly generating $400M+ in direct revenue, and management guiding for continued double-digit growth.

The disconnect appears driven by legitimate but potentially overblown fears about AI disruption. Yes, Midjourney and Canva and open-source tools are real competitors. But Adobe's moat is not just "making pictures" — it is the deeply embedded professional workflow, enterprise IT relationships, regulatory compliance (content credentials), and the network effects of file format standards (PSD, PDF, AI). These are not easily replicated by a text-to-image model.

The key question is whether the market's fear is justified. If Adobe's moat holds — even partially — the stock is meaningfully undervalued. If generative AI truly commoditizes creative tools and Adobe fails to adapt, the bear case could be worse than modeled. But at 11-15x earnings and FCF, you are being paid handsomely to take that bet.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Adobe successfully positions Firefly as the enterprise-standard AI creative platform, accelerating growth to 12%+ while expanding margins, making today's 12x forward P/E look absurdly cheap in hindsight — a potential double within 3-4 years.
- **Bear case:** Generative AI commoditizes creative tools, open-source models erode pricing power, and Adobe's growth stalls to low single digits — but even then, at current multiples, downside may be limited to 20-30% given the cash flow floor.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — The valuation discount to history and peers is undeniable and mathematically verifiable; the uncertainty is whether the business quality will persist, which depends on the evolving AI competitive landscape.

## Red Flags

- AI disruption risk is real and difficult to quantify — generative AI models are improving rapidly, and Adobe's creative tools moat could narrow faster than expected
- Stock-based compensation is substantial (~$2B/year), meaning GAAP earnings understate the true cost of running the business; the buybacks partly offset dilution rather than purely shrinking the float
- FY2026 revenue guidance of ~$25.9B implies only ~9% growth, a slight deceleration from FY2025's 11% — the market may be right that growth is slowing
- Management's $25B buyback authorization at these prices is shareholder-friendly, but aggressive buybacks at the wrong time can destroy value if the business deteriorates
- The Figma deal collapse left Adobe without a major strategic acquisition to expand its TAM in collaborative design

## Score: 8 / 10

Adobe appears meaningfully undervalued on almost every metric — trading at 40-60% discounts to its own history and peers, with record cash flows and a still-growing business. The base case suggests 35%+ upside over 3-5 years, and even the bear case implies limited permanent capital loss at these multiples. The one-point deduction from a 9 reflects genuine uncertainty about AI disruption's long-term impact on Adobe's competitive position — this is not a "no-brainer" bargain, but it is a compelling risk/reward for a patient investor who believes Adobe's workflow moat will endure.
