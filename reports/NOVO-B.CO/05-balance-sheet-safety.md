## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Novo Nordisk's balance sheet underwent a significant transformation in 2024-2025. Historically a minimal-debt company, Novo took on substantial debt to finance the acquisition of three Catalent fill-finish manufacturing sites for USD 11.7 billion. By the end of 2025, total outstanding Eurobonds had reached approximately EUR 16.3 billion (roughly DKK 120+ billion), after successive issuances of EUR 4.65 billion in 2024 and EUR 10 billion in 2025 under the company's EUR 20 billion Euro Medium Term Note Programme. The debt is predominantly fixed-rate, which is prudent given the rising-rate environment. Maturities are well-laddered: EUR 1.3 billion maturing in 2026, EUR 1.9 billion in 2027, EUR 1.45 billion in 2028, EUR 1.0 billion in 2029, and further tranches extending to 2034 and beyond. This staggered schedule means no single year presents a dangerous refinancing cliff. Total assets stood at approximately DKK 543 billion with total equity of DKK 194 billion, yielding a debt-to-equity ratio of roughly 63% — elevated compared to Novo's historically clean balance sheet, but moderate in absolute terms.

**Interest Coverage and Earnings Power.** Despite the debt build-up, Novo Nordisk's enormous earnings power keeps interest coverage extremely comfortable. FY2024 operating profit was DKK 128.3 billion, and FY2025 operating profit was DKK 127.7 billion (slightly down due to approximately DKK 8 billion in one-off restructuring costs). Net financial items in 2025 included a loss of DKK 3.2 billion, partially reflecting increased interest expenses from the Catalent-related debt. Using EBIT of roughly DKK 128 billion against interest expense in the range of DKK 3-4 billion implies an interest coverage ratio of approximately 35-45x. Even in a severe stress scenario, this provides an extraordinary cushion. The company could absorb a massive earnings decline before debt service became a concern.

**Liquidity and Cash Flow.** Cash and cash equivalents were approximately DKK 26.5 billion as of the most recent reporting. Free cash flow in 2024 was deeply negative (DKK -14.7 billion) solely because of the Catalent acquisition payment. This was a one-time event. Underlying operating cash flow generation remains very strong — FY2023 free cash flow was DKK 68.3 billion for context. As the Catalent acquisition is absorbed and no comparable large acquisitions are currently planned, free cash flow should return to strongly positive territory. Novo Nordisk also has access to its Euro Medium Term Note programme and capital markets given its AA credit rating, providing ample backup liquidity.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Items and Other Obligations.** Capital lease obligations were approximately DKK 6.8 billion at end of 2024, which is modest relative to the company's scale. Specific pension obligation data was not retrieved in the search, but Novo Nordisk primarily operates defined-contribution pension plans in Denmark and the Nordics, meaning pension risk is generally limited compared to companies with large defined-benefit plans. The company has been actively returning capital to shareholders through buybacks (DKK 20 billion program in 2024, DKK 15 billion program started February 2026) and dividends (DKK 3.75 per share interim dividend in 2025, up 7% year-over-year). These capital returns are discretionary and could be reduced or paused in a downturn, providing additional financial flexibility.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for Two Years.** FY2025 revenue was DKK 309 billion, with operating profit of DKK 128 billion (roughly 41% operating margin). If revenue dropped 30% to approximately DKK 216 billion, and assuming gross margins compress somewhat and some cost reduction is achievable, operating profit could fall to roughly DKK 50-65 billion (assuming margins compress to 23-30% under stress). Interest expense of DKK 3-5 billion would remain easily covered — even at 10x lower operating profit, coverage would still be above 10x. Annual debt maturities of EUR 1-2 billion (DKK 8-16 billion) would need to be refinanced or repaid from cash flow, which at DKK 50+ billion operating profit would remain feasible. The company could also pause the DKK 15 billion share buyback and reduce dividends, freeing up DKK 20+ billion annually. Conclusion: Novo Nordisk could survive a 30% revenue decline for two years without issuing equity, though it would need to curtail shareholder returns and potentially draw on credit facilities. Debt-to-EBITDA would rise to approximately 2-2.5x under stress, still within manageable bounds for an AA-rated company.

**The 2026 Outlook Concern.** The most notable development is the 2026 guidance: sales and operating profit expected to decline 5-13% at CER. This is driven by US pricing pressure from MFN agreements, semaglutide patent expiries in Brazil, Canada, and China, and competitive intensification. While this is not a balance sheet crisis, it is the first earnings decline Novo Nordisk has faced in over a decade and comes at a time when the company is carrying more debt than ever. If the decline proves deeper or more prolonged than guided, the trajectory toward deleveraging would slow. However, the Wegovy pill launch appears strong (170,000+ patients in the first four weeks of 2026), suggesting the franchise still has growth potential.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Novo Nordisk's AA credit rating, 40x+ interest coverage, well-laddered fixed-rate debt, and massive cash generation mean the balance sheet can absorb the Catalent acquisition debt comfortably while the GLP-1 franchise continues generating abundant cash flow for rapid deleveraging.
- **Bear case:** The unprecedented 2026 sales and profit decline guidance, combined with the highest debt load in company history, could delay deleveraging; if GLP-1 competition and pricing pressure worsen beyond guidance, the company faces simultaneous earnings erosion and elevated debt — still far from distress, but a meaningful deterioration from fortress status.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — Key debt figures, credit ratings, and operating metrics are well-sourced from 2025 annual results and rating agency actions; some granular balance sheet items (pension details, precise current ratio) were not available from web search alone.

## Red Flags

- Total debt has increased dramatically from near-zero to approximately DKK 120+ billion in just two years, entirely to fund the Catalent acquisition — this is a structural shift in the balance sheet philosophy.
- 2026 guidance for 5-13% sales and operating profit decline is the first earnings retreat in over a decade, arriving at a time of peak leverage.
- Free cash flow was negative DKK 14.7 billion in 2024; while this was acquisition-driven and non-recurring, it highlights how quickly cash can be consumed.
- The DKK 15 billion share buyback program initiated in February 2026 continues during a period of declining earnings and elevated debt, which could be viewed as prioritizing shareholder returns over deleveraging.

## Score: 7 / 10

Novo Nordisk maintains strong interest coverage (40x+), investment-grade AA/Aa3 credit ratings, well-laddered fixed-rate debt maturities, and earnings power that would comfortably service debt even in a severe downturn — but it no longer has the fortress, near-net-cash balance sheet it had two years ago, and the 2026 earnings decline guidance introduces uncertainty about the pace of deleveraging, keeping this a notch below the highest tier.
