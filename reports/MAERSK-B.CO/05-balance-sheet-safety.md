## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure**

At December 31, 2024, Maersk's balance sheet was genuinely strong. Total gross cash, cash equivalents, and term deposits were $23.0bn (including $5.6bn in cash, $15.7bn in term deposits longer than three months, and restricted cash of $0.93bn), against Moody's-adjusted total debt of approximately $16.7bn, producing a net cash position of $7.4bn. The debt-to-equity ratio was approximately 0.08 at year-end 2024, indicating very conservative headline leverage. However, total obligations are larger than headline debt suggests once capital lease liabilities ($11.4bn) are included — these are on-balance-sheet under IFRS 16 and represent mostly ship and container operating leases that Maersk has contractually committed to pay. Adding gross financial debt (bonds and bank facilities) to lease liabilities, total obligations are likely in the $20–25bn range against the cash and deposit cushion. Moody's-adjusted debt/EBITDA was expected to increase to approximately 1.7x in 2025 as EBITDA declined from the 2024 level of $12.1bn to 2025's $9.5bn.

**Interest Coverage**

Interest expense was $904m in 2023 and $1,003m in 2024, rising modestly as debt levels were maintained. EBIT coverage ratios: in 2024, $6.5bn EBIT / $1.0bn interest = approximately 6.5x — well above the comfortable 5x threshold. In 2023, with EBIT of approximately $3.9bn (group EBIT margin 7.7% on $51.1bn revenue), coverage was approximately 4.3x — still above the 3x warning level but not comfortable. In a stress scenario resembling 2023 (37% revenue decline), coverage would be strained. The key risk is that interest charges are relatively fixed while EBIT is extraordinarily variable. In a severe downturn matching 2009-style freight rate conditions, EBIT could approach zero or turn negative, at which point even Maersk's large cash buffer would be tested.

**Liquidity**

Total available liquidity at December 31, 2024 was $29.0bn: $5.6bn cash, $15.7bn term deposits, and $6.0bn undrawn revolving credit facilities. This is an exceptional liquidity position — Maersk could fund multiple years of operations and capex entirely from existing resources without accessing capital markets. Even after the drawdown through shareholder returns in 2025 (approximately $4bn), net cash remained at $2.6bn by September 30, 2025, with full-year 2025 net cash ending at approximately $2.9bn per available data. The current ratio was not precisely found in search results, but given the cash-heavy balance sheet relative to current liabilities, it is expected to be well above 1.0x. Maersk could survive a substantial revenue shock (30%+) for at least 2–3 years using its liquidity without needing to raise external capital, assuming some capex deferrals.

**Refinancing Risk**

Refinancing risk is low. Revolving credit facilities mature between 2027 and 2030, providing a multi-year runway. Maersk's investment-grade credit ratings (Baa1/BBB+) give it easy access to bond and bank markets at reasonable spreads. The company does not appear to depend on capital markets being open in the near term — it has enough internal liquidity to service obligations for an extended period without external funding. The key refinancing question would arise if net cash is fully spent and EBITDA has not recovered — at that point, bond maturities and facility renewals would require market access. Currently, that scenario is not in view.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

Capital lease liabilities of $11.4bn at end-2024 are the most significant item. These are on-balance-sheet under IFRS 16 but are sometimes excluded from simplified debt analyses. They represent Maersk's obligations under chartered vessel agreements and container leases — costs that are contractually committed and continue regardless of freight rates. In a downturn, these become a fixed cost burden that cannot easily be shed (though vessel charters can sometimes be renegotiated or sub-chartered). Pension obligations: Maersk has defined benefit plans; exact net pension liabilities were not found in search results but are disclosed in the annual report. For a company of this size, pension obligations of $500m–$1.5bn would not be unusual, but precise figures are not available and should be verified in the 2024 annual report. No material contingent liabilities or off-balance-sheet guarantees were identified in available search results, though ship recycling and environmental obligations are an industry-wide consideration.

**Stress Test: Revenue −30% for Two Years**

If Maersk experienced a 30% revenue decline from the 2024 base ($55.5bn → ~$39bn) for two consecutive years, EBITDA would likely fall to the $3–5bn range (approximating 2023 levels or worse). After capex (assume $4–5bn per year even with deferrals), lease payments, interest, and taxes, operating cash outflows could exceed cash inflows. However, Maersk entered this stress scenario with $29bn of gross liquidity and $7.4bn net cash. The company could absorb approximately two-to-three years of cash burn at $2–3bn per year before breaching any structural liquidity constraint, provided it aggressively curtailed capex and shareholder distributions. It would not need to raise equity. The one caveat: Moody's warned that debt/EBITDA approaching 3.0x or free cash flow after distributions turning negative could pressure the credit rating. A rating downgrade to non-investment grade would increase borrowing costs and potentially trigger covenant issues in some facilities.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Maersk's net cash position and investment-grade ratings mean the balance sheet is a genuine competitive advantage — it can take market share, make strategic acquisitions, and absorb cycle downturns without financial distress.
- **Bear case:** The $7.4bn net cash position at end-2024 is being actively spent down via shareholder returns and capex; if freight rates remain depressed through 2026–2027 and FCF stays negative, the fortress balance sheet could erode to a point where refinancing risk re-enters the picture.
- **Confidence:** High — the balance sheet metrics are well-documented and unambiguous. Maersk's financial position is strong as of the most recent data.

## Red Flags

- Net cash position declined from $7.4bn (December 2024) to $2.6bn (September 2025) — a $4.8bn erosion in nine months from shareholder returns and capex, without generating positive free cash flow.
- Full-year 2025 FCF was guided as at least negative $3.0bn; this trend cannot continue indefinitely without eventually drawing down to a leveraged position.
- Lease liabilities of $11.4bn are contractually committed fixed costs that do not shrink quickly in a downturn — they amplify downside earnings sensitivity.
- Interest coverage of 4.3x in 2023 (a year of moderate distress by Maersk's recent standards) is not especially comfortable and would deteriorate further in a more severe freight rate downturn.
- Pension liability detail was not findable in public search results — this should be verified in the 2024 Annual Report notes before drawing firm conclusions.

## Score: 7 / 10

Maersk's balance sheet is genuinely strong — net cash position, investment-grade ratings, $29bn of gross liquidity, and well-laddered debt maturities are real strengths. The company could survive a severe multi-year downturn without raising equity. However, the score is not higher because: (1) the balance sheet is being actively deployed via aggressive capex and shareholder returns, reducing the buffer; (2) large capital lease obligations create fixed-cost exposure; and (3) the inherent earnings volatility of the Ocean business means the required liquidity cushion is structurally higher than for a more stable business. This is a solid but not exceptional balance sheet for a highly cyclical industrial company.
