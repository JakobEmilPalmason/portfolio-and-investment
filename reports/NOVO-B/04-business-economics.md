## Detailed Analysis

**Returns on Capital: Exceptional but Declining.** Novo Nordisk has historically generated world-class returns on invested capital. From 2021-2023, ROIC ranged from 37-41%, placing it among the best capital compounders in global pharma. ROE was even more striking at 72-88% over the same period, reflecting the company's relatively light equity base and heavy share buybacks. However, the trend is unmistakably downward: ROIC fell to 34% in 2024 and 25% in 2025 as the denominator (invested capital) ballooned. The company spent DKK 60.1 billion on property, plant, and equipment in 2025, up from just DKK 6.3 billion in 2021 -- a nearly 10x increase in four years. It also spent DKK 30 billion on intangible assets in 2025 (versus DKK 4.1 billion the prior year). This massive capital deployment is primarily growth capex aimed at building GLP-1 manufacturing capacity, but it has materially diluted returns on capital in the near term. The key question is whether these investments will generate returns commensurate with historical levels once capacity comes online, or whether Novo is entering a structurally more capital-intensive phase.

**Margin Structure: Still Elite, but Under Pressure.** Novo Nordisk's margin profile has been exceptional. Gross margins have hovered in the 83-85% range for most of the past five years, reflecting the enormous pricing power of branded biologics (especially GLP-1 receptor agonists). In 2025, gross margin slipped to 81.0%, driven by amortization and depreciation from the three former Catalent manufacturing sites acquired in late 2024 and DKK 8 billion in one-off restructuring costs related to a company-wide transformation. Stripping out those items, underlying gross margins remain strong. Operating margins tell a similar story: they expanded from roughly 42% in 2021 to 44% in 2023, then peaked near 48% in 2024 before falling to approximately 41% in 2025 on a reported basis. Adjusted for restructuring, the 2025 operating margin would have been closer to 44%. Net profit margin was 33% in 2025, down from roughly 35% in 2024. The margin trajectory from 2019 to 2024 was one of consistent expansion -- a textbook demonstration of operating leverage as GLP-1 revenue scaled. The 2025 compression reflects specific one-time items and the investment cycle rather than a fundamental deterioration in pricing power or competitive position.

**Cash Generation: Strong Engine, but Capex Is Consuming It.** Operating cash flow has been robust and growing: DKK 55B in 2021, DKK 79B in 2022, DKK 109B in 2023, DKK 121B in 2024, and DKK 119B in 2025. The business clearly generates real cash. However, free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capex) has been increasingly squeezed by the capital expenditure ramp: FCF was DKK 49B in 2021, peaked at DKK 83B in 2023, then fell to DKK 74B in 2024 and DKK 59B in 2025. On a stricter basis (the DKK 28.3B figure Novo Nordisk reports as free cash flow, which includes acquisitions and intangible investments), FCF looks even more constrained. FCF conversion (FCF / net income) on the broader definition was roughly 28% in 2025 -- a red flag by Buffett standards. Even using the narrower operating-cash-flow-minus-PP&E-capex measure, conversion was about 58%, still below the 80% healthy threshold. This is not because the business is failing to generate cash -- it is generating enormous cash. It is because the company is choosing to invest aggressively. Owner earnings (net income of DKK 102B + depreciation of DKK 22B - estimated maintenance capex of ~DKK 15-20B) are likely in the DKK 104-109B range -- strong, but increasingly eaten into by growth investment.

**Capital Intensity: Transitioning from Asset-Light to Asset-Heavy.** This is the most significant shift in Novo Nordisk's economic model. Historically, pharma companies -- especially those selling branded injectable biologics -- have been relatively asset-light. Novo Nordisk's capex was just DKK 6.3B in 2021 (4.5% of revenue). By 2025, it was DKK 60.1B (19.4% of revenue), with an additional DKK 30B in intangible asset spending. Total capital investment in 2025 was roughly 29% of revenue. This is no longer an asset-light business by any definition. Novo Nordisk is building massive manufacturing capacity for semaglutide-based products across multiple continents (North Carolina, Brazil, Europe). The 2026 capex guidance of ~DKK 55B suggests spending will remain elevated. This is a deliberate strategic choice to bring manufacturing in-house and expand capacity ahead of demand, but it fundamentally changes the capital allocation math. If demand for GLP-1s remains strong, these assets will generate excellent returns. If pricing pressure accelerates or competition intensifies, Novo will be sitting on expensive fixed assets.

**Revenue Quality: Excellent but Concentrating.** Novo Nordisk's revenue is high-quality in several respects: it is largely recurring (chronic disease patients refill prescriptions monthly), switching costs are moderate to high (patients stabilized on a therapy are reluctant to change), and the addressable market is expanding rapidly. However, revenue concentration is a concern. In 2025, Obesity care (DKK 82.3B, primarily Wegovy) and Ozempic within Diabetes care dominated growth. GLP-1 products likely account for over 65% of total revenue. Growth has been both volume-driven (new patient starts, geographic expansion) and price-driven (U.S. list prices), though U.S. pricing is now moving against the company. The 2026 guidance warning reflects this: Most Favored Nations pricing, Medicaid coverage changes, compounded semaglutide competition, and patent expiries in Brazil, Canada, and China are all eroding the pricing pillar.

**Operating Leverage: Powerful Through 2024, Now Reversing.** From 2021 to 2024, Novo Nordisk demonstrated textbook operating leverage. Revenue roughly doubled while operating profit more than doubled, meaning profits grew faster than sales. This was driven by the high-margin GLP-1 portfolio scaling over a relatively fixed cost base. In 2025, this dynamic reversed: sales grew 6% in DKK but operating profit declined 1% on a reported basis (or grew 6% adjusted). The cost structure is now scaling upward with massive D&A charges from new manufacturing assets, higher R&D spending (DKK 52B, up 8%), and restructuring. The 2026 guidance of declining sales and profits suggests operating deleverage is a near-term reality.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The DKK 60B+ annual capex cycle creates a manufacturing moat that competitors cannot easily replicate, and once investments mature, ROIC rebounds toward 35%+ as GLP-1 demand continues growing into a $150B+ global market.
- **Bear case:** U.S. pricing pressure from government action and compounded generics permanently compresses margins, semaglutide patent expiries erode the competitive position, and the massive fixed-asset base becomes a drag on returns if volume growth disappoints.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The historical economics are clearly exceptional, and the underlying cash generation engine remains powerful. But the company is in the middle of a capital-intensive transition, and the 2026 outlook introduces genuine uncertainty about the margin and return trajectory over the next 2-3 years.

## Red Flags

- Capital expenditure grew from DKK 6.3B to DKK 60.1B in four years (2021-2025) -- a 10x increase -- while ROIC has fallen from 41% to 25%. The direction matters.
- Reported FCF conversion (on Novo's own definition) was just ~28% in 2025. Even the broader measure is below 60%. This is a cash-hungry phase.
- 2026 is guided for a 5-13% decline in adjusted sales and operating profit -- the first decline in over a decade. Two top executives have also departed alongside this guidance.
- DKK 8 billion in restructuring costs in 2025 is not trivial (2.6% of sales). If restructuring becomes a recurring feature, the "adjusted" margins are overstating reality.
- Revenue concentration in semaglutide-based products (Ozempic, Wegovy) creates single-molecule risk. Patent expiries are already impacting some markets.
- The Catalent acquisition ($11B) and subsequent asset integration adds execution risk and inflates the asset base, compressing returns.

## Score: 7 / 10

Novo Nordisk's underlying economic engine -- 81%+ gross margins, 40%+ adjusted operating margins, DKK 119B in operating cash flow -- remains among the best in global pharma. The reason it does not score higher is the unmistakable downward trend in ROIC (41% to 25%), the massive and still-growing capex cycle that is consuming free cash flow, and the 2026 guidance showing the first sales and profit decline in years. This is still a very good business, but the economics are getting temporarily worse before they get better, and there is real uncertainty about how much of the historical margin and return profile is sustainable in a world of increasing pricing pressure and competition.
