## Detailed Analysis

**What does this company sell, and to whom?** TSMC is the world's largest dedicated semiconductor foundry. It does not design chips or sell products under its own brand. Instead, it manufactures chips designed by other companies — fabricating silicon wafers using photolithography and other processes, then packaging and testing them. Its customers are the biggest names in tech: NVIDIA (19% of 2025 revenue), Apple (17%), MediaTek, Qualcomm, AMD, and Broadcom, among roughly 465 total clients. These are "fabless" chip designers — companies that design semiconductors but outsource manufacturing to TSMC. The end applications span AI servers, smartphones, PCs, automotive, and IoT devices.

**How does it actually make money?** TSMC charges per wafer. Customers submit chip designs, TSMC manufactures them on silicon wafers, and bills based on the number of wafers processed, the technology node used, and any additional services like advanced packaging (CoWoS). Pricing varies dramatically by node: mature process wafers cost around $3,200 each, 3nm wafers run approximately $20,000, and upcoming 2nm wafers are expected to exceed $30,000. Large customers sign long-term capacity reservation agreements and make advance payments — TSMC has collected billions in prepayments to secure capacity. This model creates sticky, recurring revenue with significant switching costs. Once a chip is designed for TSMC's process, moving it to another foundry would take 12-18 months and hundreds of millions of dollars in redesign costs.

**What are the key drivers?** Three forces drive TSMC's economics: (1) the pace of AI infrastructure buildout, which has been the dominant growth engine, with HPC revenue growing 48% YoY in 2025; (2) technology leadership at the leading edge, where TSMC's ability to manufacture at 3nm and soon 2nm with superior yields keeps customers locked in; and (3) capacity expansion, with the company investing $40.9 billion in capex in 2025 and guiding $52-56 billion for 2026 to build out both advanced node and advanced packaging (CoWoS) capacity. The CoWoS packaging technology, critical for connecting multiple chips in AI accelerators, has been a supply bottleneck — TSMC doubled CoWoS capacity to roughly 70,000 wafers per month by end-2025 and plans to quadruple it further.

**How predictable is the revenue?** Highly predictable by semiconductor standards. TSMC's customers commit to wafer volumes well in advance through long-term agreements and prepayments. The company has delivered 20+ consecutive quarters of meeting or exceeding its own revenue guidance. Revenue visibility extends 6-12 months out due to order booking patterns. That said, TSMC is not immune to semiconductor cycles — smartphone and IoT segments can fluctuate — but the structural AI demand has added a powerful secular growth layer. Management guided for approximately 30% revenue growth in 2026, suggesting confidence in near-term visibility. Full-year 2025 gross margin of 59.9% (up 3.8 percentage points YoY) demonstrates strong pricing power and operational leverage.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?** Customer concentration is meaningful but not extreme. The top two customers — NVIDIA (19%) and Apple (17%) — together account for roughly 36% of revenue. The top seven customers likely represent 70-75% of revenue. This concentration has shifted dramatically: as recently as 2024, Apple was the clear number one at 24% of revenue, but NVIDIA's share more than doubled in a single year as AI chip orders surged. The positive side of this concentration is that these are the most financially robust companies in the world with mission-critical demand. The risk is that a slowdown in AI spending by hyperscalers could meaningfully impact TSMC's growth trajectory.

**Can you explain the business to someone in 2 minutes?** Yes, and that is one of TSMC's greatest strengths as an investment. TSMC is the factory where the world's most important chips get made. Companies like Apple, NVIDIA, and AMD design chips but cannot manufacture them — they need TSMC's factories, which are the most advanced on Earth. TSMC charges per wafer, and the more advanced the chip, the more it charges. Right now, the AI boom means unprecedented demand for TSMC's most expensive, most profitable products. The company has roughly 70% of the entire foundry market, its closest competitor (Samsung) has less than 8%, and no one else can match its manufacturing quality at the leading edge. It made $122 billion in revenue in 2025 with nearly 60% gross margins. The main risk is that almost all of this manufacturing happens on a small island 100 miles off the coast of China.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** AI infrastructure spending sustains a multi-year buildout, TSMC's technology lead widens, and geographic diversification de-risks the Taiwan concentration — resulting in $150B+ revenue by 2027 with margins expanding further.
- **Bear case:** A severe geopolitical crisis involving Taiwan disrupts production, or AI spending hits a cyclical wall, exposing TSMC to a demand cliff after years of aggressive capacity expansion.
- **Confidence:** High — TSMC's business model is among the most understandable in the semiconductor industry: it is a toll bridge for chip manufacturing with no realistic alternative at the leading edge, and its financials are transparent and well-reported.

## Red Flags

- Geographic concentration in Taiwan remains the single largest risk; 90%+ of advanced production is on an island in a geopolitically sensitive region, and diversification to Arizona, Japan, and Germany will not reach meaningful scale until 2028-2030.
- Customer concentration is shifting rapidly toward AI/NVIDIA dependence; if AI capex cycles downward, TSMC's largest revenue stream could decelerate sharply.
- Capex is accelerating aggressively ($52-56B in 2026), which is rational given demand but creates risk if the AI buildout slows before this capacity is fully utilized.
- Overseas fabs (Arizona) have historically faced cost and efficiency challenges compared to Taiwan operations, which could pressure margins as geographic diversification progresses.

## Score: 9 / 10

TSMC's business model is exceptionally clear: it is the world's dominant contract chip manufacturer, charging per wafer, with transparent financials and identifiable key drivers. The only reason this is not a 10 is the geopolitical complexity layered on top of an otherwise crystalline business model.
