## Compact Checklist

1. **Business**: Copart runs an online auction marketplace for salvage vehicles, primarily sourced from auto insurance carriers, charging fees to both sellers and buyers on every transaction without owning the inventory — a model that becomes more valuable as modern cars grow too expensive to repair and total-loss rates rise.

2. **Moat**: Copart owns 10,000+ acres of permitted salvage yards near population centers — infrastructure that took decades to assemble and cannot be replicated quickly — combined with a self-reinforcing two-sided network where more insurance supply attracts more global buyers, and higher buyer competition delivers better recovery rates that lock in insurance carriers.

3. **Economics**: Copart generates a 30%+ ROIC (5-year average ~31.9%), ~35% net margins, and $1.26B in free cash flow on $4.65B revenue in FY2025, with EPS growing ~17.5% annually over the prior 5 years — the fingerprint of a genuine compounder.

4. **Management**: Founder Willis Johnson holds ~7.72% of the company (worth ~$4B) with a compensation structure that pays him nothing unless the stock appreciates 25%+ from grant, and the company has spent decades building owned-land moats instead of paying dividends — the record reflects genuine owner-operator discipline.

5. **Debt risk**: Copart holds $5.2B in cash against $82M in capital lease obligations and zero drawn financial debt, generating $1.8B in annual operating cash flow — the company could retire every liability it has in under seven months of operations and would survive a 30% revenue decline entirely from existing cash reserves.

6. **Price**: At ~$36/share (~23x trailing P/E), the market is pricing in steady 8–9% annual earnings growth over the next decade, which is reasonable but not pessimistic — the stock is near base-case fair value, not at a deep discount, and requires the current cyclical volume weakness to be temporary rather than structural.

7. **Margin of safety**: The business-level protection is exceptional — zero financial leverage, a physical moat that cannot be replicated in under 15–20 years, and 30%+ net margins even in weak quarters — but the price-level margin of safety is thin at current levels, with the stock trading near intrinsic value rather than at a 20–30% discount to it.

8. **Thesis breaker**: Sell if two or more major insurance carriers (State Farm, Allstate, GEICO, Progressive) announce material and durable volume reductions or contract shifts away from Copart, because that would signal a structural break in the supply-side relationship that underpins the entire business model.

## Key Risks

1. **Cyclical volume decline extending into structural decline.** This is the pivotal risk. Two consecutive quarters of declining insurance unit volumes (–9% globally in Q2 FY2026) could represent a temporary soft patch — or the early signal of a structural shift driven by rising uninsured rates or declining accident frequency. The bears' strongest argument is that this decline is not weather or CAT timing; management explicitly cited "market softness" and structural insurance market dynamics. If three or more quarters of sustained unit decline follow without recovery, the thesis must be revisited with urgency. Likelihood: moderate (35% probability of two additional soft quarters).

2. **Insurance carrier supply-side concentration.** Roughly 80% of vehicle volume flows from auto insurance carriers, and the top handful of carriers likely account for 40–50% of supply. A decision by State Farm, Progressive, or GEICO to shift meaningful volume to IAA/RB Global — or to experiment with in-house salvage disposition — would have an outsized and rapid impact on Copart's economics. IAA/RB Global is actively competing for carrier relationships and market share pressure was acknowledged in Q2 FY2026 earnings. Likelihood: low-to-moderate (10–20%), but impact is severe.

3. **Thin price margin of safety at current levels.** The stock at ~$36 trades near base-case intrinsic value as estimated by the valuation analysis. The upside/downside ratio is approximately 1:1 — not the asymmetry a disciplined value investor requires. The bear-case present value ($18–22/share) implies 37–50% downside if the growth assumption proves too optimistic. Investors who initiate at current prices are not buying a discount to fair value; they are betting that the base case is correct.

4. **AV disruption to accident frequency over a 10-year horizon.** The entire business model depends on a steady or growing supply of damaged vehicles. If autonomous vehicle adoption meaningfully reduces accident frequency — the industry's structural tailwind in reverse — total-loss volumes could plateau or shrink after a decade of growth. Near-term probability is low; 10-year probability is more material and warrants monitoring.

5. **CEO transition risk and SBC opacity.** Jeff Liaw became sole CEO only in April 2024 and his personal equity stake is small (reduced by 43% sale in July 2025). The founder's influence remains strong, but Liaw's independent decision-making has not been tested through a full business cycle. Additionally, precise stock-based compensation as a percentage of revenue was not confirmed from available data and should be verified against the 10-K.

# Circle of Competence — CPRT

**Analyst Role:** Business Clarity Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-11

## Detailed Analysis

**What Copart sells and to whom.** Copart is an online marketplace that connects sellers of damaged, theft-recovery, and low-value vehicles — primarily insurance carriers — with a global network of licensed dismantlers, rebuilders, dealers, and end-consumers (in markets that allow direct purchase). The core service is receiving a vehicle from a carrier, storing it at a Copart facility, photographing and grading it, listing it on the proprietary VB3 (Virtual Bidding Third Generation) auction platform, and running a timed online auction to the highest bidder. Copart does not generally own the vehicles it sells; it acts as a fee-earning intermediary.

**How Copart makes money.** Revenue splits into two streams. Service revenues (approximately 80–85% of total) comprise auction fees charged to both sellers and buyers, plus storage, transportation, titling, and processing fees layered on top. The average buyer fee runs roughly 7–13% of the hammer price; seller-side fees are negotiated contractually with insurance carriers. Vehicle sales (15–20% of revenue) reflect cases where Copart has purchased a vehicle outright — generally lower-margin transactions. For FY2025, total revenue was $4.65 billion and gross profit $2.1 billion, implying a gross margin of approximately 45%. Net margin reached nearly 34.8% — exceptionally high for a marketplace. Because vehicles are consigned rather than owned, incremental volumes absorb minimal additional capital, so cash flow grows faster than revenue as scale increases.

**Key drivers.** Four variables explain most of the business: (1) Insurance total loss frequency — as cars become more expensive to repair (ADAS sensors, EV batteries, higher labor costs), more are declared total losses and flow into Copart's pipeline; this rate has risen from 4% in 1980 to 22.6% in FY2025 and appears structurally sticky. (2) Policies-in-force and claims activity — the number of insured vehicles and how frequently claims are filed; the Q2 FY2026 decline (insurance units down 9% globally, 10.7% in the U.S.) demonstrates the sensitivity here. (3) Average selling price (ASP) — driven by the competitiveness of the buyer pool; international buyer participation (38% value premium vs. domestic) structurally lifts ASP, and EV salvage parts are a newer tailwind. (4) Yard capacity and throughput — the physical storage network constrains how many vehicles can be accepted and processed, making continued land investment essential.

**Revenue predictability.** The business is highly recurring in character, though not contractually recurring in the way a SaaS company is. Insurance carriers sign multi-year framework agreements with Copart; volume flows continuously as claims arise. Revenue has compounded at a mid-to-high single-digit rate over the past decade with only modest cyclical variation. There is a degree of seasonality: Q4 (April–July) tends to be the strongest quarter due to higher driving activity; Q1 (August–October) and Q2 (November–January) are softer. CAT events (hurricanes, floods, hailstorms) provide lumpy one-time volume boosts that distort comparisons, as seen in Q2 FY2026 where the prior-year comparison included elevated CAT units.

**Customer concentration and composition.** The supply side is concentrated: insurance carriers — the top U.S. carriers (State Farm, Allstate, GEICO, Progressive, etc.) — supply around 80% of vehicles. No single carrier has been disclosed as >10% of revenue in public filings, but the top handful represent a meaningful share. The buy side is broadly diversified across thousands of licensed buyers in 170+ countries, which is a structural strength. Dealer, dismantler, and exporter buyers compete fiercely, generating the auction dynamics that drive recovery values for carriers.

**Two-minute explanation.** Copart is a giant online auction house for wrecked cars. An insurance company totals your car after an accident. Instead of dealing with disposal themselves, they hand it to Copart. Copart stores it, photographs it, auctions it online to the highest bidder — a dealer in Germany, a dismantler in Texas, a rebuilder in Poland — and takes a fee on every transaction. The more cars are totaled (and modern cars with expensive sensors get totaled more often), the more inventory Copart processes. The more buyers participate, the higher the prices insurers get, which makes insurers want to keep using Copart. It is a two-sided marketplace with strong network effects, owned land, and a 40% U.S. market share — simple, predictable, and hard to replicate.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Total loss frequency continues rising (EV complexity, repair inflation), driving structurally higher volumes; international ASP premium expands; Copart's owned land network blocks any meaningful competitive entry.
- **Bear case:** A sustained decline in insurance coverage (rising uninsured rate) or a shift in insurer economics compresses unit volumes faster than ASP gains can offset, as partially evidenced by the 9% insurance unit decline in Q2 FY2026.
- **Confidence:** High — The business model is transparently disclosed, revenues are well-documented in public filings, and the key drivers are straightforward to monitor.

## Red Flags

- Q2 FY2026 insurance unit volumes declined 9% globally (10.7% in the U.S.), with management citing rising uninsured rates and softer claims activity. If structural rather than transitory, this compresses the volume base.
- Revenue concentration on the supply side (insurance carriers ~80% of volume) means a change in carrier strategy or contract renegotiations could be disruptive.
- CAT-adjusted U.S. insurance unit decline of –4.8% in Q2 FY2026 suggests the underlying volume trend is softer than headline total loss frequency would imply.

## Score: 9 / 10

The business model is exceptionally clear — a fee-based two-sided marketplace with well-understood drivers, high recurrence, and a logical secular tailwind in rising total loss frequency — losing one point only because the insurance volume sensitivity (as demonstrated in recent quarters) introduces a meaningful cyclical variable that requires ongoing monitoring.

# Durable Competitive Advantage — CPRT

**Analyst Role:** Moat Analyst
**Date:** 2026-03-11
**Data Sources:** Web search results (Seeking Alpha, AInvest, Yahoo Finance/investing.com analysis pieces, Alpha Spread, StockStory, HeadCount Coffee, InvestSeekers, DCF Modeling); FY2025 and H1 FY2026 financial data from Copart IR releases. Training knowledge used for historical industry context.

## Key Findings

| # | Finding | Significance (1-5) |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | Copart owns the land under its >250 storage facilities (10,000+ acres); IAA/Ritchie Bros. leases its yards — this ownership difference compounds into an enormous cost and capacity-certainty advantage. | 5 |
| 2 | Self-reinforcing two-sided network effect: more insurers → more inventory variety → more buyer participation → higher recovery values → more insurers choose Copart. | 5 |
| 3 | Net margins of ~34–35% and gross margins of ~45% materially exceed industry averages, providing direct quantitative evidence of pricing power. | 4 |
| 4 | Copart and IAA together control ~80% of the U.S. salvage auction market; Copart alone holds ~40% — a concentrated duopoly that makes entry by a third party extremely capital-intensive. | 4 |
| 5 | International segment EBITDA margins reached an all-time high of 40.2% in Q3 FY2025 after transitioning overseas operations to U.S.-style consignment model, showing the moat is exportable. | 3 |

## Detailed Analysis

**Type of moat.** Copart's competitive advantage is multi-layered, not mono-causal. The primary sources are: (1) two-sided network effects, (2) real asset / cost advantage through owned land, (3) scale economics, and (4) switching costs embedded in long-term carrier relationships. Each layer reinforces the others.

The two-sided network effect is the most powerful. Copart's auction platform attracts buyers from 170+ countries because it consistently offers the largest and most varied inventory of salvage vehicles in one place. That buyer depth generates the highest auction clearing prices for insurers, which in turn makes insurers reluctant to switch. A new entrant with fewer buyers would deliver lower recovery rates; lower recovery rates would fail to attract insurance volume; without insurance volume, the buyer pool would not develop. Breaking this cycle would require simultaneous investment at massive scale on both sides, which explains why the industry has remained a duopoly for decades.

**Land ownership as a structural barrier.** The owned-land strategy is perhaps Copart's most durable physical advantage. Salvage yard permitting is politically and environmentally contentious; acquiring and zoning 10,000+ acres in metropolitan proximity — where vehicles need to be towed quickly after accidents — took Copart decades. IAA's leased model means it faces periodic renewal risk and cannot guarantee space availability during CAT surges. Insurance carriers, who need certainty that their vehicles will be accepted and processed regardless of peak loads, have historically preferred Copart's owned-yard model. This preference is a documented reason why carriers favor Copart for contract renewals.

**Scale economics.** As throughput increases, the fixed cost base (land, technology infrastructure, processing facilities) is spread over more vehicles. The consignment model means Copart does not tie up balance sheet capital in inventory, so incremental free cash flow generation is high. This dynamic makes it increasingly difficult for a smaller competitor to match Copart's cost structure. The owned-land asset base, while capital-intensive to build, now functions as a barrier that a new entrant would need to replicate over many years.

**Margin evidence of moat.** FY2025 data is unambiguous: 34.8% net margin, ~45% gross margin. These are extraordinary numbers for any marketplace business. Over the prior decade, margins have expanded steadily as the platform scaled and international operations transitioned to higher-margin consignment. Q3 FY2025 saw the international segment reach 40.2% EBITDA margins. The sustained and widening margin profile is the quantitative fingerprint of durable pricing power.

**Durability assessment — 10-year outlook.** The moat appears durable for multiple reasons. (1) EV proliferation is a tailwind, not a threat: EV battery repair costs are extremely high, increasing total loss frequency. (2) The land assets cannot be quickly replicated; any new entrant faces a 15–20 year build-out. (3) The global buyer network is the result of decades of relationship building with international dealers and exporters. (4) The transition to online auction (VB3) has removed the geographic boundary that used to constrain buyer participation, making the platform increasingly hard to dislodge. The primary durability risk is a structural shift in how insurance carriers process total losses — e.g., if a major carrier decided to vertically integrate auctions — but there is no evidence of this trend and the economics of doing so would be poor for carriers.

**Where the moat is weakest.** The strongest near-term vulnerability is on the supply side. Copart does not own the vehicles — insurance carriers have some ability to renegotiate or shift share to IAA. IAA (now under Ritchie Bros./RB Global) is meaningfully capitalizing on Copart's volume in some carrier relationships. The Q2 FY2026 earnings call noted market share pressure. Additionally, if total loss frequency were to reverse (e.g., if autonomous vehicles dramatically reduced accident rates), the supply of vehicles would shrink — though this scenario is likely decades away and would compress the entire market, not just Copart.

**Product cycle reset risk.** Not applicable in the traditional sense. The "product" is the auction marketplace, which does not reset on technology cycles. The VB3 platform has been iterated rather than replaced, and there is no indication that a radically new technology would displace the business model. AI-assisted vehicle grading and pricing represents an opportunity being actively invested in (per Q2 FY2026 earnings commentary) rather than a disruptive threat.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The owned-land network, global buyer pool, and rising total loss frequency compound into sustained 8–10% annual volume growth and expanding margins as scale absorbs fixed costs.
- **Bear case:** IAA gains meaningful carrier share over multiple years; the uninsured vehicle rate rises enough to reduce total claims volume; geographic permitting constraints limit Copart's ability to add capacity in high-growth markets.
- **Confidence:** High — The moat sources are well-documented, verifiable in financial results, and structurally grounded in physical assets and network dynamics that are slow to erode.

## Red Flags

- Market share pressure from IAA/RB Global is real and acknowledged in Q3 FY2025 and Q2 FY2026 earnings commentary — the duopoly is not static.
- Rising uninsured vehicle rates in the U.S. are a documented supply-side risk mentioned by management in recent earnings calls.
- The international expansion, while promising, introduces execution risk: regulatory environments, title transfer rules, and consumer protection frameworks vary significantly by country.

## Score: 9 / 10

Copart possesses a wide, multi-layered moat — owned land that cannot be quickly replicated, reinforcing two-sided network effects, sustained ~35% net margins, and a dominant duopoly position — earning a 9; the one-point deduction reflects the real but manageable supply-side competition risk from IAA and the structural sensitivity to insurance unit volumes.

## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Willis J. Johnson founded Copart in 1982 and remains the largest individual shareholder, holding approximately 74.38 million shares representing ~7.72% of the company as of available 2025 data. At recent stock prices in the $50–55 range, this represents a position worth approximately $3.7–4.1 billion — a meaningful personal fortune tied entirely to Copart's long-term performance. Jay Adair (son-in-law of Willis Johnson), who served as CEO for many years before transitioning to Executive Chairman in early 2024, also holds a material stake. The Johnson family collectively maintains alignment with shareholders that is rare among companies of this size. CEO Jeff Liaw, who became sole CEO in April 2024 after serving as Co-CEO since 2022, sold 24,088 shares in July 2025 (a 43.3% reduction in his disclosed holdings to 31,527 shares), which is worth monitoring but not alarming given that this is a small position in absolute terms relative to the founder's stake. Institutional ownership stands at approximately 85.2%, indicating broad market acceptance.

**Capital allocation track record.** For most of its modern history, Copart's capital allocation has followed three priorities in order: (1) reinvest in owned land and yard capacity to protect and extend the moat; (2) fund organic growth in international markets; (3) return surplus capital to shareholders. The company has not paid dividends, preferring to retain earnings for reinvestment. The land acquisition strategy — buying rather than leasing storage yards — has proven to be one of the most consequential capital allocation decisions in the company's history, transforming a potential cost center into a structural competitive moat. Share repurchases have been minimal historically, but there are signs of a shift: after a period with no buybacks, Copart repurchased $218.2 million of stock during H1 FY2026 (six months ended January 31, 2026), and announced a new $500M buyback authorization in early March 2026. This reflects management's view that the stock was attractively valued following its ~40% drawdown from highs. Importantly, they did not repurchase shares during Q1 FY2026 (the first quarter, ending October 2025), suggesting the decision was valuation-driven rather than reflexive.

**Compensation design.** Willis Johnson's compensation structure is notable for its alignment: a $57,500 cash retainer (same as any outside director) plus stock options that vest only if the stock trades at 125% or more of the grant price for 20 consecutive trading days. This structure has no reward unless shareholders are meaningfully better off. Jeff Liaw's total 2025 compensation of $2.07M is modest for a company of Copart's size ($60B+ market cap), and is split approximately 43% salary / 57% at-risk. The annual cash bonus is tied 60% to operating income and 40% to personal objectives. Say-on-pay approval at the 2024 annual meeting was approximately 95%, indicating strong shareholder endorsement. One area worth scrutiny is stock-based compensation (SBC) as a percentage of revenue: a precise current figure was not available in search results and should be verified in the most recent 10-K. Historical SBC at Copart has been modest relative to revenue but the share count of ~977.5M should be monitored for creep from option grants.

**Candor and consistency.** Management's communication style has historically been frank about both strengths and challenges. In Q2 FY2026, CEO Jeff Liaw was direct about the 9% decline in global insurance units and 10.7% U.S. insurance unit decline, attributing it to structural shifts in insurance policies-in-force, not just weather or seasonality. The earnings commentary also acknowledged ongoing market share pressure from IAA without minimizing it. The company does not provide formal earnings guidance, which is consistent with a long-term orientation and avoids the quarterly earnings management game. Jay Adair-era and Liaw-era earnings calls have consistently returned to first principles — volume, ASP, and return on invested capital — rather than adjusted-metric storytelling.

**Governance structure.** The board of directors includes 12 members: Willis Johnson (Executive Chairman), Jeff Liaw (CEO/Director), Jay Adair (Executive Chairman emeritus), and nine independent directors including former Missouri Governor Matt Blunt and several experienced financial/operational executives. There is no dual-class share structure; all shares carry equal voting rights, which is a governance positive. The Chairman (Johnson) and CEO (Liaw) roles are separated. The board uses an independent compensation consultant (Compensia) and updated its peer group for FY2026 benchmarking. One governance consideration: the Johnson family's combined influence (ownership stake + board presence) means the founder effectively has significant informal control despite the absence of dual-class shares.

**Do they talk like owners?** The answer is yes, with strong evidence. Willis Johnson's compensation structure is designed by someone who genuinely does not need to be paid for showing up — he is paid only if the stock appreciates materially. The consistent reinvestment in owned land over decades (forgoing higher near-term returns by holding cash or paying dividends) reflects a long-term view. The decision to begin repurchasing shares only after a significant stock drawdown — rather than mechanically throughout the cycle — demonstrates valuation discipline. The language in earnings calls focuses on long-term moat maintenance (continued land investment, AI-assisted vehicle processing, international transition to consignment model) even during periods of revenue pressure.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The founder-controlled, owner-operator culture continues to make disciplined capital allocation decisions — reinvesting at high returns when organic growth is available and returning capital only when the stock is undervalued, compounding per-share value over decades.
- **Bear case:** Leadership transition risk: with Willis Johnson as Executive Chairman and Jeff Liaw as CEO since only April 2024, the long-term stewardship track record of the current CEO alone has not yet been fully tested across a full business cycle.
- **Confidence:** High — The founder's continued ownership stake, transparent compensation structure, and consistent operating philosophy provide strong evidence of owner-operator alignment.

## Red Flags

- Jeff Liaw sold 43% of his personal holdings in July 2025, reducing his direct economic stake to just 31,527 shares — a small position relative to his role. This is worth monitoring, though the founder's 7.72% stake provides the primary alignment.
- Absence of a dividend and historically minimal buybacks, while consistent with the reinvestment philosophy, means shareholders depend entirely on management's capital allocation judgment for returns beyond price appreciation.
- Precise SBC as a percentage of revenue not confirmed from available search results; should be verified against the 10-K to ensure dilution is not material.

## Score: 8 / 10

Copart's management culture is genuinely owner-oriented — the founder's enormous stake, the unusual compensation structure for Willis Johnson, and decades of disciplined reinvestment in moat-building assets all reflect outstanding stewardship; the score falls short of a 9 primarily because CEO Jeff Liaw's tenure is still short (sole CEO since April 2024), the CEO's personal stock position is small, and the first test of capital allocation under earnings pressure (H1 FY2026 revenue decline) is just beginning.

# Business Economics — CPRT

## Detailed Analysis
