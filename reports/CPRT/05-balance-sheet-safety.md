## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure**

Copart's balance sheet is one of the most conservative of any large-cap US company. Total financial debt at the most recent quarter end (October 31, 2025) is approximately $82M, consisting entirely of capital lease obligations — there is no drawn bank debt or public bonds outstanding. The debt-to-equity ratio rounds to 0%. Total liabilities are approximately $961.9M against total shareholders' equity of approximately $9.6B, giving an implied equity-to-assets ratio of ~90%. For context, the $82M in lease/debt obligations is smaller than what Copart earns in a single week of operations.

The company recently secured a new $1.25B unsecured revolving credit facility maturing January 2031, simultaneously retiring its prior facility. The new facility is unsecured, underscoring the financial community's confidence in Copart's covenant-free credit profile. Sub-facilities cover international subsidiaries (UK, Germany, Spain, Canada), and there is a $500M incremental accordion feature. None of this has been drawn.

**Interest Coverage**

With essentially no interest expense on outstanding debt, the traditional EBIT/interest expense metric is not constraining in the conventional sense. Interest income — earned on the $5B+ cash pile — is a positive contributor to the income statement, not a drain. The GuruFocus interest coverage figure is not meaningful in isolation because the company is a net receiver of interest, not a payer. There is no interest coverage risk.

**Liquidity**

Cash, cash equivalents, and restricted cash totaled $5.2B as of October 31, 2025, up from $2.78B as of July 31, 2025 — a $2.4B increase in a single quarter, reflecting the FCF generation profile and potential timing of capital deployment. The current ratio — calculated as $5.75B in current assets divided by $683M in current liabilities — exceeds 8x. Short-term assets of $6.2B exceed short-term liabilities of $781M by a factor of nearly 8. The company could fund all operational expenses and capital needs for multiple years with zero revenue from its existing cash position alone. There is no refinancing risk, no covenant risk, and no material dependence on capital markets.

**Refinancing Risk**

There is no meaningful refinancing risk. The only formal debt maturity is the 2031 revolving facility (undrawn). Copart generates $1.8B in operating cash flow per year against total liabilities of less than $1B — the entire liability stack could be retired in under 7 months of operating cash flow even under a highly conservative scenario.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations**

Operating leases for yards and equipment are consolidated under ASC 842 and are reflected in the liabilities figure. Specific operating lease liability totals were not independently verified in this search, but the total liabilities figure of $961.9M includes all such obligations. Copart has no pension obligations (it is not a legacy industrial company) and no meaningful contingent liabilities were flagged in recent SEC filings. The company does have committed real estate purchases as part of its ongoing yard expansion program, which represent forward capital commitments but are discretionary and self-funded from operating cash flow.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Decline for 2 Years**

In FY2025, Copart generated $4.65B in revenue and $1.83B in operating cash flow. A 30% revenue decline would reduce revenue to approximately $3.25B. Assuming operating margins compress proportionately (worst case), operating cash flow might fall to roughly $1.0–1.2B. Against total liabilities of $961.9M (and financial debt of only $82M), even a severe two-year revenue stress scenario would be manageable entirely from existing cash reserves — the company holds more cash ($5.2B) than all annual revenue under the stress scenario ($3.25B). There would be no equity issuance requirement, no covenant breach, and no liquidity crisis. The company would remain solidly profitable even at 30% reduced revenue given the high fixed-cost coverage provided by its asset-light, fee-heavy model.

**Capital Structure Philosophy**

Copart's management has historically maintained a conservatively capitalized balance sheet, preferring to accumulate cash rather than lever up for buybacks or dividends. This restraint has occasionally drawn criticism from investors seeking capital returns, but from a credit/safety perspective it is optimal. The company completed $2.4B in share repurchases in FY2025 while still growing its cash balance — funded entirely from operations, not debt.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Copart's fortress balance sheet ($5.2B net cash, zero financial debt, $1.25B undrawn revolver) eliminates all financial risk and positions the company to be highly opportunistic in downturns — either through acquisitions, accelerated buybacks, or simply surviving while competitors struggle.
- **Bear case:** The large cash hoard may signal a lack of high-return investment opportunities; idle cash earning money market rates is a drag on ROIC and equity returns relative to the company's underlying operational excellence.
- **Confidence:** High — the balance sheet data is drawn directly from Q1 FY2026 earnings release (October 2025) and FY2025 10-K; the figures are consistent across multiple independent sources.

## Red Flags

- None identified from a financial risk perspective.
- The large cash balance ($5.2B) is not a risk, but the opportunity cost of deploying it suboptimally (e.g., large acquisition at a premium) could be a future concern — minor and speculative.

## Score: 10 / 10

Copart earns a perfect 10 for its fortress balance sheet: $5.2B in net cash, $82M in capital lease obligations, zero drawn financial debt, a current ratio above 8x, and sufficient liquidity to survive multiple years of zero revenue — one of the safest balance sheets among large-cap US companies of comparable size.
