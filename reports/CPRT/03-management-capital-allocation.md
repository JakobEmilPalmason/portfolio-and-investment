## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Willis J. Johnson founded Copart in 1982 and remains the largest individual shareholder, holding approximately 74.38 million shares representing ~7.72% of the company as of available 2025 data. At recent stock prices in the $50–55 range, this represents a position worth approximately $3.7–4.1 billion — a meaningful personal fortune tied entirely to Copart's long-term performance. Jay Adair (son-in-law of Willis Johnson), who served as CEO for many years before transitioning to Executive Chairman in early 2024, also holds a material stake. The Johnson family collectively maintains alignment with shareholders that is rare among companies of this size. CEO Jeff Liaw, who became sole CEO in April 2024 after serving as Co-CEO since 2022, sold 24,088 shares in July 2025 (a 43.3% reduction in his disclosed holdings to 31,527 shares), which is worth monitoring but not alarming given that this is a small position in absolute terms relative to the founder's stake. Institutional ownership stands at approximately 85.2%, indicating broad market acceptance.

**Capital allocation track record.** For most of its modern history, Copart's capital allocation has followed three priorities in order: (1) reinvest in owned land and yard capacity to protect and extend the moat; (2) fund organic growth in international markets; (3) return surplus capital to shareholders. The company has not paid dividends, preferring to retain earnings for reinvestment. The land acquisition strategy — buying rather than leasing storage yards — has proven to be one of the most consequential capital allocation decisions in the company's history, transforming a potential cost center into a structural competitive moat. Share repurchases have been minimal historically, but there are signs of a shift: after a period with no buybacks, Copart repurchased $218.2 million of stock during H1 FY2026 (six months ended January 31, 2026), and announced a new $500M buyback authorization in early March 2026. This reflects management's view that the stock was attractively valued following its ~40% drawdown from highs. Importantly, they did not repurchase shares during Q1 FY2026 (the first quarter, ending October 2025), suggesting the decision was valuation-driven rather than reflexive.

**Compensation design.** Willis Johnson's compensation structure is notable for its alignment: a $57,500 cash retainer (same as any outside director) plus stock options that vest only if the stock trades at 125% or more of the grant price for 20 consecutive trading days. This structure has no reward unless shareholders are meaningfully better off. Jeff Liaw's total 2025 compensation of $2.07M is modest for a company of Copart's size ($60B+ market cap), and is split approximately 43% salary / 57% at-risk. The annual cash bonus is tied 60% to operating income and 40% to personal objectives. Say-on-pay approval at the 2024 annual meeting was approximately 95%, indicating strong shareholder endorsement. One area worth scrutiny is stock-based compensation (SBC) as a percentage of revenue: a precise current figure was not available in search results and should be verified in the most recent 10-K. Historical SBC at Copart has been modest relative to revenue but the share count of ~977.5M should be monitored for creep from option grants.

**Candor and consistency.** Management's communication style has historically been frank about both strengths and challenges. In Q2 FY2026, CEO Jeff Liaw was direct about the 9% decline in global insurance units and 10.7% U.S. insurance unit decline, attributing it to structural shifts in insurance policies-in-force, not just weather or seasonality. The earnings commentary also acknowledged ongoing market share pressure from IAA without minimizing it. The company does not provide formal earnings guidance, which is consistent with a long-term orientation and avoids the quarterly earnings management game. Jay Adair-era and Liaw-era earnings calls have consistently returned to first principles — volume, ASP, and return on invested capital — rather than adjusted-metric storytelling.

**Governance structure.** The board of directors includes 12 members: Willis Johnson (Executive Chairman), Jeff Liaw (CEO/Director), Jay Adair (Executive Chairman emeritus), and nine independent directors including former Missouri Governor Matt Blunt and several experienced financial/operational executives. There is no dual-class share structure; all shares carry equal voting rights, which is a governance positive. The Chairman (Johnson) and CEO (Liaw) roles are separated. The board uses an independent compensation consultant (Compensia) and updated its peer group for FY2026 benchmarking. One governance consideration: the Johnson family's combined influence (ownership stake + board presence) means the founder effectively has significant informal control despite the absence of dual-class shares.

**Do they talk like owners?** The answer is yes, with strong evidence. Willis Johnson's compensation structure is designed by someone who genuinely does not need to be paid for showing up — he is paid only if the stock appreciates materially. The consistent reinvestment in owned land over decades (forgoing higher near-term returns by holding cash or paying dividends) reflects a long-term view. The decision to begin repurchasing shares only after a significant stock drawdown — rather than mechanically throughout the cycle — demonstrates valuation discipline. The language in earnings calls focuses on long-term moat maintenance (continued land investment, AI-assisted vehicle processing, international transition to consignment model) even during periods of revenue pressure.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The founder-controlled, owner-operator culture continues to make disciplined capital allocation decisions — reinvesting at high returns when organic growth is available and returning capital only when the stock is undervalued, compounding per-share value over decades.
- **Bear case:** Leadership transition risk: with Willis Johnson as Executive Chairman and Jeff Liaw as CEO since only April 2024, the long-term stewardship track record of the current CEO alone has not yet been fully tested across a full business cycle.
- **Confidence:** High — The founder's continued ownership stake, transparent compensation structure, and consistent operating philosophy provide strong evidence of owner-operator alignment.

## Red Flags

- Jeff Liaw sold 43% of his personal holdings in July 2025, reducing his direct economic stake to just 31,527 shares — a small position relative to his role. This is worth monitoring, though the founder's 7.72% stake provides the primary alignment.
- Absence of a dividend and historically minimal buybacks, while consistent with the reinvestment philosophy, means shareholders depend entirely on management's capital allocation judgment for returns beyond price appreciation.
- Precise SBC as a percentage of revenue not confirmed from available search results; should be verified against the 10-K to ensure dilution is not material.

## Score: 8 / 10

Copart's management culture is genuinely owner-oriented — the founder's enormous stake, the unusual compensation structure for Willis Johnson, and decades of disciplined reinvestment in moat-building assets all reflect outstanding stewardship; the score falls short of a 9 primarily because CEO Jeff Liaw's tenure is still short (sole CEO since April 2024), the CEO's personal stock position is small, and the first test of capital allocation under earnings pressure (H1 FY2026 revenue decline) is just beginning.
