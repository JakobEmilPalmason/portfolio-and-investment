## Detailed Analysis

**What does this company sell, and to whom?** A.P. Moller-Maersk is the world's second-largest container shipping company. Its core business is moving standardized shipping containers for manufacturers, retailers, and commodity traders between ports around the world. In plain terms: if a factory in China needs to get goods to a distribution center in Europe or the United States, Maersk can pick up the cargo, load it onto one of its 700+ vessels, and deliver it to the port. Beyond ocean transport, Maersk now also offers customers warehousing, air freight (via Senator International), customs brokerage, inland trucking, and final-mile delivery. The target customer is a large multinational shipper (a retailer, an auto manufacturer, a consumer goods company) who wants to hand off their entire physical supply chain to a single vendor rather than coordinating between a shipping line, a freight forwarder, and a dozen logistics providers.

**How does it actually make money?** The revenue model has three tiers. First, Ocean: Maersk charges per-container (TEU) freight rates for moving goods across trade lanes. Some rates are set annually under long-term contracts; others float with spot market conditions. This is the majority of revenue and profit. Second, Terminals: APM Terminals operates 53 terminals in 28 countries and charges port handling fees to shipping lines (including Maersk's own vessels) and cargo owners. Third, Logistics & Services: Maersk charges fees for warehousing, air freight, customs processing, last-mile delivery, and supply chain management. The logistics division is growing but currently produces thin margins — approximately 4.9% EBIT in FY2025 — well below what a pure logistics integrator like DSV or Kuehne+Nagel earns.

**Key drivers.** Two variables account for the majority of earnings variance: (1) container freight rates, which fluctuate with global trade volumes, fuel costs, fleet supply, and geopolitical disruptions such as the Red Sea conflict; and (2) global container trade volume growth, historically tracking roughly 1–1.5x global GDP. Secondary drivers include vessel utilization (fleet capacity vs. demand), fuel costs (bunker fuel prices), and the speed of the logistics business scaling to margin targets. The Red Sea disruptions in 2024 artificially inflated freight rates by forcing vessels on longer routes around the Cape of Good Hope, boosting revenue; as routes normalize, that tailwind reverses.

**Revenue predictability.** This business is materially cyclical and should be understood as such. Container freight rates can move 80–90% in a single year — the 2020–2022 pandemic surge saw rates rise 5–10x before collapsing again. Maersk mitigates this through annual contracts (71% of long-haul volumes), but contracts reset annually and follow spot rates with a lag. The Logistics & Services segment is somewhat more stable but is growing from a small base. Terminals revenue is the most predictable (volume-based port fees), but it is the smallest segment. Seasonal patterns exist — peak season for retail imports is Q3 before the holiday season — but they are overwhelmed by macro/geopolitical cyclicality.

**Customers.** Maersk's customers are primarily large beneficial cargo owners (BCOs) — multinational manufacturers, retailers, and commodity traders — as well as freight forwarders who act as intermediaries. The company's largest 200 Ocean customers have a high penetration rate into Logistics & Services, suggesting meaningful customer depth. No single customer is publicly disclosed as representing more than a small fraction of total revenue. The customer base is diversified by geography, industry, and trade lane, which limits concentration risk. However, as a B2B commodity service, customer relationships are transactional in many cases and switching between carriers is meaningful, particularly for spot cargo.

**Can you explain the business in 2 minutes?** The Ocean and Terminals businesses are straightforward: Maersk moves boxes on ships and handles them at ports. The complexity — and the honest difficulty for investors — is the ongoing transformation into a logistics integrator. The question of whether Maersk can generate sustainable, above-commodity margins from logistics (competing directly with DSV, Kuehne+Nagel, and Flexport) has not yet been answered definitively after eight years of investment and approximately USD 8 billion in acquisitions. The core shipping business is explainable to a teenager. The transformed business is more complex and the outcome is genuinely uncertain.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Maersk successfully becomes the dominant end-to-end logistics integrator for global multinationals, pushing Logistics & Services margins above 6% while the Ocean business generates stable mid-cycle cash flows, creating a more durable and less cyclical earnings stream than pure shipping.
- **Bear case:** The logistics integration strategy fails to reach target margins, capital is destroyed on M&A, and the Ocean business continues to face structural overcapacity and rate compression as MSC, CMA CGM, and COSCO grow aggressively.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the Ocean business is well understood; the logistics transformation outcome is genuinely uncertain and depends on strategic execution over several years.

## Red Flags

- Container freight rates are volatile commodity prices outside management's control; EBIT swung from USD 6.5 billion (FY2024) to USD 3.5 billion (FY2025) in a single year.
- The logistics transformation has consumed roughly USD 8 billion in acquisitions since 2020 but Logistics & Services EBIT margin of 4.9% in FY2025 still undershoots the 6% target and is far below what pure-play logistics firms earn.
- Organic revenue growth in Logistics & Services was approximately 3.2% in the trailing 12 months of 2025, well below the stated 10% target — raising questions about whether scale is being captured.
- The company is materially harder to model and value today than it was before the diversification strategy, introducing model risk for investors.

## Score: 6 / 10

The Ocean business is straightforward to understand, but the ongoing integrator transformation, multi-segment complexity, and extreme earnings cyclicality make this a moderate-effort business to track — understandable with study but requiring conviction about a strategic bet that has yet to prove itself.
