## Detailed Analysis

**Type of moat.** Maersk's competitive position rests on three partially overlapping advantages, none of which individually constitutes a wide, durable moat in the classic Buffett sense. The first is scale economics: operating 707 vessels with 4.3 million TEU of capacity requires enormous fixed costs that most entrants cannot replicate. The capital investment required to match Maersk's fleet size runs into the tens of billions. The second is infrastructure control: APM Terminals' 60 facilities in 28 countries, including key gateway ports, give Maersk priority berth access and handling efficiency that adds genuine logistical advantage. The third — and still developing — is integration switching costs: a shipper who uses Maersk for ocean, warehousing, customs, and last-mile delivery on a single contract faces meaningful operational disruption and switching friction. However, this third moat only applies to customers who have fully adopted the integrated offering, and mass adoption has not yet occurred.

**How durable is the moat?** The ocean shipping moat is narrow and under structural pressure. Container shipping has exhibited persistent features of a commodity industry: excess capacity gets built during boom years (as happened after 2021), rates collapse, and all carriers suffer together. Five carriers — MSC, CMA CGM, COSCO, Ocean Network Express, and Evergreen — now have orderbooks larger than Maersk's 442,374 TEU orderbook, suggesting further competitive capacity will arrive. Maersk has explicitly capped its fleet at 4.1–4.3 million TEU, meaning it is deliberately conceding scale leadership to focus on logistics integration. The terminal infrastructure moat is more durable — a 10+ year competitive advantage — because land at major ports is finite and governments are reluctant to grant new terminal licenses. The integrated logistics moat, if achieved, could provide 10-year durability through switching costs, but it is not yet proven.

**Evidence in the numbers.** Margins are the clearest evidence. In FY2024, Ocean EBIT margin was 12.7% and Terminals EBIT margins were at record highs. These are solid absolute numbers but they are cyclically elevated — Maersk's 2023 reported a sharp earnings decline from 2022 peaks, and FY2025 saw EBIT fall to USD 3.5 billion from USD 6.5 billion in FY2024. A truly wide-moat business does not see EBIT halve in a single year. Pricing power in Ocean is largely absent: Maersk is a price-taker on spot rates set by the market. Logistics margins (4.9% EBIT in 2025) are thin and below logistics industry peers. Terminals is the most moat-like segment — steady, volume-driven, with high barriers to new competition.

**Schedule reliability as a differentiator.** The Gemini Cooperation with Hapag-Lloyd is a recent and meaningful development. Achieving 93.2% schedule reliability by June 2025 — versus an industry average roughly 35 percentage points lower — creates a real product advantage for customers who value supply chain predictability over pure price. Premium customers in pharmaceuticals, automotive, and retail are willing to pay for reliability. This is a nascent but genuine moat-building element that deserves monitoring.

**Where is the moat weakest?** The moat is weakest in the Ocean business itself, particularly for spot cargo and freight forwarder customers who switch carriers freely based on rate differentials. The moat is also weak in Logistics & Services, where Maersk competes against entrenched specialists (DSV, Kuehne+Nagel, DHL) who have decades of customer relationships and better logistics-native margins. Maersk's logistics business is subscale relative to its stated ambition, and after USD 8 billion of acquisitions, margins remain below target.

**Do advantages reset every cycle?** Partially. Fleet capacity advantage resets whenever competitors order ships. The alliance architecture resets when alliances restructure (as THE Alliance and Ocean Alliance did in 2025). Terminal concessions are multi-decade and do not reset. Integration relationships with anchor customers build over time and accumulate. The technology platform (Maersk's digital booking and supply chain visibility tools) requires ongoing investment to stay competitive.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The Gemini Cooperation's reliability advantage attracts premium shippers willing to pay above-market rates, the logistics integration strategy reaches scale and generates 6%+ EBIT margins, and Terminals continues to compound at record returns — together creating a business that earns through-cycle returns materially above its cost of capital.
- **Bear case:** Container shipping remains a commodity with no sustainable pricing power, the logistics strategy fails to differentiate from pure-play competitors, and continued overcapacity from aggressive competitor fleet expansion permanently compresses Ocean margins below mid-cycle levels.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the shipping moat is observable but narrow; the logistics moat is a thesis not yet proven by the numbers.

## Red Flags

- Maersk has lost global market share in container capacity for five consecutive years (from 19.4% in 2018 to ~14.2% in 2025), suggesting structural competitive pressure from better-capitalized or more aggressive rivals.
- EBIT halved from FY2024 to FY2025, consistent with a commodity business with no genuine pricing power in freight rates.
- Logistics & Services EBIT margin of 4.9% in FY2025 remains below the 6% target and is far below the ~8–12% EBIT margins that leading logistics specialists (DSV, Kuehne+Nagel) regularly produce.
- The decision to cap fleet size at 4.1–4.3 million TEU while MSC, CMA CGM, and COSCO continue expanding creates asymmetric risk: if logistics integration fails, Maersk has surrendered scale leadership without building the replacement moat.
- Pure container shipping is a capital-intensive business with no structural barriers to supply growth — any carrier with access to capital can order new ships.

## Score: 5 / 10

Maersk has real but fragile advantages — scale in terminals, a nascent integration ecosystem, and a new reliability edge via Gemini — but the core Ocean business is fundamentally a commodity, market share has declined for five straight years, and the logistics moat is not yet proven in the margin data.
