## Detailed Analysis

**Holding Period Suitability.** TSMC is one of the rare businesses where compounding alone justifies a multi-decade holding period. Revenue grew ~36% in 2025 and is guided to grow ~30% in 2026. CEO C.C. Wei has guided for approximately 25% long-term revenue CAGR in USD through 2029, with AI accelerator revenue growing at a mid-to-high 50% CAGR over the same period. The technology roadmap extends clearly through N2 (volume production started Q4 2025), N2P and A16 (both second half 2026), with capacity sold out years in advance. This is not a thesis dependent on a single catalyst or a short-term earnings beat. The moat is widening: TSMC holds roughly 72% market share in advanced foundry, and nobody else can manufacture at the leading edge at comparable volume and yield. Samsung and Intel are years behind. The massive capex ($52-56B in 2026) would be alarming in most industries, but TSMC has a track record of disciplined capital allocation -- spending only when backed by long-term customer commitments. The dividend was raised 28% in 2026. This is a business you could own for 10-20 years and let compounding do the work.

**Volatility Tolerance.** This is where TSM demands honest self-assessment. The stock dropped roughly 42% in 2022 (peak to trough, bottoming in October 2022). More recently, TSM hit a 52-week low of $134.25 in April 2025 before surging to a high of $390.21 -- nearly a 3x move in under a year. The beta is 1.27, meaning TSM is meaningfully more volatile than the market. The ADR structure adds a layer: TSM trades in US dollars but the underlying business reports in New Taiwan dollars, creating currency exposure (TWD/USD). Critically, much of TSM's volatility is not business volatility -- it is geopolitical sentiment and macro fear. The 2022 drawdown happened while TSMC's revenue was still growing. The April 2025 low likely reflected broader market fear, not a fundamental deterioration. A Buffett-style investor needs to be able to buy into a 30-40% drawdown with full conviction that the business is intact. If you cannot stomach seeing a $100K position become $60K while the business keeps posting record revenues, TSM is not for you.

**Catalysts.** Multiple catalysts exist, most within a 6-24 month window: (1) Arizona Fab 21 reaching 2nm production capability by late 2026, which could partially de-risk the geopolitical narrative and attract risk-averse institutional capital; (2) continued CoWoS advanced packaging capacity expansion from 75K wafers/month (end 2025) to 130K wafers/month (end 2026), which directly enables AI chip revenue growth; (3) the AI infrastructure buildout itself -- NVIDIA has secured over 60% of TSMC's 2026 CoWoS capacity, and AI accelerator demand shows no signs of slowing; (4) potential geopolitical de-escalation across the Taiwan Strait, which would remove the single largest discount the market applies to TSM. The risk: most of these catalysts are partially or fully outside TSMC's control. The AI cycle could slow. Geopolitical tensions could escalate. The Arizona fab could face further delays or cost overruns. Catalyst dependency is not the primary reason to own TSMC -- the compounding is -- but catalysts could unlock significant multiple expansion.

**Position Sizing Logic.** The central tension for any TSM position is this: the business quality justifies a large position (5-10% of portfolio), but the geopolitical tail risk argues for moderation (2-5%). Buffett himself answered this question when he sold: he acknowledged TSMC was a fantastic business but could not get comfortable with the Taiwan risk at a large position size. A reasonable framework: start at 3-4% of portfolio and scale to 5-6% on significant pullbacks (20%+ from highs) if the business thesis remains intact. Going above 7-8% exposes you to a scenario where a Taiwan crisis could inflict severe, potentially permanent portfolio damage. The current forward P/E of ~26x is not cheap enough to justify aggressive sizing -- you are not getting a fat pitch on valuation. But with a PEG of 0.64, you are not overpaying for growth either. The 0.65% dividend yield provides negligible income support during drawdowns.

**Correlation and Portfolio Overlap.** TSM is highly correlated with the semiconductor complex -- NVDA, ASML, AMD, and broad semiconductor ETFs like SMH all move together during risk-on/risk-off cycles. Owning TSM alongside large NVDA or ASML positions means you are making concentrated bets on the same AI infrastructure theme. TSM adds geographic/currency diversification (Taiwan/TWD exposure), but this is a double-edged sword given geopolitical risk. If you already own NVDA, buying TSM is not diversification -- it is doubling down on the same structural trend through a different link in the supply chain. Be honest about total semiconductor exposure across the portfolio. TSM also carries emerging market / Asia-Pacific factor exposure that most US-centric tech portfolios lack.

**Process Discipline.** Entry plan: TSM at a forward P/E below 20x (roughly $260 or below at current earnings estimates) would represent a clear buy with a meaningful margin of safety. At current levels (~26x forward), it is fairly valued -- not a screaming buy, not overpriced. A 20-25% pullback from current levels ($255-$270) would be an attractive entry for adding to a position. Sell rules: (1) thesis break -- if TSMC loses its technology lead (Samsung or Intel closes the gap meaningfully at leading edge), sell; (2) geopolitical escalation -- if a Taiwan crisis moves from theoretical to imminent (military mobilization, blockade), reduce immediately regardless of price; (3) valuation extreme -- if forward P/E exceeds 35-40x without a corresponding acceleration in growth, trim; (4) customer concentration risk materializes -- if AI spending collapses and TSMC's HPC revenue drops significantly. Review cadence: quarterly, timed to TSMC's earnings reports (January, April, July, October). Between earnings, monitor monthly revenue reports (TSMC uniquely discloses monthly revenue) and geopolitical developments. Do not react to daily stock price noise.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** AI infrastructure spending sustains a multi-year supercycle, TSMC's technology lead widens further with N2/A16 ramp, Arizona fabs reduce geopolitical discount, and the stock re-rates to 30-35x forward earnings -- implying $400-$470 within 12-18 months.
- **Bear case:** Taiwan Strait crisis escalates to the point of supply disruption or military confrontation, AI spending hits a cyclical wall, and the stock revisits $150-$200 -- a 40-50% drawdown from current levels with uncertain recovery.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The business quality is high-confidence (exceptional), but the geopolitical tail risk is genuinely unhedgeable and could overwhelm all fundamental analysis. The AI demand trajectory also has meaningful uncertainty beyond 2027.

## Red Flags

- Taiwan Strait geopolitical risk remains the single largest unhedgeable risk. No amount of fundamental analysis eliminates the possibility of a severe adverse event.
- Buffett -- the archetype of patient, long-term investing -- could not hold this stock for even six months due to this risk. That signal deserves serious weight.
- The stock's 52-week range of $134 to $390 (nearly 3x) indicates a level of volatility that can test even disciplined investors.
- Customer concentration is increasing toward AI/HPC (55% of Q4 revenue), making TSMC more cyclically exposed to AI spending trends than its historical smartphone-era diversification.
- Arizona fabs are progressing but Apple has stated its most advanced chips will continue to be made in Taiwan for the foreseeable future -- geographic de-risking is slower than headlines suggest.
- Trailing P/E of ~34x is 53% above the 10-year average, meaning any growth disappointment will be punished severely.

## Score: 6 / 10

TSMC is an exceptional business that presents a genuinely difficult temperament challenge. The compounding story is real and durable -- this is a 10-year+ hold on business merits alone. But the geopolitical tail risk is not theoretical, the volatility is extreme (42% drawdown in 2022, 3x range in the past year), and even Warren Buffett decided the risk-reward at portfolio scale was not worth it. A disciplined investor can own this at moderate size (3-5% of portfolio) with clear entry/exit rules and quarterly review, but it requires the emotional fortitude to hold through 30-40% drawdowns without business deterioration. That honest assessment lands this at a 6: a good fit for patient investors who can size appropriately, but not the easy, sleep-well-at-night hold that a truly great temperament match would be.
