## Detailed Analysis

**Return on Invested Capital.** DSV's ROIC trajectory tells the story of a company moving through multiple phases. During the 2021–2022 freight supercycle, ROIC before tax (excluding goodwill) reached approximately 19.7% in mid-2021 and settled around 14.6% for full-year 2022. The normalisation of freight rates in 2023–2024 pushed ROIC (including goodwill and customer relationships) down to roughly 15.9% for the 12 months ended June 2024, and then to 11.0% for the 12 months ended June 2025. On a trailing-twelve-month basis that fully captures the Schenker acquisition (completed April 30, 2025), ROIC has compressed further to approximately 6%, reflecting the step-up in the goodwill base (~DKK 75–80bn in new goodwill from Schenker) and the partial-year earnings contribution. Management explicitly targets restoring returns as synergies accumulate; DKK 4,000m+ in incremental synergies are guided for 2026 alone, against a total synergy target of DKK 8,000–10,000m at run-rate. The structural question is whether DSV can achieve 12–15% ROIC on a post-Schenker invested capital base — achievable, but not certain, and likely to take 3–5 years.

**Margins.** DSV operates as a freight forwarder-integrator and therefore its gross profit (net revenue after direct transport costs) is the more meaningful "gross margin" proxy. Reported gross margins on total revenue have historically been in the 20–21% range (2020: ~20.2%; 2021: ~20.0%; 2022: ~20.7%; 2023: ~20.6%). EBIT margins (before special items) on total revenue improved materially from ~4.6% in 2020 to a peak of ~11.8% in 2023, before declining to 9.6% in 2024 due to softening freight rates. In 2025, with Schenker revenue consolidated, EBIT before special items reached DKK 19,611m on DKK 247.3bn revenue — an EBIT margin of approximately 7.9%. This apparent compression is largely mechanical: Schenker has lower margins at the point of acquisition, and the Road and Contract Logistics divisions (which Schenker adds significantly to) carry structurally lower EBIT margins than Air & Sea. The 2026 guidance mid-point of DKK 24,250m EBIT on expected revenue suggests management believes it can expand EBIT margins above 10% on a combined basis within 12 months.

**Free Cash Flow and FCF Conversion.** Adjusted FCF of DKK 5,550m in 2024 compared with a net income attributable to shareholders of approximately DKK 9,000–10,000m (implied by DKK 51.6 diluted adjusted EPS on a roughly 175–180m diluted share count post-equity raise) yields an FCF conversion rate of approximately 55–60% for 2024. This below-average conversion reflected working capital build from rising freight rates and elevated DSPs. In 2025, adjusted FCF leapt to DKK 16,335m, materially exceeding the DKK 10,000–11,000m of adjusted net income implied by DKK 50.9 EPS on ~215m diluted shares, suggesting FCF conversion above 100% — a function of D&A (substantial given Schenker amortization) significantly exceeding maintenance capex. This pattern is characteristic of freight forwarding businesses post-large acquisition.

**Owner Earnings and Capital Intensity.** DSV's capex requirement is structurally low. Prior to the Schenker acquisition, the company guided net capex at approximately 1% of revenue. On the enlarged business, expected annual capex is ~DKK 5bn, representing approximately 2% of DKK 247.3bn revenue in 2025 — still very modest. The business does not own aircraft, ships, or substantial fixed infrastructure; it leases capacity from carriers and owns primarily IT systems, some terminals, and warehouses (in the Contract Logistics segment). This asset-light model means owner earnings (net income + D&A - maintenance capex) are likely higher than reported net income in absolute terms, as D&A from acquired intangibles substantially exceeds true economic wear on the asset base.

**Revenue Quality and Operating Leverage.** Freight forwarding revenue is volume-driven and inherently cyclical — it is not recurring in the subscription sense, but the underlying customer relationships tend to be sticky and multi-year contracted. Approximately 55% of the pro-forma 2025 revenue mix comes from Air & Sea (the highest-margin segment), with Road and Contract Logistics contributing the remainder following the Schenker add. DSV has historically demonstrated operating leverage: during the 2021–2023 freight boom, EBIT grew far faster than revenue, and the ongoing integration of Schenker is expected to deliver substantial fixed-cost synergies as two global networks are merged. Management cites incremental synergies of at least DKK 4,000m in 2026, rising toward DKK 8,000–10,000m at run-rate — largely from network consolidation, back-office rationalisation, and procurement benefits.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Schenker integration delivers DKK 8–10bn of annual synergies by 2027–2028, ROIC recovers to 12–15%, and DSV becomes a structurally higher-margin business than either standalone entity — rewarding patient shareholders who bought through the acquisition noise.
- **Bear case:** Freight rates remain depressed (as in 2023–2024), Schenker integration is slower and costlier than advertised, and the goodwill-laden balance sheet permanently suppresses ROIC below 10%, making this a mediocre capital allocator disguised by acquisition accounting.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The asset-light model and track record of M&A integration (Panalpina in 2019, Agility GIL in 2021) support the bull case, but the Schenker deal is by far the largest test, and 2025 data are only partial-year.

---

## Red Flags

- ROIC on a post-acquisition invested capital basis has compressed to ~6% TTM — below the company's cost of capital and well below the 15–20% levels that historically justified DSV's premium valuation
- EPS was essentially flat in 2025 vs. 2024 (DKK 50.9 vs. 51.6) despite a 51% revenue increase, reflecting substantial dilution from the ~50% equity raise and elevated integration/amortisation costs
- Schenker is structurally less profitable than DSV's legacy Air & Sea business; the Road and Contract Logistics divisions carry lower inherent margins, and investors should not expect pre-Schenker margin profiles to return
- Special items of DKK 4,527m in 2025 (transaction and integration costs) are large enough to materially distort reported profitability for 2–3 years

---

## Score: 6 / 10

DSV has the right model — asset-light, capital-efficient freight forwarding with proven integration skills — but the Schenker acquisition has temporarily inflated the capital base and suppressed ROIC to below-cost-of-capital levels; the score reflects current economics rather than the 3-year potential.
