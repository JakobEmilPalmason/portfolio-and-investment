## Detailed Analysis

**The Foundation: Benevolent Dictator or Governance Risk?** The Novo Nordisk Foundation controls 77% of votes with only 28% of economic ownership through a dual-class A/B share structure. In theory, this provides long-term stability — the Foundation is a charitable entity focused on scientific research, not a quarterly-earnings-obsessed hedge fund. In practice, 2025 revealed the dark side: when the Foundation decided the board was not moving fast enough to address competitive threats, it simply replaced everyone. Chairman Helge Lund, Vice Chairman Henrik Poulsen, and five independent directors did not stand for re-election after failing to agree with the Foundation on board composition. The Foundation installed former CEO Lars Rebien Sorensen as chair and added three new members. This is extraordinary. The Foundation is pledging to increase independent board members to at least four by the March 2026 AGM, but the message is clear — minority shareholders have no real say when the Foundation decides to act. The replacement of the CEO one month before the board overhaul reinforces that the Foundation is now running the company directly, not through arm's-length governance.

**Capital Allocation: Mostly Rational, with One Major Question Mark.** On the positive side, Novo Nordisk has a track record of consistent buybacks (DKK 20B in 2024, DKK 15B authorized for 2026) that have steadily shrunk the share count by about 1% per year. The dividend payout ratio sits at a sensible ~48-50% of earnings, with steady growth (DKK 3.75 interim dividend in 2025, up 7% from 2024). R&D spending jumped to ~16.5% of revenue in 2024, up from ~14% in 2023, reflecting heavy investment in the GLP-1 pipeline and next-generation obesity treatments. These are all signs of a company reinvesting intelligently. The major question is the $11 billion Catalent site acquisition. Buying manufacturing capacity from your own parent company is inherently conflicted, even with a fairness opinion from Evercore. The strategic logic is sound — fill-finish capacity was the bottleneck holding back Wegovy supply. But the price raises eyebrows, and the Bloomington, Indiana site received an FDA warning letter in November 2025 for serious cGMP violations including contamination issues (mammalian hair in vial stoppers, pest infestations). This suggests Novo Nordisk may have overpaid for assets with known quality problems, and the mid-single-digit drag on 2025 operating profit growth exceeded initial low-single-digit estimates.

**Compensation: Real Pain When Performance Lags.** To Novo Nordisk's credit, executive compensation genuinely fell when the company underperformed. Total executive remuneration dropped 38% in 2025 to DKK 193.3 million as LTIP programs tracked below targets. Former CEO Jorgensen's pay had already declined from DKK 68.2M in 2023 to DKK 57.1M in 2024. New CEO Doustdar received DKK 20.7M for his partial 2025 year (annualized ~DKK 34.6M), meaningfully below predecessor levels. Stock-based compensation for the whole company was $332M in 2024, which against ~$42B in revenue is less than 1% — extremely modest by pharma standards. The LTIP structure uses three-year performance periods with two-year holding requirements for 40% of shares, which encourages long-term thinking. However, the specific performance metrics are tied to broad "Strategic Aspirations" rather than explicit per-share value metrics like ROIC or free cash flow per share. This is a minor concern — the targets are clearly not sandbagged given the below-target payouts in 2025.

**Candor: Improving Under Duress.** Novo Nordisk's guidance track record in 2024-2025 was poor. The company repeatedly cut guidance through 2025 — from initial expectations of strong double-digit growth, down to 13-21% sales growth in May, then 8-14% in Q3, with actual results of 10% sales growth and 6% operating profit growth. The 2026 outlook is even more sobering: adjusted sales and operating profit growth both expected at -5% to -13% at constant exchange rates, reflecting pricing pressure from the Most Favored Nations agreement, compounded GLP-1 competition, and Medicaid coverage reductions. The serial guidance cuts under Jorgensen were a red flag — the company was chronically optimistic. To the new management's credit, the 2026 guidance appears deliberately conservative, and Doustdar's early communication has been more direct. His "three non-negotiable priorities" framework (commercial execution, pipeline progression, financial discipline) and the swift restructuring suggest a more candid, action-oriented approach. The real test will be whether 2026 guidance proves achievable.

**Insider Ownership: A Weakness.** At approximately 1.48% insider ownership, executives do not have meaningful skin in the game through personal share purchases. In 2024-2025, multiple executives sold shares (including former CEO Jorgensen disposing of ~34K shares at $86-87 in February 2025), and no notable insider buying was recorded. This is partly structural — the Foundation's 77% voting control means executive ownership will never be a meaningful governance lever. But the absence of insider buying during the stock's significant decline (from peak levels) is notable. When a stock drops 40%+ and no insider buys, it tells you something.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The Foundation's aggressive intervention — replacing the CEO, restructuring the board, installing a proven operator as chair — could be exactly the wake-up call needed, and the new management's swift cost cuts and conservative guidance suggest realistic, disciplined stewardship going forward.
- **Bear case:** The Foundation's willingness to override the board reveals that minority shareholders have no real governance protection; combined with the conflicted Catalent transaction, FDA quality issues at acquired sites, and zero insider buying, this is a company where outside shareholders must trust a single controlling entity to act in their interest.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The management regime is less than a year old, making it impossible to judge Doustdar's capital allocation skills over a full cycle. The Foundation's track record is long and generally positive, but the 2025 board takeover was unprecedented and concerning.

## Red Flags

- The Novo Nordisk Foundation forced a complete board overhaul in October 2025, demonstrating that independent governance is an illusion when the Foundation disagrees with the board. Minority shareholders have no real check on Foundation power.
- The $11 billion related-party Catalent site acquisition from Novo Holdings creates an inherent conflict of interest, even with a fairness opinion. The FDA warning letter at Bloomington within months of closing suggests due diligence may have been insufficient.
- No insider buying during a 40%+ stock decline in 2024-2025. Multiple executives were net sellers. This does not signal confidence from people who know the business best.
- Serial guidance cuts throughout 2025 indicate either poor forecasting or chronic over-optimism under the prior CEO.
- The 2026 outlook of -5% to -13% sales and profit decline represents a sharp deceleration for a company that was recently a hyper-growth story, raising questions about whether the GLP-1 competitive moat is narrowing.

## Score: 5 / 10

Novo Nordisk scores average on management and capital allocation. The positives are real — sensible buybacks, low SBC, sustainable dividends, high R&D investment, and genuine pay-for-performance. But the negatives are also real — a Foundation that overrides governance when it chooses, a conflicted $11B related-party deal, zero insider buying, serial guidance misses, and a brand-new untested CEO. The Foundation's long-term orientation is valuable, but the 2025 board takeover showed minority shareholders have no seat at the table when it matters. This is a company in transition, and the jury is out on whether new management will be good stewards or just the Foundation's operators.
