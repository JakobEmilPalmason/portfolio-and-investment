## Detailed Analysis

**What does this company sell, and to whom?** Novo Nordisk sells pharmaceutical drugs, primarily for diabetes and obesity. Its flagship products are Ozempic (semaglutide injection for type 2 diabetes), Wegovy (semaglutide injection for obesity/weight management), and Rybelsus (oral semaglutide for diabetes). It also sells older insulin products (NovoLog, Levemir, Tresiba) and has a smaller Rare Disease division covering hemophilia and growth disorders (DKK 19.6 billion in 2025 sales). Its customers are patients with chronic metabolic conditions, but the actual buyers are healthcare systems, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), hospitals, and government payers. In the US, three major PBMs (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) effectively control formulary access. Internationally, national health systems and government procurement agencies are the key buyers.

**How does it actually make money?** This is a classic pharmaceutical business model: spend heavily on R&D to develop patented drugs, then sell them at high margins for the duration of patent protection. Revenue comes from recurring prescription refills for chronic conditions -- a patient on Ozempic or Wegovy typically stays on it indefinitely, creating predictable recurring revenue. In 2025, Obesity Care generated DKK 82.3 billion (up 31% at constant exchange rates), Diabetes Care was roughly flat (insulin at DKK 53.1 billion), and Rare Disease contributed DKK 19.6 billion. The company's gross margins are typical of branded pharma, though net realized prices are under severe pressure. The MFN pricing agreement with the US government has cut listed prices on key products by roughly 65%, and this will flow through 2026 results.

**What are the key drivers?** There are four variables that matter most for Novo Nordisk right now: (1) **Net realized pricing** -- the MFN deal and PBM rebate negotiations are the single biggest near-term driver; Wegovy/Ozempic going from ~$1,000/month to ~$350/month in the US fundamentally changes the unit economics. (2) **Volume growth in GLP-1 obesity** -- the global branded GLP-1 obesity market grew 104% by volume in 2025, and Novo holds a 59.6% branded volume share; but volume must grow fast enough to offset price cuts. (3) **Competitive share vs. Eli Lilly** -- Lilly's tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) has proven superior in head-to-head trials and is gaining share rapidly; Novo's global GLP-1 market share fell from 55.7% to 49.3% by mid-2025. (4) **Pipeline execution** -- oral semaglutide (as Wegovy pill) is expected to launch soon, and next-generation molecules like CagriSema are critical to maintaining relevance. Regulatory outcomes on these pipeline assets will shape the next 3-5 years.

**How predictable is the revenue?** Moderately predictable with a significant caveat. The underlying demand is highly recurring -- diabetes and obesity are chronic conditions requiring ongoing treatment, and patients rarely discontinue voluntarily. However, the revenue per patient is now subject to large, discrete pricing shocks (MFN deal, formulary renegotiations, patent expiries in international markets). In 2025, Novo Nordisk had to narrow its guidance four times due to shifting pricing dynamics and competitive trends. The 2026 guidance range of negative 5-13% is unusually wide for a company of this scale, reflecting genuine uncertainty about how pricing and volume will net out. This is not a business where you can confidently project next year's revenue within a tight band right now.

**Who are the customers and how concentrated are they?** Customer concentration is a real factor. In the US (roughly half of revenue), three PBMs control the vast majority of commercial formulary access, giving them enormous negotiating leverage. The US government (Medicare, Medicaid, VA) is an increasingly important payer, especially with MFN pricing now in effect. Internationally, single-payer systems in Europe and government procurement in emerging markets mean a small number of decision-makers control large revenue pools. Novo Nordisk has attempted to diversify channels -- launching NovoCare Pharmacy to sell Wegovy directly at $499/month and pursuing direct-to-employer models -- but these are nascent. Switching costs for patients are moderate: moving from semaglutide to tirzepatide requires a new prescription but is clinically straightforward, and payers can shift formulary preference with a single contract renegotiation.

**Can you explain the business to someone in 2 minutes?** Yes, easily. Novo Nordisk makes drugs that treat diabetes and help people lose weight. Their biggest product, Ozempic, is a weekly injection for type 2 diabetes. Wegovy is essentially the same drug approved for weight loss. These drugs work by mimicking a natural hormone (GLP-1) that controls blood sugar and appetite. The company makes money because patients need these drugs continuously -- they are treating chronic conditions, not curing them. The business is simple to understand. What is harder to understand right now is the pricing environment: the US government has forced dramatic price cuts, a major competitor (Eli Lilly) has a product that works better, and patents are starting to expire in some countries. The underlying business model is clear; the near-term economics are in flux.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** GLP-1 obesity treatment is still in early innings of global adoption, volume growth overwhelms pricing pressure, and Novo's pipeline (oral Wegovy, CagriSema) maintains its market leadership position while the $350 price point actually accelerates adoption.
- **Bear case:** Eli Lilly's superior efficacy captures the majority of new GLP-1 starts, MFN pricing permanently compresses margins, generic semaglutide enters major markets by the early 2030s, and Novo becomes the "legacy insulin company" of the GLP-1 era.
- **Confidence:** Medium -- The business model is completely understandable, but the pricing/competitive dynamics are shifting faster than historical norms for pharma, making forward estimates unusually uncertain.

## Red Flags

- 2026 guidance of negative 5-13% sales growth is a dramatic reversal from years of double-digit growth; management credibility on forecasting has eroded after four guidance revisions in 2025.
- The MFN pricing agreement represents a permanent structural reduction in US unit economics, not a temporary headwind.
- Eli Lilly's tirzepatide has demonstrated clinical superiority and is gaining market share; Novo's semaglutide may be the second-best product in its most important category.
- The 9,000-person layoff and $1.3 billion cost-cutting program under a new CEO suggest the prior cost structure was not sustainable at lower price points.
- Semaglutide patent expiries in Canada, India, and China in 2026 open the door to generic competition in large markets years before US patent expiry.
- The company's foundation ownership structure (Novo Nordisk Foundation controls ~28% of share capital with ~76% of votes) limits shareholder influence on strategic decisions.

## Score: 8 / 10

The business model is highly understandable -- Novo Nordisk sells branded drugs for chronic conditions, generating recurring revenue from ongoing prescriptions. A non-expert can grasp how it makes money in under two minutes. The complexity lies not in the business model itself but in the rapidly shifting pricing and competitive environment, which introduces legitimate uncertainty about the magnitude (though not the direction) of future revenue. This is a clear, comprehensible business facing a complicated moment -- the "what they do" deserves a 9, but the "what happens next" knocks it down a point.
