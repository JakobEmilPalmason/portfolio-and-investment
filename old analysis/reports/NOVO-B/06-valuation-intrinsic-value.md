## Detailed Analysis

**Owner Earnings Today.** Novo Nordisk reported 2025 net income of DKK 102.4 billion and operating profit of DKK 127.7 billion (depressed by roughly DKK 8 billion in one-time restructuring charges). However, owner earnings — net income plus depreciation/amortization minus maintenance capex — are significantly lower than net income suggests, because the company is in a massive capital spending cycle. Total capex in 2025 was approximately DKK 90 billion (DKK 60 billion PP&E plus DKK 30 billion intangibles), and free cash flow was just DKK 28.3 billion. Not all of that capex is maintenance — a large portion is growth investment to expand manufacturing for the GLP-1 ramp. If we estimate maintenance capex at roughly DKK 20-25 billion (a fraction of the DKK 60 billion PP&E spend, reflecting what it takes to sustain current operations rather than expand), and depreciation/amortization at roughly DKK 17-20 billion, then owner earnings are approximately DKK 95-100 billion. This is roughly in line with net income, because D&A and maintenance capex roughly offset. But if the growth capex does not earn adequate returns — a real risk given CagriSema's setback and pricing headwinds — then the true economic cost is higher and owner earnings are overstated. For this analysis, I will use DKK 95 billion as a reasonable starting point for normalized owner earnings in 2025.

**Scenario Analysis.** The critical question is where owner earnings land in 2030-2031 (Year 5). Three scenarios:

*Bear case (15% probability):* GLP-1 competition intensifies sharply. Eli Lilly's tirzepatide and orforglipron take significant share. MFN pricing and international exclusivity losses permanently compress margins. Novo's operating margin falls from the current roughly 40% to 30%. Revenue stagnates at roughly DKK 280-300 billion. Owner earnings drop to around DKK 65-70 billion. Apply a 12x multiple (mature pharma with declining competitive position) = enterprise value of DKK 780-840 billion. Discounted back at 10% = roughly DKK 520 billion today, or about DKK 115-120 per share. This implies roughly 50% downside from here.

*Base case (60% probability):* The 2026 revenue decline of 5-10% is a trough. The Wegovy pill gains traction, international expansion continues, and CagriSema gets approved (even if not best-in-class, it still adds to the portfolio). Revenue recovers to DKK 330-360 billion by 2030. Operating margins stabilize at 35-38% (lower than peak due to pricing pressure, but still excellent). Owner earnings reach DKK 100-115 billion. Apply a 16-18x multiple (quality pharma with moderate growth) = enterprise value of DKK 1,600-2,070 billion. Discounted back at 10% = DKK 1,050-1,350 billion, or roughly DKK 235-300 per share. This range brackets the current price, suggesting the stock is roughly fairly valued under base assumptions.

*Bull case (25% probability):* The GLP-1 market proves far larger than current penetration suggests (only a fraction of eligible patients are on treatment globally). Novo maintains 40%+ share of an expanding market. Oral Wegovy opens a massive new patient pool. CagriSema, even at 23% weight loss, finds a differentiated niche. Revenue grows to DKK 400-450 billion by 2030. Margins hold at 38-42%. Owner earnings reach DKK 140-165 billion. Apply a 20-22x multiple (market leader in structural growth category) = enterprise value of DKK 2,800-3,630 billion. Discounted back at 10% = DKK 1,840-2,390 billion, or roughly DKK 410-535 per share. This implies 70-120% upside.

*Probability-weighted value:* (0.15 x DKK 118) + (0.60 x DKK 268) + (0.25 x DKK 473) = roughly DKK 296 per share. Against a current price of roughly DKK 245, this suggests approximately 20% upside, though with wide uncertainty.

**Multiples in Context.** The current trailing P/E of roughly 11-13x is extraordinary for Novo Nordisk. Over the past five years, the stock has traded at a median P/E of roughly 22-32x. The current EV/EBITDA of roughly 8-11x compares to a 13-year median of 17.6x and peers like Eli Lilly at 33x and J&J at 15x. The P/FCF ratio at roughly 28x (based on depressed 2025 FCF of DKK 28.3 billion) is misleading because FCF is temporarily suppressed by growth capex. On normalized owner earnings of DKK 95 billion, the market cap of DKK 1,100 billion implies roughly 11.6x owner earnings. For a business that still generates 40%+ operating margins, has a dominant position in a large and growing therapeutic area, and has been growing revenue 10-25% annually for three years, 11-12x is a genuinely cheap multiple. What is the multiple implying? At 11-12x, the market is pricing in roughly zero growth and some risk of earnings decline. That is essentially a "value trap" or "ex-growth pharma" multiple. If that is correct — if Novo is genuinely entering secular decline — then the multiple is fair. If not, there is a meaningful gap between price and value.

**Reverse-Engineering the Current Price.** At DKK 245 per share and roughly 4.45 billion shares, the market cap is approximately DKK 1,090 billion. If you apply a 12x multiple to this, the implied owner earnings are DKK 91 billion — roughly what Novo earns today. In other words, the market is paying for today's earnings and assigning zero value to future growth. For this to be correct, you would need to believe that (a) GLP-1 revenue is at or near its peak, (b) competition will erode Novo's share and pricing power materially, and (c) the massive capex program will not generate adequate returns. Points (a) and (b) are plausible risks, but point (c) is hard to square with the underlying market opportunity. The GLP-1 obesity market is still in early innings globally — penetration is low, oral formulations are just launching, and new indications (cardiovascular, MASH, kidney disease) are expanding the addressable market. The bearish thesis requires believing that all of this growth will flow primarily to competitors, not to Novo. That is possible but not the most likely outcome.

**Implied Expectations vs. Reality.** The market is pricing in a near-worst-case scenario. Novo is being valued as if it were a mature, ex-growth pharmaceutical company with patent cliffs and declining revenue. While the 2026 guidance is genuinely concerning (first revenue decline in over a decade), the structural drivers of the GLP-1 market remain intact. The MFN pricing impact is real but largely priced in. The CagriSema setback is disappointing but CagriSema at 23% weight loss is still a highly effective drug — it simply is not best-in-class versus tirzepatide. The Wegovy pill launch in January 2026 provides a credible volume offset. The market appears to be pricing in pessimism rather than perfection, which is the opposite of where Novo traded 18 months ago (at 40-50x earnings, pricing in perfection). The pendulum has swung from one extreme to the other.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The GLP-1 market is still in early innings, Novo's oral Wegovy opens a massive new patient pool, and the current 11-12x earnings multiple re-rates to 18-20x as the 2026 trough proves temporary, delivering 70-100% upside over 3-5 years.
- **Bear case:** Competition from Eli Lilly's superior pipeline, permanent MFN-driven margin compression, and semaglutide exclusivity losses cause a structural decline in earnings to DKK 65-70 billion, justifying or even exceeding the current discount — with 30-50% further downside.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The financial data is current and well-sourced, but the range of outcomes is genuinely wide; the 2026 guidance introduces real uncertainty about the trajectory, and the competitive dynamics in GLP-1 are shifting rapidly.

## Red Flags

- Novo Nordisk is guiding for a 5-13% decline in sales AND operating profit in 2026 — the first decline in over a decade. This is not a one-off; it reflects structural pricing and competitive pressures.
- CagriSema lost the head-to-head trial vs. tirzepatide, weakening the pipeline narrative and Novo's claim to best-in-class obesity treatment.
- Total capex of DKK 90 billion in 2025 (nearly equal to net income) signals an enormous investment cycle. If GLP-1 demand disappoints, these become stranded assets.
- Semaglutide exclusivity losses in Canada, Brazil, and China in 2026 introduce generic/biosimilar risk in key international markets.
- MFN pricing reduces US semaglutide prices from roughly $1,000-$1,350/month to $350/month for Medicaid/TrumpRx patients, a roughly 65-75% haircut on an expanding segment of the payer mix.
- Free cash flow was only DKK 28.3 billion in 2025 vs. DKK 102.4 billion in net income — the gap is enormous and makes dividends/buybacks increasingly dependent on the capex cycle moderating.

## Score: 7 / 10

The stock is modestly undervalued at current levels, with the market pricing in a near-worst-case outcome for a business that still dominates a structurally growing therapeutic category, generates 40% operating margins, and trades at a historically deep discount to its own valuation range and its primary peer (Eli Lilly at 45x). The risks are real — 2026 will be a difficult year and competitive positioning has weakened — but the probability-weighted expected value suggests roughly 20% upside with meaningful further upside if the bear case does not materialize. This is not a screaming bargain (which would require a score of 9-10), but it is a price where patient investors are being compensated for the uncertainty.
