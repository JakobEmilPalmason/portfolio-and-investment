## Detailed Analysis

**Debt Levels and Structure.** Orsted carries significant debt appropriate to its role as a large-scale infrastructure developer. Total debt was reported at approximately DKK 83.2 billion in 2024 (with total shareholder equity of DKK 96.7 billion, implying a debt-to-equity ratio of 86%), rising to approximately DKK 91.8 billion by March 2025. Net debt (subtracting DKK 22.9 billion in cash and short-term investments in 2024) was approximately DKK 60 billion, suggesting a net debt/equity ratio of around 62%. Against adjusted EBITDA of DKK 24.8 billion, net debt/EBITDA was approximately 2.4-2.5x at end-2024, which is reasonable for a utility. The concern is not the current snapshot but the trajectory: before the rights issue, debt was rising faster than EBITDA as development capex continued. Specific maturity data is partially available: Orsted has bonds maturing from 2026 through 2042, including EUR 3.625% Notes due 2026, GBP 2.125% Notes due 2027, and longer-dated paper. The revolving credit facilities mature in September 2027 and October 2028, providing near-term liquidity backstop. The mix of fixed-rate bonds and these floating-rate credit facilities means Orsted has some exposure to interest rate movements, though the long-dated bond portfolio is predominantly fixed rate.

**Interest Coverage.** Based on 2024 data, EBIT was approximately DKK 15.2 billion against interest expense, yielding an interest coverage ratio of approximately 6.1x. This is technically above the 5x "comfortable" threshold. However, this measure is sensitive to impairment treatment: including the DKK 15.6 billion in 2024 impairments, reported EBIT was only DKK 6.2 billion, which would imply a coverage ratio of roughly 2.5x — below the 3x warning level. The critical S&P credit metric, FFO-to-net-debt, declined to 13.9% in H1 2025 (compared to 22% in H1 2024), far below the 25% floor S&P requires for BBB. This is the metric that drove the August 2025 downgrade to BBB-. Any further deterioration could push Orsted into sub-investment-grade territory (BB+), which would significantly increase borrowing costs and potentially trigger covenant breaches.

**Liquidity.** Cash and short-term investments were DKK 22.9 billion at end-2024. The two EUR 2 billion revolving credit facilities (approximately DKK 30 billion combined) add material backup liquidity, for a total liquidity buffer of around DKK 53 billion. This is substantial and covers near-term maturities. The completion of the DKK 60 billion rights issue in November 2025 further improved the liquidity position, though the proceeds are earmarked to fund the Sunrise Wind project rather than sitting as unrestricted cash. Current ratio data was not available in the search results. Orsted's stated funding strategy specifically emphasizes minimizing refinancing risk by diversifying funding sources and maintaining undrawn committed credit lines — this is consistent with a company that knows its heavy reliance on capital markets is a structural vulnerability.

**Refinancing Risk.** This is Orsted's most significant balance sheet risk. The company is unambiguously dependent on capital markets being open. With DKK 48-55 billion in annual gross capex, operating cash flow of DKK 24-25 billion (EBITDA level, before interest, tax, and working capital), and farm-downs contributing variable amounts, Orsted requires continuous access to bond markets, bank credit, project finance, and equity markets. The 2025 rights issue was the clearest demonstration of this dependency: when the Sunrise Wind farm-down stalled (because no buyer could be found at an acceptable price), the company had no organic path to fund the project and was forced into a DKK 60 billion equity raise at highly dilutive terms. S&P's warning is explicit — if FFO/debt does not recover above 25% by 2025/2026, further downgrades follow. A BBB- rating (current) is one notch from junk; falling below investment grade would trigger forced selling by investment-grade-only bond investors, spike borrowing costs, and could cascade into a genuine liquidity crisis.

**Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations.** Operating lease obligations exist but were not quantified in available search results. The most important off-balance-sheet exposure is construction commitments and cancellation fee obligations. In 2023, Orsted set aside provisions of up to DKK 11 billion ($1.55 billion) for Ocean Wind contract cancellation fees — the final settled amount came in better than feared, with a DKK 7.3 billion net reversal booked in 2024. However, the episode illustrates that cancellation fees on large infrastructure contracts can be significant contingent liabilities. The company has pension obligations (standard for a large Danish corporation) but specific figures were not found in search results.

**Stress Test: 30% Revenue Drop for Two Years.** Orsted's 2024 revenue was DKK 71 billion. A 30% cut would reduce revenue to approximately DKK 50 billion. At current EBITDA margins (adjusted ~35%), EBITDA would fall to roughly DKK 17-18 billion. Against net debt of approximately DKK 60 billion post-rights-issue, net debt/EBITDA would spike to 3.3-3.5x. Interest costs on DKK 83 billion gross debt at an average rate of approximately 3-4% would be DKK 2.5-3.3 billion — so EBITDA would still cover interest, but barely. The more critical constraint is capex: Orsted cannot easily stop building wind farms mid-construction without massive cancellation fees and impairments. In a severe stress scenario, the company would need to tap its credit facilities, issue further equity, or sell assets under duress. Given the BBB- rating, a 30% revenue shock would likely result in a sub-investment-grade credit rating and forced equity issuance — survival would depend on government or major shareholder support (the Danish state owns approximately 50.1% of Orsted through Ørsted State). That state ownership is a genuine safety net that private companies do not have.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The DKK 60 billion rights issue and reduced capex programme stabilize the balance sheet, FFO/debt recovers above 25% by 2026, S&P restores a stable BBB outlook, and Orsted funds its remaining pipeline through operating cash flow and disciplined farm-downs without further equity issuance.
- **Bear case:** Farm-down market remains difficult, FFO/debt fails to recover, S&P cuts Orsted to BB+ (junk), borrowing costs spike, and the Danish state is forced to either recapitalize the company again or allow significant asset sales at distressed prices.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The rights issue has reduced near-term liquidity risk substantially, and state ownership provides an implicit backstop; but the credit rating trajectory (BBB+ to BBB to BBB- in three years) and continued dependence on capital markets availability make this a balance sheet with real structural vulnerability.

## Red Flags

- Credit rating downgraded three notches in three years (BBB+ to BBB-), currently one notch above junk — a further downgrade would be catastrophic for funding costs.
- FFO-to-net-debt of 13.9% is far below the 25% floor required for investment-grade rating maintenance; the company is operating outside the metrics S&P considers acceptable.
- The DKK 60 billion rights issue in 2025 was an emergency measure forced by the inability to sell assets — this is not a sign of balance sheet strength but of structural dependence on external capital.
- DKK 44 billion in cumulative impairments over 2023-2024 has permanently eroded the equity base and creditworthiness without commensurate cash recovery.
- Orsted cannot meaningfully survive a capital market shutdown: annual funding requirements (~DKK 25-30 billion net of operating cash flow) are too large to bridge from internal sources alone.
- Specific maturity schedule beyond headline bond maturities was not available — refinancing risk of near-term maturities (EUR notes due 2026, GBP notes due 2027) needs monitoring.

## Score: 3 / 10

Orsted's balance sheet is structurally strained — a BBB- credit rating one notch from junk, FFO/debt at 13.9% vs a 25% floor, forced equity dilution in 2025, and an ongoing dependency on capital markets that would be dangerous in a risk-off environment; the state ownership backstop and DKK 53 billion liquidity buffer prevent this from being a 1-2, but this is not a balance sheet that provides safety margin for an investor.
