## Detailed Analysis

**Insider ownership and skin in the game.** Meaningful personal insider ownership is essentially absent at Orsted in the traditional sense. The dominant owner is the Danish state (50.1% voting rights), which holds its stake through Energinet and the broader state holding mechanism. Professional management and board members hold small equity positions typical of Danish governance. The new CEO Rasmus Errboe does not hold a controlling or significant personal economic stake. This means management's incentives are not aligned with shareholders in the owner-operator sense that Buffett values — management is ultimately accountable to a state shareholder whose priorities include energy security, employment, and green transition policy objectives, not solely total shareholder return.

**Capital allocation track record.** The record of the past five years is materially poor. The decisive failure was the aggressive expansion into the US offshore wind market: Orsted committed DKK 28.4 billion in impairments in 2023 alone (cancellation of Ocean Wind 1 and 2 in New Jersey) and a further DKK 15.6 billion in 2024 (Sunrise Wind delays, seabed lease write-downs, interest rate impacts). Total US-related capital destruction exceeded DKK 44 billion across 2023-2024. The proximate causes were rising interest rates and adverse US regulatory conditions, but the root cause was management committing massive irreversible capital to a market where regulatory risk (Jones Act, OREC adjustment rules, permitting timelines) and macroeconomic sensitivity (long-duration IRR model at the margin vs. interest rates) were clearly elevated. Simultaneously, the prior CEO presided over a DKK 270 billion investment plan to 2030 that was subsequently cut by 25%. The counter-argument is that European operations continued to execute well — EBITDA from offshore sites was resilient at ~DKK 24 billion annually — but the US failure was large enough to wipe out years of European operating value creation.

**Compensation design.** Specific compensation details are not available in public web searches, but Danish governance norms and Orsted's prior disclosures indicate management compensation is tied to EBITDA, ROCE, and safety/sustainability metrics. The suspension of dividends and massive rights issue dilution (DKK ~60 billion) suggests there was no mechanism to claw back compensation from the executives responsible for the US capital misallocation. The ousting of CEO Nipper in January 2025 — after the share fell ~83% from its peak — was reputational accountability rather than financial clawback. The new CEO's appointment from within raises questions about whether the compensation and incentive structure has actually changed.

**Candor and consistency.** Credit where it is due: Orsted has been relatively transparent in acknowledging its failures. The company issued clear public statements on each impairment event, named the specific causal factors (interest rates, OREC adjustments, Jones Act vessel constraints, seabed lease write-downs), and did not attempt to obscure the scale of losses. Guidance has historically been met or beaten at the operating level — DKK 24.8 billion EBITDA in 2024 vs. guidance of DKK 24-26 billion; DKK 25.1 billion in 2025 vs. guidance of DKK 24-27 billion (though the range was narrowed mid-year after H2 wind speed guidance reduction). However, the company's multi-year strategic guidance (DKK 270 billion investment plan through 2030, capacity targets) has been significantly revised downward, which represents a consistency failure at the strategic level even if annual operating guidance is honest.

**Governance structure.** Orsted has a two-tier board structure: a Supervisory Board (Board of Directors) with majority independent members plus employee representatives, and an Executive Board (Group President/CEO and registered executives). The Danish state, as 50.1% voting rights holder, has de facto veto power over all major decisions including capital raises, dividend policy, and strategic direction. This is not a traditional dual-class structure (the state's shares are ordinary shares representing economic ownership), but the practical effect is that minority shareholders cannot override state preferences. The Chairman Thomas Thune Andersen stepped down following the 2024 annual report period — the February 2024 capital markets update noted he would not stand for re-election at the upcoming AGM. This was itself a governance accountability response to the impairment crisis. Employee board representation (as required by Danish corporate law) adds a further stakeholder voice that may not always align with shareholder returns.

**Do they talk like owners or politicians?** Largely like managers of a quasi-public utility. The language in strategic communications emphasizes "green transition," "energy security," "Denmark's contribution to European renewable targets" alongside financial metrics. This is not inherently wrong for a company with a 50% state owner, but it means investors should expect capital allocation to sometimes reflect policy priorities. The February 2026 announcement describing the year as a "defining year" after "navigating a challenging industry" is measured rather than bluntly accountable. The new CEO Errboe's public framing emphasizes "stricter value-driven capital allocation" and disciplined investment — appropriate given the failures, but it remains language rather than demonstrated track record.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** The management crisis has been acknowledged and acted upon — CEO replaced, investment plan cut 25%, US market exited, balance sheet repaired via rights issue, dividends reinstated from 2026 — and the business is now managed by someone with deep institutional knowledge who has explicitly committed to capital discipline.
- **Bear case:** The new CEO was part of the same management team that made the US decisions, the state's 50% ownership means capital will occasionally be deployed for non-commercial reasons, and shareholders have already absorbed DKK 44+ billion in impairments and a massively dilutive rights issue with no clawback mechanism.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The governance structure and state ownership create inherent limits on how owner-operator aligned this management can be; whether the new regime actually practices the capital discipline it professes will only be visible over the next 2-3 years of investment decisions.

## Red Flags

- Orsted's share fell approximately 83% from its peak to the time of CEO replacement — one of the worst capital destruction records in the European utility sector in the 2020s.
- DKK 44+ billion in impairments over 2023-2024 from the US market — management committed irreversible capital to a geography where regulatory and macroeconomic risks were knowable.
- DKK ~60 billion dilutive rights issue in 2025 forced by management's prior over-leverage and over-investment — minority shareholders paid for the mistakes.
- CEO replacement comes from within the same leadership team; no external accountability mechanism.
- State ownership: the controlling shareholder has objectives (energy security, green transition targets, Danish industrial policy) that are not purely financial.
- Dividend suspended for three years (2023-2025); reinstatement from 2026 is a commitment, not yet a demonstrated reality.

## Score: 4 / 10

A track record of severe capital misallocation in the US, a 83% peak-to-trough share price collapse, a dilutive DKK 60 billion rights issue, suspended dividends, and the appointment of an insider successor rather than a clean-break CEO all combine to score well below average on stewardship — offset only modestly by transparent communication and a credible (if overdue) pivot to capital discipline under Errboe.
