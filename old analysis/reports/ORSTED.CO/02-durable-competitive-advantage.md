## Detailed Analysis

**What type of moat exists?** Orsted possesses a narrow-to-moderate moat built primarily from three overlapping sources: (1) **Scale economics and learning curve advantages** — with over 30 years of offshore wind experience, more installed capacity than any non-Chinese competitor, and a fully integrated development-to-operations value chain, Orsted has institutional knowledge about site assessment, turbine installation logistics, cable routing, and operations that newer entrants lack. It has built relationships with the scarce supply of specialized installation vessels and turbine manufacturers. (2) **Regulatory/license positioning** — offshore wind requires governments to grant seabed leases and grid connection rights in specific geographic zones. Orsted's existing license portfolio in the North Sea, Baltic, UK, and Taiwan represents positions that cannot simply be bought. (3) **Vertical integration** — covering development, EPC, and long-term operations within one company reduces transaction costs and creates end-to-end accountability, which has historically enabled Orsted to deliver projects that smaller or less experienced developers cannot.

**How durable is the moat?** The durability is medium and weakening at the margins. The experience advantage was overwhelming 10-15 years ago when Orsted was essentially the only large-scale offshore wind developer. Today, well-capitalized peers — RWE, Vattenfall, Equinor, BP, Shell, and several national utility champions — have accumulated meaningful track records. The UK AR7 result (Orsted received no new contracts while RWE won 6.9 GW) is a concrete signal that the competitive gap is narrowing. The seabed license positions remain valuable for the next 5-10 years in the existing portfolio, but as auctions shift to new geographies (US East Coast exited, focus now on Europe and APAC), Orsted must continuously re-earn its position through bidding. Technology disruption risk in offshore wind itself is low — the technology is mature — but the economics of floating offshore wind (which opens deeper water sites) could partially reset competitive rankings if Orsted does not maintain its development edge.

**Evidence of moat in numbers.** The clearest evidence is Orsted's 30+ year market-leadership position and its ~24% global market share (ex-China) despite the industry growing rapidly and attracting major competitors. On financials, the picture is less compelling: adjusted ROCE of 10.1% in 2024 and 8.4% in 2025, targeting ~13% in 2028-2030. These are decent infrastructure returns but fall short of the 15%+ that characterizes high-quality moats. EBITDA margins at the segment level (approximately 34% on group revenue, offshore wind EBITDA of DKK 24.3 billion in 2025) are reasonable for capital-intensive infrastructure but not exceptional. The company does not exhibit pricing power in the sense of raising prices on incumbents; rather, it demonstrates cost discipline relative to competitors in auction bidding environments. Importantly, Orsted's awarded contracts in its existing European portfolio pre-date the cost inflation of 2021-2023, meaning those returns were locked in at attractive economics.

**Where is the moat weakest?** Three specific weaknesses stand out. First, the **auction bidding process** is zero-sum and price-competitive — Orsted must bid at or near its cost of capital, so any cost disadvantage relative to a competitor (e.g., a state-backed rival with cheaper financing) eliminates the advantage in that tender. Second, the **US exit was a moat failure** — Orsted had committed first-mover capital to US seabed leases, an apparent moat position, but rising interest rates and adverse regulatory changes (lack of OREC adjustment, Jones Act complications, tax credit uncertainty) made the economics collapse. Moats that depend on regulatory benevolence and stable interest rates are fragile. Third, **supply chain constraints** affect Orsted and its competitors roughly equally — Orsted's scale gives some preferential access to key suppliers, but the constraint is industry-wide, limiting differentiation.

**Do advantages reset every product cycle?** Offshore wind is not a product-cycle business — it is a site-and-contract business where each project is unique and long-lived. There is no "next iPhone" moment. However, the shift from fixed-bottom to floating offshore wind (which opens deep-water sites globally) is a genuine technology transition over the next decade that could partially reset competitive positions. Orsted is investing in floating wind R&D, but so are all major competitors. The practical implication is that the current moat from fixed-bottom offshore wind experience does not fully transfer to floating wind, which remains pre-commercial at scale.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Orsted's three-decade head start in offshore wind, integrated value chain, and European seabed license portfolio represent a genuine cost and execution advantage that keeps it competitive in the government auction system that governs this industry for decades.
- **Bear case:** The moat is narrowing faster than expected — the UK AR7 zero allocation and the catastrophic US exit demonstrate that Orsted's competitive advantage does not reliably protect against adverse regulatory and macroeconomic environments, and peers are catching up rapidly.
- **Confidence:** Medium — The existence of some advantage is clear from the historical record, but quantifying its durability and width requires predicting future auction results and regulatory environments that are genuinely uncertain.

## Red Flags

- Zero UK CfD AR7 contract awards in 2025 to Orsted despite being the UK's largest existing offshore wind operator — competitors outcompeted it in its core home market.
- US market exit after DKK 40+ billion in impairments proves that accumulated expertise and license positions are not durable when the macroeconomic and regulatory environment turns adverse.
- Government-set auction prices mean Orsted cannot unilaterally raise prices; in an inflationary cost environment, this becomes an existential rather than a competitive issue.
- Returns (adjusted ROCE ~8-10% currently, targeting 13%) are ordinary infrastructure returns, not wide-moat returns.

## Score: 5 / 10

Orsted has a real but narrowing scale/experience advantage in offshore wind, supported by regulatory license positions, but it cannot demonstrate pricing power or above-normal returns over a sustained period, and the UK AR7 result shows that even in its core market its competitive edge is now contestable.
