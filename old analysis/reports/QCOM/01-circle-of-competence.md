## Detailed Analysis

**What Qualcomm sells and to whom.** Qualcomm designs (but does not manufacture) semiconductor chips and licenses wireless communication patents. Its QCT (Qualcomm CDMA Technologies) segment produces Snapdragon system-on-chips for smartphones, automotive infotainment and ADAS systems, and IoT devices ranging from consumer electronics to industrial routers. Its QTL (Qualcomm Technology Licensing) segment licenses portions of its intellectual property portfolio — primarily patents essential to CDMA, 4G LTE, and 5G NR wireless standards — to handset and device manufacturers worldwide. Customers include every major smartphone OEM (Apple, Samsung, Xiaomi, OPPO, Vivo, Motorola), automotive Tier 1 suppliers (Bosch, Continental, GM), and industrial equipment manufacturers. Qualcomm does not fab its own chips; it is a fabless designer that outsources production to TSMC and Samsung Foundry.

**Revenue model.** QCT earns revenue by selling chips into an arm's-length supply chain. Within QCT, handsets are the dominant sub-segment ($7.8 billion in Q1 FY2026 alone), followed by IoT ($1.7 billion in Q1 FY2026) and Automotive ($1.1 billion in Q1 FY2026). QTL earns royalties calculated as a percentage of the end-device selling price, typically ranging from 2.5% to 5% of the wholesale selling price for devices covered under their licensing terms. QTL requires no chip sale — any manufacturer using CDMA/3G/4G/5G in a covered device owes a royalty. This creates a double revenue stream on many devices: Qualcomm sells the chip *and* collects a royalty. In FY2025, QCT contributed $38.37 billion and QTL contributed $5.58 billion, for a total of approximately $44.3 billion GAAP revenue.

**Key drivers.** The four variables that matter most are: (1) global smartphone unit volumes and ASPs — since both chip sales and licensing royalties scale with device sales; (2) the Apple relationship — Apple is Qualcomm's largest single customer and the renewal of both chip supply and licensing agreements directly affects revenue; (3) automotive and IoT design-win cycles — these are multi-year design-in engagements that provide forward revenue visibility once won, and Qualcomm has publicly guided toward $22 billion in combined Automotive + IoT revenue by FY2029; and (4) the 5G upgrade cycle — faster 5G adoption globally drives both QCT chip content per device and QTL royalty-bearing device volumes.

**Revenue predictability.** QTL is the more predictable segment — royalties are contractually locked in for multi-year periods, geographically diversified, and tied to the installed base of global wireless devices. It is quasi-recurring. QCT revenue is more cyclical and customer-concentrated: it tracks smartphone production volumes, which are subject to inventory correction cycles (as seen in 2023–2024) and OEM decisions on in-house chip development. Handset seasonality follows a strong second-half bias aligned with flagship phone launch cycles. Automotive and IoT revenues are less seasonal but lumpy due to design-win timing. Free cash flow for FY2025 was a record $12.8 billion, reflecting the business's strong cash generation even in a transitional period.

**Customer concentration.** This is a material risk. Apple, Samsung, and Xiaomi each represented more than 10% of FY2025 consolidated revenues — meaning roughly 30–40%+ of revenues could be attributed to three buyers. Apple is both a chip customer (5G modems in iPhones) and a patent licensee. Samsung is likewise both a chip customer (Exynos co-exists with Snapdragon depending on geography and model) and a licensee. This concentration means any deterioration in one relationship — whether Apple transitioning to its own modem chip, or Samsung shifting to in-house Exynos designs — has an outsized income impact. Samsung's Snapdragon content is already expected to decline from 100% to 75% share of its flagships by Q2 FY2026.

**Two-minute explanation.** Qualcomm is a patent-and-chip company that sits at the center of the global wireless device ecosystem. Every time a manufacturer builds a smartphone, laptop, car, or IoT device using modern cellular technology, they likely buy a Qualcomm chip and/or pay Qualcomm a royalty. The chip business is competitive but Qualcomm's Snapdragon line holds premium positioning in Android flagships, with expanding presence in automotive and industrial markets. The patent business is an annuity — it prints cash at 70%+ margins with minimal incremental cost. The risk is that the business is concentrated in three customers and Apple is working to cut Qualcomm out entirely on the chip side while the licensing agreement also faces eventual renegotiation.

## Signal Summary

- **Bull case:** Qualcomm's diversification into automotive and IoT is accelerating, adding durable, long-cycle revenue streams that will partially insulate the business from Apple chip dependency while 5G growth sustains both QCT and QTL globally.
- **Bear case:** Apple successfully deploys its in-house modem at scale, Samsung further reduces Snapdragon content, and QTL royalty rates face regulatory compression, collectively removing the premium economics underpinning the investment thesis.
- **Confidence:** Medium — FY2025 and Q1 FY2026 financials are from official SEC filings and earnings releases; customer concentration data confirmed via 10-K disclosure. The trajectory of Apple modem development and its ultimate chip share impact involves meaningful uncertainty.

## Red Flags

- Apple, Samsung, and Xiaomi each at 10%+ of revenue — customer concentration is among the highest for a large-cap semiconductor company
- Apple chip supply deal expires in 2026; Apple has repeatedly delayed its in-house modem but direction of travel is clear
- Samsung Snapdragon content share declining from FY2026 onward
- Q2 FY2026 guidance came in below consensus, indicating near-term revenue pressure from memory shortage affecting handset OEM demand

## Score: 7 / 10

Qualcomm's two-segment model is understandable and its economics are well-documented, but the heavy customer concentration and structural threat from Apple modem internalization introduce meaningful complexity into the forward revenue picture.
